What Healthy Independence Looks Like

A healthy independent pet displays the following behaviours:

  • Calm when alone. They settle quietly when you leave—whether for an hour or a full workday. No panic, no destructiveness, no endless barking.
  • Content in their own company. They entertain themselves with toys, sleep, or simply observe the world from their favourite spot.
  • Appropriate greeting behaviour. They are happy to see you return, but the reunion is measured—not frantic, not obsessive.
  • Confident in various environments. They adapt well to different spaces, people, and situations without excessive fear or stress.
  • Eating normally. They maintain a healthy appetite whether you are home or away.

Independent does not mean aloof. Many independent pets enjoy affection and interaction—but they are also perfectly comfortable in their own company. This trait is partly personality, partly breed, and partly learned through positive early experiences.

What Separation Anxiety Looks Like

Separation anxiety is different from a pet simply preferring their own company. It is a genuine distress response triggered by the anticipation or experience of being separated from their attachment figure—usually their primary owner.

Signs include:

  • Distress immediately before or after you leave. Panting, pacing, whining, or trembling as you grab your keys or put on your shoes.
  • Excessive vocalisation. Non-stop barking, whining, or meowing that neighbours notice.
  • Destruction. Chewing doors, scratching frames, or tearing furniture—often targeting items that smell like you.
  • Inappropriate toileting. Accidents even in house-trained pets, particularly near exit points (doors, windows).
  • Physical symptoms. Drooling, vomiting, diarrhoea, or loss of appetite when alone.
  • Self-injury. Patching at skin, broken nails from frantic digging.
  • Following you constantly. Shadowing you from room to room, unable to settle when you move to another area.

The key differentiator is intensity and duration. A brief moment of excitement when you return is normal. A pet that cannot settle for hours after you leave, or who shows symptoms every single time you depart, is signalling distress.

Common Causes in NZ Pets

Understanding the root cause helps guide treatment.

Sudden routine changes. Returning to the office after months working from home, moving house, or a new family member (partner, baby, or another pet) can trigger anxiety.

Previous abandonment or loss. Rescue pets, especially those with a history of rehoming, are more prone to attachment issues.

Insufficient early socialisation. Puppies or kittens who were not exposed to periods of alone time during critical development phases may struggle as adults.

Breed predisposition. Certain breeds are more prone to separation anxiety. In dogs, this includes Labrador Retrievers, German Shepherds, and Cavalier King Charles Spaniels. In cats, breeds like the Siamese (known for vocalising) may show higher anxiety.

Medical issues. Sometimes anxiety is rooted in pain or illness. A sudden onset of separation anxiety warrants a veterinary check to rule out underlying health problems.

Building Healthy Independence

The goal is not to ignore your pet, but to help them develop resilience. Here are evidence-based strategies for New Zealand pet owners.

Practice Graduated Departures

Start with extremely short absences—just seconds. Build up slowly over weeks. The aim is to prove to your pet that you always return, and that departures are nothing to fear.

Keep departures low-key. Do not make a drama of leaving—no long goodbyes, no overly emotional farewells. A simple "be right back" in a neutral tone works better than an emotional exchange.

Create Positive Associations with Your Absence

Give special treats only when you leave. A stuffed Kong, a puzzle feeder, or a long-lasting chew takes the focus off your departure. The key is making your absence predict something wonderful.

Consider leaving the television or radio on. Background noise creates a sense of company and masks outside sounds that might trigger anxiety.

Exercise Before Departure

A tired pet is a calm pet. A vigorous walk or play session before you leave helps release pent-up energy and reduces the likelihood of anxious behaviours.

For dogs in NZ, the morning walk is non-negotiable—not just for exercise but for mental stimulation. A dog who has had a enriching morning is far more likely to settle peacefully while you are at work.

Maintain a Consistent Routine

Pets thrive on predictability. Try to keep departure and arrival times roughly consistent. If your schedule varies significantly, help your pet adjust by maintaining set feeding times and walk schedules.

When to Seek Professional Help

If your pet\'s anxiety is affecting their quality of life—or your neighbours\' peace—professional intervention is warranted. Signs that it is time to consult expert help include:

  • Symptoms that do not improve despite consistent training efforts
  • Self-injury or destruction causing property damage
  • Vomiting, diarrhoea, or complete refusal to eat
  • Anxiety that is getting worse over time
  • The stress of leaving your pet is significantly impacting your own life (missing work, avoiding travel)

Your veterinarian can rule out medical causes and may prescribe medication for severe cases. A certified animal behaviourist in New Zealand can develop a tailored desensitisation plan.

Never punish anxious behaviour. Punishment increases fear and makes anxiety worse, not better.

For Pet Sitters: Managing Both Types

When sitting a pet, understanding their personality is essential.

For independent pets: Respect their space. Do not force affection if they prefer not to receive it. They may be content with a comfortable bed, fresh water, and a brief check-in. Give them the freedom to entertain themselves.

For anxious pets: Ask the owner about specific triggers. What helps? What makes it worse? Establish trust slowly—do not rush physical affection. Sit on their level, let them approach you. Maintain the owners routine as closely as possible, including feeding times, walks, and sleep arrangements.

Send owners of anxious pets regular updates. Even a quick photo reassures them that their pet is settling. For independent pets, a brief daily check-in is usually sufficient.
