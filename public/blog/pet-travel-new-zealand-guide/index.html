Pet Travel in New Zealand: Complete Guide 2026

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  March 12, 2026  •  11 min read

New Zealand is one of the world\'s most pet-friendly countries, with stunning landscapes, numerous pet-welcoming accommodations, and a culture that embraces dogs and cats as family. Whether you are planning a weekend getaway to the Bay of Islands, a road trip through the South Island, or flying domestically with your furry friend, this guide covers everything you need for safe, stress-free pet travel in Aotearoa.

1. Preparing Your Pet for Travel

Good preparation is the foundation of stress-free travel. Taking time to get your pet ready before departure will make the journey smoother for everyone.

Health check and vaccinations

Before any trip, ensure your pet is in good health and fit for travel. Book a vet visit if your pet has any underlying conditions, especially heart or respiratory issues that can worsen with stress or temperature changes.

Confirm that vaccinations are up to date, particularly if staying at accommodation or visiting areas with high dog populations. If travelling to certain regions, your vet may recommend additional protections or treatments.

Microchipping and registration

All dogs in New Zealand must be microchipped and registered with their local council under the Dog Control Act. Ensure your contact details are current in the National Dog Database. If you are travelling to a new region, you may need to update your registration.

While cats are not legally required to be microchipped in all areas, it is strongly recommended, especially for travel. A microchip ensures your pet can be identified if they become lost in an unfamiliar area.

Pet carrier requirements

A secure, well-ventilated pet carrier is essential for both car and air travel. Choose a carrier that allows your pet to stand, turn around, and lie down comfortably. For car travel, the carrier should be secured with a seatbelt or placed in a cargo area.

Introduce the carrier well before your trip by leaving it open at home with treats and bedding inside. This turns the carrier into a safe space rather than a source of anxiety.

Tip: For anxious pets, consider a trial run in the car around the block. Short practice trips can help desensitise your pet to the motion and sounds of travel.

2. Road Trips in New Zealand

With winding roads, stunning scenery, and plenty of rest stops, New Zealand is a fantastic country for road trips with pets. Here is how to make the journey safe and comfortable.

Car safety for dogs

Unrestrained pets in vehicles are a safety risk. In New Zealand, dogs should be secured in a crate, carrier, or with a properly fitted dog seatbelt harness. An unsecured dog can become a projectile in an accident or distract the driver.

Never allow your dog to put their head out of the window. While it seems enjoyable, debris, insects, and sudden stops can cause injury. Keep dogs restrained safely inside the vehicle at all times.

Regular rest stops

Plan to stop every 2-3 hours for bathroom breaks, water, and a stretch. New Zealand has numerous rest areas along state highways, including dog-friendly spots where your pet can relieve themselves and walk safely.

Always use a leash when stopping, even in seemingly quiet areas. Your pet may be tempted to chase wildlife or bolt if startled by unfamiliar sounds.

Managing car anxiety

Some pets experience motion sickness or anxiety in cars. Signs include excessive drooling, vomiting, panting, or whining. If your pet is prone to car anxiety:

  • Withhold food 2-3 hours before travel to reduce nausea
  • Keep the car cool and well-ventilated
  • Use calming pheromone sprays (Adaptil for dogs, Feliway for cats) in the car
  • Consider natural calming supplements or speak to your vet about anti-nausea medication
  • Create positive associations with car rides by pairing them with fun destinations

Heat safety

Never leave your pet in a parked car, even on cloudy days. Temperatures inside a car can reach dangerous levels within minutes, causing heatstroke and potentially death. If you need to stop and cannot bring your pet, skip that destination or find pet-friendly alternatives.

Critical: New Zealand summers can see temperatures exceed 30°C in many regions. Always carry fresh water, a collapsible bowl, and know the signs of heatstroke (excessive panting, drooling, vomiting, collapse). If heatstroke is suspected, move your pet to a cool area immediately and contact a vet.

3. Flying with Pets in New Zealand

Air travel with pets in New Zealand is more limited than in many other countries, but it is possible with proper planning.

Air New Zealand pet policy

Air New Zealand allows dogs and cats to travel as checked baggage or cargo on domestic flights within New Zealand. However, there are strict requirements:

  • Pets must be at least 10 weeks old
  • They must be in an approved IATA-compliant container
  • Health certificates from a vet issued within 10 days of travel are required
  • Certain brachycephalic (flat-faced) breeds may face restrictions due to breathing risks

Contact Air New Zealand Cargo directly for current requirements, fees, and availability, as policies can change.

Other domestic airlines

Air New Zealand is the primary domestic carrier accepting pets. Smaller regional airlines generally do not transport pets, so check your specific route in advance. Some charter services may accommodate pets on a case-by-case basis.

Alternative: pet transport services

Professional pet transport companies operate throughout New Zealand and can arrange ground transport for pets between cities. This is often less stressful for animals than flying and may be a better option for anxious pets or those with health concerns.

4. Pet-Friendly Accommodation in NZ

Finding pet-friendly accommodation is generally straightforward in New Zealand, though standards and policies vary significantly.

Holiday parks and camping

Many New Zealand holiday parks welcome pets, particularly those with dog-friendly cabins or sites. Bookpetbook and Kiwi Holiday Parks are useful resources for finding pet-friendly camping options.

When staying at holiday parks, keep your dog controlled in designated areas and clean up after them. Not all sites allow pets, so always confirm before booking.

Hotels and motels

Many motels and hotels in New Zealand are pet-friendly, though policies differ. Chain motels like Comfort & Quality Suites often accept small pets for an additional fee. Luxury hotels are less likely to accommodate pets, though this is changing in some regions.

Always disclose your pet when booking and confirm the specific policy. Some accommodation has size restrictions, breed restrictions, or limits on the number of pets allowed.

Airbnb and holiday homes

Platforms like Airbnb and Bookabach have many pet-friendly options throughout New Zealand. Filter by "pets allowed" when searching. Read reviews carefully to understand the reality of pet policies, as some listings claim to be pet-friendly but have unexpected restrictions.

Farm stays and rural accommodation

New Zealand farm stays are often exceptionally pet-welcoming, as farms naturally accommodate working dogs and pets. These can be wonderful options for pets who enjoy outdoor space and rural environments.

What to bring for accommodation stays

  • Your pet\'s bed or blanket to help them feel at home
  • Enough of their regular food to avoid digestive upset from diet changes
  • Water bowl and familiar water source (some pets are sensitive to new water)
  • Leash, collar with ID tags, and any medications
  • Toys and comfort items
  • Waste bags for cleaning up after your pet
Good etiquette: Always confirm pet policies in writing, keep your pet well-behaved and supervised, and clean up any mess inside or outside the property. Leaving a property in good condition ensures it remains pet-friendly for future guests.

5. Pet-Friendly Activities in New Zealand

One of the joys of travelling with your pet in New Zealand is the abundance of outdoor activities you can enjoy together.

Walking and hiking

New Zealand has thousands of walks suitable for dogs, from easy beach strolls to more challenging tracks. Many Department of Conservation (DOC) tracks allow dogs, though some have restrictions, particularly in sensitive wildlife areas.

Key things to remember:

  • Check track-specific dog access rules before heading out
  • Keep dogs on leads in most areas to protect wildlife
  • Pack water and check conditions, especially in summer heat
  • Be aware of seasonal restrictions, such as during DOC bird nesting season

Beaches

Many New Zealand beaches allow dogs, though rules vary by region and time of year. Some popular dog-friendly beaches include:

  • Off-leash areas in most regional parks
  • Many Northland beaches
  • Selected beaches in Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch
  • East Coast beaches in both islands

Check local council rules, as some beaches have seasonal restrictions or require dogs to be on leads. Always clean up after your pet and be mindful of wildlife, especially shorebirds.

Dog parks

Most New Zealand cities have dedicated off-leash dog parks where your pet can socialise and burn off energy. These are great for letting your dog stretch their legs after a long drive. Popular options include:

  • Auckland: Western Springs, Grey Lynn, and numerous suburb parks
  • Wellington: Frank Kitts, Scott Reserve, and Macalister Park
  • Christchurch: Bottle Lake, Victoria Street, and Pomeroy\'s Pond
  • Dunedin: Andersons Bay and various South Otago parks

6. International Travel with Pets

Travelling internationally with pets from New Zealand requires significant planning, as New Zealand has strict biosecurity requirements for returning animals.

Export requirements

If you plan to take your pet overseas, you will need to meet the import requirements of your destination country. Most countries require:

  • Microchip identification
  • Rabies vaccination and blood titre test
  • Health certificate from an accredited vet
  • Parasite treatments
  • Import permit (for many countries)

Start planning at least 6 months in advance, as some requirements involve waiting periods after vaccinations or tests.

Returning to New Zealand

New Zealand has very strict biosecurity rules to protect our unique environment. Returning pets must:

  • Undergo a minimum stay in an approved country (typically Australia or New Zealand)
  • Complete required vaccinations and treatments
  • Travel on approved flights with documentation
  • Undergo quarantine in some cases

Contact MPI (Ministry for Primary Industries) for the most current requirements before planning international travel with your pet.

7. What to Pack for Pet Travel

Having the right supplies makes travel smoother. Here is a comprehensive packing list for pet travel in New Zealand:

Category Items
Identification Collar with ID tags, microchip confirmation, council registration tag
Health Vaccination records, health certificate, medications, vet contact details
Food and water Regular food, treats, collapsible bowl, water bottle, food bowls
Comfort Pet bed or blanket, favourite toys, familiar-smelling item
Safety Leash, harness or seatbelt, carrier or crate, first aid kit
Hygiene Waste bags, paper towels, pet-safe cleaning spray, grooming supplies

8. Emergency Planning

Before travelling, know how to access veterinary care in the area you are visiting. Save emergency vet clinic numbers for your destination, as after-hours clinics operate differently than in cities.

NZ emergency vet services

Most main centres have 24-hour emergency veterinary clinics. Before your trip, research options along your route and at your destination. If your pet has a pre-existing condition, carry a summary of their medical history and current medications.

Natural hazard awareness

New Zealand is prone to earthquakes, floods, and other natural events. Have a basic emergency plan that includes your pet. Carry a portable pet carrier if you might need to evacuate quickly, and know which areas are safe in your destination region.

9. Your Pet Travel Checklist

  • Book vet check-up and confirm health for travel
  • Ensure vaccinations are current
  • Update microchip and council registration details
  • Research pet-friendly accommodation and book in advance
  • Pack carrier, bed, toys, food, and supplies
  • Practice short car trips before the main journey
  • Plan rest stops every 2-3 hours
  • Carry fresh water and avoid leaving pet in hot cars
  • Save emergency vet contacts for your destination
  • Check local dog access rules for beaches and tracks
  • Use leash in unfamiliar areas
  • Clean up after your pet everywhere you go

Final Thoughts

New Zealand is a magnificent country to explore with your pet. With proper preparation, thoughtful planning, and a few practical precautions, you and your furry companion can enjoy unforgettable adventures together.
