Pet-Safe Pest Control Guide for New Zealand

Published 18 June 2026 — ZiggySitters NZ

New Zealand homes face a range of pests — from fleas and ticks in summer to rodents seeking warmth in winter. But if you share your home with cats, dogs, or other pets, many common pest control products are downright dangerous. This guide covers how to deal with household pests in NZ while keeping your animals safe, plus what to tell your pet sitter before you travel.

Why Pet-Safe Pest Control Matters

Standard pest control products contain chemicals that can cause anything from mild stomach upset to seizures and death in pets. Cats are especially vulnerable because their livers lack the enzymes to process certain compounds that dogs can tolerate. Key risks include:

  • Pyrethrins and permethrins — Found in many fly sprays, ant killers, and flea products. Highly toxic to cats even in small amounts. A dog flea product applied to a cat can be fatal within hours.
  • Anticoagulant rodenticides — Common in rat and mouse baits. If a pet eats the bait directly, or eats a rodent that has consumed it, the result can be life-threatening internal bleeding.
  • Metaldehyde — Used in slug and snail pellets. Extremely toxic to dogs. Even a small amount can cause tremors, seizures, and death.
  • Organophosphates — Older insecticide sprays. Can cause muscle tremors, drooling, and respiratory failure in pets.

The good news is that effective pet-safe alternatives exist for almost every common NZ pest problem.

Flea and Tick Control (Pet-Safe Approach)

Fleas and ticks are the number one pest concern for NZ pet owners, especially in the warmer months from November to April.

On-Your-Pet Treatments (Safe When Used Correctly)

  • Prescription preventatives — NexGard, Bravecto, and Revolution are vet-recommended in NZ. Always use the correct weight range and species-specific product. Never use dog products on cats.
  • Natural alternatives — Products containing diatomaceous earth can help with light infestations. They are not as effective as prescription treatments but are safer around sensitive pets.

In-Home Flea Treatment (Pet-Safe)

  • Vacuum daily — Vacuuming removes up to 50% of flea eggs and larvae. Empty the bag or canister outside immediately after.
  • Wash bedding weekly — Pet bedding, your bedding, and any fabric your pets contact should be washed at 60 degrees Celsius to kill flea eggs.
  • Diatomaceous earth — Sprinkle food-grade DE along baseboards, under furniture, and in cracks. It dehydrates fleas without chemicals. Keep pets away until settled, then vacuum after 24–48 hours.
  • Salt — Fine table salt sprinkled on carpets dehydrates flea larvae. Leave for 24 hours, then vacuum thoroughly. Safe for pets once vacuumed up.
  • Steam cleaning — Professional steam cleaning of carpets kills fleas at all life stages without chemicals. Many NZ carpet cleaning companies offer this service.

What to Avoid for Fleas

  • Bomb or fogger treatments — these leave chemical residue on every surface your pet touches
  • Pyrethrin-based sprays in homes with cats
  • Over-the-counter flea collars — often ineffective and can cause skin reactions

Rodent Control (Mice and Rats)

As the weather cools in autumn, mice and rats move indoors across New Zealand. Here is how to deal with them safely:

Pet-Safe Methods

  • Snap traps in enclosed stations — The most effective and pet-safe approach. Place inside locked bait stations that pets cannot access. Check daily and dispose of caught rodents.
  • Live catch traps — Humane options that catch mice alive. Release them at least 100 metres from your home. Check traps at least twice daily — trapped mice die of stress quickly.
  • Electronic traps — Battery-powered traps that kill instantly. Safe because the trap is enclosed. Brands like Victor are available at Mitre 10 and Bunnings.
  • Seal entry points — The best long-term solution. Mice can squeeze through gaps as small as 6mm. Use steel wool and caulk to seal holes around pipes, vents, and foundations.
  • Peppermint oil — Mice dislike strong scents. Soak cotton balls in peppermint oil and place near potential entry points. This is a deterrent, not a solution for an active infestation.

What to Avoid for Rodents

  • Poison baits (brodifacoum, difenacoum, etc.) — These cause secondary poisoning. Your pet can be poisoned by eating the bait directly OR by eating a rodent that has consumed it. This is the most common cause of pet poisoning in NZ.
  • Glue traps — Inhumane and dangerous. Pets can get stuck on them, causing fur loss, skin damage, and panic injuries.

If you must use a professional pest controller for rodents, specify that you need a pet-safe approach. Many NZ pest control companies now offer non-toxic options.

Ant Control

Ants are a year-round problem in many parts of New Zealand, especially in Auckland and the upper North Island.

Pet-Safe Methods

  • White vinegar — Mix equal parts vinegar and water. Spray along ant trails and entry points. Ants will not cross the vinegar line. Safe once dry.
  • Diatomaceous earth — Sprinkle along ant trails and around entry points. The sharp particles damage ant exoskeletons. Use food-grade only.
  • Cinnamon — Ants avoid cinnamon. Sprinkle ground cinnamon at entry points. Safe for cats and dogs in small amounts.
  • Cornmeal or borax-based ant baits in sealed stations — If you use borax-based baits, ensure they are in enclosed stations that pets cannot open. Place in cabinets or behind appliances where pets cannot reach.
  • Clean food sources — The best ant control is prevention. Store all food in sealed containers, wipe down surfaces immediately, and keep pet food bowls clean between meals.

What to Avoid for Ants

  • Ant sand or granules spread on the ground — pets walk through them and lick their paws
  • Pyrethrin-based ant sprays in homes with cats
  • Open ant bait stations that pets can access

Cockroach Control

Cockroaches are common in warmer parts of NZ, particularly Auckland, Northland, and the Bay of Plenty.

Pet-Safe Methods

  • Boric acid — Mix with sugar as a DIY bait. Place in small lids behind appliances and under cabinets where pets cannot reach. Boric acid is low-toxicity but should still be kept away from pets.
  • Diatomaceous earth — Spread in cracks and under appliances. Effective against cockroaches and safe once settled.
  • Enclosed bait stations — Commercial roach baits in locked stations are pet-safe. Place under sinks, behind the fridge, and in cabinets.
  • Eliminate water sources — Cockroaches need water to survive. Fix leaky taps, dry sinks at night, and reduce humidity in bathrooms.
  • Bay leaves — Place dried bay leaves in kitchen cabinets. A natural deterrent that is completely harmless to pets.

Spider Control

New Zealand has mostly harmless spiders, but the katipo and redback (found in parts of the North Island) are venomous. Most spider control is simple:

  • Regular vacuuming — Removes webs and egg sacs. Empty the vacuum outside.
  • Declutter — Spiders love undisturbed areas. Keep storage areas organised and reduce clutter.
  • Seal gaps — Weather stripping on doors and sealing cracks prevents spiders from entering.
  • Essential oils — Peppermint, eucalyptus, and tea tree oil deter spiders. Mix with water in a spray bottle. Note: tea tree oil is toxic to cats if ingested, so use with caution in cat households.
  • Spider traps — Sticky traps placed along baseboards are non-toxic. Check regularly and dispose of.

Slugs and Snails

A major NZ garden pest, especially in damp regions. The standard control — metaldehyde-based slug pellets — is one of the most dangerous products for dogs.

Pet-Safe Methods

  • Copper tape or mesh — Slugs and snails will not cross copper. Wrap around plant pots and raised garden beds.
  • Beer traps — Sink a small container of beer at ground level. Slugs are attracted to the yeast and fall in. Empty daily. Keep away from curious dogs who might drink the beer.
  • Iron phosphate pellets — Brands like Sluggo and Escar-Go use iron phosphate, which is safe for pets and wildlife. Available at most NZ garden centres.
  • Hand-picking — The most reliable method. Go out after rain or in the evening with a torch. Drop slugs into soapy water.
  • Coffee spray — A weak caffeine solution deters slugs. Mix one part strong coffee with ten parts water and spray on plants.

What to Tell Your Pet Sitter About Pest Control

If you use a pet sitter, clear communication about pest control products in and around your home is essential for your pet’s safety:

  • Document all pest products — Make a list of anything you have in the house: baits, sprays, powders, and where they are placed.
  • Mark areas that are off-limits — If you have bait stations in the garage, under the house, or in ceiling spaces, tell your sitter so they do not accidentally expose your pets.
  • Provide vet contact details — Include your regular vet and the nearest after-hours emergency vet. If you are travelling, your sitter needs to know where to go fast.
  • List symptoms of poisoning — Drooling, vomiting, tremors, lethargy, difficulty breathing, and pale gums are all warning signs. Make sure your sitter knows to seek veterinary help immediately, not wait to see if symptoms improve.
  • Remove or secure hazardous products — Before you leave, lock away all pest control products. Do not assume your sitter will know which cupboard has the ant spray.

Emergency: What to Do If Your Pet Is Exposed

If you suspect your pet has ingested or been exposed to a pest control product:

  1. Call your vet immediately — Do not wait for symptoms. The sooner treatment begins, the better the outcome.
  2. Have the product label ready — Your vet needs to know the active ingredient to provide the right treatment.
  3. Do not induce vomiting — Unless specifically instructed by a vet. Some substances cause more damage coming back up.
  4. Call the NZ Animal Poisons Centre — 0800 TOXICALL (0800 869 422). Available 24/7 for free advice.

Pet-Safe Pest Control Product Checklist

Stock these pet-safe items for common NZ pest problems:

  • Food-grade diatomaceous earth (for fleas, ants, cockroaches)
  • White vinegar (for ants, general cleaning)
  • Iron phosphate slug pellets (for slugs and snails)
  • Enclosed snap traps (for mice and rats)
  • Enclosed bait stations for ants and cockroaches
  • Copper tape (for slug barriers in gardens)
  • Peppermint essential oil (deterrent for ants, spiders, mice)

Products available at NZ retailers like Bunnings, Mitre 10, and pet stores including Animates and Pet.co.nz.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are natural pest control methods as effective as chemical ones?

Natural methods can be very effective for prevention and mild infestations. For severe infestations, you may need professional help — but specify that you need a pet-safe approach. Many NZ pest control companies now offer integrated pest management (IPM) that minimises chemical use.

Can I use dog flea treatment on my cat?

Absolutely not. Dog flea products containing permethrin are lethal to cats. Even small amounts applied to a cat’s skin can cause seizures and death. Always use species-specific products and consult your vet.

How long after spraying insecticide is it safe for pets?

For standard household insecticide sprays, wait until the product is completely dry — typically 2–4 hours. Ventilate the area thoroughly. For professional treatments, ask the technician for their specific re-entry time. Some products require 24 hours or more.

Is diatomaceous earth really safe around pets?

Food-grade diatomaceous earth is safe around pets when used as directed. Avoid breathing the dust (it can irritate lungs). Apply thinly, let it settle, and keep pets out of the area during application. Pool-grade DE is NOT the same and is toxic.

Conclusion

Keeping your New Zealand home pest-free does not have to put your pets at risk. With pet-safe alternatives available for every common pest problem — from fleas and ants to rodents and slugs — there is no reason to use dangerous chemicals around your animals. The key is knowing which products are safe, which are not, and always communicating clearly with your pet sitter about what is in your home.
