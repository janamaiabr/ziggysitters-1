FIP in Cats: A Complete New Zealand Guide to Symptoms, Treatment, and Hope

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  July 22, 2026  •  14 min read

Feline Infectious Peritonitis (FIP) was once considered a death sentence for cats. Today, antiviral treatments like GS-441524 have changed the landscape dramatically. This guide covers everything New Zealand cat owners need to know: recognising symptoms early, navigating diagnosis, accessing treatment, and how a knowledgeable pet sitter can support your cat through recovery.

What Is FIP and How Does It Develop?

FIP is caused by a mutation of the feline enteric coronavirus (FeCV), a common and usually harmless virus that infects the intestinal tract. Most cats exposed to FeCV either show no symptoms or experience mild, transient diarrhoea. In approximately 5 to 10 percent of infected cats, however, the virus mutates into the FIP-causing form.

The mutation allows the virus to replicate inside white blood cells called macrophages, which then carry it throughout the body. The immune system responds with a severe inflammatory reaction that damages organs and tissues. This is not a contagious disease between cats — the mutation happens inside an individual cat, and it is the host immune response that causes the damage.

In New Zealand, FeCV is particularly common in multi-cat households, shelters, and catteries. Kittens and cats under two years of age are at highest risk, as their immune systems are still developing. Cats with compromised immunity, such as those with feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV), are also more vulnerable.

Key Fact: FIP is not transmitted directly from cat to cat. The coronavirus is, but the mutation that causes FIP arises independently within each affected cat. Having a cat with FIP does not mean your other cats will develop it.

Two Forms of FIP: Wet and Dry

FIP presents in two primary forms, though some cats show overlapping features of both.

Wet (Effusive) FIP

The wet form accounts for roughly 60 to 70 percent of FIP cases and tends to progress more rapidly. It is characterised by fluid accumulation in body cavities:

  • Abdominal effusion: Fluid builds up in the abdomen, causing visible swelling and distension. The belly may look bloated despite the cat losing weight elsewhere.
  • Thoracic effusion: Fluid in the chest cavity compresses the lungs, causing laboured breathing, open-mouth breathing, or rapid respiratory rate.
  • Pericardial effusion: Less commonly, fluid collects around the heart, further compromising circulation.

The fluid in wet FIP is typically straw-coloured to yellowish, sticky, and high in protein. Your vet can extract a sample through a procedure called abdominocentesis or thoracocentesis for diagnostic testing.

Dry (Non-Effusive) FIP

The dry form progresses more slowly but is often harder to diagnose. Instead of fluid, it produces granulomatous lesions — small nodules of inflammatory tissue — in organs throughout the body:

  • Kidneys: Enlarged, irregular kidneys visible on ultrasound
  • Liver: Hepatic lesions causing jaundice and elevated liver enzymes
  • Eyes: Uveitis, retinal haemorrhage, or changes visible in the iris
  • Brain and spinal cord: Neurological symptoms including incoordination, seizures, or behavioural changes
  • Intestines: Thickened bowel walls, chronic diarrhoea, weight loss
Urgent: If your cat shows rapid abdominal swelling, difficulty breathing, sudden neurological signs, or persistent fever that does not respond to antibiotics, seek veterinary care immediately. Early intervention dramatically improves treatment outcomes.

Recognising the Symptoms of FIP

Early symptoms of FIP are frustratingly vague and can mimic many other conditions. This is part of what makes diagnosis so difficult. Be alert to the following signs, particularly in kittens and young cats:

General Symptoms

  • Persistent or recurrent fever that does not respond to antibiotics
  • Lethargy and decreased activity levels
  • Weight loss despite normal or increased appetite
  • Poor coat condition — the fur loses its shine and becomes rough
  • Decreased grooming behaviour
  • Muscle wasting, especially along the spine and hindquarters

Wet FIP-Specific Symptoms

  • Progressive abdominal distension (fluid buildup)
  • Laboured or rapid breathing
  • Exercise intolerance — the cat stops moving after very little exertion
  • Muffled heart sounds on veterinary examination

Dry FIP-Specific Symptoms

  • Eye inflammation — cloudiness, redness, or colour changes in the iris
  • Neurological signs — wobbly gait, head tilt, circling, seizures
  • Jaundice — yellow tint to gums, ear pinnae, or skin
  • Enlarged abdominal organs palpable by a veterinarian
  • Chronic diarrhoea or vomiting
Rule of Thumb for NZ Cat Owners: Any kitten or young cat with an unexplained fever lasting more than 48 hours, especially combined with weight loss or lethargy, should be evaluated for FIP. Time matters — earlier treatment with antivirals produces significantly better outcomes.

Diagnosing FIP in New Zealand

FIP has historically been one of the most challenging diseases to diagnose in cats. There is no single definitive test. Veterinarians in NZ use a combination of clinical findings, blood work, imaging, and specialised tests to reach a diagnosis.

The Diagnostic Challenge

Many New Zealand vets follow a diagnostic algorithm: start with basic blood work, assess the albumin-to-globulin ratio, and proceed to more specific testing if FIP is suspected. The combination of a low A:G ratio (below 0.8), high total protein, and clinical signs consistent with FIP gives a reasonably strong presumptive diagnosis.

Definitive diagnosis requires identifying the mutated coronavirus within macrophages — typically through immunocytochemistry on fluid samples or tissue biopsies. NZ referral centres in Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch can perform these advanced diagnostics.

NZ Vet Tip: If your local vet suspects FIP, ask about referral to a veterinary specialist. Massey University Veterinary Teaching Hospital in Palmerston North and several private referral practices in Auckland have experience with FIP diagnosis and can access the most current testing protocols.

Treatment: The GS-441524 Revolution

For decades, FIP was considered untreatable. Euthanasia was the standard recommendation. That changed dramatically with the discovery that GS-441524 — an antiviral nucleoside analogue originally developed for Ebola — could effectively treat FIP in cats.

How GS-441524 Works

GS-441524 works by inhibiting the RNA-dependent RNA polymerase that the coronavirus uses to replicate. By stopping viral replication, the drug allows the cat immune system to recover and clear the infection. Treatment typically requires 84 days (12 weeks) of daily injections or oral medication.

Treatment Protocol Overview

  • Duration: Minimum 84 days, often extended based on response
  • Dosage: Based on cat weight and FIP form — wet FIP typically requires 2 mg/kg, dry or neurological FIP may need higher doses (4-10 mg/kg)
  • Administration: Subcutaneous injections (the original method) or oral tablets (now widely available)
  • Monitoring: Regular blood work every 2-4 weeks to track globulin levels, white cell counts, and organ function
  • Cure rate: Published studies show approximately 80-85% cure rates with appropriate treatment protocols

How a Pet Sitter Can Help During FIP Treatment

If your cat is undergoing FIP treatment, daily medication administration is critical. Missed doses can compromise the entire course. This is where an experienced pet sitter becomes invaluable.

What to Tell Your Pet Sitter

  • The exact medication schedule — GS-441524 must be given at consistent times each day
  • How to administer injections (subcutaneous) or oral tablets, including any special techniques
  • Signs of adverse reactions: vomiting after medication, injection site swelling, lethargy beyond baseline
  • Emergency vet contact details and after-hours clinic address
  • Your cat appetite preferences — nutrition is critical during recovery
  • Fluid intake monitoring requirements

Supporting a Cat Through Treatment

Cats on GS-441524 often experience a "miracle turnaround" within the first one to two weeks. Fever resolves, appetite returns, and energy improves. However, the full 84-day course must be completed to prevent relapse. A pet sitter providing daily visits during your absence ensures:

  • Consistent medication timing
  • Appetite and water intake monitoring
  • Injection site checks for reactions or abscesses
  • Weight tracking if a scale is available
  • Behavioural observation — changes in energy, litter box habits, or mood
  • Immediate communication if anything seems off
ZiggySitters Tip: When booking a pet sitter for a cat on FIP treatment, explicitly mention the medical condition and injection requirements in your sitter request. Not all sitters are comfortable administering injections — finding one with nursing or veterinary experience can make the difference between a successful treatment course and a stressful experience for everyone.

Prevention and Risk Reduction

While you cannot completely prevent FIP, you can reduce the risk factors that make the coronavirus mutation more likely.

Reducing Coronavirus Exposure

  • Limit multi-cat density: Households with five or fewer cats have significantly lower FeCV prevalence than larger colonies
  • Separate litter boxes: One box per cat plus one extra, cleaned at least twice daily
  • Low-stress environment: Stress suppresses immune function, which may increase the likelihood of mutation
  • Good nutrition: High-quality, protein-rich diet supports immune competence
  • Regular veterinary check-ups: Early detection of any health issues reduces overall immune burden

The FIP Vaccine Question

An intranasal FIP vaccine (Felocell FIP) exists but is not widely recommended. The American Association of Feline Practitioners has noted insufficient evidence of efficacy in cats already exposed to coronavirus, which includes the vast majority of cats in multi-cat environments. It is not available in New Zealand as of 2026. The most effective prevention remains reducing coronavirus transmission and maintaining strong overall health.

When Treatment Ends: Monitoring for Relapse

After completing the 84-day GS-441524 course, your cat will need continued monitoring. Relapse, while uncommon with proper treatment completion, is possible. Key monitoring steps include:

  • Blood work at 2 weeks, 3 months, and 6 months post-treatment
  • Watching for recurring symptoms: fever, lethargy, appetite changes, abdominal distension
  • Annual wellness checks with FIP-related blood parameters
  • Maintaining a low-stress environment to support continued immune health

Most cats that complete the full treatment protocol and show clear blood work at the 3-month post-treatment mark are considered cured. The prognosis for these cats is excellent, with a normal life expectancy.
