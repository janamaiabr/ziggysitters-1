Maltese Care Guide NZ 2026

By ZiggySitters NZ • Updated March 2026 • 12 min read

Maltese dogs are one of New Zealand’s most loved small breeds — and for good reason. They’re affectionate, adaptable to apartment living, and genuinely enjoy human company. But that silky white coat, sensitive temperament, and tendency toward separation anxiety mean Maltese dogs need owners (and pet sitters) who understand their unique needs. This guide covers everything you need to keep your Maltese healthy and happy in the New Zealand climate.

Breed Overview: Is a Maltese Right for You?

The Maltese is one of the oldest toy breeds in the world, with a history stretching back over 2,000 years. In New Zealand, they’re prized for their gentle nature, low-shedding coat, and suitability for city apartments and smaller homes. They typically weigh between 3 and 4 kilograms and live 12–15 years, making them a long-term companion.

Grooming Your Maltese in NZ

The Maltese’s coat is arguably its most defining feature — and its most demanding. Unlike most dogs, Maltese have hair rather than fur, which grows continuously and doesn’t shed in seasonal cycles. This makes them a great choice for allergy sufferers, but it also means regular grooming is non-negotiable.

Daily Grooming Routine

  • Brushing: Daily brushing with a soft-bristle or pin brush prevents matting. Pay special attention to behind the ears, armpits, and around the collar — areas prone to tangles.
  • Eye cleaning: Maltese are prone to tear staining, which creates a reddish-brown residue beneath the eyes. Wipe gently each day with a damp cloth or a pet-safe eye wipe.
  • Face wash: After meals, check and clean around the mouth to prevent food staining.

Professional Grooming

Most Maltese owners in NZ opt for a “puppy cut” — keeping the coat trimmed short (around 5–8 cm) for easier daily management. A professional groom every 6–8 weeks is typical. Full show coat maintenance is beautiful but demanding; it requires daily brushing sessions of 20–30 minutes and careful wrapping to prevent breakage.

NZ Groomer Tip: In Auckland’s humid summer months, the puppy cut is especially practical — it keeps your Maltese cool and dramatically reduces grooming time. Ask your groomer for a trim that suits your lifestyle, not just the breed standard.

Bathing

Bathe your Maltese every 1–2 weeks using a gentle, whitening dog shampoo. Their white coat can yellow over time without the right products. Always use a conditioner to keep the coat silky and tangle-free, and dry thoroughly — especially around the ears — to prevent skin irritation in NZ’s often damp autumn and winter conditions.

Exercise: How Much Does a Maltese Need?

Despite their small size, Maltese are lively dogs that need daily activity to stay mentally and physically healthy. They’re not couch potatoes — but they’re also not marathon runners.

Daily Exercise Guidelines

  • 20–30 minutes of exercise per day is sufficient for most adult Maltese.
  • This can be split into two short walks — morning and afternoon — with indoor play making up the difference.
  • Avoid long, strenuous hikes. The Maltese’s small joints can be stressed by rough terrain.
  • Off-leash play in a securely fenced garden or dog park is ideal, but watch carefully: their small size makes them vulnerable to larger, boisterous dogs.
NZ Summer Tip: On hot days — especially in the Waikato, Hawke’s Bay, or Nelson regions — walk your Maltese in the early morning or evening. Their small bodies overheat faster than larger breeds, and hot pavement can burn their paws.

Feeding Your Maltese

Maltese are small dogs with small stomachs, and their diet needs to reflect that. Overfeeding is a common problem — even a few hundred extra grams on a 3 kg dog is proportionally significant.

Recommended Diet

  • Choose a premium small-breed kibble specifically formulated for dogs under 10 kg. These have smaller kibble sizes appropriate for a Maltese jaw.
  • Feed twice daily — morning and evening — rather than once a day. Maltese can be prone to low blood sugar (hypoglycaemia), particularly as puppies.
  • Measure portions carefully. Most adult Maltese need only 80–120g of dry food per day, depending on activity level and brand.
  • Fresh, cool water should be available at all times.

Foods to Avoid

The usual suspects apply: no grapes, raisins, onions, garlic, chocolate, macadamia nuts, or xylitol (found in some peanut butters and sugar-free products). Additionally, avoid feeding large amounts of dairy — Maltese can be lactose-sensitive.

Hypoglycaemia Warning: Maltese puppies and very small adults are at risk of low blood sugar. Signs include weakness, trembling, confusion, or collapse. If you notice these symptoms, offer a small amount of honey or glucose gel and contact your vet immediately. This is especially relevant when a new sitter is caring for your dog.

Health: What Maltese Owners in NZ Need to Know

The Maltese is generally a healthy breed, but like all purebreds, they’re prone to certain conditions. Being aware of these helps you catch problems early.

Common Health Issues

  • Luxating patella (slipping kneecap): Very common in small breeds. Watch for occasional skipping or holding up a back leg during walks. Most cases are managed conservatively, but severe cases may require surgery.
  • Dental disease: Small mouths mean crowded teeth, which leads to plaque buildup. Daily tooth brushing is the gold standard; dental chews and water additives can help. Annual veterinary dental checks are important.
  • Tracheal collapse: The windpipe can weaken in small breeds. Symptoms include a honking cough, especially when excited. Use a harness rather than a collar to reduce pressure on the neck.
  • Eye conditions: Progressive retinal atrophy can occur in the breed. Annual eye checks with a vet are worthwhile.
  • Skin sensitivities: Some Maltese have sensitive skin or allergies. If your dog is scratching frequently or has redness, consult your vet about diet and shampoo choices.
Vet Tip: Find a vet who has experience with toy breeds. Small dogs have different anaesthetic protocols and drug dosages, so choosing a practice familiar with small-breed care matters — particularly for dental procedures.

Managing Separation Anxiety in Maltese

Maltese bond intensely with their owners. This is one of their most endearing traits — and one of the most challenging. Separation anxiety is extremely common in the breed, and it can manifest as barking, destructive behaviour, house-training accidents, or refusing to eat when left alone.

Strategies That Work

  • Gradual desensitisation: Practice short absences from puppyhood and gradually increase duration. Never make a big fuss of arrivals and departures.
  • Enrichment before you leave: A puzzle feeder or Kong stuffed with their favourite treat occupies their mind during the first 20 minutes — the most stressful period for anxious dogs.
  • A consistent routine: Maltese thrive on predictability. Feeding, walking, and sleep times at the same hour each day significantly reduce anxiety.
  • A second dog: Many Maltese owners in NZ find that a second small-breed companion dramatically reduces anxiety. This isn’t practical for everyone, but for chronic cases it can transform a dog’s quality of life.

Maltese in New Zealand’s Climate

New Zealand’s climate varies enormously from Northland to Southland, and your Maltese’s needs will shift with the seasons.

Summer (December–February)

Maltese can overheat quickly. Keep walks to early morning and late evening. Ensure your dog has access to shade and fresh water at all times. Never leave a Maltese in a parked car, even with windows cracked. Their white coat actually reflects some solar heat, but their small body mass means they lose thermoregulation rapidly.

Autumn & Winter

New Zealand winters are mild by global standards, but a short-coated or recently groomed Maltese may need a dog coat for outdoor walks, particularly in Wellington, Christchurch, or Central Otago. Their low body fat offers little insulation. Indoors, ensure they have a warm, draught-free sleeping spot.

Rain & Humidity

Auckland’s high humidity can cause the Maltese coat to curl and mat faster than usual. After wet walks, dry your dog thoroughly — especially around the ears — to prevent skin infections. A microfibre towel works better than a standard bath towel for getting moisture out of a long coat.

Training Your Maltese

Maltese are intelligent and generally eager to please, but they can be stubborn — particularly when it comes to house training. Patience and consistency are everything.

House Training

Small dogs have small bladders. Young Maltese puppies may need to go outside every 1–2 hours. Reward-based training works well — use small, high-value treats (tiny pieces of chicken breast are ideal) and praise immediately after they toilet in the right spot. Avoid punishment, which increases anxiety and is counterproductive in a breed already prone to nervousness.

Basic Obedience

Maltese respond well to short, positive training sessions of 5–10 minutes. They can learn sit, stay, drop, and even tricks readily. Enrol in a puppy class early for socialisation — NZ has excellent reward-based trainers across Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch.

“Small Dog Syndrome”

This term describes the pattern of unwanted behaviours (growling, snapping, ignoring commands) that can develop when small dogs are not given consistent boundaries. Maltese are not aggressive by nature, but they can become demanding if rules aren’t applied consistently. Treat your Maltese with the same firmness and clarity you’d apply to a larger breed.

Choosing a Pet Sitter for Your Maltese

When you need to travel or work long hours, choosing the right sitter for your Maltese is as important as choosing the right vet. This is a breed that does not do well in noisy boarding kennels or with inexperienced caregivers.

What to Look For

  • Experience with small breeds: A sitter who has cared for Maltese or similar toy breeds will understand the specific grooming, feeding, and anxiety-management needs.
  • In-home sitting: For Maltese, in-home sitting — where the sitter stays in your home — is almost always preferable to boarding. It maintains the dog’s routine and familiar environment.
  • Willingness to follow your care instructions: Share your Maltese’s grooming schedule, feeding amounts, and any medical needs in a care sheet. A good sitter will welcome this detail.
  • Patience and a calm temperament: Maltese pick up on anxiety. A sitter who is calm and gentle will help your dog settle far faster than someone who is rushed or unfamiliar with sensitive breeds.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Maltese good for first-time dog owners in NZ?

Yes, with some caveats. They’re gentle, adaptable, and don’t need huge amounts of exercise. But the grooming commitment is real, and their tendency toward separation anxiety means they suit owners who work from home or can arrange reliable daytime care.

Do Maltese shed much?

Very little — they have hair rather than fur and don’t go through seasonal moults. This makes them a popular choice for allergy sufferers, though no dog is truly hypoallergenic.

How much does a Maltese cost in NZ?

From a registered breeder, expect to pay $2,500–$4,500 NZD. Be wary of significantly lower prices, which can indicate unethical breeding practices. Always visit the puppy with its mother, and ask to see health testing records for the parents.

Can Maltese be left alone all day?

In general, no — especially without gradual training to build tolerance. Most Maltese will become distressed if left alone for 8+ hours consistently. A midday dog walker, a trusted neighbour, or a pet sitter service can make all the difference for owners who work full-time.
