Why Reviews Matter More Than You Think

Pet sitting is deeply personal. Your cat, dog, rabbit, or bird is not luggage — they are a living creature with needs, quirks, and anxieties. A sitter who seems lovely in a profile bio might behave very differently once you leave. Reviews from other pet owners are your window into what actually happens when you are not there.

In New Zealand, the pet sitting industry is largely unregulated. There is no mandatory licence, no central register, and no minimum qualification required to call yourself a pet sitter. That makes reviews one of the few reliable signals you have. A strong body of authentic reviews from verified bookings tells you this person has been tested by real situations and come through for real pet owners.

Understanding Star Ratings

Most platforms use a five-star system. Here is what each tier generally means in practice:

  • 5.0 stars — Every client gave top marks. This is common for new sitters with only a handful of reviews, so it does not automatically mean they are the best. Check the review count alongside the rating.
  • 4.7 to 4.9 stars — Consistently excellent with enough volume to be meaningful. This is the sweet spot for experienced sitters. A few less-than-perfect scores often reflect minor communication hiccups rather than genuine problems.
  • 4.0 to 4.6 stars — Generally good but with occasional issues. Read the lower-scored reviews carefully to see if the complaints are things that matter to you.
  • Below 4.0 stars — Caution. Multiple clients have had meaningful problems. There may be exceptions, but you should investigate thoroughly before booking.

The number of reviews matters as much as the average. A sitter with a perfect 5.0 from two reviews tells you far less than a sitter with 4.8 from thirty reviews. Volume is a proxy for reliability.

What to Actually Read in Reviews

Skimming star ratings is not enough. The text of the reviews contains the information you actually need. Here is what to pay attention to:

1. Specific Details About the Pet

Reviews that mention the type of animal, breed, or temperament are far more useful than generic praise. A review that says "Sarah was amazing with our anxious rescue greyhound" is worth ten reviews that say "Great sitter, would recommend." Look for reviewers whose situation mirrors yours — if you have a reactive dog, reviews from owners of reactive dogs are gold.

2. Communication Quality

The most common theme in positive sitter reviews is communication. Look for phrases like "kept us updated with photos," "responded quickly to messages," or "let us know immediately when our cat was off its food." Good sitters do not just care for your pet — they keep you in the loop so you can enjoy your time away without worrying.

3. How Problems Were Handled

No sitter is perfect, and things go wrong — pets get sick, weather changes plans, dogs escape through an open gate. The best reviews are the ones that mention a problem and describe how the sitter handled it. A sitter who stayed calm, contacted the owner, and took appropriate action during an emergency is worth their weight in gold. Look for these stories.

4. Follow-Up and Aftercare

Did the sitter leave the house as they found it? Did they provide a thorough handover when you returned? Reviews mentioning a clean home, clear communication about what happened during the sit, and notes about your pet is behaviour show a sitter who takes the whole job seriously, not just the cute cuddles.

5. Repeat Bookings

When a review mentions "we have booked Sarah three times now" or "our go-to sitter," that is one of the strongest trust signals available. People do not rebook sitters who let them down. Repeat bookings mean the sitter has earned lasting trust.

Red Flags in Reviews

Just as important as knowing what to look for is knowing what to avoid. Watch for these warning signs:

Vague or Generic Praise

Reviews that say "Nice person, good with animals" without any specific details could be genuine — or they could be written by friends. The more specific a review, the more likely it is authentic.

Inconsistent Feedback

If several reviews mention "great with dogs" but a couple say "seemed nervous around my dog," that inconsistency matters. It might mean the sitter is selective about which dogs they handle well, which is fine — but you need to know if your dog fits their comfort zone.

Complaints About Communication

The number one issue in negative pet sitting reviews is poor communication. If multiple reviews mention the sitter was hard to reach, slow to respond, or did not send updates, treat that as a serious warning. When you are away from your pet, being able to reach your sitter is not a luxury — it is essential.

No Reviews at All

A sitter with zero reviews is not necessarily bad — everyone starts somewhere. But it does mean you are relying entirely on their profile, your meet-and-greet, and your gut. If you are considering a new sitter, invest extra time in the meet-and-greet process and ask for non-platform references.

How to Spot Fake Reviews

Fake reviews exist on every platform, though good ones work hard to prevent them. Here is how to identify reviews that might not be genuine:

  • Clustered timing — Five reviews posted on the same day or within a 48-hour window often signals a review swap or a request to friends.
  • Overly similar language — If multiple reviews use identical phrasing like "punctual, professional, and caring," they may have been prompted with a script.
  • No reviewer profile — On platforms where reviewers have profiles, check whether the person reviewing has reviewed other services. A brand-new account with one review is less trustworthy than an established user.
  • Too perfect — Real reviews often include minor caveats. A wall of flawless five-star reviews with no nuance can indicate curation or selective display.

Reviews and Your Specific Pet

Not all reviews are equal when it comes to your situation. A sitter with brilliant reviews from cat owners might be average with dogs, and vice versa. Here is how to filter reviews to match your needs:

For Dog Owners

Look for reviews from people with dogs of a similar size, breed, or temperament to yours. A sitter who excels with placid Labradors might struggle with a high-energy Huntaway. Reviews mentioning leash manners, recall, and off-lead exercise are particularly relevant for New Zealand dogs who are used to wide-open spaces.

For Cat Owners

Cats are territorial and often anxious when their routine changes. Look for reviews mentioning how the sitter handled shy or nervous cats, whether they followed feeding instructions precisely, and if they kept the cat is environment calm. Indoor cats especially need a sitter who understands feline body language and stress signals.

For Small Animals, Birds, and Reptiles

If you have a rabbit, guinea pig, bird, or reptile, reviews from other small-animal owners are essential. These pets have very specific care requirements around temperature, diet, and handling. A sitter who is wonderful with dogs might not know that a rabbit should not be picked up by the scruff, or that a budgie is cage needs specific daily attention.

New Zealand-Specific Considerations

Several factors make pet sitting reviews in NZ slightly different from overseas:

  • Geographic isolation — In smaller NZ towns, there may only be a handful of sitters. Do not dismiss someone just because they have fewer reviews than you would expect in Auckland or Wellington. Context matters.
  • Rural and lifestyle blocks — If you live rurally, reviews mentioning experience with lifestyle blocks, farm dogs, or multi-pet households are particularly valuable. Urban sitters may not be prepared for the reality of rural animal care.
  • Holiday peaks — NZ has sharp holiday peaks at Christmas, summer, and long weekends. Reviews that mention a sitter is availability and reliability during these periods are especially useful, as this is when you need dependability most.
  • Earthquake readiness — In Wellington and Christchurch especially, reviews that mention how a sitter handled unexpected events (not just quakes, but also severe weather) carry extra weight.

Making Your Final Decision

Reviews are a powerful tool, but they should not be your only one. Here is a recommended approach to choosing a sitter:

  1. Start with reviews and ratings — Use them to create a shortlist of three to five sitters who have strong, specific, verified feedback from pet owners similar to you.
  2. Read the sitter is profile thoroughly — Check their experience, qualifications, photos, and any certifications they list. A great profile reinforces great reviews.
  3. Book a meet-and-greet — This is non-negotiable. Meet the sitter in person (or via video call if you are booking from overseas). See how they interact with your pet. Ask direct questions based on what you read in the reviews.
  4. Trust your instincts — If reviews are glowing but something feels off in person, keep looking. Reviews are data points, not a substitute for your own judgment.

Choosing a pet sitter is choosing someone to care for a family member. Reviews and ratings make that decision more informed, more confident, and more likely to result in a positive experience for everyone — especially your pet.
