Cat Pregnancy & Kitten Care in New Zealand: Complete Guide

2026-06-06 · 11 min read · ZiggySitters Team

Finding out your cat is pregnant can feel overwhelming, especially if it was not planned. Cat pregnancies move fast, roughly nine weeks from mating to birth, and the decisions you make during that window matter enormously for the health of both the mother and her kittens. This guide walks NZ cat owners through every stage: recognising pregnancy signs, preparing for birth, supporting the queen during labour, and raising healthy kittens through their first twelve weeks.

Signs Your Cat Is Pregnant

Cat pregnancy, also called the gestation period, typically lasts 63 to 67 days. The early signs are subtle and easy to miss, especially if your cat is naturally aloof or has a thick coat. The most reliable early indicator is a change in nipple colour and size. Around two to three weeks after mating, a pregnant cat's nipples become pinker and more prominent, a change known as "pinking up." If you suspect your cat mated, gently part the fur on her belly and check for this telltale sign.

Behavioural changes often accompany physical ones. Many pregnant cats become more affectionate and seek out their owners more than usual. Some cats experience mild morning sickness around week three, appearing uninterested in food or vomiting occasionally. Appetite typically increases from week four onward as the growing kittens demand more calories.

By week five, your vet can confirm pregnancy via ultrasound. By week six, the kittens are large enough to feel through the abdomen, though this should be done very gently to avoid causing distress. Your cat's belly will be visibly distended by this point. X-rays taken after day 45 can reveal the exact number of kittens, which is useful for planning the birth.

Nutrition During Pregnancy

A pregnant cat needs significantly more calories and nutrients than usual, particularly protein and calcium. Switch your cat to a high-quality kitten food from the moment pregnancy is confirmed. Kitten formulas contain the elevated protein, fat, and micronutrient levels required to support healthy fetal development without depleting the mother's reserves.

Feed smaller, more frequent meals. As the pregnancy advances, the growing kittens compress the mother's stomach, making large meals uncomfortable. Four to six small meals per day work better than two big ones. Ensure fresh water is always available, as hydration is critical throughout pregnancy.

Avoid supplementing with calcium unless your vet specifically recommends it. Excessive calcium supplementation during pregnancy can actually increase the risk of eclampsia, a dangerous drop in blood calcium that occurs after birth. Trust the balanced nutrition in quality kitten food to provide what your cat needs.

Preparing for the Birth

Setting Up a Queening Box

Prepare a quiet, warm nesting area, often called a queening box, about two weeks before your cat's due date. A large cardboard box with low sides works well, or you can use a plastic storage bin with one side cut down for easy access. Line the bottom with several layers of newspaper and top with soft, washable bedding. The newspaper allows you to easily remove soiled layers during and after birth.

Place the queening box in a warm, draft-free room where your cat already feels comfortable. Avoid high-traffic areas or rooms with loud televisions. Many NZ homes have open-plan living spaces; if that is your setup, consider a quiet spare bedroom, a study, or even a walk-in wardrobe. The key is privacy, warmth, and minimal disturbance.

Encourage your cat to investigate the box before labour begins. Place some of her favourite treats inside and let her explore at her own pace. Some cats take to their queening box immediately; others may choose a different spot entirely. If your cat picks a location you did not plan for, move the box there rather than trying to force her preference.

Vet Check Before Birth

Schedule a vet visit around day 40 to 45 of pregnancy. Your vet can confirm the number of kittens via X-ray, assess the mother's overall health, and discuss what to do if complications arise. Ask your vet for their after-hours emergency number and the location of the nearest 24-hour emergency clinic. In New Zealand, major centres like Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch have dedicated emergency vet hospitals. In smaller towns, your regular vet may handle after-hours calls on a roster basis.

This is also the right time to discuss worming and flea treatment. Some products are safe during pregnancy while others are not. Your vet will recommend the appropriate product and timing. Do not administer any parasite treatment without veterinary guidance during pregnancy.

Stages of Labour and Birth

Stage One: Preparation (6 to 12 Hours)

The first stage of labour involves uterine contractions that the queen experiences as restlessness and discomfort. Your cat may pace, vocalise, groom excessively, or visit the litter tray repeatedly without producing anything. She may stop eating and seek out her queening box or another quiet spot. Some cats become very clingy during this stage; others prefer solitude. Follow her lead.

Stage Two: Delivery (Variable Duration)

Active labour begins when your cat starts pushing. The first kitten usually arrives within an hour of active pushing. Each subsequent kitten can take anywhere from ten minutes to an hour. The mother will break the amniotic sac, bite through the umbilical cord, and clean each kitten herself. This is normal and important; do not interfere unless a kitten is clearly in distress.

If your cat is straining for more than an hour without producing a kitten, contact your vet immediately. This could indicate a kitten stuck in the birth canal, which is a medical emergency. Similarly, if more than two hours pass between kittens and you suspect there are more to come, call your vet.

Stage Three: Placenta Delivery

Each kitten is followed by a placenta. Your cat will typically eat the placenta, which is natural and provides her with nutrients and hormones that support milk production. Count the placentas to ensure one is delivered per kitten. A retained placenta can cause serious infection, so if the count does not match, contact your vet.

Newborn Kitten Care (0 to 4 Weeks)

Keeping Kittens Warm

Newborn kittens cannot regulate their body temperature. The room where the queening box sits should be kept at approximately 25 to 28 degrees Celsius for the first week. In many parts of New Zealand, especially during winter, this means providing supplementary heat. A pet-safe heat pad placed under half the box works well, giving kittens the option to move toward or away from the heat source as needed. Never use a heat lamp or electric blanket directly on the bedding, as these create burn and dehydration risks.

Feeding and Weight Gain

Kittens should nurse within the first two hours of birth. Colostrum, the first milk the queen produces, contains vital antibodies that protect the kittens from disease. If a kitten is not latching, gently guide it to a nipple and hold it in position until it starts suckling. Newborn kittens feed every two to three hours, around the clock.

Weigh each kitten daily using a kitchen scale that measures in grams. A healthy kitten gains roughly 10 to 15 grams per day. Any kitten that is not gaining weight, or that is losing weight, needs immediate veterinary attention. Record each kitten's weight in a simple log so you can spot trends early.

When the Queen Cannot Feed Her Kittens

Sometimes the mother cat cannot or will not nurse. This can happen due to illness, mastitis, an exceptionally large litter, or rejection. In these cases, you will need to bottle-feed kittens using a commercial kitten milk replacer. Never use cow's milk, which causes diarrhoea and can be fatal to kittens. Brands like Wombaroo and Divetelact are available from NZ pet shops and vet clinics. Follow the mixing instructions precisely and feed at the temperature specified on the packaging.

Socialisation and Development (4 to 12 Weeks)

The socialisation window for kittens opens at around two weeks of age, when their eyes and ears begin to function, and closes at roughly twelve weeks. This is the most critical developmental period of a cat's life. Kittens that receive positive exposure to humans, handling, gentle play, household sounds, and novel experiences during this window grow into confident, well-adjusted adult cats. Kittens deprived of this exposure often become fearful, reactive adults that struggle with new situations.

Handle each kitten gently for short periods multiple times daily. Stroke their paws, ears, and bellies so they learn that human touch is safe and pleasant. Introduce them to the sounds of vacuum cleaners, washing machines, and televisions gradually. If you have other friendly, vaccinated pets in the household, supervised introductions during this period help kittens develop strong social skills.

Kittens begin eating solid food at around four weeks. Start with a slurry of wet kitten food mixed with kitten milk replacer, gradually reducing the liquid until they are eating solid food by six to seven weeks. By eight weeks, kittens should be eating wet and dry kitten food independently, though they may still nurse occasionally for comfort.

Vaccinations and De-sexing

Kittens in New Zealand should receive their first vaccination at six to eight weeks of age, typically a core F3 vaccine covering feline panleukopenia, calicivirus, and herpesvirus. Booster vaccinations follow at approximately four-week intervals until the kitten is 16 weeks old. Your vet will set the exact schedule based on the specific vaccine brand and the kitten's health status.

Desexing, also called spaying or neutering, is recommended at four to five months of age for most kittens. Early desexing, as young as eight to twelve weeks, is sometimes practised by shelters and rescue organisations, but discuss the timing with your vet. Desexing prevents unwanted litters, reduces the risk of certain cancers, and generally leads to longer, healthier lives. In New Zealand, some councils and SPCA branches offer subsidised desexing programmes for low-income households.

Finding Homes for Kittens

If you are not keeping all the kittens, start planning their future homes early. Responsible rehoming means more than just giving a kitten to the first person who asks. Screen potential adopters carefully. Ensure they understand the long-term commitment, can afford ongoing veterinary care, and are prepared to desex the kitten at the appropriate age.

The SPCA and other rescue organisations across New Zealand can help with rehoming if you are unable to find suitable homes yourself. Many will take entire litters and handle vet checks, desexing, vaccinations, and placement. Contact your local branch early, as demand for kitten care surges during breeding season and they may have capacity limits.

Never advertise kittens as "free to a good home" without careful screening. Unfortunately, free kittens are sometimes acquired by people who do not have the animals' best interests at heart. A small adoption fee and a thorough conversation about the adopter's experience with cats, their home environment, and their plans for veterinary care helps ensure your kittens land in genuine, loving homes.

Preventing Unwanted Pregnancies

The most effective way to prevent unwanted cat pregnancies is desexing. Female cats can become pregnant as early as four months of age, and the breeding season in New Zealand typically runs from spring through late summer. An undesexed female cat that has access to the outdoors will almost certainly become pregnant during this period. Talk to your vet about the right desexing schedule for your cat. Most NZ veterinarians recommend desexing at four to five months, before the first season.

If your cat is not yet desexed and has outdoor access, keep her indoors during the breeding season. This is easier said than done if your cat is determined to find a mate, but it is the only reliable temporary measure short of desexing. Remember that cats are expert escape artists and can slip through doors, windows, and even torn fly screens with remarkable speed when motivated.
