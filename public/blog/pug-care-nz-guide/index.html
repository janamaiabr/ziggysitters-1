Understanding Brachycephalic Health in Pugs

The most defining feature of a Pug is its short, flat face. This makes them a brachycephalic breed, which directly impacts their respiratory system. In New Zealand, where summer temperatures can soar and humidity levels fluctuate, managing a Pug's breathing is the single most important aspect of their care. Pugs cannot cool themselves down through panting as effectively as long-nosed dogs, making them highly susceptible to heatstroke and heat exhaustion.

During the warmer months, especially from December to March, exercise should be strictly limited to the early morning or late evening when the air is cooler. A simple walk around the block in the midday sun can be dangerous for a Pug. It is essential to ensure they always have access to fresh, cool water and a shaded, air-conditioned, or well-ventilated space to rest. If you notice your Pug panting excessively, wheezing, or struggling to catch their breath, they need to be cooled down immediately and checked by a veterinarian.

Many Pugs also suffer from Brachycephalic Obstructive Airway Syndrome (BOAS). Symptoms include loud snoring, snorting, and gagging. If your Pug's breathing sounds consistently laboured, consult your vet. In some cases, minor surgical procedures to widen the nostrils or shorten the soft palate can drastically improve their quality of life.

Diet and Weight Management for Pugs

Pugs love to eat, and they are notorious for using their expressive eyes to beg for extra treats. Combined with their lower exercise tolerance, this makes Pugs incredibly prone to obesity. Carrying extra weight is dangerous for any dog, but for a brachycephalic breed, it is life-threatening. Excess fat around the chest and neck further restricts their airways and puts unnecessary strain on their joints, exacerbating issues like arthritis and hip dysplasia.

Maintaining a healthy weight requires strict portion control. Choose a high-quality, nutritionally balanced dog food formulated for small breeds. Measure their meals accurately using a kitchen scale rather than a measuring cup, and factor any training treats into their daily caloric allowance. If your Pug is looking a little too round, speak to your vet about a safe weight-loss plan. You should be able to feel their ribs without pressing hard, and they should have a visible waist when viewed from above.

Grooming Your Pug: Coat, Wrinkles, and Eyes

Despite having a short coat, Pugs are heavy shedders. They have a dense double coat that sheds year-round, with heavier periods during spring and autumn. Regular brushing—at least two to three times a week—will help manage the loose fur and keep their skin healthy. A rubber grooming mitt or a deshedding tool works well to remove dead hair.

The signature wrinkles on a Pug's face are adorable, but they are also a breeding ground for bacteria and yeast if not kept clean and dry. Moisture, food particles, and tears can easily become trapped in the skin folds, leading to painful infections. You must clean your Pug's facial wrinkles daily using a damp cloth, unscented baby wipe, or a specialized veterinary wipe. Crucially, you must ensure the folds are dried thoroughly afterwards to prevent moisture buildup.

A Pug's large, prominent eyes are also vulnerable to injury and irritation. Check their eyes daily for redness, discharge, or signs of scratching. Because their eyes bulge slightly, they are prone to dry eye and corneal ulcers. If your Pug is squinting or rubbing their face on the carpet, a vet visit is necessary.

Training and Socialisation

Pugs are intelligent but can be notoriously stubborn. They have a strong independent streak and will quickly lose interest if training sessions become repetitive or boring. The key to training a Pug is positive reinforcement, patience, and keeping sessions short and engaging. Because they are highly food-motivated, high-value treats are an excellent training tool—just remember to adjust their main meals accordingly.

Early socialisation is vital for Pugs. Exposing them to different people, environments, sounds, and other animals during their puppyhood will help them grow into confident, well-adjusted adults. Pugs are generally sociable and friendly, but without proper socialisation, they can become anxious or overly attached to their owners, leading to separation anxiety when left alone.

Finding the Right Pet Sitter for Your Pug

When you travel or work long hours, finding a pet sitter who understands the specific needs of a Pug is crucial. A standard dog walker who takes high-energy breeds for hour-long hikes in the midday sun is not suitable for a Pug. You need a sitter who prioritizes safety over strenuous exercise and knows how to monitor brachycephalic breathing.

When interviewing potential sitters, ask if they have experience with flat-faced breeds. They should know the signs of heatstroke and understand why a Pug needs to be walked on a harness rather than a collar to protect their delicate trachea. Ensure they are willing to adhere strictly to your feeding schedule and resist the temptation to overfeed, no matter how much your Pug begs.

If you are booking a house sitter or drop-in visits, provide a detailed care sheet. Include instructions on cleaning their facial wrinkles, monitoring their eyes, and managing their temperature during warmer weather. A knowledgeable sitter will appreciate the detail and ensure your Pug remains safe, healthy, and happy while you are away.
