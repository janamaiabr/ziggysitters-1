Rat & Mouse Care Guide NZ: Housing, Feeding, Health & Enrichment

Published 10 July 2026 — ZiggySitters NZ — 8 min read

Pet rats and mice are intelligent, affectionate, and surprisingly entertaining companions — yet many New Zealand owners struggle to find quality care advice tailored to local conditions. This guide covers everything from choosing your first fancy rat to setting up the perfect cage, feeding a balanced diet, recognising health issues early, and finding a pet sitter who actually knows how to handle small animals.

Why Rats and Mice Make Great Pets in NZ

Pet rats (often called fancy rats) are among the most rewarding small animals you can keep. They learn their names, come when called, enjoy cuddling, and can even learn tricks. Mice are smaller and more delicate but equally curious and fun to watch. Both species are legal to keep as pets throughout New Zealand.

NZ-specific note: While rats and mice are common pets, some local councils have specific bylaws about keeping rodents. Check your council rules before setting up, especially if you live in an apartment complex with body corporate rules.

Choosing Your Pet Rat or Mouse in NZ

Always source your pet from a reputable breeder rather than a pet shop. Good breeders health-test their lines, handle babies from birth, and can tell you about temperament and genetic history. In New Zealand, look for breeders through:

  • NZ Rat Club — Facebook community with breeder listings and advice
  • Rodent Rescue NZ — rehoming service for rats and mice needing forever homes
  • Local SPCA branches — occasionally have rats and mice available for adoption
  • Trade Me — check that sellers are breeders, not backyard operations
Never buy a single rat. Rats are deeply social. A lone rat will become depressed and develop behavioural problems. Always adopt at least two same-sex rats from the same litter or introduce them carefully under guidance.

Housing: Setting Up the Perfect Cage

Cage Size and Type

Rats need vertical space — they are climbers. A multi-level wire cage with a solid floor (or solid shelf covers) is ideal. Avoid aquariums and plastic tubs; they trap ammonia and cause respiratory infections.

  • Bar spacing: No wider than 1.5 cm for adult rats, 1 cm for mice and baby rats
  • Flooring: Solid shelves or covered wire to prevent bumblefoot (a painful foot infection)
  • Location: Indoors, away from direct sunlight and drafts. NZ houses can be cold and damp — keep the room between 18–26 °C

Bedding Options in NZ

  • Paper-based bedding (Breeders Choice, Carefresh) — best overall; low dust, absorbent
  • Fleece liners — popular with NZ rat owners; wash and rotate daily
  • Hemp bedding — available from rural supply stores; good odour control
  • Avoid: Pine and cedar shavings (toxic phenols), sawdust (respiratory hazard), newspaper ink

Essential Cage Accessories

  • Water bottle (glass is best — rats chew plastic) with fresh water daily
  • Food bowl (ceramic, heavy base so it does not tip)
  • Multiple hideouts (wooden bridges, coconut huts, hanging cubes)
  • Hammocks and climbing ropes (rats love to sleep elevated)
  • Exercise wheel — 30+ cm diameter for rats; 20+ cm for mice (solid surface only)
  • Chew toys — untreated apple sticks, lava ledges, wooden blocks
  • Digging box — a shallow tray filled with coco coir for natural foraging behaviour

Feeding: A Balanced Diet for NZ Rats and Mice

Daily Diet Breakdown

A healthy diet consists of three components:

  1. Base mix (60%) — a high-quality pellet or seed mix formulated for rats/mice. In NZ, look for Vetafarm Origins Rat & Mouse or Mazuri Rat Diet. Avoid cheap seed mixes heavy on sunflower seeds (too much fat).
  2. Fresh vegetables (30%) — daily variety of leafy greens, broccoli, peas, capsicum, zucchini, carrots
  3. Protein (10%) — cooked egg, plain chicken, mealworms, or a small piece of lean fish 2–3 times per week

Foods to Avoid

  • Chocolate, caffeine, alcohol
  • Raw kidney beans (toxic)
  • Raw onion and garlic in large quantities
  • Citrus (can cause kidney issues in male rats)
  • Blue cheese (mould toxicity)
  • Licorice (toxic in quantity)
  • Sticky, sugary treats that can cause choking
NZ foraging tip: You can grow wheatgrass, basil, and parsley indoors year-round for fresh treats. Dandelion leaves from untreated lawns are also safe and free. Always wash foraged greens thoroughly.

Health: Common Issues and When to See a Vet

Respiratory Infections

The most common health problem in pet rats. Symptoms include sneezing, porphyrin (red discharge) around eyes and nose, laboured breathing, and lethargy. NZ humidity can worsen respiratory issues, so keep cages clean and well-ventilated. If your rat is sneezing persistently or breathing noisily, see an exotic vet immediately — respiratory infections can become chronic.

Tumours

Mammary tumours are extremely common in female rats, especially over 18 months. They are often benign but grow quickly. Surgical removal by an experienced exotic vet is the standard treatment in NZ and typically costs $200–$500.

Dental Problems

Rats and mice have continuously growing incisors. If teeth become misaligned (malocclusion), they can overgrow and prevent eating. Provide chew toys and monitor tooth length. An exotic vet can trim teeth if needed.

Finding an Exotic Vet in NZ

Not all vets treat rodents. Look for vets with exotic pet experience. Good starting points:

  • Auckland: The Strand Vet, Vetcare Grey Lynn, Unitec Vet Clinic
  • Wellington: The Furry Friends Vet, Willis St Vet
  • Christchurch: Veterinary Centre, Halswell Vet
  • Nationwide: Check the NZVA (New Zealand Veterinary Association) directory for exotic-practice vets

Enrichment: Keeping Your Small Pet Stimulated

Rats and mice are intelligent and need daily mental and physical stimulation. Without it, they develop stereotypical behaviours like bar-biting, pacing, and over-grooming.

Daily Enrichment Ideas

  • Free-range time: At least 30–60 minutes daily in a rat-proofed room or playpen
  • Foraging toys: Hide treats in toilet rolls, paper bags, or digging boxes
  • Rotation: Swap cage accessories weekly to prevent boredom
  • Training: Rats can learn to come when called, spin, shake, and navigate agility courses using clicker training
  • Water play: A shallow dish with water and frozen peas — rats love fishing for peas
  • DIY obstacle course: Cardboard boxes, tunnels, and ladders cost nothing and provide hours of fun

Seasonal Care in New Zealand

Summer (December–February)

  • Keep cage temperature below 28 °C — rats cannot sweat and are prone to heatstroke
  • Use frozen water bottles wrapped in fleece as cool pads
  • Ensure constant access to fresh water
  • Avoid direct sunlight on the cage

Winter (June–August)

  • Maintain room temperature above 18 °C — NZ homes can be draughty
  • Add extra bedding, cosy hammocks, and fleece sleeping pouches
  • Consider a SnuggleSafe heat disc (microwavable, safe, no wires)
  • Watch for respiratory flare-ups — cold, damp conditions trigger them

Finding a Pet Sitter for Your Rats or Mice

Most pet sitters specialise in cats and dogs, but small animals need experienced care too. When searching for a sitter on ZiggySitters, look for:

  • Small animal experience — ask specifically about rat and mouse handling
  • Understanding of respiratory health — a good sitter recognises sneezing or laboured breathing
  • Willingness to follow your feeding routine — small animals have sensitive digestion
  • Daily cage checks — not just feeding, but checking water, bedding, and overall health
  • Emergency plan — make sure the sitter knows which exotic vet to call and has your authorisation to seek treatment
Pro tip: Write a one-page care sheet for your sitter covering feeding amounts, medication schedules, vet contact details, and normal behaviour vs. warning signs. Rats and mice can deteriorate fast — your sitter needs to know when to act.

What to Tell Your Pet Sitter

  1. Feeding schedule and portion sizes
  2. Fresh water change routine
  3. Which treats are allowed (and which are toxic)
  4. Normal behaviour vs. signs of illness
  5. Cage cleaning schedule
  6. Free-range time routine and rat-proofing rules
  7. Your exotic vet contact and authorisation for emergency treatment
  8. Each rat or mouse by name and appearance (they look similar to others!)

Lifespan and End-of-Life Care

The hardest part of loving rats and mice is their short lifespan. Most rats live 2–3 years, and mice 1.5–2.5 years. Quality care — good diet, clean environment, low stress, and prompt vet treatment — gives them the best chance at the longer end of that range.

Common signs of ageing in rats include reduced activity, weight loss, thinning fur, and tumours. For mice, watch for decreased grooming and hunched posture. When the time comes, talk to your vet about quality-of-life assessments and humane euthanasia. Many NZ exotic vets offer home-visit euthanasia for small animals, which is less stressful for your pet.

Key Takeaways

  • Always keep rats in same-sex pairs or groups — never alone
  • Invest in a tall, multi-level wire cage with solid shelves
  • Feed a balanced diet: quality pellets + fresh veg + moderate protein
  • Monitor respiratory health closely — it is the number one issue in NZ
  • Provide daily enrichment and free-range time
  • Find a sitter experienced with small animals before you need one
  • Prepare a care sheet for sitters and emergency vet contacts
