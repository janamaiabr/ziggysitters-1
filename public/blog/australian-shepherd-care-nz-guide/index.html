Australian Shepherd Care in New Zealand: The Complete Owner's Guide

Published 24 March 2026 • 10 min read

Despite the name, the Australian Shepherd was actually developed in the western United States — but Kiwi dog owners have fallen for this strikingly beautiful, intensely intelligent breed in growing numbers. If you own or are thinking about getting an Aussie in New Zealand, this guide covers everything you need to know: exercise requirements, coat care, health considerations, training approaches, and what to look for when finding a pet sitter who can handle one of the most capable dogs in the world.

Quick Answer: Is an Australian Shepherd Right for You?

Australian Shepherds are outstanding dogs for active, experienced owners who have time to exercise, train, and mentally stimulate them every single day. They thrive in homes with space, regular outdoor activity, and people who actually want to engage with their dog. If you're looking for a low-maintenance companion, an Aussie is not the right fit — but if you want a loyal, brilliant, deeply rewarding dog, few breeds compare.

Exercise Requirements: The Most Important Thing to Understand

If you remember nothing else from this guide, remember this: Australian Shepherds need substantial daily exercise — not a 20-minute stroll around the block, but genuine physical and mental exertion. A bored, under-exercised Aussie will find ways to keep itself busy that you almost certainly won't enjoy: chewing furniture, herding children, barking incessantly, or escaping the yard.

Daily Minimum

Plan for at least 90 minutes of exercise per day, ideally split across two sessions. A morning run or energetic fetch session, followed by an evening walk, is a solid baseline. On weekends or days off, Aussies genuinely benefit from extended hikes, beach runs, or agility sessions.

Mental Stimulation Is Non-Negotiable

Physical exercise alone is not enough. Australian Shepherds were bred to make decisions, problem-solve, and work alongside humans for hours at a time. Without mental stimulation, they become frustrated and difficult to manage. Good options include:

  • Obedience training and trick work (Aussies excel at advanced commands)
  • Agility, flyball, or disc dog — all growing sports in NZ
  • Puzzle feeders and interactive toys at home
  • Nose work and scent detection activities
  • Dog sports clubs — many cities have dedicated agility clubs welcoming Aussies
NZ Outdoor Opportunities for Aussies New Zealand's trail network is a genuine gift for Aussie owners. Many DoC tracks welcome dogs on leads, and open farmland areas (with permission) give Aussies the space their herding instincts crave. Tramping with your Aussie is one of the best ways to genuinely tire them out.

Coat Care and Grooming

The Australian Shepherd's coat is one of its most striking features — and one of its most demanding. Their medium-length double coat comes in four base colours (black, red, blue merle, and red merle), often with white and tan markings. That beautiful coat requires consistent attention.

Regular Grooming Routine

  • Brushing: Two to three times per week minimum using a slicker brush and an undercoat rake. Daily brushing during seasonal coat blows (typically spring and autumn in NZ).
  • Bathing: Every six to eight weeks, or when genuinely dirty. Over-bathing strips the natural oils that keep the double coat healthy.
  • Ears: Check weekly for wax buildup or moisture — Aussies with floppy ear feathering can be prone to ear infections.
  • Nails: Trim every three to four weeks. Active dogs who walk on concrete will naturally wear nails down faster.
  • Coat trimming: The Aussie coat should never be shaved — this damages the double coat's insulating properties and can cause long-term coat issues. Tidying around the ears, paws, and tail is fine.

Health Considerations for NZ Aussie Owners

Australian Shepherds are generally robust dogs, but there are several health considerations every NZ owner should be aware of before getting one.

MDR1 Gene Mutation (Critical)

This is the most important health issue specific to the breed. The MDR1 (Multi-Drug Resistance 1) gene mutation affects a significant percentage of Australian Shepherds and causes severe — sometimes fatal — reactions to certain medications, including common antiparasitic drugs (ivermectin, milbemycin), some anaesthetics, and anti-diarrhoeal medications.

Have your Aussie DNA tested for the MDR1 mutation before any drug treatment, and inform your vet and any pet sitter of the result. Reputable breeders in NZ will often test for this before selling puppies.

Important: MDR1 and Your Pet Sitter If your Aussie carries the MDR1 mutation, make sure your pet sitter and your vet both have this information documented. Some routine flea and worm treatments that are safe for most dogs can cause serious neurological symptoms in MDR1-affected Aussies.

Training Your Australian Shepherd in NZ

The good news: Australian Shepherds are among the most trainable dogs in the world. The caveat: that intelligence means they learn bad habits just as quickly as good ones, and they'll test boundaries if you're inconsistent.

Key Training Principles

  • Start early: Puppy socialisation classes from 8–16 weeks are essential. The NZ environment — traffic, beaches, livestock, diverse people — should be introduced positively as early as possible.
  • Positive reinforcement only: Aussies are sensitive dogs. Harsh corrections damage trust and often worsen behavioural problems. Reward-based training produces far better results.
  • Be consistent: Aussies read inconsistency quickly and will exploit it. Every household member should use the same commands and rules.
  • Channel herding instincts: Aussies may try to herd children, cyclists, or other dogs. This is not aggression — it's instinct. Redirect with structured activities rather than punishment.

Recommended NZ Resources

  • Dog New Zealand (Dogs NZ) affiliated obedience and agility clubs
  • Local flyball and disc dog clubs — particularly active in Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch
  • Certified professional dog trainers (CPDT-KA) for behavioural issues

Finding a Pet Sitter Who Understands Australian Shepherds

Not every pet sitter is equipped to care for a high-drive herding dog. When you're looking for someone to look after your Aussie, ask specific questions:

  1. Have you cared for high-energy herding breeds before? Border Collies, Kelpies, and Cattle Dogs are comparable. Experience with any of these is a good sign.
  2. How much exercise will you provide per day? An Aussie that gets one short walk per day during a five-day trip will be wound up and difficult by day three. Confirm the sitter can genuinely meet your dog's needs.
  3. Are you aware of the MDR1 gene mutation? A sitter caring for an Aussie should know not to administer any medication without veterinary guidance.
  4. Do you have a secure, fenced property? Aussies are problem-solvers with energy to burn. A secure yard is essential for home boarding.
  5. Can you manage herding behaviour? If your Aussie tends to herd other dogs or people, the sitter needs to understand this and handle it calmly.
What to Leave for Your Aussie's Sitter Write up a care sheet covering: feeding schedule (amounts, times, any supplements), exercise routine (routes, duration, preferred activities), MDR1 status and any medication notes, vet details and emergency authorisation, and any known triggers or sensitivities. The more specific you are, the more consistent the care your dog receives.

Common Mistakes Australian Shepherd Owners Make

  • Underestimating exercise needs: An Aussie that looks calm indoors has probably just learned to cope — not necessarily thriving. Two hours of genuine activity per day is the target, not the exception.
  • Skipping mental enrichment: Physical exercise without mental challenge produces a tired but frustrated dog. Training, puzzles, and sports address the mental side.
  • Shaving the coat in summer: This is a widespread misconception. The double coat actually insulates against heat as well as cold. Shaving disrupts thermoregulation and can cause permanent coat damage.
  • Not testing for MDR1: This is a potentially life-saving test that costs around $100–$150 at most NZ veterinary clinics or through postal DNA testing kits. Every Aussie owner should know their dog's status.
  • Leaving them alone for long periods: Aussies are not independent dogs. Regular, predictable human contact and structured activity are essential for their wellbeing.

Key Takeaways

  • Australian Shepherds need 90–120 minutes of genuine exercise and daily mental stimulation — this is not negotiable.
  • Test for the MDR1 gene mutation early and document the result for your vet and pet sitter.
  • Their double coat should never be shaved — brush regularly and accept the seasonal shedding.
  • Positive reinforcement training works exceptionally well with Aussies, who are among the most intelligent and trainable of all breeds.
  • When choosing a pet sitter, confirm they have experience with high-energy breeds and can genuinely meet your dog's exercise requirements.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Australian Shepherds good family dogs in NZ?

Yes — with the right family. Aussies are loyal, affectionate, and protective of their people. They do well with children when properly socialised from puppyhood. The key is ensuring the whole family is active and engaged with the dog daily, and that young children learn to interact with the dog respectfully to avoid triggering herding instincts.

Can Australian Shepherds live in apartments in NZ cities?

It's possible but genuinely challenging. An Aussie in an apartment requires a truly committed owner who takes them out for multiple substantial exercise sessions every day without exception. Most Aussies do significantly better with access to a securely fenced yard. If you live in an Auckland or Wellington apartment and want an Aussie, plan your exercise routine before you get the dog, not after.

How much does an Australian Shepherd puppy cost in NZ?

Prices from reputable, health-tested NZ breeders typically range from $2,500 to $4,000 for a puppy in 2026. Be cautious of puppies priced significantly below this range — reputable breeders invest in health testing (MDR1, hip scoring, eye testing) that affects their costs. Always visit the breeder, meet the parents, and ask for health certificates before committing.

What is the best diet for an Australian Shepherd in NZ?

A high-quality commercial dog food appropriate for medium-large active breeds is a solid foundation. Many Aussie owners in NZ supplement with raw components. The key is feeding a diet appropriate for your dog's age and activity level — working or sport Aussies need significantly more calories than a companion pet. Consult your vet for personalised recommendations.

How do I find a good Australian Shepherd breeder in New Zealand?

Start with the New Zealand Kennel Club (Dogs NZ) breeder directory, which lists members who have agreed to a code of ethics. Look for breeders who health-test breeding stock for MDR1, hip dysplasia, and eye conditions. A good breeder will ask you questions too — they want to know their puppies are going to suitable homes.
