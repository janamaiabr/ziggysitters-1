Pet Microchipping NZ: Complete Guide 2026

By ZiggySitters NZ • March 2026 • 10 min read

Microchipping is one of the simplest and most important things you can do for your pet’s safety. In New Zealand, it is also a legal requirement for dogs. This guide explains everything NZ pet owners need to know — how microchips work, what it costs, how to register and update your details, and why it matters especially when you’re travelling or using a pet sitter.
Quick Summary
  • Microchipping is compulsory for all dogs in New Zealand under the Dog Control Act 1996
  • Cats are not nationally required to be microchipped, but it is strongly recommended and required by some councils
  • The chip is registered in the New Zealand Companion Animal Register (NZCAR)
  • Cost: typically $30–$80 NZD at a vet clinic
  • The microchip itself never expires — but your contact details must be kept current

What Is a Pet Microchip and How Does It Work?

A microchip is a tiny electronic device — roughly the size of a grain of rice — implanted under your pet’s skin, typically between the shoulder blades. It contains a unique 15-digit identification number that is permanently linked to your contact information in a national database.

When a lost pet is brought to a vet clinic, SPCA, council pound, or animal shelter, staff use a handheld scanner to read the chip. The number is then searched in the national registry to find the owner’s contact details. The process takes seconds and is the fastest and most reliable way to reunite lost pets with their families.

What Microchips Do NOT Do

A common misconception is that microchips are GPS trackers. They are not. A microchip only works when scanned from close range (a few centimetres). It does not track your pet’s location in real time. For live location tracking, you would need a separate GPS collar attachment — several brands are available in NZ from pet stores and online retailers.

Tip: A microchip and a GPS tracker serve different purposes. A microchip is permanent and passive. A GPS tracker is battery-powered and proactive. Many NZ owners use both, especially for dogs known to escape.

NZ Law: Is Microchipping Compulsory?

Dogs

Yes. Under the Dog Control Act 1996, all dogs in New Zealand must be microchipped. This is enforced at the council level as part of the annual dog registration process. Most territorial authorities require proof of microchipping to issue a dog registration tag. Failure to register a dog — which includes microchipping — can result in fines and seizure of the dog.

Dogs must be microchipped:

  • Before or at the time of first registration with your local council
  • When ownership is transferred
  • If required following a dangerous dog classification

Cats

There is currently no nationwide law requiring cats to be microchipped in New Zealand, but this is an evolving area. Several councils — including Wellington and Palmerston North — have local bylaws requiring microchipping as part of cat registration or as a condition of keeping cats in certain zones. Check your local council’s website for specific requirements in your area.

Regardless of legal requirement, microchipping your cat is strongly recommended by the SPCA and most vets. Cats roam widely and are frequently picked up as strays — a microchip is often the only way to identify them.

Important: In New Zealand, cats (especially in rural and semi-rural areas) are subject to trapping by neighbours and regional councils as part of predator control programmes. A microchipped cat can be immediately identified and returned; an unchipped cat may be euthanised if unclaimed.

The New Zealand Companion Animal Register (NZCAR)

The New Zealand Companion Animal Register (NZCAR) is the primary national database for microchipped pets in New Zealand. When your vet microchips your pet, they submit the chip number and your details to NZCAR. You can access your record at nzcar.co.nz.

NZCAR stores:

  • Your pet’s microchip number
  • Your name, address, and contact phone number
  • Your pet’s name, species, breed, and date of birth
  • Your vet’s details

Other Registries in NZ

New Zealand has several microchip registries. Vets can register chips in:

  • NZCAR (most common for dogs and cats)
  • New Zealand Animal Registration (NZAR)
  • Companion Animal Register (operated by the SPCA)

Scanners used by vets and shelters can search across multiple databases simultaneously, so the specific registry your chip is in matters less than ensuring your contact details are current in whichever registry holds your record.

How to Register and Update Your Pet’s Microchip Details

The microchip itself is only as useful as the contact information attached to it. An estimated 30% of microchipped pets in Australasia have out-of-date or incomplete owner details — meaning that even when found and scanned, they cannot be returned.

Steps to Register a New Microchip

  1. 1 Have your pet microchipped at a vet clinic.
  2. 2 Obtain the 15-digit chip number (on your paperwork or vaccination card).
  3. 3 Visit nzcar.co.nz and create an account or search for your existing record.
  4. 4 Confirm your details are correct. Many vets register the chip on your behalf — verify this happened.
  5. 5 Register the chip with your local council as part of dog registration if applicable.

Updating Your Details (Critical After Moving)

Every time you move house, change phone number, or change email, you must update your pet’s microchip record. This takes less than five minutes online:

  1. Log in at nzcar.co.nz
  2. Search for your pet by chip number or your name
  3. Update your address and phone number
  4. Save changes — updates are usually immediate
Best Practice: Set a calendar reminder to check your pet’s microchip details every year at the same time as their annual vaccination. If you move house, update NZCAR that same week.

Transferring Ownership

If you rehome or sell a pet, the new owner must update the microchip record to their name and contact details. Both parties should confirm this has been done. NZCAR offers an ownership transfer function online.

The Procedure: What to Expect When Your Pet Is Microchipped

Many owners worry that microchipping is painful or distressing for their pet. In practice, the procedure is quick and well-tolerated by most animals.

  • The microchip is injected using a sterile needle — slightly larger than a standard vaccination needle.
  • In dogs and cats, it is typically inserted under the skin between the shoulder blades.
  • No anaesthetic is required for a healthy adult animal. Puppies and kittens may be microchipped at the same time as desexing if they are anaesthetised.
  • Most animals react no more than they would to a vaccination.
  • Mild localised tenderness for 24–48 hours is normal. Significant swelling, discharge, or persistent discomfort should be reported to your vet.

Microchip Migration: Is It Common?

Very occasionally, a microchip can migrate slightly from the original implant site over time. When scanning a pet, a thorough vet or shelter worker will scan the entire body — not just the shoulder area — to locate a migrated chip. This does not affect the chip’s function.

Microchipping and Pet Sitting: Why It Matters

When you leave your pet with a pet sitter — whether in your home or theirs — there is always a small risk of escape or accidental loss. A microchipped pet has a dramatically higher chance of being returned if they go missing during a sitting stay.

Before your pet stay, make sure:

  • Your pet is microchipped and your contact details are current in NZCAR.
  • Your pet’s collar tag also has your current mobile number.
  • Your pet sitter has your emergency contact number and knows your vet’s details.
  • You have provided your vet’s address and after-hours emergency number to your sitter.
  • If travelling overseas, nominate a local emergency contact your sitter can also reach.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a microchip replace a collar tag?

No — they complement each other. A collar tag is immediately readable by anyone who finds your pet; a microchip requires a scanner. Both together give your pet the best chance of a quick return. Collar tags can fall off or be removed; the microchip is permanent.

Can a microchip cause cancer?

This concern arose from a small number of case reports in mice in laboratory settings. Major veterinary organisations worldwide, including the New Zealand Veterinary Association (NZVA), have reviewed the evidence and concluded that microchipping is safe. The benefit of identification far outweighs any theoretical risk.

What if my pet was microchipped overseas?

ISO standard 15-digit microchips (the international standard) are readable by scanners used in New Zealand. If your pet was microchipped overseas with an ISO chip, have your vet scan it to confirm the number, then register it in NZCAR and with your local council. Some older non-ISO chips used in the US (10-digit) may require a specific scanner — check with your vet.

Can I scan my own pet’s microchip?

Consumer-grade pet microchip scanners are available online (roughly $40–$80 NZD). They allow you to confirm your pet’s chip is still readable and check the number. However, you still need a vet, shelter, or council facility to search the national registry for your details.

My dog is already registered with the council. Do I still need NZCAR?

Yes. Council registration and NZCAR registration are separate. Council registration records your dog for local bylaws and dog control purposes. NZCAR is what vets, shelters, and animal control officers check when a found animal is scanned. Both records should have your current contact details.
