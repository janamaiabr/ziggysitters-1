Pet Fish & Aquarium Care in New Zealand: The Complete Guide

Fish are one of the most popular pets in New Zealand, and for good reason. They are quiet, relatively low-maintenance, and a well-set-up aquarium is a stunning addition to any home. But fishkeeping is not as simple as filling a tank with water and dropping in some food. A healthy aquarium requires the right setup, consistent maintenance, and an understanding of the nitrogen cycle that keeps your fish alive. Whether you are setting up your first tank or looking to improve your existing aquarium, this guide covers everything Kiwi fish keepers need to know.

Choosing the Right Aquarium for Your NZ Home

Before you buy fish, you need the right tank. The biggest mistake new fish keepers make is starting too small. Small tanks may seem easier, but they are actually harder to maintain because water parameters fluctuate rapidly in a small volume of water. A tank of at least 60 litres is a good starting point for most freshwater setups.

Freshwater vs Tropical vs Marine

  • Freshwater (cold water): The easiest entry point. Goldfish and white cloud mountain minnows thrive at room temperature without a heater. Ideal for beginners and cooler NZ climates.
  • Tropical freshwater: Requires a heater to maintain water between 24-28 degrees Celsius. Guppies, tetras, and bettas are popular choices. More variety in species and colours.
  • Marine (saltwater): The most challenging and expensive option. Requires precise salinity, protein skimmers, and often live rock. Best left for experienced keepers willing to invest significant time and money.

Essential Equipment

Every aquarium in New Zealand needs these basics:

  • Filter: Internal canister filters or hang-on-back filters are most common for beginners. The filter houses beneficial bacteria that process fish waste. Never replace all filter media at once, as this crashes your biological filtration.
  • Heater (tropical): A reliable submersible heater with a thermostat. In New Zealand, winter indoor temperatures can drop low enough to stress tropical fish, even in North Island homes.
  • Lighting: LED aquarium lights are energy-efficient and bring out fish colours. Aim for 8-10 hours of light per day to avoid algae blooms.
  • Thermometer: A simple stick-on or digital thermometer helps you monitor water temperature daily.
  • Water conditioner: New Zealand tap water contains chlorine and chloramines that are toxic to fish. Always treat tap water with a dechlorinator before adding it to your tank.
  • Substrate: Gravel or sand. Avoid sharp gravel that can injure bottom-dwelling fish.

The Nitrogen Cycle: The Most Important Thing to Understand

If there is one concept that separates successful fish keepers from those whose fish keep dying, it is the nitrogen cycle. Fish produce ammonia through their waste and uneaten food. Ammonia is highly toxic. Beneficial bacteria in your filter convert ammonia into nitrite, which is also toxic, and then into nitrate, which is much less harmful. This process is the biological filtration that keeps your fish alive.

A new tank does not have these bacteria yet. Cycling a tank means establishing this bacterial colony before adding fish. There are two approaches:

  • Fishless cycling: Add ammonia source (available from pet shops) and wait 4-6 weeks for bacteria to establish. Test water regularly. Once ammonia and nitrite read zero, you can add fish. This is the humane and recommended method.
  • Fish-in cycling: Adding hardy fish immediately and doing frequent water changes to keep ammonia low while bacteria develop. This stresses the fish and is not recommended for beginners.

Invest in a liquid test kit (API Freshwater Master Test Kit is widely available in NZ pet stores). Test strips are convenient but less accurate. You need to know your ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate levels at all times during cycling and regularly afterwards.

Best Pet Fish for New Zealand Beginners

Cold Water Fish

  • Goldfish (fancy varieties): Fantails, orandas, and ryukins are hardy and personable. A single fancy goldfish needs at least 40 litres. Common goldfish need ponds, not tanks.
  • White Cloud Mountain Minnows: Small, peaceful, and tolerate cool temperatures. Great for nano tanks of 20 litres or more.

Tropical Fish

  • Guppies: Colourful, hardy, and easy to breed. A great first tropical fish. Keep two or three females per male to reduce harassment.
  • Neon Tetras: Peaceful schooling fish with striking blue and red stripes. Keep groups of six or more.
  • Betta (Siamese Fighting Fish): Beautiful, personable, and can live in smaller tanks (minimum 15 litres with heater and filter). Keep males alone as they will fight other bettas.
  • Corydoras Catfish: Bottom-dwelling scavengers that help keep the substrate clean. Keep in groups of four or more.
  • Platies: Livebearers like guppies, available in a range of colours. Hardy and peaceful.

Feeding Your Fish Properly

Overfeeding is the single most common mistake fish keepers make. Uneaten food decomposes and produces ammonia, which spikes water parameters and can kill fish quickly. A good rule: feed only what your fish can consume in two minutes, once or twice a day. It is far better to underfeed slightly than to overfeed.

Vary the diet for better health:

  • Flake food: A good staple for most community fish.
  • Pellets: Better for larger fish and bottom feeders. Sinking pellets suit corydoras and goldfish.
  • Frozen food: Bloodworms, brine shrimp, and daphnia make excellent treats once or twice a week. Available from most NZ pet shops in blister packs.
  • Vegetables: Blanch a slice of zucchini or cucumber for goldfish and plecos. Remove uneaten portions after 24 hours.

Water Changes and Tank Maintenance

Regular water changes are non-negotiable. Even with a great filter, nitrates build up over time, and trace minerals need replenishing. In a cycled tank:

  • Change 20-30% of the water every one to two weeks.
  • Always treat new tap water with dechlorinator before adding it to the tank.
  • Match the temperature of new water to the tank water to avoid shocking your fish.
  • Vacuum the substrate during water changes to remove trapped waste.
  • Rinse filter media in old tank water (never tap water, which kills beneficial bacteria).

New Zealand tap water quality varies by region. In some areas, water may contain heavy metals or high pH. Test your source water and adjust as needed with buffers available from aquarium shops.

Common Fish Health Problems in NZ

  • White spot (Ich): Small white spots on fins and body. Caused by stress, poor water quality, or temperature fluctuations. Treat with commercial white spot remedy and raise temperature gradually to 28-30 degrees Celsius to speed the parasite life cycle.
  • Fin rot: Ragged, disintegrating fins. Usually caused by poor water quality. Improve water conditions first before medicating.
  • Swim bladder issues: Fish floating upside down or struggling to swim down. Common in fancy goldfish. Often caused by overeating or constipation. Try fasting for two days, then feeding peeled peas.
  • Ammonia poisoning: Red gills, gasping at the surface, lethargy. Do an immediate 50% water change and check your filter is functioning properly.

Prevention is always better than treatment. Consistent water changes, proper feeding, and not overcrowding your tank prevent the vast majority of fish health issues.

Fish Care When You Travel in New Zealand

This is where many fish owners get caught out. Fish cannot be left for a week without attention, even with automatic feeders. Here is what you need to plan before going away:

  • Automatic feeders: Work for short trips of 2-4 days. Test the dispenser before you leave to ensure it releases the right amount.
  • Feeding blocks: Dissolve slowly over several days. Useful as a backup but should not be the sole food source for longer trips, as they can affect water quality.

New Zealand Regulations and Responsible Fish Keeping

New Zealand has strict biosecurity laws, and for good reason. Our unique freshwater ecosystems are vulnerable to invasive species. Responsible fish keepers should be aware of the following:

  • Never release fish into the wild. This includes drains, streams, rivers, and ponds. Released goldfish and koi have caused significant damage to NZ waterways. It is illegal under the Biosecurity Act 1993.
  • Check the National Pest Pet Accord. Some species are banned from sale and keeping in New Zealand. Always buy from reputable NZ pet shops.
  • Dispose of tank water responsibly. Pour it on the garden, not down stormwater drains. This prevents potential introduction of exotic organisms into waterways.
  • Clean equipment between uses. If you move plants, decorations, or equipment between tanks, rinse thoroughly to avoid transferring diseases or pests.

Setting Up Your First Tank: Step by Step

  1. Choose your location: Avoid direct sunlight (causes algae) and areas near heaters or air conditioning vents. A sturdy stand that can hold the weight (1 litre of water = 1 kg).
  2. Rinse substrate and decorations: Use water only, no soap or chemicals. Arrange hardscape before adding water.
  3. Add water and conditioner: Fill the tank, add dechlorinator at the recommended dose.
  4. Install equipment: Set up filter, heater (for tropical), and lighting. Run the tank for at least 24 hours to check everything works and temperature stabilises.
  5. Cycle the tank: Begin your fishless cycle. Add ammonia source and test water every 2-3 days. This takes 4-6 weeks. Be patient.
  6. Add fish gradually: Once cycled, add a few hardy fish first. Wait a week, test water, then add more. Never add all your fish at once, as the bioload will overwhelm your filter bacteria.

Finding Fish and Supplies in New Zealand

New Zealand has a solid network of aquarium shops and online suppliers. Major pet store chains like Animates, Petstock, and independents in most cities stock equipment, food, and common fish species. For more unusual tropical fish, specialist aquarium shops in Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch offer the widest selection. Online retailers like Fishwise and Trade Me also have active fish-keeping communities where you can find livestock and second-hand equipment.

Conclusion

Fish keeping in New Zealand is a rewarding hobby that brings life, colour, and calm to your home. The key is starting right: a properly sized tank, a completed cycle, appropriate fish for your setup, and consistent maintenance. Fish are not disposable pets. With good care, many common aquarium fish live 3-5 years, and goldfish can live well over a decade. Invest the time upfront, and your aquarium will reward you with years of enjoyment.
