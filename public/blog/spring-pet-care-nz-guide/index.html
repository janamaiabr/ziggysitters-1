Spring Pet Care Guide NZ 2026

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  March 9, 2026  •  10 min read

Spring in New Zealand arrives with longer days, blooming gardens, and warmer temperatures that both you and your pet have been waiting for. But with the change in season comes a new set of hazards, health considerations, and care requirements. This guide covers everything NZ pet owners need to know to keep dogs and cats healthy and happy as we head into spring.

1. Seasonal Allergies in NZ Pets

Just like humans, dogs and cats can suffer from seasonal allergies as pollen counts rise during spring. In New Zealand, the main culprits are grass pollens (especially ryegrass, which is widespread across Canterbury, Waikato, and Southland), tree pollens from species like pine and birch, and airborne mould spores that become more active with warm, wet spring conditions.

Signs Your Pet May Have Spring Allergies

  • Excessive scratching, rubbing, or licking paws
  • Red, watery, or weeping eyes
  • Sneezing or a runny nose (more common in cats)
  • Hot, irritated skin or recurring hot spots
  • Recurrent ear infections (a classic sign in dogs)
  • Coat thinning or bald patches from over-grooming

Environmental allergies in dogs are often called atopic dermatitis, and they tend to worsen year on year if not managed. Breeds like Labradors, Golden Retrievers, French Bulldogs, and West Highland White Terriers are particularly prone. If you suspect allergies, speak to your vet early in the season before symptoms escalate.

Tip: Wipe your dog down with a damp cloth after walks during high-pollen days. Paying close attention to paws, belly, and face where pollen lands and lingers. This simple step can dramatically reduce allergen load.

Managing Allergies at Home

Your vet may recommend antihistamines, medicated shampoos, or newer prescription options like Apoquel or Cytopoint for dogs. For cats, corticosteroids are commonly used in the short term. Feeding a high-quality diet with omega-3 fatty acids (from fish oil or salmon-based food) supports skin barrier function and can reduce inflammatory response.

Vacuum carpets and wash bedding more frequently during peak pollen season, and consider HEPA air purifiers in rooms where your pet sleeps.

2. Parasite Prevention in Spring

Spring is when flea, tick, and worm burdens rise sharply across New Zealand. Warmer soil temperatures activate flea pupae that have been dormant all winter, and tick populations become active across bush-heavy areas in Northland, Bay of Plenty, Hawke’s Bay, and the lower North Island.

Fleas

A single flea can lay up to 50 eggs per day, meaning an infestation can spiral quickly once spring warmth arrives. The key is to start prevention before you see fleas, not after. Monthly spot-on treatments, flea collars, or chewable tablets (such as Bravecto or NexGard) should be applied or administered at the start of spring. Treat all pets in the household simultaneously even if only one is scratching.

Don’t forget to treat your home. Flea eggs and larvae live in carpets, soft furnishings, and floor crevices. A flea bomb or spray combined with thorough vacuuming can help break the lifecycle. Pay particular attention to your pet’s favourite sleeping spots.

Ticks

New Zealand has several tick species, with the native bush tick (Haemaphysalis longicornis) being the most common threat to pets. While NZ is free from Lyme disease, ticks can still cause tick paralysis and transmit other pathogens. Check your dog thoroughly after walks in long grass or bush, running your fingers through the coat around the neck, ears, between toes, and under the collar.

If you find a tick, use a purpose-designed tick remover tool and twist it out slowly. Avoid squeezing the body. Consult your vet if you notice signs of lethargy, weakness, or wobbling after a tick is found.

Intestinal Worms

Puppies and kittens should be wormed every two weeks until three months of age. Adult pets should be wormed every three months year-round. Spring is a good time to reset your worming calendar if it has slipped over winter. Hydatid tapeworm is a particular concern in rural New Zealand where dogs may encounter infected livestock carcasses or offal.

Important: Never use dog flea or tick treatments on cats. Many canine products contain permethrin, which is highly toxic to cats and can be fatal even from indirect contact. Always read labels carefully or ask your vet.

3. Spring Shedding: Managing the Coat Change

Many dogs and cats experience a significant shedding event in spring as they shed their winter undercoat. Double-coated breeds like German Shepherds, Huskies, Labradors, and Golden Retrievers can shed enormous amounts of fur over several weeks. This is entirely normal but requires active management.

Grooming Tips for the Spring Shed

  • Brush double-coated dogs daily during peak shedding using an undercoat rake or deshedding tool (the Furminator is popular with NZ groomers)
  • Book a professional groom or deshedding bath at the start of spring to get ahead of the coat change
  • For cats, daily brushing prevents matting and reduces hairball formation significantly
  • Hoover pet areas more frequently to prevent fur from building up in vents and on soft furnishings
  • Ensure your pet is well hydrated and eating a coat-supporting diet with adequate omega fatty acids
Grooming note: Never shave a double-coated dog thinking it will keep them cool in spring. The double coat actually provides insulation against heat as well as cold, and shaving can permanently damage the coat structure. Regular brushing is always the better option.

4. Garden and Outdoor Hazards to Watch

Spring is gardening season across New Zealand, and that brings an array of hazards that are easy to overlook. Many popular NZ garden plants are toxic to pets, and spring is the time when bulbs are planted, fertilisers are applied, and snail bait is put out.

Common Spring Garden Hazards

Hazard Risk to Pets Action
Snail and slug bait (metaldehyde) Highly toxic to dogs and cats — can be fatal Use pet-safe alternatives (iron chelate products)
Daffodil and tulip bulbs Toxic — causes vomiting, diarrhoea, collapse Keep pets away from bulb planting areas
Azalea and rhododendron Toxic — causes drooling, heart abnormalities Remove from pet-accessible areas or fence off
Compost bins Mouldy food produces mycotoxins that cause seizures Ensure compost bins are securely lidded
Fertilisers Bone meal and blood meal are attractive to dogs but toxic in quantity Store securely and water in well after application
Bee and wasp stings Rise sharply in spring; severe reactions possible Know your vet’s emergency number and watch for swelling

If you suspect your pet has ingested anything toxic, contact your vet or the 24-hour animal poison helpline immediately. Speed is critical — the sooner you act, the better the outcome.

5. Spring Walks and Exercise

Spring is a wonderful time to ramp up outdoor activity, but there are a few practical points to consider before heading out.

Watch for Hot Pavement Early

New Zealand spring temperatures can spike unexpectedly, particularly in Auckland, Hawke’s Bay, and Canterbury. Asphalt absorbs heat rapidly, and pavement that feels comfortable to your hand may be hot enough to burn a dog’s paw pads within seconds. The simple rule: if it’s too hot for the back of your hand held down for seven seconds, it’s too hot for paws. Walk dogs early in the morning or after 6pm as a good habit to establish now before summer arrives.

Increased Exposure to Other Dogs

More people venture outside in spring, which means more dog-to-dog contact at parks and on walking tracks. If your dog hasn’t interacted much during winter, ease them back in gently. Watch for kennel cough, which can circulate through dog parks and grooming salons. Ensure your dog’s vaccinations are current before spring socialising begins in earnest.

Water Safety

Spring rains cause rivers and streams to run faster and colder. Debris in waterways can trap or injure dogs that swim enthusiastically in familiar spots. Always check water conditions before allowing your dog to swim in rivers, and be aware that blue-green algae (cyanobacteria) can begin forming in still water from late spring onwards. It is highly toxic and potentially fatal to dogs. If water looks greenish or smells musty, keep pets out.

6. Spring Health Check-up

Spring is an excellent time to book a general health check with your vet if your pet hasn’t been seen for six months or more. A spring check-up is the ideal opportunity to:

  • Review and update parasite prevention protocols
  • Check vaccination status and arrange boosters if due
  • Address any skin, coat, or ear issues that developed over winter
  • Discuss weight management heading into the more active summer months
  • Arrange a dental check if your pet has been showing signs of discomfort eating
  • Update microchip registration details if you’ve moved address
NZ microchipping reminder: Under the Dog Control Act, all dogs in New Zealand must be registered with their local council. Spring is a common time for registration renewals, so make sure you’re not overdue. Cats are not legally required to be microchipped in all regions, but it is strongly recommended and required by many councils as part of desexing programmes.

7. Planning Around Spring Events and Holidays

Spring in New Zealand includes a number of public holidays and school holidays that often mean travel, gatherings, or disrupted routines — all of which can stress pets.

ANZAC Day (25 April)

Dawn services and community events across NZ mean many owners are out of the house early. If your pet is anxious when left alone, plan ahead by arranging a pet sitter or having a family member stay home during the service. Fireworks are occasionally part of community events, so have a plan if your pet is noise-sensitive.

School Holidays

The April school holidays fall during NZ’s spring season. Many families take trips away, which means forward planning for pet care. Booking a trusted in-home pet sitter well in advance is especially important during school holiday periods when demand for quality pet care surges.

8. Your Spring Pet Care Checklist

  • Start monthly flea and tick prevention treatment
  • Worm all pets (cats and dogs)
  • Book spring vet check-up and confirm vaccinations
  • Check council dog registration renewal date
  • Brush out winter undercoat and book groomer if needed
  • Audit the garden for toxic plants and snail bait
  • Switch to pet-safe slug and snail control products
  • Wipe paws after walks on high-pollen days
  • Check water bowls are full and refreshed daily as temperatures rise
  • Update microchip registration if address has changed
  • Book a pet sitter for ANZAC weekend or April school holidays
  • Monitor for signs of seasonal allergies and contact vet early if concerned

Final Thoughts

Spring is one of the most enjoyable seasons to be a pet owner in New Zealand. Longer evenings, more outdoor time, and a general lift in mood for both pets and people. With a little proactive planning around parasites, allergies, and garden safety, your cat or dog can thrive through the season safely.
