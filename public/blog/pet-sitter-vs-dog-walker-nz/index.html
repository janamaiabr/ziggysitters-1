What a Dog Walker Actually Does

A dog walker provides a specific service: they come to your home, pick up your dog, and take them for a walk. The visit typically lasts 30 to 60 minutes. Some walkers offer individual walks; others walk small groups of two to four dogs together.

Core responsibilities of a dog walker:

  • Collect your dog from your home at a scheduled time
  • Walk your dog for 30–60 minutes along a predetermined or varied route
  • Provide fresh water and a basic wipe-down if weather is messy
  • Return your dog to your home
  • Send a photo or brief update (varies by walker)

What a dog walker does NOT typically do:

  • Stay in your home
  • Feed your pet full meals
  • Administer medication
  • Provide overnight care
  • Care for cats, birds, or small animals
  • Manage behavioural issues beyond basic leash manners

What a Pet Sitter Actually Does

A pet sitter takes on a broader caregiving role. Depending on the arrangement, they may visit your home multiple times a day, stay overnight, or even move in for the duration of your absence. Pet sitters work with dogs, cats, birds, rabbits, guinea pigs, reptiles—virtually any animal you keep at home.

Core responsibilities of a pet sitter:

  • Follow your pet\'s full daily routine: feeding, walks, play, rest
  • Administer medication (oral, topical, injectable for qualified sitters)
  • Provide companionship and monitor behaviour changes
  • Manage overnight stays or multiple daily visits
  • Handle minor household tasks: bringing in mail, watering plants, taking out rubbish
  • Respond to emergencies: take your pet to the vet if needed
  • Send regular updates: photos, videos, and written reports

Pet sitting is comprehensive care. Your sitter becomes a temporary guardian for your pet, maintaining as much normalcy as possible while you are away.

When a Dog Walker Is the Right Choice

You Work Full-Time and Your Dog Needs a Midday Break

If you are out of the house from 8am to 6pm, your dog is spending 10 hours alone. A midday walk breaks up the day, provides exercise, and reduces the risk of destructive behaviour. This is the single most common reason Kiwis hire dog walkers, and it is a good one.

Your Dog Is Healthy, Social, and Low-Anxiety

A confident, well-socialised dog who enjoys other dogs and new environments is a perfect candidate for group walks. They get exercise and socialisation in one package, and they are genuinely happier for it.

You Need Exercise, Not Caregiving

If your dog is physically fine but not getting enough exercise because of your schedule, a walker solves that specific problem efficiently. You do not need someone to stay overnight—you need someone to run your dog around the park for 45 minutes.

Budget Is a Factor

Dog walking is significantly cheaper than pet sitting. If you need daily help, a walker at $25–$35 per walk five days a week costs $125–$175. A daily pet sitter for the same period would cost considerably more. For ongoing weekday support, a walker is often the practical choice.

When a Pet Sitter Is the Right Choice

You Are Going Away Overnight or Longer

This is the clearest case. If you are gone for more than a working day, your pet needs more than a walk. They need feeding, companionship, overnight supervision, and someone present in case something goes wrong. A dog walker cannot provide this.

You Have Cats or Small Animals

Dog walkers, by definition, walk dogs. If you have cats, rabbits, guinea pigs, birds, or fish, you need a pet sitter. Cats in particular do poorly with change—keeping them at home with a familiar routine is far less stressful than a cattery.

Your Pet Has Medical Needs

If your pet requires medication, special feeding, post-surgical monitoring, or regular health checks, a pet sitter is the right choice. Many experienced sitters are vet nurses or have formal animal care qualifications. A dog walker is not equipped to manage these needs during a 45-minute visit.

Your Pet Has Separation Anxiety

Anxious pets do not just need exercise—they need presence. A walker who comes and goes in under an hour can actually worsen separation anxiety by creating another departure event. A sitter who stays, or visits multiple times, provides the continuity these pets need.

You Want Home Security While Away

A live-in pet sitter keeps your house occupied. They bring in mail, put out rubbish bins, turn lights on and off, and generally make the house look lived-in. In NZ, where burglaries spike during holiday periods, this is a genuine benefit. A walker cannot offer this.

You Have Multiple Pets

Walking two or three dogs is expensive and logistically tricky for most walkers. A pet sitter can care for your whole household—dogs, cats, and the guinea pig—for one daily rate. The per-pet cost drops dramatically compared to multiple individual walks.

Can You Use Both?

Absolutely—and many NZ pet owners do. Here are some common combinations:

  • Weekday walker + weekend sitter. You hire a walker for daily midday walks during the workweek, and book a sitter when you go away for the weekend.
  • Walker for exercise + sitter for holidays. Your dog gets regular walks year-round from the same walker, and your sitter steps in when you travel.
  • Transitioning an anxious pet. Start with a sitter for initial trust-building, then shift to a walker for daily exercise once your pet is comfortable being alone for longer periods.

The best setups involve clear communication between you, your walker, and your sitter. Share your pet\'s routine, preferences, and any behavioural notes with both. If they ever need to cover for each other, they will already know your pet well.

Choosing Between Them: A Quick Decision Guide

Ask yourself these five questions:

  1. How long will you be away? Under 10 hours = walker may work. Overnight or longer = sitter.
  2. What kind of pet do you have? Dog only = either could work. Cat, bird, small animal = sitter only.
  3. Does your pet have medical or behavioural needs? Yes = sitter with relevant experience.
  4. Do you need someone in your home? For security or multi-pet care = sitter.
  5. What is your budget? Ongoing daily help = walker. Occasional absences = sitter.

If you answered "sitter" to three or more, that is your answer. If you answered "walker" to most, start there—but have a sitter on standby for when life happens.

Red Flags in Both Services

Whether you hire a walker or a sitter, watch for these warning signs:

  • No meet-and-greet offered. Any professional pet carer should want to meet you and your pet before the first booking.
  • No references or reviews. Ask for them. Check them. A trustworthy carer has nothing to hide.
  • Unwillingness to follow your routine. Your pet\'s care plan is not a suggestion. If someone pushes back on feeding times, walk routes, or medication schedules, find someone else.
  • Inconsistent communication. If they are slow to reply during booking, they will be slow to update you during the sit.
  • No emergency plan. Ask what they would do if your pet needed urgent vet care. "Call you" is not a complete answer—they need a vet authorisation and a backup plan.

Making It Work Long-Term

The best arrangement is one you can sustain. Here is how Kiwi pet owners build lasting relationships with their walkers and sitters:

  • Start with a trial. Book a single walk or a short sit before committing to a regular arrangement.
  • Be consistent with timing. Dogs thrive on routine. If your walker comes at 12pm every Tuesday, keep it at 12pm.
  • Pay fairly and on time. Good walkers and sitters are in demand. Paying promptly and tipping for exceptional care keeps them available for you.
  • Communicate changes early. Holiday schedules, new medication, or a change in your pet\'s behaviour—all of this should be shared promptly.
  • Leave reviews. Help other pet owners find great carers, and help your carer build their business. It is a virtuous cycle.

The Bottom Line

Dog walkers solve the exercise problem. Pet sitters solve the care problem. If your dog needs a midday run while you are at work, a walker is efficient and affordable. If you are heading away for a weekend, have cats or small animals, or need someone to manage medical needs and keep your home secure, a pet sitter is the right call.

Many NZ pet owners end up using both—a walker for daily exercise and a sitter for travel. The key is matching the service to the actual need, not just the cheapest or most convenient option.
