Understanding the Pomeranian Personality

Despite weighing between just 1.5 to 3 kilograms, Pomeranians possess the confidence and alertness of much larger dogs. Historically bred down from larger Spitz-type sled dogs, they have retained a bold, protective instinct. This makes them excellent watchdogs, though their tendency to bark at perceived threats—be it the courier driver, a passing cat, or a leaf blowing across the lawn—requires consistent training to manage, especially in high-density NZ suburbs.

Poms are highly intelligent, deeply affectionate with their families, and love to be the centre of attention. However, this strong bond can sometimes translate into separation anxiety. They thrive on companionship and generally prefer not to be left alone for long periods. When arranging pet care, in-home pet sitting is often the best option, as it allows the Pomeranian to stay in their familiar environment while receiving the one-on-one attention they crave.

Mastering the Pomeranian Coat

The defining feature of a Pomeranian is their glorious double coat, consisting of a short, dense undercoat and a longer, harsher outer coat. Maintaining this coat is a commitment. In New Zealand’s varied climate, regular grooming is non-negotiable to prevent painful mats and keep their skin healthy.

Daily brushing is highly recommended. Using a slicker brush, part the hair and brush from the skin outward to ensure you are reaching the undercoat, not just smoothing the top layer. Pay special attention to friction areas like behind the ears, under the armpits, and around the tail. Regular visits to a professional groomer every 4 to 6 weeks for a trim and sanitary tidy-up will help keep the coat manageable.

During summer in places like Auckland or the Hawke's Bay, it might be tempting to shave a Pomeranian to keep them cool. However, professional groomers strongly advise against this. A double coat acts as insulation against both the cold and the heat. Shaving it can permanently damage the coat's texture, interfere with their natural temperature regulation, and expose their sensitive skin to sunburn.

Exercise and Enrichment Needs

While they are energetic, a Pomeranian's exercise requirements are relatively modest due to their small size. A daily walk of 20 to 30 minutes, combined with some indoor playtime, is usually sufficient to keep them physically fit. However, it is crucial to protect them from extreme weather. During the hot NZ summer months, walk them early in the morning or late in the evening to avoid heatstroke, to which their thick coats make them susceptible.

Mental enrichment is just as important as physical exercise. Pomeranians are smart and love to learn tricks or solve puzzles. Puzzle toys, snuffle mats, and short, positive-reinforcement training sessions will help burn mental energy and prevent boredom-induced behaviours like excessive barking or chewing.

When walking a Pomeranian, it is highly recommended to use a well-fitted harness rather than attaching the lead to a collar. Like many toy breeds, Poms are prone to a condition called a collapsing trachea. Pulling on a collar can put dangerous pressure on their fragile windpipes. A harness distributes the force safely across their chest.

Diet and Nutrition

Because they are so small, Pomeranians have fast metabolisms but require very small daily food portions—often just a quarter to half a cup of high-quality dry food per day, divided into two meals. It is remarkably easy to overfeed a Pom, and obesity can lead to severe health issues, particularly stressing their small joints.

Choose a premium dog food formulated specifically for small or toy breeds. These kibbles are appropriately sized for their tiny mouths and jaws. Be mindful of treats; even a small piece of cheese or a single dog biscuit constitutes a significant percentage of a Pomeranian's daily caloric intake. If you are using treats for training, factor them into their overall daily food allowance.

Common Health Considerations

Pomeranians are generally a healthy breed with a long lifespan of 12 to 16 years, but they are predisposed to certain genetic conditions. Dental health is a major concern. Due to their small mouths, their teeth are often crowded, leading to rapid plaque buildup and periodontal disease. Daily teeth brushing with dog-safe toothpaste and regular veterinary dental checks are essential.

Luxating patellas (slipping kneecaps) are common in the breed. You might notice your Pom occasionally skipping or holding up a hind leg while walking. Maintaining a healthy weight helps reduce stress on their joints, but severe cases may require surgical intervention.

As mentioned earlier, tracheal collapse is another risk. Always use a harness, and be observant of any chronic, honking coughs, particularly when the dog is excited or pulling on the lead.

Tips for Pet Sitters Minding Pomeranians

If you are booked to pet sit a Pomeranian in New Zealand, here are a few breed-specific tips to ensure a successful stay:

Watch their step: Poms are incredibly small and quick. Be very careful where you step, and caution children about picking them up. Dropping a Pomeranian, even from a low height, can cause serious bone fractures.

Manage the barking: If the Pom is prone to alert barking, don't yell at them to stop, as they will simply think you are joining in. Use distraction techniques or calmly acknowledge the "threat" and redirect their attention.

Stick to the grooming routine: If you are sitting for more than a couple of days, ask the owner to show you their brushing routine. Spending 10 minutes a day brushing will prevent the coat from matting while the owners are away and provides a great bonding opportunity.

Harness only: Never walk a Pomeranian by the collar. Always ensure their harness is secure before leaving the property.
