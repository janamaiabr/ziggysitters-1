25 Essential Pet Sitter Interview Questions Every NZ Pet Owner Should Ask

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  May 14, 2026  •  12 min read

You wouldn\'t hire a babysitter without asking a few questions. Your pet deserves the same diligence. Before handing over your keys and your fur baby to a stranger, these 25 interview questions will help you separate genuine animal lovers from weekend hobbyists — and give you confidence that your pet is in safe hands while you are away.

Why Interviewing Your Pet Sitter Matters

New Zealand has no mandatory licensing or registration system for pet sitters. Anyone can create a profile and call themselves a professional. That means the vetting process falls entirely on you — and a quick glance at a profile is not enough.

A thorough interview tells you three things:

  • Experience depth: Have they handled pets like yours? A sitter who has only walked Labradors may struggle with an anxious rescue cat.
  • Problem-solving ability: How do they respond when something goes wrong — a pet escapes, refuses food, or shows signs of illness?
  • Personality fit: Do they communicate the way you need? Are they calm, confident, and genuinely interested in your pet?

A 15-minute conversation can save you days of worry — or reveal that someone is not the right fit before it is too late.

The 25 Questions (Organised by Category)

Experience and Background

1. How long have you been pet sitting, and how many pets have you cared for?

Look for specific numbers. "A few years" is vague. "Three years, roughly 200 sits, mostly dogs and cats" tells you they have real volume and can handle variety.

2. What types of pets do you have the most experience with?

If you have a cat and they have only sat dogs, that matters. Cats, rabbits, birds, and reptiles all have very different needs and stress signals.

3. Have you handled pets with medical conditions or special needs?

Diabetic cats needing insulin, arthritic dogs that need help standing, epileptic pets on medication — these require experience and confidence. If your pet has any special requirements, this question is non-negotiable.

4. Are you comfortable with [your specific breed or species]?

Breed-specific knowledge matters. A sitter who knows Staffordshire Bull Terriers will handle one very differently from someone who has only sat Cavalier King Charles Spaniels. Same species, different energy levels, different triggers.

5. Do you have any professional training or certifications?

While not required in NZ, a pet first aid certificate, animal behaviour course, or veterinary nursing background shows they take the role seriously.

Emergency Handling

6. What would you do if my pet escaped or went missing?

The right answer includes: searching the immediate area, contacting you, notifying neighbours, checking local vet clinics and animal control (council pound), and posting on local lost-pet Facebook groups. A blank stare is a red flag.

7. How would you handle a medical emergency?

They should ask about your preferred vet, know the location of the nearest 24-hour emergency clinic, and have a plan for transporting your pet. In NZ, after-hours emergency vets are available in main centres — your sitter should know this.

8. Have you ever dealt with a pet emergency? What happened?

Experience handling a real crisis — and learning from it — is more reassuring than someone who says "nothing has ever gone wrong."

9. What is your policy on contacting the owner when something seems off?

Some sitters hesitate to bother owners on holiday. You want someone who will call you at 2am if your cat is vomiting blood. Set the expectation: always call, no matter the hour.

Daily Routine and Care

10. Can you follow a detailed care sheet exactly as written?

This sounds obvious, but some sitters think they know better. Your pet\'s routine — feeding times, walk routes, medication schedule — exists for a reason. The answer should be an unequivocal yes.

11. How do you handle a pet that refuses to eat?

Refusing food is one of the most common issues when pets are stressed. A good sitter will try warming the food, offering a different texture, sitting with the pet, and monitoring for 24 hours before escalating to a vet.

12. What is your approach to exercise for an energetic dog?

If they say "a walk around the block," that may not be enough for a working breed. They should ask about your dog\'s usual exercise routine and commit to matching or exceeding it.

13. How do you manage pets that are anxious or nervous with strangers?

The best sitters give the pet space, avoid direct eye contact, sit on the floor, and let the pet come to them. Forcing interaction with a scared animal always backfires.

14. Will you send me daily photo and video updates?

This is standard practice with platforms like ZiggySitters. If a sitter resists sending updates, consider it a warning sign.

Logistics and Boundaries

15. What is your cancellation policy?

Life happens. But if a sitter cancels the night before your trip, you need a backup plan. Know their policy upfront.

16. Are you available for a meet-and-greet before the sit?

A sitter who refuses a meet-and-greet is a red flag. This initial meeting lets your pet sniff them, and lets you see how they interact with animals in your home.

17. How many pets do you sit at the same time?

If they are juggling five sits simultaneously, your pet may not get the attention they need. One or two at a time is typical for a dedicated sitter.

18. Do you have your own transport?

In NZ, having a car is essential for reaching vet clinics, especially outside central Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch. A sitter relying on public transport is a liability in an emergency.

19. What are your rates, and what is included?

Clarify whether the rate includes daily walks, medication administration, plant watering, mail collection, and overnight stays. Surprises on the invoice are avoidable if you ask upfront.

20. Do you carry pet sitting insurance?

Professional sitters on platforms like ZiggySitters carry insurance. Independent sitters may not. If something goes wrong — your pet is injured, or your home is damaged — insurance protects both parties.

Red Flags and Green Flags

21. Can I speak with a previous client as a reference?

A confident sitter will gladly connect you with past clients. Hesitation here is telling.

22. Have you ever had a sit go wrong? What did you learn?

Everyone makes mistakes. The question is whether they are honest about them and whether they changed their approach afterwards. A sitter who claims perfection is either inexperienced or dishonest.

23. How do you handle disagreements with pet owners?

You want someone who listens, explains their reasoning, and finds a middle ground. Defensive or combative responses are a deal-breaker.

24. What would you do if you could not make a scheduled visit?

A professional will have a backup plan — a trusted colleague, a notification system, and a commitment to finding cover rather than leaving your pet unattended.

25. Is there anything you would like to ask me about my pet?

This is perhaps the most revealing question of all. A great sitter will have their own questions: What are their fears? Where do they hide? What is their favourite treat? A sitter with no questions has not been paying attention.

Red Flags to Watch For

Stop and reconsider if your sitter:
  • Refuses a meet-and-greet or home visit beforehand
  • Cannot provide any references or reviews
  • Is vague about experience or avoids specific questions
  • Seems irritated by detailed questions about routine
  • Says "it will be fine" without asking about your pet\'s specific needs
  • Has no plan for emergencies or after-hours vet care
  • Quotes a price dramatically below market rate (often means cutting corners)
  • Pressures you to skip the meet-and-greet or pay upfront in cash

Green Flags to Look For

A great pet sitter will:
  • Ask you detailed questions about your pet before you even offer them
  • Arrive at the meet-and-greet with a notepad or phone to take notes
  • Get down on the floor to greet your pet rather than looming over them
  • Know the location of your nearest vet and after-hours emergency clinic
  • Send unprompted updates during the first sit
  • Have clear, fair pricing with no hidden extras
  • Carry insurance and be happy to show proof
  • Follow up after the sit to see how your pet is settling back in

The Meet-and-Greet: Your Most Important Tool

The meet-and-greet is not a formality — it is the single best predictor of a successful sit. Here is how to make it count:

  • Watch how they enter your home. Do they wait to be invited in? Do they remove their shoes? Calm, respectful entry sets the tone.
  • Observe your pet\'s reaction. Most pets take 10-15 minutes to warm up. If your pet is still hiding after 30 minutes, the fit may not be right.
  • Show them the full routine. Walk them through feeding, walks, medication, and bedtime. Pay attention to whether they take notes.
  • Discuss boundaries. Which rooms are off-limits? Where do they sleep? Is it okay to give treats? Clear expectations prevent conflict.
  • Leave your contact details and vet info. A responsible sitter will ask for this before you offer it.

NZ-Specific Considerations

New Zealand has a few unique factors that make pet sitting different from other countries:

  • Dog registration: All dogs must be registered with the local council. Ensure your sitter knows your dog is registered and where the tag is.
  • Leash rules: Most NZ beaches and parks have on-leash requirements. A sitter who lets your dog off-lead in an on-leash area risks a $300 fine — and worse, a lost dog.
  • Ticks and fleas: In warmer regions (Northland, Auckland, Bay of Plenty), tick season runs from September to March. Ask your sitter to check for ticks after outdoor walks.
  • 1080 poison drops: DOC regularly drops 1080 bait for pest control, especially in the South Island and central North Island. Dogs that scavenge dead possums or bait are at serious risk. If you live near a drop zone, make sure your sitter keeps your dog on-lead during walks in bush areas.
  • Earthquake preparedness: NZ sits on a fault line. Your sitter should know your earthquake plan — where the emergency kit is, which room is safest, and how to secure your pet if aftershocks hit.
  • Rural distances: If you live rurally, your nearest vet might be 30 minutes away. Ensure your sitter knows the route and has a reliable vehicle.

After the Interview: Making Your Decision

Use this checklist to evaluate your sitter after the interview:
  • Did they answer all 25 questions confidently and specifically?
  • Did they ask questions about your pet without prompting?
  • Did they interact well with your pet at the meet-and-greet?
  • Do they have relevant experience with your pet type and breed?
  • Are they insured and willing to provide references?
  • Do they know the location of your nearest vet and emergency clinic?
  • Is their pricing transparent with no hidden costs?
  • Do you feel genuinely comfortable trusting them in your home?

If the answer to all of these is yes, you have found a keeper. If even one feels uncertain, trust your gut and keep looking. There are plenty of excellent sitters in New Zealand — and your pet deserves one of them.
