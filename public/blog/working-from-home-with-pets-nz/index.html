Working From Home With Pets in NZ: The Complete 2026 Guide

By ZiggySitters NZ • Updated March 2026 • 11 min read

Remote work changed everything for New Zealand pet owners. Suddenly we were home all day, and our dogs and cats were thrilled — at first. But working from home with pets is a balancing act. Your pet gets more company, but they can also develop unhealthy habits, interrupt your most important calls, and become genuinely stressed when your routine suddenly shifts again. This guide covers what actually works.

The WFH Pet Reality Check

Since 2020, remote and hybrid work has become the norm for hundreds of thousands of New Zealand workers. In Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch especially, many households adopted dogs or cats during lockdowns — animals that have only ever known a human at home all day.

This created a generation of pets who struggle profoundly when their owner returns to the office, even part-time. But it also created real challenges for those still working remotely: constant attention-seeking, barking during video calls, destruction when briefly ignored, and a genuine blurring of the boundary between “my time” and “work time.”

The good news? With the right strategies, you can have a productive workday and a well-adjusted pet. This guide shows you how.

The Core Problem: Your Pet Thinks Every Day Is Saturday

Dogs and cats are creatures of routine. When you worked in an office, your pet learned: “they leave at 8, they come home at 6, and in between I rest.” That’s actually a psychologically healthy pattern for most pets.

When you work from home, your pet sees you constantly available — but unpredictably so. You’re there, then suddenly you’re on a call and “not there.” You play with them, then ignore them. You’re present at lunch but stressful during a deadline. This inconsistency can be more anxiety-inducing than a regular absence, particularly for dogs.

The Hybrid Work Risk: Pets who are home with you full-time can develop severe separation anxiety when you return to the office, even part-time. Proactively building tolerance for alone time — even while working from home — protects your pet from this adjustment shock.

Setting Up a WFH Pet Routine That Works

Structure is the single most powerful tool you have. A predictable daily rhythm reduces anxiety for your pet and helps you stay productive.

The Ideal Daily Structure (With a Dog)

  1. 7:00–7:30 AM — Morning Walk: Get the walk in before your workday starts. A well-exercised dog is significantly calmer during work hours. This is non-negotiable on WFH days.
  2. 8:00 AM — Settle Signal: After the walk and breakfast, give your dog their enrichment (a Kong or puzzle feeder). This signals: “stimulation time is over, settle time starts.”
  3. 8:00 AM–12:00 PM — Deep Work Block: Close the office door or use a baby gate. Your dog rests. You work without interruption.
  4. 12:00–12:30 PM — Midday Break: Short toilet break, a few minutes of play or grooming, then back to rest. This is not a long play session — that signals “afternoon off,” which sets up restlessness.
  5. 12:30–5:00 PM — Afternoon Block: Another work period. Dog rests or self-entertains.
  6. 5:00 PM+ — End-of-Day Activity: Longer walk, garden play, or active training session. This is the quality time your dog has been patiently waiting for.
Consistency is the magic: The first 2–3 weeks feel rigid. After that, your dog starts to anticipate the rhythm and settles into it naturally. Many owners report their dog going to their bed unprompted at 8:00 AM once the pattern is established.

The Video Call Problem (And How to Solve It)

Every remote worker in NZ has a horror story: the dog who barked through the entire board meeting, the cat who walked across the keyboard mid-presentation, the enthusiastic Labrador who knocked over the camera. These are more than embarrassing — they can genuinely undermine professional relationships.

Practical Solutions

  • Frozen Kongs for important calls: A Kong stuffed with mashed banana, peanut butter (xylitol-free), or wet food and frozen the night before will occupy most dogs for 20–40 minutes — enough for most meetings. Keep several in the freezer so you always have one ready.
  • Designate a “call-free zone” in your home: If your dog knows that when you close the study door they go to their mat in the lounge, you’ve solved 80% of the problem. This takes training, but it’s very achievable over 2–3 weeks.
  • Invest in a good microphone: A directional microphone significantly reduces ambient pet noise picked up in calls. This is not a substitute for good management, but it helps.
  • For cats: A tall cat tree in a different room, with a bird feeder visible outside the window, will keep most cats occupied for hours. Cats are also less likely to interrupt calls if they have high-value resting spots away from your workspace.

Don’t Reinforce Demand Behaviour: The hardest rule in WFH pet ownership is this — when your dog nudges your arm during a deadline, and you give them a quick pat “just to settle them down,” you have told them that nudging works. Do it once and you will get nudged forever. The only cure is consistent, complete non-response to demand behaviour.

Breed Considerations for WFH in NZ

Not all breeds respond equally well to the WFH lifestyle. Understanding your breed’s natural tendencies helps you prepare.

High Independence Breeds

Easier WFH companions. Generally content to rest independently.

  • Greyhound / Whippet
  • Basset Hound
  • Shiba Inu
  • Most terrier breeds
  • Cats (most breeds)

High Attachment Breeds

Need more proactive management to prevent WFH problems.

  • Border Collie
  • Labrador & Golden Retriever
  • German Shepherd
  • Maltese & Cavalier
  • Vizsla & Weimaraner
Border Collie owners especially: A bored Border Collie in a home office is an active problem-creator. Daily mental enrichment — training sessions, puzzle feeders, scentwork — is essential. Without it, they will find their own entertainment, and you will not enjoy it.

Good Reasons to Book a Sitter or Walker on WFH Days

  • High-energy young dogs: A 6–18 month old dog can genuinely not settle enough to let you work effectively without additional exercise. A midday walk or play visit transforms the afternoon.

Protecting Your Pet When You Return to the Office

If you’ve been full-time WFH and are returning to the office — even partially — your pet needs a gradual adjustment. Springing an 8-hour absence on a dog who has never experienced one is a recipe for severe separation anxiety and neighbour complaints.

A 4-Week Return-to-Office Transition

  1. Week 1: While still home, practise 30–60 minute separations daily. Leave the house for short errands. Do not make a fuss when you leave or return.
  2. Week 2: Extend absences to 2–3 hours. Begin leaving the dog unsupervised for a morning or afternoon. Use a puzzle feeder before you go.
  3. Week 3: Trial a half-day absence. Book a midday dog walker for the first few weeks back at the office — this breaks the day into manageable segments.
  4. Week 4: Move to full-day absences, still with a midday walk or drop-in. Monitor for signs of distress: neighbour reports of barking, destruction, toilet accidents, or your dog seeming shut-down when you arrive home.
NZ-specific resource: The SPCA NZ has excellent free resources on separation anxiety management, and many Auckland and Wellington trainers now offer “return to office” preparation packages. A few sessions with a certified trainer can save enormous stress.

Quick Tips by Animal

Dogs

  • Exercise before your workday starts — always
  • Teach a solid “place” command (go to mat and stay)
  • Use a baby gate to create physical separation without isolation
  • Rotate toys to keep them novel and engaging
  • Never reward demand behaviour, even with a glance

Cats

  • Create vertical space — cat trees and shelves away from your desk
  • Window bird feeders are the cheapest entertainment you can buy
  • Scheduled play sessions (morning and evening) reduce daytime attention-seeking
  • Consider a cat flap for outdoor access — cats who can self-regulate outside time are calmer indoors
  • Two cats are often easier than one for WFH households — they entertain each other

Rabbits and Other Small Animals

  • These animals generally adapt well to WFH environments, as they are most active at dawn and dusk
  • Ensure their enclosure is away from your workspace — the stress of constant human presence can actually be harmful to prey animals
  • Free-range time can be scheduled around your breaks rather than interrupting deep work blocks
WFH Pet Ownership: The Key Principles
  • Establish a consistent daily routine from day one
  • Exercise your dog before your workday begins
  • Never reward demand behaviour — not even with eye contact
  • Practise short separations even while home to prevent over-attachment
  • Prepare pets for any office return gradually over 3–4 weeks
  • Use professional pet care on high-demand days — it’s a productivity investment

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it actually good for my dog that I work from home?

It can be — but it depends on how you manage it. A dog with a calm, structured WFH owner who gets good exercise and learns to settle independently is in an excellent situation. A dog whose owner is inconsistent, stressed, and constantly “shushing” them can develop anxiety that wouldn’t have arisen with a traditional office routine.

My cat meows constantly when I’m on calls. What should I do?

This is almost always learned attention-seeking behaviour. The cat has discovered that meowing during calls gets a reaction (you look at them, shush them, or push them away — all forms of attention). The solution is complete non-response combined with enriching their environment so they have better options than interrupting you.

My dog has become aggressive toward visitors since I started WFH. Why?

A dog who rarely experiences the outside world — fewer walks, less socialisation, fewer visitors — can become increasingly territorial. If this is happening, prioritise daily socialisation: walks in busy areas, dog park visits, and controlled visitor introductions. A certified trainer can help if the behaviour is severe.

When should I see a vet or behaviourist about my WFH pet?

If your pet is showing self-destructive behaviour, prolonged refusal to eat, constant vocalising, or aggression, it’s time to involve a professional. A vet can rule out medical causes; a certified animal behaviourist (there are several excellent ones in Auckland and Wellington) can design a specific programme for your dog’s situation.
