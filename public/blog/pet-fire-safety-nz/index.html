Pet Fire Safety NZ Guide: Protecting Your Pets from House Fires

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  May 20, 2026  •  13 min read

Each year, New Zealand records over 5,000 house fires, and pets are among the most vulnerable victims. They cannot dial 111, open doors, or escape on their own. Whether it is a kitchen fire, a heater mishap in winter, or a rural wildfire threatening your property, being prepared can save your pet\'s life. This guide covers fire prevention, evacuation planning, emergency kits, and wildfire-specific advice for New Zealand pet owners.

The Scale of the Problem

House fires are a year-round risk in New Zealand, but they spike dramatically in winter. Between May and August, Fire and Emergency NZ responds to significantly more residential fires, driven by:

  • Portable heaters and electric blankets
  • Fireplaces and wood burners (common in NZ homes)
  • Dryers running unattended
  • Candles and heating elements left near flammable materials

Pets left home alone are at particular risk because they are often confined to certain rooms and cannot self-evacuate. A study by the US Fire Administration found that roughly 40,000 pets die in house fires annually in the US alone; while NZ numbers are smaller proportionally, the risk is real and preventable.

Fire Prevention: Stop It Before It Starts

Kitchen Safety

  • Never leave cooking unattended — the leading cause of house fires in NZ
  • Keep pets off benchtops when the stove is on (curious tails can knock over pots)
  • Install a stove guard if you have cats that jump on surfaces
  • Keep tea towels, paper, and packaging away from heat sources

Heating

  • Maintain at least 1 metre of clear space around any heater
  • Never dry clothes on or near a heater
  • Have your fireplace and chimney professionally cleaned annually
  • Use a fire screen in front of open fires — sparks can travel and pets can get too close
  • Turn off electric blankets before getting into bed, not after falling asleep
  • Keep pet beds away from heaters and fireplaces — synthetic fills are highly flammable

Electrical

  • Do not overload power points or use daisy-chained extension cords
  • Check cords regularly for chewing damage, especially with puppies and kittens
  • Unplug appliances when not in use
  • Keep cords out of reach or use cord covers — chewing on live cables causes fires and electrocution

Candles and Open Flames

  • Never leave candles burning unattended or in a room with an unsupervised pet
  • Cats can knock candles off shelves — use stable holders on flat, low surfaces
  • Consider flameless LED candles as an alternative
  • Keep matches and lighters in closed cupboards (some dogs will chew them)
ZG Tip: Pet-related fire starters you might not think of: aquarium heaters malfunctioning, heat lamps for reptiles left on 24/7, and glass fishbowls magnifying sunlight onto curtains. Check these regularly.

Smoke Alarms: Your First Line of Defence

Working smoke alarms cut the risk of fire death in half. For pet safety, go beyond the minimum:

  • Install smoke alarms in every bedroom, hallway, and living area
  • Test them monthly and replace batteries annually (or use 10-year lithium batteries)
  • Consider monitored smoke alarms that alert a monitoring centre — critical when you are not home
  • Smart smoke detectors (like Google Nest Protect) send alerts to your phone, so you know immediately if there is a problem at home while you are out
  • Replace smoke alarms every 10 years — NZ Building Code requirement

Create a Pet-Inclusive Fire Evacuation Plan

Most evacuation plans assume everyone can walk out independently. Your pets cannot. Build your plan around them.

Step 1: Identify All Exits

Walk through your home and identify every door and window that could serve as an exit. For each room where a pet spends time:

  • Note the quickest route out
  • Ensure windows can be opened quickly (check that latches work)
  • Keep pathways clear of clutter

Step 2: Assign Responsibility

If you have multiple family members, assign each pet to a specific person:

  • Adult 1: Dog (grab leash by the front door)
  • Adult 2: Cat carrier from laundry (know where it is!)
  • Teen: Small pets (rabbit/guinea pig — carrier under the desk)

If you live alone, keep leashes and carriers near the main exit so you can grab them on the way out.

Step 3: Practise

  • Run a fire drill with pets at least twice a year
  • Train dogs to come when called even under stress
  • Get cats comfortable with their carrier — leave it out with a blanket inside so it is not a scary box they only see once a year
  • Time yourself: can you get all pets out in under 2 minutes?

Step 4: The Window Decal

Place a pet rescue window decal on your front and back doors. These tell firefighters how many pets are inside and what type. You can get free ones from many NZ vet clinics or order them online. Update the decal whenever your pet count changes.

What to Do During a Fire

⚠ Your LIFE comes first. If a room is filled with smoke or fire, do not enter it to search for a pet. Get out and tell firefighters immediately about any pets inside. They are trained to rescue animals.

If You Are Home When Fire Starts

  1. Evacuate immediately. Grab your go-bag and pet carriers if they are on the way out. Do not go back in.
  2. If you can safely reach a pet on your way out, scoop them up. Do not waste time searching.
  3. Call 111 once you are safely outside.
  4. Keep pets on leash or in carriers — they will be terrified and may bolt.
  5. Move away from the house and stay upwind of smoke.

If You Are Not Home

This is where monitored alarms save lives. If you get an alert:

  1. Call 111 immediately, even if you are not sure it is real
  2. Tell the operator there are pets inside and describe them (type, number, likely location)
  3. Get home as fast as safely possible, but do not enter a burning building
  4. Have your vet\'s number and an emergency vet saved in your phone

Neighbours Can Help

Give a trusted neighbour a key and your contact number. Ask them to check on your pets if there is a fire while you are out. This is one of the most effective things you can do.

Pet Emergency Fire Kit

Keep a grab-and-go kit near the main exit. It should include:

Pet Fire Go-Bag Checklist

  • ☑ Leash and harness (with name tag and your phone number)
  • ☑ Collapsible carrier or travel crate
  • ☑ 3 days of food (in a sealed bag or container)
  • ☑ Bottled water and collapsible bowl
  • ☑ Copies of vaccination records and microchip number
  • ☑ Pet first aid kit (bandages, antiseptic, tweezers, tick remover)
  • ☑ Any regular medication (2-week supply)
  • ☑ Comfort item: familiar blanket or toy (reduces stress)
  • ☑ Recent photo of your pet (for identification if they bolt)
  • ☑ List of emergency contacts: vet, animal control, local shelter
  • ☑ Waste bags and litter tray with litter

Smoke Inhalation: The Hidden Danger

Smoke kills more pets than flames. Pets that survive a fire may still suffer from smoke inhalation, which can cause serious lung damage. Signs to watch for:

  • Coughing, wheezing, or difficulty breathing
  • Bright red, pale, or blue gums
  • Squinting, red eyes, or discharge
  • Lethargy, confusion, or collapse
  • Soot around the nose or mouth
  • Vomiting or excessive drooling

If your pet has been near a fire, even briefly, take them to a vet for assessment. Smoke damage may not be immediately visible and can worsen over 24-72 hours.

New Zealand Wildfire and Bushfire Safety

New Zealand is not immune to wildfire. In hot, dry summers — particularly in Canterbury, Marlborough, Hawke\'s Bay, and the Central North Island — vegetation fires can threaten rural and lifestyle-block properties. If you live in a fire-prone area:

Before Fire Season

  • Create a defensible zone: clear dry vegetation, leaves, and debris within 10 metres of your home
  • Store firewood at least 10 metres from buildings
  • Keep gutters clear of dry leaves and debris
  • Ensure your property has clear access for fire trucks
  • Have a rural water supply plan (tank, pool, or dam with fire-fighting pump)
  • Make sure your property number is visible from the road

When a Wildfire Threatens

  • Evacuate early — do not wait for an official warning. If you feel unsafe, leave.
  • Bring all pets indoors and into carriers before you need to leave
  • Wet down animals that cannot be brought inside (horses, livestock) and open gates so they can escape
  • Move large animals to a pre-identified safe paddock (short grass, no overhanging trees)
  • Never release pets to "run free" — they will hide under the house or in bush and cannot be found later
  • Bring your pet go-bag
⚠ Rural property owners: If you have horses, alpacas, or livestock, identify a "safe paddock" now — well-grazed, away from trees and hedges, with a gate that opens onto a clear route. Practice moving animals there before fire season.

After a Fire: Returning Home with Pets

Even after the fire is out, your home may not be safe for pets:

  • Hot spots and smouldering debris can cause severe burns to paw pads
  • Collapsed structures may have sharp edges and exposed nails
  • Firefighting chemicals and foam are toxic if ingested
  • Stress and unfamiliar smells may cause pets to bolt
  • Keep pets leashed or confined until you have inspected the property
  • Do not let pets drink from puddles or standing water (contamination risk)

Before bringing pets back, check with Fire and Emergency NZ that the property is safe, and have your vet examine any animal that was exposed to smoke or heat.

Pet Sitters and Fire Safety

When you leave your pets with a sitter, fire safety communication is critical:

  • Show the sitter where carriers, leashes, and the go-bag are kept
  • Walk them through the evacuation plan — which exits, which pets first
  • Provide your vet\'s number, the after-hours emergency vet, and your contact number
  • Write down how many pets and where they usually hide when scared
  • Make sure the sitter knows not to re-enter the house once evacuated
  • Leave a pet rescue decal on the front door visible to emergency services

Key Takeaways

  • Prevent: Keep heaters, candles, and cooking attended. Check cords for pet damage. Maintain smoke alarms.
  • Plan: Have a pet-inclusive evacuation plan. Assign each pet to a person. Practise your drill.
  • Prepare: Build a pet go-bag. Get a monitored smoke alarm if pets are home alone regularly.
  • Communicate: Tell your pet sitter the fire plan. Give a neighbour a key. Put a rescue decal on your door.
  • Protect: Never re-enter a burning building. Get pets to a vet after any smoke exposure.
