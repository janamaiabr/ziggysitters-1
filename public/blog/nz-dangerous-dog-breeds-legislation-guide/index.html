New Zealand Dangerous Dog Breeds: Legislation, Restrictions & What Dog Owners Need to Know

New Zealand takes dog safety seriously. Under the Dog Control Act 1996, certain breeds are classified as menacing or dangerous, and ownership comes with extra responsibilities. Whether you own one of these breeds, are considering adopting one, or work as a pet sitter, understanding the legislation is essential. This guide covers the classified breeds, what the law requires, and how it affects everyday dog ownership and pet care in NZ.

Understanding NZ Dog Classification: Menacing vs Dangerous

The Dog Control Act 1996 establishes two key classifications that affect how a dog is managed in New Zealand. These are not the same thing, and the distinction matters.

Menacing Dog Classification

A dog can be classified as menacing if a council dog control officer believes it constitutes a threat to any person, stock, poultry, domestic animal, or protected wildlife. This classification can apply to any breed or type of dog based on its behaviour, not just the breeds listed below. However, certain breeds are automatically classified as menacing.

Once classified as menacing, the owner must:

  • Muzzle the dog in public at all times
  • Leash the dog in public at all times (no off-lead exercise)
  • Neuter the dog within one month of classification (unless exempted)
  • Not leave the dog alone with children or vulnerable people unsupervised
  • Display a warning sign at the property where the dog is kept

Dangerous Dog Classification

A dog is classified as dangerous if it has attacked a person or animal, or if the owner has been convicted of an offence under the Dog Control Act involving the dog. This is a more serious classification with stricter requirements:

  • All menacing classification requirements apply
  • The dog must be kept in a securely fenced area that prevents escape and prevents children from entering
  • Higher registration fees (typically 150% of the standard fee)
  • The owner must advise the council of any change of address
  • The dog may not be left in the sole charge of a person under 16 years old

Both classifications are serious. They appear on the dog registration record and follow the dog for life, even if ownership changes.

Automatically Classified Menacing Breeds in New Zealand

The following five breeds, along with any crossbreeds that include these breeds, are automatically classified as menacing under the Dog Control (Menacing Dogs) Amendment Act 2003 and subsequent amendments:

1. American Pit Bull Terrier

The most well-known of the classified breeds. The American Pit Bull Terrier was originally bred for dog fighting in the 19th century, though responsible owners argue the breed can be affectionate and loyal. In NZ, the classification applies regardless of the individual dog\'s temperament. Crossbreeds that include Pit Bull lineage are also covered, which creates challenges for owners of mixed-breed rescue dogs where parentage is unclear.

2. Brazilian Fila (Fila Brasileiro)

A large working breed originally used for tracking and herding cattle in Brazil. Known for strong guarding instincts and suspicion of strangers. Extremely rare in New Zealand, but the classification still applies. If you encounter one as a pet sitter, the menacing classification requirements must be followed.

3. Dogo Argentino (Argentinian Mastiff)

Bred for big-game hunting in Argentina, the Dogo Argentino is a powerful, muscular breed. While loyal and affectionate with family, their high prey drive and strength mean the NZ legislation applies strict controls. Uncommon in New Zealand but growing in popularity among experienced dog owners.

4. Japanese Tosa

Originally a fighting dog from Japan, the Tosa is rare worldwide and extremely uncommon in New Zealand. A massive, powerful breed that can weigh over 60kg. The legislation treats any Tosa or Tosa cross as automatically menacing.

5. Perro de Presa Canario (Canary Dog)

A large guardian breed from the Canary Islands with a history in dog fighting. Strong-willed and protective, these dogs require experienced handlers. Like the other classified breeds, any Presa Canario or Presa Canario cross in NZ is automatically classified as menacing.

The Crossbreed Problem

One of the most contentious aspects of NZ dog legislation is how it handles crossbreeds. If a dog is judged by a council dog control officer to be a cross of any of the five classified breeds, the menacing classification applies automatically. This creates real challenges:

  • Visual identification is unreliable. Many mixed-breed dogs are misidentified. Staffy crosses, for example, are frequently mistaken for Pit Bull crosses.
  • DNA testing is not required. Councils can classify based on appearance alone, not genetic testing.
  • Rescue dogs are particularly vulnerable. Dogs in shelters with uncertain parentage may be classified as menacing based on how they look, limiting their adoption prospects.
  • Once classified, the burden of proof is on the owner to challenge the classification through the objections process.

If your dog is classified as menacing and you believe the classification is wrong, you have the right to object in writing to your local council within 14 days of receiving the notice. You can provide evidence such as vet records, behavioural assessments, or DNA test results. Legal advice is recommended for objections.

Importing Classified Breeds into New Zealand

New Zealand has some of the strictest import rules in the world for dogs. The following breeds are prohibited from importation:

  • American Pit Bull Terrier
  • Brazilian Fila
  • Dogo Argentino
  • Japanese Tosa
  • Perro de Presa Canario

The Ministry for Primary Industries (MPI) will not issue import permits for these breeds or their crosses. This means you cannot bring a classified breed into New Zealand, even if you are moving from Australia or another country where ownership is legal. Attempting to import a prohibited breed is an offence under the Biosecurity Act 1993 and the Dog Control Act.

For all other dog breeds, importing to New Zealand requires meeting strict biosecurity requirements including vaccination records, parasite treatment, and a minimum 10-day quarantine period. See our guide to bringing pets to New Zealand for full details.

What Classification Means for Pet Sitters

If you are a pet sitter in New Zealand, you may encounter classified dogs. Here is what you need to know:

Before Accepting a Booking

  • Ask about classification. Responsible owners will disclose this upfront. Check the dog\'s council registration if you are unsure.
  • Assess your capability. Menacing and dangerous classified dogs require experienced handling. If you are not confident, it is better to decline than to take on a dog you cannot safely manage.
  • Check insurance. Some pet sitter insurance policies exclude classified breeds or charge higher premiums. Verify your coverage before accepting.

During the Sit

  • Follow muzzle and lead requirements. If a dog is classified as menacing, it must be muzzled and on a lead in public. This is not optional. Failing to comply can result in fines for the owner and for you as the person in control of the dog.
  • Keep the dog secure. Dangerous classified dogs must be kept in a securely fenced area. Ensure gates are closed and the dog cannot escape.
  • Never leave a classified dog with children. This is a legal requirement for dangerous dogs and a strong recommendation for menacing dogs.
  • Inform your own household. Everyone in your home needs to know the dog\'s classification and the rules that apply.

At the Meet and Greet

A meet and greet is especially important for classified dogs. Use this time to:

  • Observe the dog\'s behaviour with you and others
  • Review the owner\'s handling instructions and any behaviour notes
  • Practise putting on and removing the muzzle
  • Discuss emergency procedures and vet contacts
  • Walk the dog on lead to assess leash manners

Registration and Microchipping Requirements

All dogs in New Zealand must be registered with their local council and microchipped. For classified breeds, there are additional requirements:

  • Registration must include the menacing or dangerous classification. Failing to declare a known classification is an offence.
  • Microchipping is mandatory for all dogs first registered after 1 July 2006, and this is enforced more strictly for classified breeds.
  • Higher registration fees apply for dangerous dogs (typically 50% more than the standard fee). Some councils also charge higher fees for menacing dogs.
  • Change of ownership must be notified to the council within 14 days. The classification transfers with the dog.

For general microchipping information, see our NZ dog registration and microchipping guide.

What to Do If Your Dog Is Classified

Receiving a menacing or dangerous classification notice can be stressful. Here is what you should do:

1. Read the Notice Carefully

The notice will specify the classification, the reasons, and the date by which you must comply. It also explains your right to object.

2. Consider Your Options

  • Object in writing within 14 days if you believe the classification is wrong. This is your legal right.
  • Comply with the requirements while the objection is being considered. The muzzle and lead rules apply from the date of the notice, not from when your objection is resolved.
  • Seek legal advice if the stakes are high. A lawyer specialising in animal law can help navigate the objections process.

3. Comply Immediately

Even if you plan to object, you must comply with the classification requirements from the date of the notice. This means:

  • Purchase and fit an approved muzzle
  • Ensure the dog is always on a lead in public
  • Arrange neutering within one month (if required)
  • Display a warning sign at your property

4. Behavioural Assessment

A professional behavioural assessment from a qualified dog trainer or veterinary behaviourist can support an objection. Some councils accept these as evidence that a dog does not pose a threat, even if the breed classification applies. The assessment should be conducted by someone accredited by the New Zealand Companion Animal Council or equivalent body.

Responsible Ownership of Classified Breeds

Many owners of classified breeds are responsible, experienced dog handlers whose dogs are well-trained and well-socialised. The classification does not mean the dog is inherently dangerous, but it does mean the owner has additional legal obligations. Here are some best practices:

Training and Socialisation

  • Invest in professional obedience training from a positive-reinforcement trainer
  • Socialise your dog appropriately, always within the legal requirements (muzzled and on lead in public)
  • Practice impulse control exercises daily
  • Consider Canine Good Citizen certification as evidence of your dog\'s temperament

Management

  • Always carry a muzzle when out with your dog, even for short walks
  • Use a sturdy lead and harness rather than a collar, which can slip off
  • Keep your dog secure at home with fencing that meets or exceeds council requirements
  • Never leave your dog unattended in a vehicle or tied up outside a shop

Community Relations

  • Be a good neighbour. Introduce yourself and your dog to people nearby
  • Be visible about responsible ownership. Let people see you following the rules
  • Consider joining a local dog training club to demonstrate commitment to good behaviour

Common Misconceptions

"The breed ban means these dogs are illegal in NZ"

Not exactly. Owning a classified breed is legal in New Zealand. It is importing them that is prohibited. If you already own a classified breed that was in the country before the import ban, or if the dog was born here, ownership is legal with the right classifications and conditions.

"My Staffy will be classified as menacing"

Staffordshire Bull Terriers are not one of the automatically classified breeds. However, confusion between Staffordshire Bull Terriers and American Pit Bull Terriers does happen. If your Staffy is misidentified, you have the right to object to the classification.

"Once classified, always classified"

This is largely true. The classification stays with the dog for life and transfers to any new owner. However, you can apply to the council to have a menacing classification removed if you can demonstrate that the dog no longer poses a threat, typically through sustained good behaviour and professional behavioural assessments over an extended period.

"Pet sitters cannot look after classified dogs"

There is no law preventing pet sitters from caring for classified dogs. However, the sitter must comply with all classification requirements (muzzle, lead, secure containment) while the dog is in their care. Some sitters choose not to accept classified breeds, which is their right.

Key Takeaways

  • New Zealand classifies five breeds (and their crosses) as automatically menacing: American Pit Bull Terrier, Brazilian Fila, Dogo Argentino, Japanese Tosa, and Perro de Presa Canario
  • Menacing dogs must be muzzled and on lead in public at all times
  • Dangerous classification is behaviour-based and carries stricter requirements including secure fencing and higher registration fees
  • Importing the five classified breeds is prohibited
  • Owners can object to a classification within 14 days
  • Pet sitters can care for classified dogs but must follow the same legal requirements as owners
  • Responsible ownership, professional training, and community engagement help classified breed owners be good neighbours
