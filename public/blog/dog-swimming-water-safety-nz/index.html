Dog Swimming and Water Safety in New Zealand

Published 1 April 2026 • 11 min read • By ZiggySitters NZ

New Zealand is a paradise for water-loving dogs. With over 15,000 kilometres of coastline, hundreds of rivers, and dozens of spectacular lakes, our dogs have no shortage of places to splash about. But water brings real risks — from strong currents and toxic algae to hypothermia and ear infections. This guide covers everything NZ dog owners need to know to keep their dogs safe and happy around water.

Do All Dogs Naturally Know How to Swim?

A common misconception is that all dogs are natural swimmers. While most dogs can paddle instinctively if placed in water, paddling and swimming are not the same thing. Without proper technique, many dogs tire quickly, become panicked, or struggle to keep their head above water for long. Some breeds are genuinely poor swimmers and face real drowning risk without assistance.

Regardless of breed, always introduce your dog to water gradually and never force them in. A dog that has had a frightening early experience with water may develop lasting anxiety around it.

Teaching Your Dog to Swim

The best time to introduce swimming is during warmer months when water temperatures are comfortable. Puppies between 3 and 6 months often take to water most readily, but older dogs can absolutely learn.

  1. Start shallow: Let your dog explore the water’s edge at their own pace. A gently sloping beach or a shallow river bank is ideal. Do not carry them in.
  2. Use positive reinforcement: Bring high-value treats and reward every step closer to the water. Keep early sessions short and upbeat.
  3. Get in with them: Your presence in the water reassures nervous dogs. Wade in up to your knees and encourage them to follow.
  4. Use a toy: Once your dog is comfortable paddling, a floating toy can encourage them to swim slightly deeper.
  5. Support the hindquarters: New swimmers often keep their front legs working but let their back legs sink, causing them to dig down rather than swim forward. Gently support their hindquarters to help them find a horizontal swimming position.
  6. End on a positive note: Always stop before your dog becomes tired or overwhelmed. Leave the water while your dog is still keen.
NZ Tip: Many New Zealand dogs get their first swimming experience at the beach. Sandy-bottomed beaches with gentle waves and gradual slopes — such as those found at Takapuna (Auckland), Oriental Parade (Wellington), or New Brighton (Christchurch) — make ideal first swim spots.

Beach Safety for Dogs in NZ

New Zealand beaches are glorious, but they come with specific hazards to know about.

Rip Currents and Shore Breaks

Rip currents can exhaust even strong swimmer dogs very quickly. If your dog gets caught in a rip, call them out at an angle to the current rather than directly against it — the same advice given to human swimmers. Never enter surf to rescue a dog without a flotation aid; many human drownings occur when people attempt to save a dog.

Shore breaks (waves that crash directly onto the shore) can knock a dog off their feet, tumble them, and cause disorientation or water inhalation. Young dogs and small breeds are particularly vulnerable.

Salt Water Ingestion

Dogs often drink salt water while playing in the surf, particularly enthusiastic retrievers. Small amounts are usually not a problem, but excessive salt water ingestion causes vomiting, diarrhoea, lethargy, and in serious cases, salt poisoning (hypernatraemia), which can be life-threatening.

  • Bring fresh drinking water to the beach and offer it frequently
  • Limit retrieval sessions to prevent excessive water swallowing
  • Watch for signs of salt toxicity: vomiting, trembling, seizures, extreme lethargy — seek veterinary care immediately

Beach Rules and Dog Access

Beach access rules vary significantly across New Zealand. Many beaches have “dogs on lead” or “no dogs” zones, particularly near shorebird nesting areas. Always check with your local council before heading to a new beach. NZ councils take the protection of coastal wildlife seriously, and fines apply.

Shorebirds and NZ wildlife: New Zealand’s beaches host breeding colonies of endangered birds like the NZ dotterel (tuturiwhatu) and banded dotterel. Dogs running loose on beaches — even friendly ones — cause significant disturbance to these nests. Stick to designated dog exercise areas and keep your dog on lead near vegetation or rocky outcrops where birds may be nesting.

River and Lake Safety in NZ

Rivers and lakes are popular spots for dogs and their owners, but they present different hazards to beaches.

River Currents

Many New Zealand rivers are fed by snowmelt or run-off from the Southern Alps, meaning they can run cold and fast even on warm days. What looks like a calm stretch of river can have a strong current beneath the surface. If you are not sure whether a river is safe, treat it as unsafe. Avoid rivers during and immediately after heavy rainfall when flow rates increase dramatically and water levels can rise quickly.

Blue-Green Algae: A Serious NZ Hazard

Blue-green algae (cyanobacteria) is one of the most serious water-related threats to dogs in New Zealand. It thrives in warm, slow-moving freshwater — particularly in lakes and rivers during summer and early autumn — and produces toxins that can kill a dog within hours of exposure.

Blue-green algae can kill your dog very quickly. Symptoms appear within 15 minutes to a few hours of exposure: vomiting, muscle twitching, weakness, difficulty breathing, seizures, and collapse. If you suspect algae exposure, rinse your dog with fresh water immediately and go directly to a vet. There is no antidote — rapid supportive treatment is the only option.

What to look for: mats of blue-green, grey-green, or brownish scum on the water surface; a paint-like or pea soup appearance; a musty or earthy smell. If in doubt, do not let your dog in the water. Regional councils in NZ post algae alerts online — check your council website before visiting lakes or rivers in summer.

Leptospirosis

Leptospirosis is a bacterial infection spread through the urine of infected animals (particularly rats and possums) in water and damp soil. New Zealand has relatively low rates of the disease in dogs compared to some countries, but it is present. Your vet can advise on whether vaccination is recommended based on your dog’s activity profile. Dogs who swim or wade in rivers and puddles regularly are at higher risk.

Swimming Pools and Dogs

Many New Zealand homes have swimming pools, and some dogs love them. If your dog has access to a pool, safety measures are essential.

  • Teach exit points: Every dog with pool access must know how to exit the pool. Walk them to the steps or ramp repeatedly until they find it reliably. Drowning occurs when dogs enter pools and cannot find their way out.
  • Install pool fencing: Under NZ law, residential pools must have compliant fencing. Ensure gates are self-latching and that your dog cannot access the pool unsupervised.
  • Pool chemicals: Chlorinated water is generally safe for occasional swimming, but excessive exposure can irritate eyes, skin, and coat. Rinse your dog with fresh water after every pool session.
  • Algaecides: Some pool algaecides contain copper sulfate, which is toxic to dogs. Check product labels and store chemicals securely.

Do You Need a Dog Life Jacket?

A dog life jacket (personal flotation device) is a sensible investment for many NZ dogs, particularly for boating trips, stand-up paddleboarding, kayaking, or any activity where a dog might fall into deep water unexpectedly.

A good dog life jacket should:

  • Fit snugly without restricting movement or breathing
  • Have a handle on the back for easy retrieval
  • Be brightly coloured for visibility in the water
  • Cover the belly as well as the back for buoyancy

Life jackets are essential for brachycephalic breeds (Frenchies, Pugs, Bulldogs), very young or old dogs, dogs on boats or kayaks, and any dog in open water or fast-moving rivers.

After-Swim Care for Dogs

After any swim session, a bit of routine maintenance protects your dog’s health and comfort.

Rinsing

Always rinse your dog with fresh water after swimming, particularly after salt water or lake/river swims. Salt, sand, algae, and bacteria can irritate skin and coat if left to dry in. Pay particular attention to the paws, between the toes, and the groin area.

Ear Care

“Swimmer’s ear” (otitis externa) is common in dogs who swim regularly. Water trapped in the ear canal creates a warm, moist environment where yeast and bacteria thrive. Signs include head shaking, scratching at the ear, a smell from the ears, or discharge.

After swimming, gently dry the outer ear with a clean cloth. If your dog is prone to ear infections, ask your vet about using a drying ear rinse after swims. Dogs with floppy ears (Labradors, Spaniels, Poodles) are most susceptible.

Watch for Fatigue

Swimming is excellent exercise, but it is surprisingly tiring — often more so than an equivalent walk. Watch for signs that your dog has had enough: slowing down in the water, sinking hindquarters, laboured breathing. An exhausted dog in the water is at risk. Always end a swim session before your dog reaches this point.

Water Safety and Pet Sitters

If your dog loves swimming and your pet sitter will be taking them to the beach or a river, make sure you provide clear instructions. Let your sitter know:

  • Whether your dog is a confident or nervous swimmer
  • Any history of ear infections that might require post-swim ear care
  • Which beaches or parks your dog is familiar with (and authorised to enter)
  • Whether your dog wears a life jacket in certain situations
  • Your vet’s emergency contact details
For pet sitters: Never let a dog you are sitting swim in unknown water without first checking for algae warnings and assessing current strength. When in doubt, a controlled wade rather than a free swim is the right call. Always have fresh water available and keep a close eye on any dog near water — accidents happen quickly.
