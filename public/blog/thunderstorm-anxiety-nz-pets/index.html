Why Do Pets Fear Storms?

Thunderstorm anxiety in pets is more complex than a simple fear of loud noises. Several factors combine to make storms particularly distressing for dogs and cats.

Barometric pressure changes. Before a storm arrives, the atmospheric pressure drops significantly. Animals can physically feel this change, and many interpret it as a signal of approaching danger. Some pets will begin showing anxiety behaviours even thirty minutes before the first clap of thunder.

Static electricity. Research suggests that dogs, with their thick fur coats, can build up static charge during storms. This invisible tingling sensation can be uncomfortable or even painful, leading dogs to seek out unusual places—under furniture, in bathtubs, or pressed tightly against their owners.

Low-frequency sounds. Thunder produces infrasound—sound frequencies so low that humans cannot hear them, but dogs certainly can. This constant, rumbling noise can create a background sense of unease that builds over minutes or hours.

Unpredictability. Unlike the steady rain that follows a front, thunder is sharp, sudden, and seemingly random. Animals that thrive on routine find the unpredictability of storms particularly unsettling.

Recognising the Signs

Pet owners often mistake storm anxiety for general nervousness. Signs to watch for include:

  • Panting and pacing—even when the temperature is cool
  • Trembling or shaking, especially in smaller breeds
  • Seeking refuge—hiding under beds, in wardrobes, or bathrooms
  • Destructive behaviour—scratching at doors, chewing furniture
  • Excessive vocalisation—barking, whining, or meowing more than usual
  • Physical symptoms—drooling, dilated pupils, loss of appetite
  • Inappropriate toileting—accidents in previously house-trained pets

Cats may become withdrawn, hide for hours, or stop using their litter trays. Some cats will vocalise loudly or scratch at surfaces in an attempt to escape what they perceive as a threat.

Preparing Your Home Before a Storm

Prevention starts before the first drop of rain falls. Creating a storm-safe zone gives your pet a trusted retreat when anxiety spikes.

Designate a safe space. Identify the quietest, most interior room in your home—often a bathroom or wardrobe. Fill it with your pet\'s favourite bedding, toys, and an item of your clothing that carries your scent. Ensure this space is accessible at all times, including when you are not home.

Check microchip details. Storms can cause pets to panic and bolt. Ensure your pet\'s microchip is registered with the New Zealand Companion Animal Register (NZCAR) and that their ID tag is legible. This simple step dramatically increases the chances of a safe return if they escape.

Close windows and curtains. Reducing visual stimulation—the flashing lights, swaying trees, and driving rain—helps lower overall arousal. Draw curtains to block out lightning flashes.

Use white noise. Turn on the radio, television, or a fan. Consistent background sound masks the sharper crack of thunder and creates a more predictable acoustic environment.

During the Storm: What to Do

Your behaviour directly influences your pet\'s emotional state. Dogs are highly attuned to your body language and vocal tone, often reading your emotions more accurately than you realise.

Stay calm yourself. If you are visibly worried or overly comforting, your pet may interpret the situation as more dangerous than it is. Speak in a calm, neutral tone. Do not punish fear-based behaviours—this only increases anxiety.

Do not restrain your pet. Let them move to their safe space. Forcing them to stay close when they want to hide can escalate panic. Instead, sit quietly nearby so they know you are present without crowding them.

Offer distraction. A stuffed Kong, a puzzle toy, or a long-lasting chew can redirect focus away from the storm. The mental effort required to earn treats or kibble can override fear responses.

Consider calming aids. Pheromone products like Adaptil (for dogs) or Feliway (for cats) take effect over days, so integrate them into your routine before storm season. Anxiety wraps apply gentle pressure and work best when introducedpositively before a stressful event.

When to Seek Veterinary Help

For some pets, environmental modifications are insufficient. If your pet\'s storm anxiety is severe—resulting in self-injury, complete refusal to eat for hours, or lasting behavioural changes—it is time to consult your veterinarian.

Veterinary behaviourists can prescribe medication ranging from short-acting anti-anxiety drugs (useful for known storm events) to daily medications for chronic anxiety. In New Zealand, your vet may also recommend a certified animal behaviourist for specialised desensitisation training.

Never give your pet human medications. Many common drugs, including paracetamol and ibuprofen, are highly toxic to animals.

Long-Term Solutions: Desensitisation

While not a quick fix, systematic desensitisation offers the most lasting relief. This involves playing storm sounds at very low volumes while your pet engages in positive activities—eating, playing, or receiving treats. Over weeks or months, the volume is gradually increased.

A key principle is pairing the sound with something wonderful. The goal is for your pet to associate the rumble of thunder with treats, play, and relaxation rather than fear. This process requires patience and consistency. Consider working with a qualified behaviourist to develop a personalised training plan.

What Pet Sitters Need to Know

If you are a pet sitter in New Zealand, storm awareness is part of the job. During the autumn and winter months when storms are most frequent, proactive communication with pet owners is essential.

Ask the owner during the meet-and-greet whether their pet has a history of storm anxiety. Locate the safe space, identify calming products already in use, and ensure you have the emergency vet contact and the owner\'s mobile number handy. During a storm, send brief updates to reassure the owner that their pet is safe.

Your role is to maintain a calm environment. Close windows and curtains, turn on background noise, and allow the pet to retreat to their safe space without interference. Do not leave anxious pets alone during severe weather.

Conclusion

Thunderstorm anxiety is a genuine and treatable condition affecting many New Zealand pets. By understanding the causes, preparing your home in advance, and remaining calm during events, you can significantly reduce your pet\'s distress. For severe cases, professional veterinary support offers effective solutions.

Remember, every pet responds differently. What works for one dog or cat may not work for another. Be patient, stay consistent, and prioritise your pet\'s emotional wellbeing. With time and thoughtful intervention, even the most storm-phobic pet can learn to feel safer when the thunder rolls across Aotearoa.
