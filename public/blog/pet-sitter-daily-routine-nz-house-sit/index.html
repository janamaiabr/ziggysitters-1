What Does a Pet Sitter Do All Day? A Real Daily Routine for NZ House Sits

Published on 2 June 2026 | By ZiggySitters

If you have never used a pet sitter before, you might wonder what actually happens after you close the front door and head to the airport. Does the sitter just pop in for ten minutes and leave? Do they stay overnight? How does your pet\'s day look with a stranger running the household? The reality is that a professional house sit follows a structured routine designed to keep your pet healthy, stimulated, and calm. Here is an honest look at what a pet sitter does from sunrise to sunset during a typical New Zealand house sit.

Morning: The Wake-Up Routine (6:30 - 8:00 AM)

The day starts early. Most dogs in New Zealand are accustomed to a morning walk or toilet break, and delaying it leads to accidents and anxiety. A typical morning routine looks like this:

  • 6:30 AM: The sitter wakes and immediately lets the dog out for a toilet break. Cats get a fresh bowl of water and their morning feed.
  • 7:00 AM: Morning walk. In most NZ suburbs, this is a thirty to forty-five minute walk around the neighbourhood or a visit to the local dog park. The sitter follows the owner\'s preferred route to maintain consistency.
  • 7:45 AM: Breakfast. The sitter prepares the pet\'s meal according to the owner\'s instructions, including any supplements or medications. Fresh water bowls are washed and refilled.

For cats, the morning is quieter. The sitter tops up food, scoops the litter tray, and spends a few minutes offering play or gentle patting if the cat is receptive. Independent cats may simply want the sitter to exist quietly in the house while they go about their business.

Mid-Morning: Household Checks and Enrichment (8:30 - 11:00 AM)

After the initial rush, the sitter settles into the household. This period is important because it is when smaller tasks and pet enrichment happen:

  • Litter tray maintenance: For cat sits, the litter tray is checked, scooped, and cleaned as needed. A clean tray prevents cats from finding alternative spots around the house.
  • Medication administration: Some pets need morning pills, eye drops, or topical treatments. The sitter follows the exact schedule and technique the owner described.
  • Play sessions: Ten to fifteen minutes of interactive play for cats (wand toys, laser pointers) or indoor games for dogs (hide-and-seek with treats, puzzle feeders). This mental stimulation prevents boredom and associated behaviour problems.
  • House security check: The sitter checks that doors and windows are secure, brings in the mail, and generally maintains the property. This is a core part of house sitting, not just pet sitting.
  • Photo update: Most sitters send a photo or short update to the owner during the morning. On ZiggySitters, this is standard practice and owners appreciate the reassurance.

Midday: The Main Walk and Lunch (11:30 AM - 2:00 PM)

For dog sits, this is the main event of the day. New Zealand\'s temperate climate makes midday the ideal time for a longer outing:

  • Dog walk: A forty-five to sixty-minute walk, often at a local beach, regional park, or dog-friendly reserve. In Auckland, popular spots include Western Springs, Churchill Park, and the many off-leash beaches. In Wellington, sitters frequent the waterfront and Newtown Park. Christchurch sitters head to Hagley Park or the Avon River trails.
  • Lunchtime feed: Some pets get a second meal at midday. The sitter follows the owner\'s feeding schedule precisely.
  • Midday toilet break: Even if there was no formal walk, dogs get a garden break around this time. Cats get another litter tray check.

This is also when the sitter takes their own lunch break. A house sitter lives in your home, so they prepare their own meals in your kitchen, use your Wi-Fi, and generally make themselves at home while keeping your pet company.

Afternoon: Downtime and Observation (2:00 - 5:00 PM)

The afternoon is typically quieter. Many pets nap during this period, especially in warmer months. The sitter uses this time for:

  • Remote work: Many professional sitters work remotely while house sitting. Having someone present and working at the dining table means your pet has company throughout the day, which is one of the main advantages of house sitting over drop-in visits.
  • Observation: A good sitter watches for changes in behaviour, appetite, or energy levels. Is the dog drinking more water than usual? Has the cat visited the litter tray fewer times today? These observations matter because early detection of health issues can be life-saving.
  • Garden time: If the owner has requested light gardening, the sitter may water plants, pick up fallen branches, or tidy outdoor areas. This keeps the property looking lived-in and deters burglaries.
  • Afternoon play: A short play session or training practice keeps pets engaged. For dogs, this might be practicing sit, stay, or recall in the backyard. For cats, a quick wand toy session satisfies their hunting instincts.

Evening: Wind-Down Routine (5:00 - 8:00 PM)

As the day winds down, the sitter shifts into evening mode:

  • 5:00 PM: Evening walk for dogs, usually a shorter neighbourhood loop of twenty to thirty minutes. This helps the dog burn off remaining energy before settling for the night.
  • 6:00 PM: Dinner. The sitter prepares the evening meal according to the owner\'s instructions. If the pet is on a raw diet, this involves defrosting portions from the fridge and following food safety protocols.
  • 7:00 PM: Evening settle. The sitter dims lights, puts on quiet music or television, and creates a calm environment. Most pets instinctively settle when the household energy drops.
  • Evening update: A second photo or message to the owner summarising the day. Any concerns, funny moments, or notable behaviours get reported.

Overnight: Security and Supervision (8:00 PM - 6:30 AM)

One of the biggest advantages of a live-in house sitter is overnight presence. Your pet is never alone, and the house is never empty. This matters for several reasons:

  • Security: An occupied home is far less likely to be targeted by burglars. Lights going on and off at normal times, curtains opening and closing, and someone moving around the house are all deterrents.
  • Pet comfort: Many pets, especially dogs, sleep better when a human is nearby. A sitter sleeping in the house means your dog can settle on their bed knowing someone is present.
  • Emergency response: If a pet becomes ill overnight, the sitter is there to respond immediately rather than discovering the issue at the next drop-in visit twelve hours later.
  • Storm support: New Zealand\'s weather can change quickly. During thunderstorms or fireworks (common around Guy Fawkes), a sitter can comfort anxious pets immediately.

What Sitters Do Beyond Pet Care

A house sitter is also a house minder. The additional responsibilities typically include:

  • Collecting mail and parcels to prevent overflow that signals an empty home
  • Taking out and bringing in rubbish bins on collection day
  • Watering indoor and outdoor plants as requested
  • Managing pool or spa maintenance if applicable
  • Keeping the home clean and tidy, often leaving it cleaner than they found it
  • Handling any unexpected household issues: a burst pipe, a power outage, or a broken fence panel after a storm

How Routine Differs for Cats vs Dogs

Cat sits follow a different rhythm. Cats typically need two to three visits per day for feeding, litter cleaning, and social interaction. Some sitters do stay overnight for cat sits as well, which is ideal for cats that dislike being alone. The key differences:

  • Cats do not need structured walks, but they do need interactive play sessions
  • Litter tray hygiene is checked multiple times daily rather than once
  • Cats often prefer short, frequent interactions over extended periods of attention
  • Medication for cats (particularly pills) can be more challenging and may require specific techniques the owner must demonstrate

What a Good Sitter Communicates

Communication is the hallmark of a professional sitter. Expect the following:

  • Daily updates: At least one message per day with a photo and a brief summary of how the pet is doing
  • Immediate contact for concerns: If the pet stops eating, shows signs of illness, or there is a household emergency, the sitter contacts you straight away
  • Vet visits: A sitter will take your pet to the vet if needed and inform you before and after the visit
  • Honest reporting: A professional does not sugar-coat. If your pet had an accident, was anxious, or refused food, they tell you rather than painting a rosy picture
