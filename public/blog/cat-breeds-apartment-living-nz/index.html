Best Cat Breeds for Apartment Living in New Zealand: The Complete Guide

25 July 2026 — ZiggySitters

New Zealanders love cats — we have one of the highest cat ownership rates in the world. But not every home comes with a backyard, and not every cat is built for apartment life. If you are renting in central Auckland, Wellington, or Christchurch, or you have chosen apartment living by design, the breed you pick matters more than you think.

The right apartment cat is calm enough to handle close quarters, quiet enough not to annoy neighbours, and adaptable enough to live indoors full-time. The wrong breed will shred your furniture, howl at 3 AM, and pace the hallway like a caged tiger.

This guide covers the best cat breeds for NZ apartment living, what makes them suited to smaller spaces, and how to set up your apartment so your cat thrives — not just survives.

What Makes a Cat Breed Good for Apartments?

Before we get to the breeds, understand what matters. Apartment cats need:

  • Low to moderate energy: High-energy breeds (Bengal, Abyssinian, Siamese) need far more stimulation than most apartments can provide. They become destructive and vocal when bored.
  • Quiet temperament: Shared walls mean shared noise. A yappy cat is a fast track to neighbour complaints and body corporate warnings.
  • Adaptability: The cat should accept indoor-only life without constant stress. Some breeds are hardwired to roam and will never settle.
  • Affectionate but not clingy: You work. Your cat will be alone for stretches. The ideal breed is content with companionship when you are home and independent enough to entertain itself when you are not.
  • Compact to medium size: Large, active cats (Maine Coons, Norwegian Forest Cats) can manage in apartments, but they need significantly more vertical space and floor area than a British Shorthair or Scottish Fold.

The Top 8 Cat Breeds for NZ Apartment Living

1. Ragdoll

Why they work: Ragdolls are arguably the best apartment cat in existence. They are large but astonishingly docile — the name comes from their tendency to go limp when held. They are quiet, affectionate, and uninterested in scaling curtains or knocking things off shelves. A Ragdoll will follow you from room to room, greet you at the door, and spend most of the day in a sunny spot waiting for you to come home.

Energy level: Low to moderate. A daily 15-minute play session is plenty.

Noise: Very quiet. Soft, sweet voice.

Indoor adaptability: Excellent. Most Ragdoll breeders insist on indoor-only homes anyway.

NZ availability: Good — Ragdolls are one of the most popular breeds in NZ, with reputable breeders in most major cities.

Watch out for: Ragdolls are people-oriented and can become depressed if left alone for very long stretches. If you work long hours, consider a companion cat or a daily pet sitter visit.

2. British Shorthair

Why they work: The British Shorthair is the introvert of the cat world — and that is a compliment. They are calm, independent, and utterly unbothered by being alone. They do not demand attention, but they enjoy it when it comes. Their dense, plush coat makes them look like living teddy bears, and their broad faces give them a permanently thoughtful expression.

Energy level: Low. Happy to nap most of the day.

Noise: Very quiet. Rarely vocal.

Indoor adaptability: Excellent. They are naturally sedentary.

NZ availability: Good. Several established breeders across NZ, particularly in Auckland and Christchurch.

Watch out for: British Shorthairs are prone to obesity. In an apartment with limited exercise opportunities, portion control is essential. Invest in puzzle feeders and schedule short, active play sessions.

3. Scottish Fold

Why they work: Those signature folded ears are not just cute — the personality behind them is ideal for apartment living. Scottish Folds are sweet, quiet, and adaptable. They like being near their people but are not demanding. They are known for sitting in the "Buddha pose" — legs splayed, looking like a small, contemplative philosopher on your couch.

Energy level: Low to moderate.

Noise: Quiet. Soft chirps rather than loud meows.

Indoor adaptability: Very good. They enjoy routine and indoor life.

NZ availability: Moderate. Fewer breeders than Ragdolls or British Shorthairs, but available.

Watch out for: Scottish Folds can develop osteochondrodysplasia (a joint condition linked to the fold gene). Buy from a reputable breeder who tests for this. Do not buy from backyard breeders or online classifieds.

4. Persian

Why they work: Persians have been apartment cats for centuries — literally bred by royalty in palaces. They are low-energy, quiet, and content to be decorative. A Persian on a windowsill watching pigeons is the picture of apartment cat contentment.

Energy level: Very low. They are the couch potatoes of the cat world.

Noise: Very quiet. Minimal vocalisation.

Indoor adaptability: Excellent. They have no interest in the outdoors.

NZ availability: Good. Well-established breeders in NZ.

Watch out for: Grooming. Persians need daily brushing to prevent matting. They also have brachycephalic (flat-faced) airways, which can cause breathing issues and eye drainage. If you are not prepared for 10-15 minutes of grooming per day, choose a different breed.

5. Exotic Shorthair

Why they work: Think of the Exotic Shorthair as the low-maintenance Persian. Same sweet, calm personality, same flat face and round eyes, but with a short, dense coat that requires a fraction of the grooming. They are sometimes called the "lazy man's Persian," which is unfair to both the cat and the concept of laziness.

Energy level: Low to moderate.

Noise: Quiet.

Indoor adaptability: Excellent.

NZ availability: Moderate. Fewer breeders than Persians, but worth the search.

Watch out for: Same brachycephalic concerns as Persians. Choose breeders who prioritise health testing.

6. Burmese

Why they work: Burmese cats are social, playful, and dog-like in their attachment to their people. They are more active than Ragdolls or Persians, which makes them better for people who want an interactive companion rather than a decorative one. They are also one of the most affectionate breeds you will find — a Burmese will meet you at the door and follow you to the bathroom.

Energy level: Moderate. They need daily interactive play.

Noise: Moderate. They talk — softly, but persistently. Not yappy like a Siamese, but not silent either.

Indoor adaptability: Good, provided they get enough attention and play.

NZ availability: Excellent. Burmese are very popular in NZ with many established breeders.

Watch out for: Burmese do not do well alone for long periods. If you work full-time and are out most evenings, a Burmese will be miserable. Get two, or arrange daily pet sitter visits.

7. Russian Blue

Why they work: Russian Blues are the introverted intellectuals of the cat world. They are quiet, clean, and undemanding. They form deep bonds with one or two people and are reserved with strangers — which makes them less likely to bolt for the door when a guest arrives. Their short, dense coat is hypoallergenic for many people and requires almost no grooming.

Energy level: Moderate. They enjoy play but are not hyperactive.

Noise: Very quiet.

Indoor adaptability: Very good. They are naturally cautious and prefer familiar territory.

NZ availability: Moderate. Not as common as Ragdolls or British Shorthairs, but available from dedicated breeders.

Watch out for: Russian Blues can be shy with new people and new environments. Give them time to adjust when you move or introduce changes.

8. Domestic Shorthair (Moggie)

Why they work: Not a breed, but the most common cat in NZ by a mile — and often the best choice for apartments. Adopted moggies from SPCA or rescue organisations come with known temperaments, which is more useful than breed generalisations. A calm, adult moggie from a foster home can be the perfect apartment companion.

Energy level: Varies. Ask the foster carer.

Noise: Varies. Ask the foster carer.

Indoor adaptability: Varies, but many adult rescues have already lived indoors and adjust easily.

NZ availability: Abundant. SPCA and rescue organisations across NZ always have cats waiting for homes.

Watch out for: The unknown factor. If adopting a kitten, you will not know their adult personality for 12-18 months. Adopting an adult cat from a foster home gives you a much better read on whether they are suited to apartment life.

Breeds to Avoid in Apartments

Some breeds are a poor fit for apartment life, regardless of how much you love them:

  • Bengal: High energy, loud, destructive when bored. Built for large homes with outdoor access.
  • Savannah: Even more intense than a Bengal. Needs space to run, climb, and hunt.
  • Siamese: Extremely vocal. Your neighbours will know you have a cat. Also high-energy and demanding.
  • Abyssinian: Constantly active, curious, and mischievous. Will find every possible way to get into trouble in a small space.
  • Oriental Shorthair: Like a Siamese in a different coat — just as loud, just as active.

If you are dead set on one of these breeds, you can make it work — but you need to invest heavily in enrichment: tall cat trees, daily interactive play, window perches, and possibly a catio. It is not impossible, but it is significantly more work.

Setting Up Your Apartment for an Indoor Cat

Breed matters, but environment matters more. Here is how to make your apartment a great cat home:

Vertical Space Is Non-Negotiable

Cats live in 3D. In an apartment with limited floor space, going vertical is how you multiply your cat's territory without moving house. Install:

  • Cat shelves on walls — stagger them so your cat can jump between levels
  • A tall cat tree (at least 150 cm) in a corner with a good view
  • Window perches — suction-mounted or shelf-style, giving your cat a view of the outside world
  • Clear tops of bookshelves and wardrobes so your cat can use them as observation posts

The rule of thumb: your cat should be able to get from floor to the highest point in your apartment without touching the ground.

Environmental Enrichment

Boredom is the enemy of apartment cats. Rotate toys every few days. Use puzzle feeders instead of bowls. Grow cat grass near windows. Play with your cat for 15 minutes before you leave for work and 15 minutes when you get home. A wand toy and a laser pointer cover most of the exercise needs for low-to-moderate energy breeds.

The Litter Box Situation

In an apartment, the litter box is in your living space. The rule is one box per cat plus one extra. For a single cat in a one-bedroom apartment, that means two boxes. Use covered boxes or furniture-style enclosures to manage odour and aesthetics. Scoop daily — apartment air circulation is limited, and you will smell a neglected box within hours.

Safe Outdoor Access

Indoor-only cats live longer, healthier lives, and many NZ councils near conservation areas require cats to be contained. But indoor cats still benefit from outdoor sensory input:

  • Catios — enclosed outdoor spaces attached to windows or balconies. Even a small one transforms your cat's quality of life.
  • Harness training — some cats take to harness walks. Start slowly, indoors first, then try a quiet courtyard.
  • Window screens — secure screens let you open windows for fresh air without escape risk.

NZ-Specific Considerations

Body Corporate and Rental Rules

Many NZ apartments are rented, and many body corporates restrict pet ownership. Before getting a cat:

  • Check your tenancy agreement for pet clauses
  • Ask the body corporate about pet policies — some allow cats with approval
  • If renting, get written permission from your landlord. A well-behaved indoor cat with a reference from a previous landlord is hard to refuse

Cat Containment Bylaws

Several NZ councils are moving toward cat containment requirements, particularly near conservation areas. Wellington, for example, has discussed cat curfews. If you are in an apartment near a reserve or coastal area, keeping your cat indoors may not just be good practice — it may be the law. This actually makes apartment living simpler, since your cat is already contained.

Earthquake Safety

New Zealand is earthquake country. Secure your cat tree, shelves, and any tall furniture to walls. Keep a cat carrier accessible — not buried in storage. In an earthquake, a panicked cat will hide under furniture or in walls. Know your cat's likely hiding spots and have a plan for getting them out quickly.

Final Thoughts

Apartment living with a cat in New Zealand is not just possible — for the right breed, it is ideal. Indoor cats live longer, stay healthier, and avoid the car strikes, dog attacks, and wildlife predation that outdoor cats face. The key is choosing a breed whose temperament matches your lifestyle, setting up your apartment with enough vertical space and enrichment, and arranging care for when you are away.

The best apartment cat is not the cutest one on the internet. It is the one whose energy, noise level, and companionship needs match your daily routine. Get that right, and you will have a happy neighbour, a happy cat, and a home that feels complete.
