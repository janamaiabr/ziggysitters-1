German Shepherd Care Guide NZ 2026

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  March 6, 2026  •  12 min read

German Shepherds are one of New Zealand’s most popular dog breeds — and for good reason. Loyal, intelligent, and deeply bonded to their families, a well-cared-for GSD is an extraordinary companion. But they are also a high-commitment breed. This guide covers everything NZ owners need to know, from daily exercise needs to finding the right pet sitter when you’re away.

The German Shepherd in New Zealand

German Shepherds consistently rank among the top five dog breeds registered with the New Zealand Kennel Club. You’ll find them in cities like Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch, as well as on rural properties where their natural working instincts can shine. Their versatility makes them popular as family pets, farm dogs, and working dogs for New Zealand Police and Search & Rescue.

Despite their adaptability, GSDs have specific needs that owners must understand before bringing one home. Underestimating their exercise, mental stimulation, or social needs is the number one reason German Shepherds develop problem behaviours.

Exercise: How Much Does a GSD Need in NZ?

German Shepherds need a minimum of 1.5 to 2 hours of exercise per day for adult dogs. This is non-negotiable. A bored or under-exercised GSD will find their own entertainment — usually in the form of destroyed furniture, excessive barking, or fence-pacing.

Great Exercise Options for NZ German Shepherd Owners

  • Off-lead parks: Many NZ councils have designated off-lead areas. Auckland’s Cornwall Park, Wellington’s Otari-Wilton’s Bush tracks, and Christchurch’s Victoria Park are popular GSD walks.
  • Hiking: GSDs thrive on NZ’s trails. The Waitakere Ranges (Auckland), Port Hills (Christchurch), and Remutaka Forest Park (Wellington region) are excellent options.
  • Swimming: Many GSDs enjoy water. NZ’s coastline and rivers offer great opportunities, though always check for blue-green algae warnings before letting your dog swim in lakes or slow-moving rivers.
  • Fetch and structured play: Ball launchers and Frisbee sessions in a secure backyard supplement daily walks effectively.
  • Dog sports: Obedience trials, agility, and tracking are available through New Zealand German Shepherd Dog League clubs nationwide — and GSDs absolutely excel in these environments.
💡 NZ Tip: During New Zealand’s hot summer months (December–February), exercise your GSD in the early morning or evening to avoid heat stress. Their thick double coat means they feel the heat more than you do. Always carry water on walks.

Mental Stimulation: The Overlooked Need

Physical exercise alone is not enough for German Shepherds. These dogs were bred to work alongside humans, solving problems and following complex commands. Without mental stimulation, even a physically tired GSD can become anxious or destructive.

Mental Enrichment Ideas

  • Puzzle feeders: Replace the regular food bowl with a Kong, snuffle mat, or Licki Mat. Available at most NZ pet stores and online from Pet Direct NZ.
  • Training sessions: Even 10–15 minutes of obedience training per day provides significant mental exercise. Teach new tricks, work on scent games, or practice heel work.
  • Scent work: Hide kibble or treats around the garden and let your GSD hunt for them. This engages their powerful nose and tires them out quickly.
  • Chew time: Raw meaty bones (sourced from your local NZ butcher or pet store) provide both mental engagement and dental benefits.

Feeding Your German Shepherd in NZ

Adult German Shepherds typically eat 2–3 cups of high-quality dry kibble per day, split into two meals. The exact amount depends on your dog’s size, age, and activity level. Overfeeding is a common problem — GSDs are prone to obesity as they age, which puts strain on their joints.

NZ Feeding Options

  • Premium dry kibble: Brands like Advance, Royal Canin, and Eukanuba are widely available in NZ. Look for formulas with chicken, lamb, or beef listed as the first ingredient.
  • Raw feeding (BARF diet): Many NZ GSD owners feed raw. Suppliers like Raw Essentials (Auckland and Wellington) and Paws Raw Pet Food deliver nationwide.
  • Wet food supplementation: Adding wet food to kibble can increase palatability and hydration.
⚠️ Bloat Warning: German Shepherds are at elevated risk for gastric dilatation-volvulus (GDV), commonly known as bloat. Never exercise your GSD immediately before or after meals. Feed from a raised bowl only if recommended by your vet — research on elevated bowls and bloat risk is mixed. Know the signs: distended abdomen, unproductive retching, and restlessness are emergencies requiring immediate veterinary care.

Grooming Your GSD in NZ

German Shepherds have a dense double coat that sheds year-round, with two major shedding seasons (known as “coat blows”) typically in spring and autumn. Regular grooming keeps your home manageable and your dog comfortable.

Grooming Routine

  • Daily brushing during shedding seasons: Use an undercoat rake or de-shedding tool like the FURminator. During coat blow, you may fill multiple grocery bags with fur every session.
  • Weekly brushing outside shedding season: A slicker brush and metal comb are sufficient maintenance tools.
  • Bathing: Every 6–8 weeks, or when genuinely dirty. Over-bathing strips natural oils and can cause skin irritation.
  • Nail trimming: Monthly, or when you can hear nails clicking on hard floors.
  • Ear checks: Weekly inspection for redness, odour, or discharge. GSDs are not particularly prone to ear infections, but regular checks catch issues early.
  • Teeth: Daily brushing with dog-safe toothpaste is ideal, or at minimum a weekly brush combined with dental chews.

Health: Common Issues in NZ German Shepherds

Responsible ownership means knowing what health issues your GSD may face. Work with a NZ vet experienced with large breeds — the New Zealand Veterinary Association directory can help you find a good clinic in your area.

Hip and Elbow Dysplasia

Hip dysplasia is the most significant health concern in German Shepherds. The condition involves abnormal development of the hip joint, leading to arthritis and pain. Reputable NZ breeders will provide hip and elbow scores from the New Zealand Veterinary Association scoring scheme. Ask to see the scores of both parents before purchasing a puppy.

Signs to watch for as your dog ages: reluctance to exercise, difficulty rising from rest, bunny-hopping gait, or obvious lameness. Maintain a healthy weight, avoid high-impact exercise in puppies, and consider joint supplements like fish oil from early adulthood.

Degenerative Myelopathy

This progressive neurological disease affects the spinal cord and is more common in German Shepherds than most other breeds. It typically appears in dogs aged 7 and older and causes gradual hindlimb weakness. Genetic testing is available; ask your breeder whether their breeding dogs have been tested.

Skin Issues and Allergies

GSDs can be prone to hot spots, flea allergy dermatitis, and environmental allergies. NZ’s grass pollen and native plants can trigger reactions in sensitive dogs. Year-round flea prevention is essential — use a vet-recommended product rather than supermarket brands.

Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI)

German Shepherds have a higher-than-average rate of EPI, a condition where the pancreas fails to produce sufficient digestive enzymes. Signs include chronic diarrhoea, weight loss despite a large appetite, and pale, greasy stools. EPI is manageable with enzyme supplementation added to every meal.

💡 Vet Tip: Register your GSD with a vet clinic shortly after bringing them home, even before any health issue arises. Good NZ vet practices fill up quickly, and established patients tend to get faster appointments in emergencies.

Training and Socialisation

German Shepherds are among the most trainable dogs in the world — ranked third in Stanley Coren’s intelligence rankings — but that intelligence works against you if training is neglected. A poorly socialised or untrained GSD can become reactive, fearful, or difficult to manage given their size and strength.

Puppy Socialisation

The critical socialisation window closes at around 14 weeks. During this period, expose your GSD puppy to:

  • Different people (children, elderly, people in hats or uniforms)
  • Other vaccinated dogs of different sizes
  • Common NZ sounds (lawn mowers, traffic, sirens, skateboards)
  • Different surfaces (grass, gravel, wooden decks, wet surfaces)
  • Car travel
  • Vets, groomers, and boarding kennels in positive contexts

Puppy classes are available across NZ through the SPCA, Dog Training Clubs, and private trainers. Look for force-free trainers who use positive reinforcement methods.

Ongoing Training

GSDs thrive with consistent, structured training throughout their lives. Once your dog has mastered basic obedience, consider joining a local dog sport club. The New Zealand German Shepherd Dog League has clubs in Auckland, Wellington, Christchurch, Hamilton, and other centres, offering obedience, tracking, and herding activities.

Finding a Pet Sitter for Your German Shepherd in NZ

When you need to travel or work long hours, your GSD needs more than a basic dog-walking service. German Shepherds are a breed-specific challenge for pet sitters — their size, intelligence, and loyalty to their owners means not just anyone can step in and manage them confidently.

What to Look for in a GSD-Experienced Sitter

  • Large dog experience: Ask specifically whether the sitter has cared for German Shepherds or similar large working breeds (Malinois, Rottweilers, Huskies).
  • Confidence and calm energy: GSDs read human energy. A nervous sitter will create a nervous dog. Look for a sitter who is experienced, calm, and direct.
  • Understanding of routine: Your GSD will be significantly happier if their feeding schedule, exercise routine, and sleeping arrangements are maintained as closely as possible.
  • Secure property: If booking in-home boarding, confirm the property has fully fenced, secure yards. GSDs are escape artists when bored or motivated.
  • Willingness to follow your commands: Share the obedience cues your dog knows. A good sitter will practice these consistently, not let your dog’s training slide.

The Meet-and-Greet is Non-Negotiable

Always arrange a meet-and-greet before booking a pet sitter for your German Shepherd. Watch how your dog responds — relaxed body language, willing engagement, and no excessive stress within the first 10 minutes are positive signs. A dog who remains rigid or highly vigilant after 20 minutes may need more time, or may not be the right fit with that particular sitter.

💡 Pet Care Sheet: Prepare a detailed care sheet for your sitter covering feeding times and amounts, exercise requirements, known triggers or sensitivities, emergency vet contact, medical conditions and medications, and household rules your dog follows (e.g., no jumping on furniture). Final Thoughts

German Shepherds are remarkable companions for the right owner. They reward consistency, patience, and genuine engagement with total loyalty and a level of intelligence that continues to impress even experienced dog people. In New Zealand’s outdoor lifestyle, they have everything they need to thrive — space, trails, and a culture of active dog ownership.

The key to a well-adjusted GSD is simple: meet their needs every day. Exercise, mental stimulation, training, and connection. Do that, and a German Shepherd will be the best dog you’ve ever owned.
