Pet Sitting Contracts & Legal Protection in New Zealand

Published 27 June 2026 · 10 min read · ZiggySitters NZ

You would not let a stranger move into your house without a written agreement. Yet thousands of NZ pet owners hand over their keys, their pets, and their trust to sitters with nothing more than a verbal “she&rsquo;ll be right.” A pet sitting contract protects both sides: the owner knows exactly what care their pet will receive, and the sitter knows exactly what is expected of them. This guide covers what to include, what NZ law says, and how to protect yourself whether you are hiring a sitter or offering your services.

Why You Need a Written Agreement

In New Zealand, a pet sitting arrangement is a service contract governed by the Contractual Remedies Act 1979 and general contract law. Verbal agreements are legally binding in NZ, but they are almost impossible to enforce when things go wrong. Here is what can happen without a written contract:

  • Disagreements over scope. The sitter thought “feed the cat twice a day” meant fill the bowl. You meant a specific measured portion at 7am and 6pm.
  • Property damage with no recourse. A sitter&rsquo;s friend spills red wine on your couch. Without a written agreement about guests, insurance may not cover it.
  • Vet bills with no clarity on who pays. Your dog eats a sock. The sitter takes it to the vet. The bill is $1,200. Who covers it?
  • Early termination. The sitter cancels the day before your flight. You have no written cancellation terms and no deposit to offset the emergency replacement cost.

A contract does not mean you do not trust the person. It means you both care enough about the arrangement to make it clear.

What a Pet Sitting Contract Must Include

Whether you use a template or write your own, these are the non-negotiable elements for any NZ pet sitting agreement:

1. Parties and Contact Details

  • Full legal names of both the pet owner and the sitter
  • Phone numbers, email addresses, and physical addresses
  • Emergency contacts for both parties (someone else who can make decisions if you are unreachable)

2. Pet Information

  • Pet name, breed, age, sex, and desexing status
  • Microchip number and registration details
  • Council registration number (required for dogs in NZ under the Dog Control Act 1996)
  • Vet clinic name, phone number, and address
  • Existing medical conditions, medications, and dosages
  • Dietary requirements and feeding schedule
  • Behavioural quirks: anxiety triggers, aggression history, escape tendencies

3. Scope of Services

Be specific. “Look after my dog” is not a scope. Write down exactly what the sitter will do each day:

  • Feeding (what food, how much, what times)
  • Walking (duration, route preferences, lead rules)
  • Medication administration (which meds, how, when)
  • Grooming (brushing, bathing, nail trims)
  • Litter tray or yard cleaning schedule
  • Play and enrichment activities
  • Plant watering, mail collection, bin day

4. Duration and Schedule

  • Exact start and end dates and times
  • Whether the sitter stays overnight or visits (and if visiting, how many visits per day and their approximate timing)
  • What happens if your return is delayed (a backup contact, extended stay terms, additional pay)

5. Financial Terms

ZiggySitters Tip: Always include a cap on vet expenses the sitter can authorise without contacting you. A common arrangement is: sitter can approve up to $500 for urgent treatment, with phone authorisation required for anything above. Put the cap in writing. This protects both the sitter (who should not have to pay out of pocket) and the owner (who knows the sitter will not spend thousands without checking).

6. Veterinary Care Authorisation

This is the section most people skip and the one that matters most in an emergency. Include:

  • Authorisation for the sitter to seek emergency veterinary care
  • The spending cap (see above)
  • Your preferred vet clinic and after-hours emergency clinic
  • A written statement: “I authorise [sitter name] to make emergency veterinary decisions on my behalf for [pet name] between [dates].”
  • A signed credit card authorisation form or pre-loaded account at your vet (many NZ vet clinics allow this)

7. House Rules and Property Access

  • Which areas of the house the sitter may use
  • Whether guests are allowed (and if so, how many and under what conditions)
  • Alarm codes and key location
  • Wi-Fi password
  • Appliance instructions (heat pump, oven, washing machine)
  • Neighbour contact details in case of urgent building issues
  • Rubbish and recycling days
Important: If you are renting, check your lease before allowing a house sitter. Some NZ tenancy agreements restrict who can occupy the property. Under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986, you generally need your landlord&rsquo;s consent to sublet, and a long-term house sitter may qualify as subletting. Short pet visits (drop-ins, dog walking) do not usually require consent, but overnight stays might.

New Zealand Law: What Applies

Dog Control Act 1996

If you are sitting a dog in NZ, you must know this law. Key points:

  • All dogs must be registered with the local council and wear a registration tag.
  • Dogs must be under “continuous control” at all times in public. This means on a lead unless in a designated off-leash area.
  • If a dog in your care rushes at, attacks, or injures a person or animal, the owner can face a $3,000 fine, and the dog can be classified as “menacing” or “dangerous.” As the sitter, you could share legal liability.
  • Barking complaints go to the council. If the dog you are sitting is barking excessively, you are responsible for managing it.

Animal Welfare Act 1999

Both owners and sitters have obligations under this act:

  • All animals must have access to adequate food, water, and shelter.
  • Failure to provide proper care is an offence carrying fines up to $50,000 for individuals or $250,000 for organisations.
  • A pet sitter is effectively the “person in charge” of the animal during the sitting period and carries legal responsibility for its welfare.

Privacy Act 2020

If you collect personal information about pet owners (addresses, alarm codes, medical details), you must handle it in accordance with the Privacy Act. This means:

  • Only collect what you need for the sitting arrangement
  • Store it securely (not in a shared Google Doc or text message chain)
  • Destroy it after the engagement ends, unless the owner requests otherwise

Red Flags in a Pet Sitting Arrangement

Whether you are an owner or a sitter, watch for these warning signs:

Owner Red Flags

  • Refuses to put anything in writing
  • Downplays pet aggression or anxiety
  • Will not share vet details or medical history
  • Asks you to break house rules (e.g., “just let the dog off the lead, he&rsquo;s fine”)
  • Expects 24/7 availability with no boundaries

Sitter Red Flags

  • No references or reviews
  • Unwilling to do a meet-and-greet first
  • Vague about schedule or availability
  • Asks for full payment before the job starts (a deposit is normal; full payment upfront is not)
  • Cannot provide proof of insurance or police check

What Happens When Things Go Wrong

Property Damage

If a sitter damages your property, your home and contents insurance is usually the first port of call. However, if the damage is deliberate or reckless, you can pursue the sitter directly through the Disputes Tribunal (for claims under $30,000) or the District Court. The Disputes Tribunal is faster and cheaper — the filing fee is $45 to $180 depending on the claim amount.

Pet Injury or Death

This is emotionally devastating and legally complex. In NZ, pets are legally property, which means compensation is based on the animal&rsquo;s market value (or vet bills), not emotional suffering. If a sitter&rsquo;s negligence caused the injury or death:

  • Document everything immediately (photos, vet reports, communications)
  • File a claim with the sitter&rsquo;s insurance if they have CCC coverage
  • If uninsured, the Disputes Tribunal can award up to $30,000
  • The pet insurance policy (if the pet is insured) should cover vet bills regardless of fault

Sitter No-Shows

If a sitter cancels at the last minute or does not show up, your contract should specify:

  • How much notice is required for cancellation (usually 48 to 72 hours)
  • What percentage of the deposit is refundable
  • Whether the sitter is responsible for helping find a replacement

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I really need a contract for a friend who is watching my pet?

For a single evening with a trusted friend who lives nearby, a detailed contract may be overkill — but at minimum, write down your vet details, feeding instructions, and emergency contacts, and share them in a text message so there is a written record. For anything longer than one night, a written agreement protects the friendship as much as the arrangement.

Can I write my own contract or do I need a lawyer?

You do not need a lawyer for a standard pet sitting agreement. A clear, written document covering the items listed above is enforceable in NZ. The key is being specific — vague terms cause disputes. If the arrangement is high-value (e.g., sitting a property worth millions or caring for a very valuable animal), get legal advice.

What if my pet injures someone while with a sitter?

Under the Dog Control Act 1996, the dog&rsquo;s owner is primarily liable for damage caused by their dog. However, if the sitter was negligent (e.g., let the dog off the lead in an on-leash area), they can share liability. Your pet insurance or home contents policy may cover this. Check with your insurer before the sitting period begins.

Are unpaid sitters covered differently?

Yes. Under NZ law, unpaid (volunteer) sitters are not employees, which means ACC does not cover them if they are injured at your home. If a volunteer sitter trips on your stairs, your home liability insurance is their recourse. Always check that your policy covers visitors and volunteers.

What if the sitter wants to bring their own pet?

Address this in the contract. Default answer: no, unless both parties agree in writing. If the sitter&rsquo;s pet and your pet have not met, a meet-and-greet is essential. Make sure the sitter&rsquo;s pet is also vaccinated, registered (if a dog), and flea-treated.
