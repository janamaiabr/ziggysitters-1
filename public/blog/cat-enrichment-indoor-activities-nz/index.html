Why Enrichment Matters for Indoor Cats in NZ

Cats are natural hunters. In the wild, a cat might travel several kilometres per day, stalk prey, use complex problem-solving to make a kill, and interact with a complex social territory. An indoor cat in a Wellington apartment has none of that stimulation by default.

The result? Boredom-driven behaviours that many owners mistakenly attribute to personality: excessive grooming leading to bald patches, repetitive vocalisation, furniture destruction, aggression toward other pets, and over-eating. The underlying cause in most cases is a lack of appropriate mental and physical stimulation.

Research in animal behaviour consistently shows that enriched environments reduce stress hormones, improve immune function, and extend healthy lifespan in cats. Enrichment is not a luxury. For indoor cats, it is welfare.

NZ Context: New Zealand’s Department of Conservation (DOC) and many local councils strongly encourage or require cats to be kept indoors overnight or full-time, particularly in ecologically sensitive areas. Palmerston North, Wellington, and parts of Christchurch have introduced cat management bylaws. Indoor living is increasingly the norm — enrichment makes it work.

The Five Pillars of Cat Enrichment

Animal welfare scientists have identified five core categories of enrichment for domestic cats. A genuinely enriched environment addresses all five.

1. Physical Environment

Cats are vertical animals. They feel safest when they can observe their environment from height and have multiple escape routes if something startles them. A flat, ground-level home does not meet these needs.

  • Cat trees and shelves: Floor-to-ceiling cat trees provide climbing, scratching, and elevated resting spots in a single unit. Wall-mounted shelving (available from pet stores and hardware shops across NZ) creates a “cat highway” that uses vertical space your furniture does not.
  • Hiding spots: Covered beds, cardboard boxes, and cat tunnels give cats a place to retreat when they feel overstimulated. Every cat should have at least one hiding spot per room they frequently use.
  • Window access: A window perch is one of the highest-value enrichment items you can provide. “Cat TV” — watching birds, insects, and foot traffic — is genuinely engaging for most cats. A bird feeder positioned outside a regular window dramatically increases its value.
  • Scratching surfaces: Both vertical and horizontal options in different textures (sisal, cardboard, carpet). Scratching is a normal cat behaviour that serves to stretch muscles, maintain claws, and deposit scent. Providing appropriate surfaces prevents furniture damage.

2. Sensory Stimulation

Beyond what they see, cats engage with their world through smell, sound, and texture. Sensory variety keeps the brain active and prevents the monotony of identical daily experience.

  • Catnip and silver vine: About 50–70% of cats respond to catnip (the remainder lack the gene). Silver vine (Actinidia polygama) works on a higher proportion of cats and is growing in availability in NZ pet stores. Responses typically last 10–15 minutes before the cat becomes temporarily desensitised.
  • Novel scents: A small amount of dried valerian root, honeysuckle wood, or even a worn item of clothing from a family member creates olfactory novelty. Rotate scents rather than leaving them out permanently.
  • Auditory enrichment: Many cats respond to nature sounds (bird calls, running water) or dedicated “cat music” — compositions with frequencies tuned to feline hearing ranges. YouTube and Spotify have playlists specifically designed for cats.
  • Texture variety: Different bedding materials, a tray of dry grass or wheat grass to walk on, and varied toys with different feels (crinkle, fuzzy, smooth) all contribute to tactile enrichment.
NZ Plant Note: Grow a small pot of cat grass (wheat or oat grass) or catnip on a sunny windowsill. It is inexpensive, easy to grow in NZ’s climate, and provides both sensory enrichment and a safe plant for cats to nibble. Keep it away from NZ native plants, which may not be safe for cats to ingest.

3. Feeding Enrichment

Wild cats spend roughly 40% of their waking hours hunting. Placing food in a bowl takes about 30 seconds. The difference between these two experiences is a significant contributor to indoor cat boredom.

Introduce puzzle feeders gradually. Cats that have only ever eaten from a bowl may be frustrated by puzzles at first. Start with the easiest option and work up. The goal is to give them a satisfying challenge, not a frustrating one.

4. Play and Predatory Behaviour

Interactive play — with a human, not just a toy left on the floor — is irreplaceable. It mimics the predatory sequence (stalk, chase, pounce, catch, kill, consume) that cats are hardwired to engage in.

How to Play Effectively

  • Use wand toys: Feather wands, ribbon toys, and fishing-rod style toys that you control provide the unpredictable movement cats find irresistible. Toys moved too regularly or left in the same place become “dead prey” that cats lose interest in quickly.
  • Mimic prey movement: Move toys under furniture, behind cushions, and in slow, then fast, then slow-again patterns. Real prey animals do not move in straight lines at constant speed.
  • Always end with a “catch”: Let your cat catch the toy at the end of each session and give them a small treat or meal immediately after. This completes the predatory sequence and prevents the frustration of a hunt that never ends.
  • Two short sessions over one long one: Two 10-minute play sessions (morning and evening) are more effective than one 20-minute session. Evening play before bed can also reduce early-morning wake-up behaviour.

5. Social Enrichment

Cats are more social than their reputation suggests. Many cats actively enjoy companionship — both with humans and with carefully chosen animal companions.

  • Quality human interaction: Regular grooming sessions, lap time, and “conversation” (yes, talking to your cat genuinely engages them) all count as social enrichment.
  • A second cat: For owners who are away from home long hours, a compatible feline companion can significantly reduce boredom. Introductions should be gradual and careful — especially for adult cats with established territory.
  • Cat TV and live streams: Videos of birds, fish, and squirrels are widely available on YouTube and are specifically designed to engage feline attention. Many cats watch with genuine interest for extended periods.

Enrichment for Different Life Stages

Kittens (Under 12 Months)

Kittens need more play than adult cats — three to five short play sessions per day. This is also the critical socialisation period: exposure to different textures, sounds, people, and handling now shapes how adaptable they will be for life. Do not skimp on variety during kittenhood.

Adult Cats (1–10 Years)

Two interactive play sessions daily plus environmental enrichment. Rotate toys every few days to prevent habituation. If your cat is a solo indoor cat in a small home, consider investing in a “catio” — an enclosed outdoor enclosure that provides safe access to fresh air, sun, and natural sounds while keeping them contained.

Senior Cats (10+ Years)

Senior cats may have less energy for active play but often benefit enormously from sensory enrichment (scents, bird-watching, gentle music) and social interaction. Watch for signs of cognitive decline — disorientation, vocalising at night, changes in litter habits — and discuss management strategies with your vet. Puzzle feeders should be easier to prevent frustration.

DIY Enrichment on a Budget

Cat enrichment does not require expensive equipment. Some of the most effective enrichment tools cost nothing:

  • Paper bags: Open a paper grocery bag on the floor. Most cats cannot resist the crinkle, the darkness, and the exploration opportunity.
  • Cardboard boxes: Stack them, cut holes in them, connect them into tunnels. Boxes are endlessly fascinating to cats.
  • Crumpled paper balls: Thrown down a hallway, these satisfy the chase instinct instantly.
  • Toilet rolls with kibble: Fold the ends to create a foraging toy that dispenses treats as it is batted around.
  • Sock filled with dried herbs: Lavender (safe for cats in small amounts), catnip, or silver vine in an old sock creates a reusable scent toy.
  • Cardboard egg carton: Place kibble pieces in the cups and fold it closed. Your cat will figure out how to open it.
NZ-Specific Idea: Many New Zealand pet owners find that placing a bird feeder just outside a cat-safe window creates hours of “TV time” for indoor cats. Native species like wax-eyes and sparrows are common in suburban gardens. This gives your cat natural, moving stimulation without any risk to the birds — a perfect compromise for wildlife-conscious NZ cat owners.

Briefing Your Pet Sitter on Enrichment

When you leave your cat with a ZiggySitters pet sitter, the quality of care depends partly on how well you communicate your cat’s enrichment routine. An experienced cat sitter will ask — but it helps to be prepared.

What to Include in Your Sitter Brief

  • Play toys and preferences: Which toys does your cat respond to? Do they prefer wand play, chase toys, or independent puzzle feeders? How many sessions per day?
  • Feeding enrichment: Do you use a puzzle feeder? Which one? What is the difficulty level your cat is used to?
  • Off-limits items: Are there any plants, essential oils, or cleaning products in the home the sitter should be aware of? Some common NZ household items are toxic to cats.
  • Favourite hiding spots: Where does your cat retreat when stressed? A sitter who panics when the cat disappears for four hours will cause unnecessary disruption.
  • Signs of stress vs. normal behaviour: Does your cat normally hide, or is hiding a sign something is wrong? Is vocalisation normal for your cat or cause for concern?
ZiggySitters Tip: The best pet sitters approach cat care with patience, not performance. Cats do not need to be constantly entertained — they need available enrichment they can choose to engage with, plus a calm, predictable human presence. Brief your sitter not to force interaction, but to offer it consistently and let the cat set the pace.

Signs Your Cat Needs More Enrichment

How do you know if your current setup is not enough? Watch for these indicators:

  • Over-grooming or pulling out fur (often on the belly or inner legs)
  • Sleeping significantly more than usual (over 18 hours per day)
  • Aggressive play — attacking your feet, hands, or other pets without clear provocation
  • Redirected aggression — lashing out when they cannot reach something they want to chase (e.g., a bird outside)
  • Excessive vocalisation, particularly at night
  • Obsessive, repetitive behaviours such as pacing or staring at walls
  • Inappropriate elimination outside the litter box (always rule out medical causes first)

If you notice several of these, consult your vet to rule out medical causes first, then review your enrichment strategy. A veterinary behaviourist referral is sometimes warranted for severe cases.

Final Thoughts

Keeping a cat indoors full-time in New Zealand is increasingly responsible ownership. But it comes with a genuine obligation: to provide the stimulation and variety that outdoor access would otherwise supply. The good news is that this does not require hours of effort or significant expense. A well-designed environment, two daily play sessions, and a thoughtful feeding strategy can transform an anxious, bored indoor cat into a relaxed, engaged companion.

And when you need to travel? Briefing your pet sitter on your cat’s enrichment needs — not just their feeding schedule — is one of the most meaningful things you can do for their wellbeing while you are away.
