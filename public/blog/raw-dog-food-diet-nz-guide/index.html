What Is Raw Feeding?

Raw dog food diet — often called BARF (Biologically Appropriate Raw Food) or RMB (Raw Meaty Bones) — involves feeding dogs uncooked ingredients that mirror what their wild ancestors consumed. The core philosophy: dogs are facultative carnivores whose digestive systems evolved to process raw meat, bone, and organ tissue, not processed grains and rendered proteins.

Modern raw feeding typically includes muscle meat, bone (raw meaty bones), organ meats (liver, kidney, spleen), small amounts of vegetables and fruit, and sometimes dairy. The exact ratios and ingredients vary based on which model you follow.

The Two Main Raw Feeding Models

BARF Diet (80/10/5/5 Ratio)

The BARF model, popularised by Australian veterinarian Ian Billinghurst, recommends approximately:

  • 80% muscle meat (including heart, which is technically a muscle)
  • 10% bone (edible raw bone, not weight-bearing bones from large animals)
  • 5% liver (essential for vitamin A and copper)
  • 5% other organ meat (kidney, spleen, brain, etc.)

BARF also incorporates plant matter — roughly 10-20% of the diet — believing this reflects the gut contents of prey animals that wild dogs would consume. Commercial BARF products in NZ typically follow these ratios closely.

Prey Model (80/10 Ratio)

The prey model simplifies to 80% muscle meat and 10% bone, with no plant matter. Advocates argue dogs lack the dental and gut adaptations to properly digest carbohydrates and that plant matter should only come from the stomach contents of prey animals — which dogs would eat whole in the wild.

The prey model\'s simplicity appeals to many NZ owners, though critics argue achieving complete nutrition without organ variety requires careful supplementation.

Raw Feeding in NZ: Legal Framework

In New Zealand, raw meat for pet consumption is legal and widely available. However, there are regulations around sale and display. The Animal Products Act 1999 governs pet food safety, and MPI (Ministry for Primary Industries) has specific rules for commercial raw pet food manufacturers.

Key NZ regulations:

  • Commercial raw pet food must be stored and displayed separately from human food
  • Raw pet food labelled for pet consumption only cannot be sold as human food
  • Commercial manufacturers must have risk-based food safety plans

You can legally feed any raw meat to your dog that you purchase for yourself — the regulations primarily affect commercial pet food producers. Many NZ owners use human-grade meat from supermarkets, but this can become expensive at scale.

What to Feed: Complete Ingredient Guide

Muscle Meat (80% of diet)

Any lean muscle meat works well. Popular choices in NZ:

  • Beef — affordable, widely available, good for everyday feeding
  • Chicken — cost-effective but higher in fat; remove skin for leaner options
  • Lamb — readily available in NZ but more expensive
  • Pork — must be from trusted sources due to parasites; rarely recommended
  • Venison — excellent for dogs with poultry allergies; expensive
  • Kangaroo — very lean, novel protein, increasingly popular in NZ

Heart is classified as muscle meat and should make up a portion of your muscle meat allocation. Chicken heart, beef heart, and lamb heart are all excellent — heart is particularly rich in taurine, an amino acid important for cardiac health.

Raw Meaty Bones (10% of diet)

Bones provide calcium, phosphorus, and dental benefits that ground or processed diets cannot replicate. Appropriate raw bones for dogs include:

  • Chicken wings, necks, and frames
  • Lamb ribs and neck vertebrae
  • Beef ribs and brisket bones (from younger animals)
  • Rabbit whole carcasses
  • Duck necks and wings

Never Feed These Bones

Cooked bones of any kind are dangerous — cooking makes bones brittle and they can splinter, causing internal damage. Weight-bearing bones (leg bones from large animals) are too hard and can crack teeth. Always supervise bone consumption and remove bones once cleaned.

Organ Meats (10% total, half should be liver)

Organ meats are nutrient-dense and essential for a complete raw diet:

  • Liver — critical vitamin A source; feed in moderation (5% max)
  • Kidney — rich in B vitamins and selenium
  • Spleen — excellent iron source
  • Brain — high in omega-3 fatty acids
  • Tripe — green tripe provides probiotics and nutrients

Safety Protocols: Preventing Bacterial Contamination

Raw meat carries inherent risks of bacterial contamination — Salmonella, E. coli, and Campylobacter can be present in any raw animal product. NZMPI notes that raw pet food carries higher risk than cooked alternatives, and proper handling is essential.

Safe Handling Practices

  • Store raw food at or below -12°C in the freezer; thaw in the refrigerator, never at room temperature
  • Use dedicated containers and utensils for raw pet food — never the same as human food prep
  • Clean and sanitise all surfaces that contact raw meat with bleach solution (1:10 dilution)
  • Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water for at least 20 seconds after handling raw meat
  • Feed your dog in areas that can be easily cleaned; avoid feeding on carpets or furniture
  • Dispose of uneaten raw food within 30 minutes in summer, 2 hours in winter

Immunocompromised Household Members

If anyone in your household has a compromised immune system — young children, elderly, pregnant women, or those with medical conditions — raw feeding carries increased zoonotic risk. Consult your GP and veterinarian before continuing with raw diets in these situations.

What Do NZ Veterinarians Think?

The New Zealand Veterinary Association (NZVA) acknowledges that raw diets are popular but does not recommend them as a primary diet, citing nutritional deficiencies and safety concerns as primary issues. Most NZ veterinarians take a neutral stance — neither actively recommending raw nor outright condemning it — but will advise on ensuring complete nutrition if you choose to raw feed.

Common veterinary concerns include:

  • Nutritional imbalances — DIY raw diets frequently lack adequate calcium, phosphorus, vitamin E, or iodine without careful formulation
  • Bacterial risk — handling raw meat creates household contamination risks
  • Bone hazards — improper bone selection or size can cause obstructions, perforations, or dental fractures
  • Parasite exposure — certain raw fish (especially salmon) can carry parasites requiring freezing treatment

Proponents counter that commercial kibble carries its own risks (recalls, contamination, ingredient quality) and that wolves and wild dogs thrive on raw diets without veterinary supervision. The debate continues, and NZ dog owners should make informed decisions with full knowledge of both perspectives.

Transitioning from Kibble to Raw

If your dog has been on commercial kibble their entire life, a sudden switch to raw can cause digestive upset — vomiting, diarrhoea, or constipation are common during the transition period. Most raw feeders recommend a gradual transition over 7-14 days:

  1. Days 1-3: Replace 25% of kibble with raw, maintaining regular feeding schedule
  2. Days 4-6: Increase to 50% raw, monitor stool quality and energy levels
  3. Days 7-9: Increase to 75% raw, most dogs adapt by this point
  4. Days 10-14: Complete transition to 100% raw diet

Some dogs experience a \'detox\' period during raw transition — loose stools, increased flatulence, and lethargy as their digestive system adjusts. This typically resolves within 2 weeks. If symptoms persist beyond 3 weeks, consult your veterinarian.

Common Mistakes in Raw Feeding

  • Skipping organ meats: Feeding only muscle meat leads to severe nutritional deficiencies, particularly vitamin A and copper
  • Too much bone: Excess bone causes constipation and can damage teeth; keep bone at exactly 10%
  • Feeding raw pork: Pork carries higher parasite risk in NZ and is not recommended without special processing
  • Improper fish: Raw salmon, trout, and catfish must be frozen for at least 7 days at -20°C to kill salmon parasites before feeding
  • No supplementation: Even complete commercial BARF may need added vitamins depending on your dog\'s specific needs

Frequently Asked Questions

Can puppies eat raw food?

Yes, puppies can thrive on raw diets, but portion sizes and calcium:phosphorus ratios are particularly critical during growth stages. Many breeders start puppies on raw from 5-6 weeks of age. Consult your vet or a raw-feeding-savvy veterinarian to ensure correct calcium intake for your pup\'s expected adult size.

Can I mix raw and kibble?

Technically possible but generally not recommended. Digesting raw and kibble requires different enzyme environments and pH levels; mixing can cause digestive upset. If you want to transition gradually, feed raw in one meal and kibble in another, separated by at least 4-6 hours. Never feed raw and kibble in the same bowl simultaneously.

How do I know if my raw-fed dog is healthy?

Healthy indicators include: shiny coat, clean teeth, regular firm stools (no blood or mucous), normal energy levels, healthy weight maintenance, and clear bright eyes. Most raw feeders report improved coat condition, better breath, and higher energy within 4-8 weeks of starting. Annual vet checkups with blood work help ensure long-term health on any diet.

What about parasites in raw meat?

Commercial raw pet food in NZ is inspected and frozen to eliminate parasites. If sourcing from butchers or hunting your own meat, freeze fish (especially salmon and trout) for 7+ days at -20°C before feeding. Beef and lamb from NZ suppliers carry low parasite risk when properly stored. Always check meat quality before feeding — if it smells off or looks discoloured, discard it.

Is raw feeding expensive in NZ?

Raw feeding costs more than budget kibble but can be comparable to premium commercial foods. A medium dog typically costs $120-$240 per month on commercial BARF, or $70-$140 self-prepared. The cost premium is offset by many owners reporting fewer vet visits and better overall health, though evidence is anecdotal.
