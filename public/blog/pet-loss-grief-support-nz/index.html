Coping With Pet Loss in New Zealand: Grief Support, Memorials & What to Do Next

Published 9 July 2026 • 11 min read • ZiggySitters NZ

If you are reading this, you have probably just lost a pet, or you know someone who has. We are sorry. Losing a companion animal is not like losing a pet — it is losing a family member. The grief is real, it is heavy, and in a culture that sometimes minimises pet loss, it can feel isolating. This guide exists because you deserve practical, compassionate, and New Zealand-specific support. What to feel, what to do, where to turn, and how to eventually move forward without forgetting.

Your Grief Is Valid

Let us start with the most important thing: grieving a pet is not an overreaction. Research consistently shows that the bond between humans and companion animals activates the same attachment systems as human relationships. When that bond breaks, the grief pathways in your brain light up identically to losing a person you love.

Studies from the University of Auckland and Massey University have documented what pet owners already know: the grief intensity after pet loss can match or exceed grief after losing certain human relationships. Not because the person is cold, but because the pet relationship is uniquely pure — no complications, no mixed feelings, just love.

Common grief responses after pet loss include:

  • Shock and disbelief: Even when you expected it, the finality hits hard
  • Guilt: “Did I wait too long?” “Did I not do enough?” — this is nearly universal and almost always unfounded
  • Anger: At the vet, at the disease, at yourself, at the universe
  • Loneliness: The house is too quiet. The routine is gone. The absence is loud.
  • Physical symptoms: Insomnia, appetite changes, fatigue, chest heaviness
Remember: There is no timeline for pet grief. Some people feel functional again in weeks; others carry the weight for months or longer. Both are normal. The goal is not to “get over it” — it is to learn to carry it without it crushing you.

The Euthanasia Decision: Making the Hardest Call

If you are reading this before the decision, know this: most vets in New Zealand will tell you that the number one regret owners have is waiting too long, not acting too soon. This is not a push toward any decision. It is simply what the data shows.

Quality of Life Scale

Dr. Alice Villalobos created a quality-of-life scale used by vets worldwide. Score each category from 0 (very poor) to 10 (excellent):

  • Hurt: Is pain managed? Can the vet help with this?
  • Hunger: Is the pet eating enough to sustain themselves?
  • Hydration: Are they drinking? Can you syringe-feed fluids?
  • Hygiene: Can they keep clean? Are they getting pressure sores?
  • Happiness: Do they still show interest in life? Tail wags? Purrs? Engaging with you?
  • Mobility: Can they move around enough to be comfortable?
  • More Good Days Than Bad: This is the gut-check. If bad days outnumber good, it is time to have the conversation.

A total score above 35 suggests a reasonable quality of life. Below 35, it is time for an honest talk with your vet.

ZiggySitters Tip: Most NZ vets offer home euthanasia services. Being at home, in their favourite spot, with you beside them, is the kindest goodbye. Ask your vet about this option — it costs more ($50–$150 extra depending on location) but the peace it gives both of you is immeasurable.

What Happens During Euthanasia

Knowing what to expect reduces fear. In New Zealand, the standard process is:

  1. Sedation: Your pet gets a sedative first. Within minutes they are deeply asleep and feel nothing. Some pets groan or sigh as they relax — this is normal, not pain.
  2. The injection: Once sedated, the vet gives the final injection. It stops the heart within seconds. Your pet is already unconscious.
  3. After: Muscles may twitch. There may be a final breath. The bladder may release. These are reflex actions after consciousness is gone — your pet does not feel them.

You can stay for as much or as little of the process as you need. You can hold them. You can leave the room. There is no wrong choice.

Aftercare Options in New Zealand

New Zealand has several respectful aftercare options. Your vet will usually coordinate this, but knowing your choices helps you decide.

Cremation

  • Communal cremation: Your pet is cremated with other animals. Ashes are scattered at a memorial garden. Cost: $50–$150. You do not get ashes back.
  • Individual cremation: Your pet is cremated alone and ashes returned to you in a urn or box. Cost: $200–$500 depending on size. Most popular option in NZ.
  • Providers: Aftercare NZ, Pets Rest, and Dream Gardens operate nationwide. Many vets have preferred partners.

Home Burial

New Zealand law allows home burial of pets on your own property, provided:

  • The burial is at least 1.2 metres deep (to prevent disturbance)
  • The property is not a rental without landlord consent
  • You are not near a waterway or bore water supply
  • The pet has not been euthanised with barbiturates in an area where the groundwater could be affected (check with your local council)
Important: If you are renting, do not bury your pet in the garden without the landlord and knowing you will leave them behind. Many pet owners who have moved regret not having their pet is ashes. Consider individual cremation if you might move.

Pet Cemeteries in NZ

If home burial is not possible, New Zealand has dedicated pet cemeteries:

  • Auckland: Pet Rest Memorial Park (Swanson), Dream Gardens (Silverdale)
  • Wellington: Pets Rest (Lower Hutt)
  • Christchurch: Pets Rest (Hornby)
  • Hamilton, Tauranga, Dunedin: Local providers available — ask your vet

Memorial Ideas: Honouring Your Pet

Creating a memorial is not morbid. It is a way to channel grief into something tangible and lasting. Here are ideas that NZ pet owners have found meaningful:

Physical Memorials

  • Plant a native tree: Puriri, kowhai, or pohutukawa (depending on your region). These trees outlast us and become a living tribute. Check your local council for native planting guides.
  • Garden stone or plaque: Engraved with their name and dates. Companies like Memorial Plaque NZ create beautiful natural stone markers for $80–$200.
  • Memory box: Collar, favourite toy, a lock of fur, photos, their leash. Simple and personal.
  • Custom jewellery: A small amount of ashes can be set into a pendant or ring. Several NZ jewellers offer this service.

Digital Memorials

  • Photo album or video: Gather every photo and video. Set it to their favourite song. Watch it when you need to remember them at their best.
  • Online memorial: Rainbow Bridge NZ and local Facebook pet loss groups allow you to post a tribute.
  • Donation in their name: Give to the SPCA, local rescue, or a breed-specific charity. Many rescues will send you a certificate acknowledging your donation in your pet is honour.

Support Resources in New Zealand

You do not have to grieve alone. These NZ-specific resources understand pet loss:

Pet Loss Support Lines & Counselling

  • NZ Pet Loss Support Line: 0800 PET LOSS (0800 738 567) — free, confidential, run by trained volunteers
  • Lifeline: 0800 543 354 — for any grief that feels overwhelming, not just pet loss
  • Youthline: 0800 376 633 — for young people coping with pet loss
  • Counselling: Many NZ counsellors specialise in grief and pet loss. Your vet can recommend someone local. ACC does not cover pet loss counselling, but some EAP (Employee Assistance Programme) plans do.

Online Communities

  • Pet Loss Support NZ (Facebook group) — active, compassionate community of NZ pet owners
  • Rainbow Bridge NZ — online memorial wall and resources
  • r/Petloss (Reddit) — international but deeply supportive community
When Grief Gets Too Heavy: If your grief is affecting your ability to function for more than a few weeks, or if you are having thoughts of self-harm, please call Lifeline (0800 543 354) or text 4357. Grief can become complicated. Getting help is not weakness — it is what your pet would want for you.

Helping Children Through Pet Loss

Children process grief differently than adults. Here is what NZ child psychologists recommend:

  • Be honest. Do not say the pet “ran away” or “went to live on a farm.” Children need truthful language they can understand. “Buddy was very sick and the vet helped him die so he would not hurt anymore” is better than a lie they will later unravel.
  • Let them grieve their way. Some children cry. Some draw pictures. Some do not seem affected at all, then burst into tears a week later. All of these are normal.
  • Include them in the goodbye. If age-appropriate, let them attend the euthanasia or say goodbye beforehand. Let them choose a burial spot, pick a memorial stone, or draw a picture to place with the ashes.
  • Avoid replacement language. “We will get another cat” minimises the loss. Instead: “We can always remember Mittens, and someday when we are ready, we might open our hearts to another cat.”
  • Read together. “The Tenth Good Thing About Barney” by Judith Viorst is excellent for younger children. “Saying Goodbye to Lulu” by Corinne Demas for ages 4–8.

Helping Surviving Pets

If you have other pets, they are grieving too. Dogs and cats form deep bonds with their housemates, and the signs of grief in animals are well-documented:

  • Searching behaviour: Pacing, looking in favourite spots, waiting at the door
  • Appetite changes: Eating less or refusing food
  • Vocalising: Increased meowing, howling, or barking
  • Clinginess: Following you everywhere, not wanting to be alone
  • Withdrawal: Hiding, sleeping more, reduced playfulness
What Helps Surviving Pets: Maintain their routine. Keep walk times, feeding times, and bedtime consistent. Let them smell their companion is belongings before you wash or remove them — scent is how animals understand death. Give them extra attention but do not force it. And if your surviving pet develops symptoms that persist beyond two weeks, see a vet — stress can trigger real illness.

When You Are Ready: Getting Another Pet

There is no right timeline. Some people adopt a week later. Some wait years. Some never do. None of these choices are wrong.

What to consider before bringing a new pet home:

  • Are you adopting to fill the void, or because you genuinely have love and space for a new animal? If it is the former, give yourself more time. A new pet cannot replace the one you lost, and expecting them to sets both of you up for disappointment.
  • Is your surviving pet ready? Some pets welcome a new companion quickly. Others need months. Watch their behaviour, not your own feelings.
  • Consider a different breed or type. Getting the same breed as the pet you lost can trigger constant comparison. A different look, size, or personality helps you appreciate the new pet for who they are, not who they are not.
  • Adopt, do not impulse buy. The SPCA, rescue organisations, and breed-specific rescues across NZ have wonderful animals waiting for homes. Take your time to find the right match.

A Final Word

The writer Roland Tessmer said: “The reality is that you will grieve forever. You will not ‘get over’ the loss of your pet. You will learn to live with it. You will heal and you will rebuild yourself around the loss you have suffered. You will be whole again, but you will never be the same. Nor should you be the same, nor would you want to.”

Your pet gave you their entire life. The grief you feel now is the price of that love, and it is worth every moment of it. Take your time. Be gentle with yourself. And when you are ready, consider honouring them by opening your home to another animal who needs the kind of love only someone like you can give.
