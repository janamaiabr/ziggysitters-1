Shih Tzu Care Guide NZ: Everything New Zealand Owners Need to Know

By ZiggySitters • Updated March 2026 • 12 min read

Quick Summary: Shih Tzus are loyal, low-shedding companions that suit New Zealand lifestyles from Auckland apartments to Queenstown cottages. Their main care needs are regular grooming, face cleaning, and protection from summer heat due to their flat faces. This guide covers everything from daily routines to choosing the right pet sitter.

Ask any Shih Tzu owner in New Zealand and they will tell you the same thing: these little dogs are absolute characters. Confident, playful, and surprisingly stubborn, the Shih Tzu (pronounced "sheed-zoo") has been a beloved companion breed for centuries. Originally bred for Chinese royalty, they are now one of New Zealand's most popular small breeds, and for good reason.

But owning a Shih Tzu comes with specific care responsibilities that differ significantly from other small breeds. Their beautiful flowing coats demand consistent attention. Their flat faces (a trait shared with Bulldogs and Pugs, known as brachycephalic) require extra vigilance in summer heat. And their social nature means they do not thrive when left alone for long periods.

Whether you are a first-time Shih Tzu owner or looking to improve your care routine, this guide covers everything you need to know about raising a happy, healthy Shih Tzu in New Zealand.

Understanding the Shih Tzu Breed

Temperament and Personality

Shih Tzus are bred to be companions, and it shows in every aspect of their personality. They are affectionate, outgoing, and thrive on human interaction. Unlike some small breeds that bond closely with one person, most Shih Tzus are friendly with everyone — visitors, children, and other pets included.

They have a playful, mischievous streak that owners adore, and they can be surprisingly vocal when they want attention. Shih Tzus are not guard dogs by nature, but they will alert you to strangers with a confident bark. They are also known for being adaptable: equally happy going for a walk in the park or snuggling on the couch for the afternoon.

The flip side of their social nature is that Shih Tzus can develop separation anxiety if their routine is disrupted or they are left alone for extended periods. This is an important consideration when planning holidays or long work days.

Size and Lifespan

Adult Shih Tzus typically weigh between 4.5 and 8 kg and stand about 25-28 cm at the shoulder. They are a robust little dog for their size. With good care, the average Shih Tzu lifespan is 10-16 years, and many NZ owners report their dogs living well into their mid-teens with proper health management.

Grooming: The Most Important Part of Shih Tzu Care

If there is one area where Shih Tzu care differs most from other breeds, it is grooming. Their double coat grows continuously and will mat and tangle without regular attention. This is non-negotiable — neglecting grooming leads to painful skin issues and can trap moisture against the skin, causing infections.

Daily and Weekly Grooming Routine

  • Daily: Clean the eye area with a damp cloth. Shih Tzus have large, prominent eyes that accumulate discharge. Left uncleaned, this causes staining and can lead to eye infections.
  • Every 2-3 days: Brush the entire coat with a slicker brush and a metal comb, working in sections from the skin outward. Focus on areas behind the ears, under the armpits, and around the collar.
  • Weekly: Check and clean the ears. Shih Tzus are prone to ear infections, particularly in humid northern NZ summers. Wipe gently with a vet-recommended ear cleaner.
  • Monthly: Trim nails and check paw pads. NZ footpaths can be rough on small paws, so regular checks for cracks or soreness are worthwhile.

Professional Grooming in NZ

Most NZ Shih Tzu owners take their dog to a professional groomer every 6-8 weeks. A full groom typically includes a bath, blow-dry, haircut, ear clean, nail trim, and anal gland check. Expect to pay $65-$110 depending on your location and coat condition.

The most popular cuts for NZ Shih Tzus are the puppy cut (short all over, easy to maintain) and the top knot style (longer body coat with the face hair tied up). In New Zealand's humid summers, many owners prefer the puppy cut from November through February for comfort.

Tip: If you are leaving your Shih Tzu with a pet sitter, provide a grooming kit with instructions for the face cleaning routine. A good sitter will maintain this daily — it takes only two minutes but makes a significant difference to your dog's comfort and eye health.

Exercise Needs: Less Than You Think

One of the reasons Shih Tzus suit New Zealand apartment living so well is their moderate exercise requirements. An adult Shih Tzu is content with 20-30 minutes of walking per day, split into two shorter outings. They enjoy sniffing and exploring far more than sustained running, so a slow stroll around the neighbourhood satisfies them just as well as a formal walk.

That said, mental stimulation is just as important as physical exercise. Puzzle toys, short training sessions, and play with other dogs all contribute to a well-balanced, happy Shih Tzu.

Summer Exercise Caution

Brachycephalic (flat-faced) breeds like the Shih Tzu are at significant risk of heatstroke. In New Zealand's summer months, particularly in Auckland and Northland where humidity is high, avoid exercising your Shih Tzu during the hottest part of the day (10am-3pm). Early morning or evening walks are far safer.

Signs of overheating include heavy panting, drooling, bright red gums, lethargy, and vomiting. If you suspect heatstroke, move your dog to a cool area immediately, apply cool (not cold) water to their body, and contact your vet.

Nutrition and Diet

Shih Tzus do well on high-quality commercial food formulated for small breeds. Their small mouths and unique jaw structure mean kibble size matters — choose a small-breed formula with appropriately sized pieces. Many NZ owners supplement dry food with wet food or fresh protein sources.

Common Dietary Issues

  • Weight gain: Shih Tzus are prone to obesity, which worsens their breathing and stresses joints. Avoid overfeeding and limit treats.
  • Food allergies: Some Shih Tzus develop skin issues linked to food allergies. If you notice persistent itching, ear infections, or gut issues, discuss a hydrolysed or limited-ingredient diet with your vet.
  • Tear staining: Diet can influence tear staining. Some owners find reducing artificial colours and preservatives helps reduce brown staining around the eyes.

Health: What NZ Shih Tzu Owners Should Watch For

Shih Tzus are generally a healthy breed, but their anatomy creates some specific vulnerabilities that every owner should understand.

Brachycephalic Obstructive Airway Syndrome (BOAS)

This is the most significant health consideration for Shih Tzus. Their flat faces can result in narrowed nostrils, an elongated soft palate, and a narrow windpipe. Mild cases are manageable with lifestyle adjustments; severe cases may require corrective surgery. If your Shih Tzu snores loudly, tires quickly on walks, or struggles in heat, consult a vet experienced with brachycephalic breeds.

Eye Problems

Their large, prominent eyes are prone to corneal ulcers, dry eye, and progressive retinal atrophy. Regular eye cleaning is preventative. Any cloudiness, excessive squinting, or discharge warrants a vet visit.

Hip Dysplasia and Joint Health

While more common in large breeds, Shih Tzus can develop hip dysplasia. Keeping them at a healthy weight is the most effective preventative measure.

Dental Health

Small breeds are disproportionately affected by dental disease. Shih Tzus, with their crowded teeth, are particularly vulnerable. Daily tooth brushing with a dog-safe toothpaste, dental chews, and annual professional scaling all help. Poor dental health in small dogs is linked to heart and kidney disease over time.

Socialisation and Training

Shih Tzus are intelligent but can be independent thinkers. Early socialisation — exposing puppies to different people, animals, environments, and sounds — builds the confident, well-adjusted adult dog that the breed is known for. NZ puppy classes are excellent for this.

Training works best with positive reinforcement. Shih Tzus respond well to praise and treats; harsh corrections cause them to shut down. Keep sessions short (5-10 minutes) and fun. Their independent streak means they may occasionally "decide" a command is optional — consistency is key.

NZ Note: If you are in Auckland, Wellington, or Christchurch, look for small-breed specific puppy socialisation classes. These give Shih Tzu puppies the right-sized experience without the risk of being overwhelmed by larger dogs.

Finding the Right Pet Sitter for Your Shih Tzu in NZ

When you need to travel or work long hours, finding the right pet sitter for a Shih Tzu requires a little more care than for some other breeds. Here is what to look for:

Brachycephalic Experience

Not all pet sitters understand the specific needs of flat-faced breeds. Ask directly: "Have you cared for brachycephalic breeds before?" and "Are you aware of the signs of overheating?" A sitter experienced with Shih Tzus, Pugs, or French Bulldogs will already know the daily routines involved.

Grooming Comfort

Your sitter does not need to be a groomer, but they should be comfortable with the daily face-cleaning routine and able to do a quick brush-through every couple of days. Provide your grooming kit and written instructions.

Separation Anxiety Management

Because Shih Tzus bond closely with their humans, in-home pet sitting (where your dog stays in their own environment) often works better than boarding. Ask whether the sitter will stay overnight or make multiple visits per day.

What to Include in Your Pet Care Sheet

  • Feeding schedule and amounts
  • Face and eye cleaning routine with products used
  • Walk times and distance (and summer heat restrictions)
  • Any medications, supplements, or health conditions
  • Vet contact details and pet insurance information
  • Emergency signs to watch for (breathing distress, overheating)
  • Favourite toys and comfort items

Shih Tzu and New Zealand Life

Apartments and Urban Living

Shih Tzus are ideally suited to apartment living in cities like Auckland and Wellington. Their size, low exercise needs, and quiet nature (they are not excessive barkers) make them good neighbours. Just ensure access to outdoor space for toileting and that the apartment does not overheat in summer.

Families with Children

Shih Tzus generally get on very well with children, but supervision is important with young kids due to the dog's small size. They can be accidentally hurt by rough handling. Teaching children how to interact gently with the dog is as important as training the dog itself.

Multi-Pet Households

Shih Tzus typically coexist happily with cats and other dogs when introduced properly. Their non-aggressive, playful nature makes them easy housemates. Proper introductions — on neutral ground, with positive reinforcement — go a long way.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I groom my Shih Tzu in NZ?

Brush at least every 2-3 days and book a professional groom every 6-8 weeks. In humid northern NZ summers, more frequent coat maintenance helps prevent skin issues.

Are Shih Tzus suitable for New Zealand apartments?

Absolutely. They are one of the best apartment breeds — low exercise needs, adaptable, and quiet enough to keep neighbours happy.

Do Shih Tzus cope with NZ winters?

Final Thoughts

Owning a Shih Tzu in New Zealand is a genuinely rewarding experience. They are loyal, funny, and endlessly affectionate companions that slot into a wide range of lifestyles. The main commitment is to their grooming — invest the time in a proper routine and professional grooming schedule, and your Shih Tzu will repay you with years of cheerful company.

The other key is understanding their brachycephalic nature: manage the heat, watch their weight, and stay on top of dental care. With these basics covered, Shih Tzus are robust little dogs that tend to live long, happy lives.

And when you need someone to care for them while you are away, look for a sitter who gets the Shih Tzu difference — one who will keep up the face-cleaning routine, keep them cool in summer, and give them the companionship they thrive on.
