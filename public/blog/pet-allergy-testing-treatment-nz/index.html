Pet Allergy Testing & Treatment in New Zealand (2026 Guide)

Signs Your Pet Has Allergies

Allergies in pets look different than in people. Your pet will not sneeze and reach for tissues — instead, the signs are mostly skin-related and behavioural.

In Dogs

  • Persistent licking or chewing of paws (especially front paws)
  • Red, inflamed skin, particularly on the belly, armpits, and groin
  • Recurring ear infections (head shaking, dark discharge, smell)
  • Rubbing face on furniture or carpet
  • Hot spots — moist, raw, and painful patches of skin
  • Scaly or greasy coat, especially along the back
  • Scooting or licking the anal area

In Cats

  • Excessive grooming — bald patches, especially on the belly and inner thighs
  • Miliary dermatitis — small crusty bumps along the back and neck
  • Recurring ear infections or itchy ears
  • Facial scratching, especially around the chin and ears
  • Vomiting or diarrhoea (common with food allergies)
  • Asthma-like symptoms: coughing, wheezing, laboured breathing
When to see a vet immediately: If your pet has facial swelling, difficulty breathing, sudden hives, or collapse — these are signs of anaphylaxis, which is a medical emergency. Call your nearest emergency vet in New Zealand on 0800 VETCHAT (0800 838 242) or your regular vet after-hours line.

Types of Pet Allergies

Understanding which type of allergy your pet has determines the testing and treatment path. The three main categories are:

1. Environmental Allergies (Atopic Dermatitis)

The most common type in New Zealand. Triggered by pollens (grasses, trees, weeds), dust mites, mould spores, and storage mites. These are seasonal in many dogs but can be year-round, especially for indoor allergens like dust mites.

In NZ, the main environmental culprits are:

  • Ryegrass and tall fescue pollen — peak October to January
  • Birch and wattle pollen — peak August to October
  • House dust mites (Dermatophagoides farinae and pteronyssinus) — year-round, worse in humid conditions
  • Mould spores — peak in damp autumn and winter months

Atopic dermatitis typically appears between 1 and 3 years of age and tends to worsen with each successive allergy season.

2. Food Allergies

True food allergies (involving the immune system) are less common than many owners think, but they do exist. The most common food allergens in NZ dogs and cats are:

  • Beef
  • Dairy products
  • Chicken
  • Wheat
  • Soy
  • Fish (more common in cats)

Food allergies cause year-round symptoms — they do not come and go with the seasons. The gold standard for diagnosis is an elimination diet trial, not a blood test.

3. Flea Allergy Dermatitis (FAD)

In a flea-allergic pet, a single flea bite can trigger intense itching lasting days. This is the most common allergy in NZ cats and a significant problem in dogs, particularly in the warmer North Island regions. If your pet is itchy but you cannot see fleas, do not rule them out — flea-allergic animals groom obsessively and remove the evidence.

Rule out fleas first. Before investing in expensive allergy testing, ensure your pet is on a quality flea preventative (such as NexGard, Bravecto, or Advocate) for at least 4-6 weeks. Many suspected allergies resolve once flea infestation is properly controlled.

Allergy Testing Options

New Zealand veterinary practices offer several approaches to allergy testing. Here is what you need to know about each.

Intradermal Skin Testing

The gold standard for environmental allergy diagnosis. Small amounts of allergen extracts are injected into a clipped area of skin, and the vet measures the reaction at each site. This test is typically performed by veterinary dermatologists or referral centres.

  • Accuracy: Highest for environmental allergies
  • Requires: Sedation or light anaesthesia, clipped skin area, 1-2 hours at the clinic
  • Limitations: Cannot test for food allergies. Must be off antihistamines and steroids for 2-4 weeks beforehand.
  • Availability: Referral centres in Auckland, Hamilton, Wellington, and Christchurch

Blood Allergy Testing (Serology)

A blood sample is taken and sent to a laboratory (often overseas) to measure IgE antibodies against specific allergens. This is more convenient than intradermal testing because it does not require sedation, but it is less accurate.

  • Accuracy: Moderate — false positives are common
  • Requires: A single blood draw, no sedation needed
  • Limitations: Results must be interpreted by a vet experienced in dermatology. A positive result does not always mean the allergen is clinically relevant.
  • Availability: Most vet clinics can draw blood and send it to the lab

Elimination Diet Trial

The only reliable method for diagnosing food allergies. Your pet eats a novel protein (such as venison, kangaroo, or rabbit) and a novel carbohydrate (such as sweet potato) exclusively for 8-12 weeks. No treats, no table scraps, no flavoured medications — nothing else passes your pet's lips.

  • Accuracy: Highest for food allergies when done correctly
  • Requires: Strict compliance for 8-12 weeks (this is harder than it sounds)
  • Limitations: Time-consuming. Requires significant owner discipline. Not suitable if your pet has concurrent environmental allergies (which will muddy the results).
ZiggySitters Tip: If your pet is on an elimination diet, tell your pet sitter explicitly. One stolen treat from a well-meaning neighbour or sitter can restart the entire 12-week clock. Write it in your pet-sitting instructions and provide only the approved food.

Treatment Options

Allergy treatment in NZ follows a stepwise approach. Your vet will typically start with the least invasive, most broadly effective options and escalate as needed.

Step 1: Parasite Control

Before anything else, eliminate fleas. A quality monthly preventative applied to all pets in the household (not just the itchy one) for a minimum of 3 consecutive months is the baseline. In multi-pet homes, treating only the allergic pet is insufficient — asymptomatic carriers will reinfest them.

Step 2: Symptomatic Relief

While investigating the cause, your vet can prescribe medications to break the itch cycle:

  • Apoquel (oclacitinib): A targeted drug that blocks itch signals. Works within hours. Safe for short and long-term use. Does not suppress the immune system broadly. Available throughout NZ.
  • Cytopoint: An injectable biologic that neutralises a specific itch-inducing protein (IL-31). Given by injection every 4-8 weeks. Extremely safe with virtually no side effects. Increasingly available from general practice vets, not just specialists.
  • Short steroid courses: Prednisone or methylprednisolone for acute flare-ups. Effective but not suitable for long-term use due to side effects (increased thirst, urination, appetite, and risk of diabetes and Cushing's disease with chronic use).
  • Antihistamines: Less effective in dogs than in people, but some dogs respond. Often used as an adjunct rather than a primary treatment. Your vet will specify the correct type and dose — human antihistamines vary in safety and effectiveness for pets.

Step 3: Identify and Avoid Triggers

Allergy testing (as described above) identifies what your pet is reacting to. Once you know the triggers, you can reduce exposure:

  • Grass pollen: Wipe your dog's paws and belly with a damp cloth after outdoor walks during pollen season (spring and early summer). Keep lawns mowed short. Avoid walking in long grass during peak pollen times (early morning).
  • Dust mites: Wash pet bedding weekly in hot water (60°C minimum). Use dust-mite-proof covers on pet beds. Vacuum frequently. Consider a dehumidifier — dust mites thrive above 50% relative humidity.
  • Mould: Ensure good ventilation in your home, especially in bathrooms and laundries. Clean visible mould promptly.

Step 4: Immunotherapy (Desensitisation)

If allergy testing identifies specific triggers, your vet can formulate custom allergy shots or oral drops that gradually desensitise your pet's immune system. This is the closest thing to a cure for environmental allergies.

  • Effectiveness: Approximately 60-70% of pets show significant improvement
  • Timeline: 6-12 months to see full effect
  • Duration: Most pets need maintenance doses for life
  • Administration: Injections at home (after vet training) or oral drops under the tongue

Step 5: Dietary Management

If food allergies are confirmed through an elimination trial, the long-term solution is avoiding the trigger ingredient. Options include:

  • Prescription hypoallergenic diets (hydrolysed protein — the protein molecules are broken down so small the immune system cannot recognise them)
  • Novel protein diets (kangaroo, venison, rabbit — proteins your pet has never eaten before)
  • Home-cooked diets designed by a veterinary nutritionist (for precise control over ingredients)

Long-Term Management Strategies

Allergies are almost always a lifelong condition. The goal is management, not cure. Here is a practical framework for keeping your allergic pet comfortable in New Zealand conditions.

Bath Routine

Regular bathing with a medicated shampoo (prescribed by your vet) removes allergens from the skin surface and helps restore the skin barrier. For dogs with atopic dermatitis, bathing every 1-2 weeks with a chlorhexidine or miconazole-based shampoo can significantly reduce flare-ups. Let the shampoo sit on the skin for 5-10 minutes before rinsing — contact time matters.

Skin Barrier Support

The skin barrier in allergic pets is fundamentally compromised. Support it with:

  • EfaVet or Dermcare Omega-3 supplements: Available from NZ vets, these reduce inflammation and support the skin barrier
  • Topical lipid sprays: Products like Virbac's Allerderm Spot-On or Douxo S3 restore the lipid layer to the skin
  • Hypoallergenic conditioning rinses: Applied after bathing, these lock in moisture and reduce allergen penetration

Environmental Controls in NZ

  • Wipe paws and belly after every walk during spring and summer (pollen season)
  • Wash bedding weekly in hot water
  • Run a dehumidifier in humid parts of NZ (Auckland, Waikato, Bay of Plenty) — aim for 40-50% indoor humidity
  • Use HEPA air filters in the rooms where your pet sleeps
  • Avoid walking on freshly mown grass during peak pollen hours
  • Rinse your dog off with fresh water after beach visits (salt irritates compromised skin)

Monitoring and Flare Plan

Work with your vet to create a written flare plan — a step-by-step guide for what to do when your pet's allergies worsen. This typically includes:

  • When to increase baths
  • When to restart or increase medication
  • When to schedule a vet visit versus when you can manage at home
  • Emergency contacts for after-hours veterinary care
Share your flare plan with your pet sitter. If your pet has a flare-up while you are away, a sitter who knows exactly what to do — and has your vet's phone number and your authorisation to seek treatment — can prevent a minor itch from becoming a major infection.

How a Pet Sitter Helps Allergic Pets

Allergic pets need consistency. A change in routine, diet, or environment can trigger a flare. When you travel, a professional pet sitter provides:

  • Strict dietary adherence: No unauthorised treats, no table scraps — exactly what your vet prescribed
  • Medication administration: Pills, topical treatments, and injections given on schedule
  • Bathing and wipe-down routines: Maintaining the schedule that keeps your pet comfortable
  • Environmental monitoring: Recognising early signs of flare-ups and acting before they escalate
  • Vet liaison: If your pet needs urgent care, your sitter can take them to your vet with your pre-authorised treatment plan
