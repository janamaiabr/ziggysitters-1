Winter Pet Care Guide NZ 2026: Keeping Your Dog or Cat Warm and Healthy

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  March 15, 2026  •  11 min read

New Zealand winters may not rival the Antarctic, but they bring enough cold, wet, and wind to leave pets uncomfortable, stiff, and under the weather. Whether you have a short-coated Greyhound in Wellington or a fluffy Maine Coon in Queenstown, your pet needs a few seasonal adjustments to stay happy from June through August.

As autumn turns to winter across Aotearoa, pet owners often underestimate how much the cold affects their animals. Dogs and cats are more vulnerable to temperature changes than many people realise, particularly seniors, puppies, kittens, and short-coated breeds. This guide covers everything you need to know about winter pet care in New Zealand — from keeping your home warm and pet-safe, to adjusting exercise routines, nutrition, and vet checks.

How Cold Is Too Cold for NZ Pets?

New Zealand winters vary dramatically by region. Dunedin and Queenstown can drop to near-freezing overnight, while Auckland rarely dips below 8°C. That said, even mild cold combined with dampness can be harder on pets than pure cold in a dry climate.

As a general guide:

  • Below 10°C: Small dogs, short-coated breeds, cats, puppies, kittens, and senior pets should not spend extended time outdoors.
  • Below 5°C: Even large, double-coated dogs (Huskies, German Shepherds, Malamutes) need monitoring outside.
  • Below 0°C: Any pet left outdoors is at serious risk of hypothermia. This applies to South Island alpine regions from June onward.
Tip: If you are cold, your pet probably is too. A simple rule of thumb — if you need a jacket outside, your dog likely does as well.

Signs your pet is too cold include shivering, reluctance to walk, lifting paws off cold ground, hunching the body, seeking warmth against walls or under furniture, and in serious cases, lethargy or disorientation. Act immediately if you see these signs.

Keeping Your Pet Warm Indoors

Beds and Sleeping Spots

Cold floors are one of the biggest winter hazards for pets, especially those with arthritis or joint issues. In New Zealand homes — many of which have minimal insulation and no central heating — floor temperatures can drop significantly overnight.

  • Raise your pet's bed off the floor using a raised cot-style bed or place a thick blanket underneath.
  • Choose a bed with raised sides or a hood to trap body heat.
  • Place beds away from draughts, external walls, and cat flaps.
  • If you use a heat pump, make sure your pet can access a room where it runs overnight — but not so close that they overheat.

Safe Heating Around Pets

Many NZ households use portable heaters, heat pumps, or log fires in winter. All of these carry risks for curious pets.

Warning: Open fires, bar heaters, and portable gas heaters are a serious burn risk. Always use a safety guard and never leave pets unsupervised near open flames or exposed heating elements.
  • Heat pumps are generally the safest option — keep pets from sitting directly under the unit to avoid dry air irritating their eyes and nose.
  • Electric blankets should not be used under a pet's bed unless specifically designed for pets. Human electric blankets can overheat if a heavy dog lies on them.
  • Carbon monoxide from faulty gas heaters is dangerous for both people and pets. Ensure gas appliances are serviced annually.

Winter Clothing for Dogs

Dog coats and jumpers are not just fashion accessories — for the right breeds, they are essential winter gear in New Zealand.

Breeds that genuinely benefit from a winter coat include:

  • Greyhounds, Whippets, and Italian Greyhounds (very low body fat and no undercoat)
  • Chihuahuas and other toy breeds
  • Short-coated breeds like Boxers, Dobermanns, and Staffordshire Bull Terriers
  • Puppies under 12 weeks
  • Senior dogs with reduced muscle mass
  • Dogs recovering from illness or surgery

Look for waterproof outer shells for wet Wellington days, or wool-lined fleece for cold Southland nights. Ensure the coat fits properly — it should cover the neck to the base of the tail without restricting movement or chafing under the legs.

Adjusting Exercise in Winter

Dogs still need daily exercise in winter — skipping walks for weeks is not the answer. But you may need to adjust timing, duration, and intensity.

Walk Timing

In New Zealand winters, the warmest part of the day is typically between 11am and 2pm. If your dog is sensitive to cold, shift morning walks later or split exercise into shorter midday sessions rather than one long early-morning outing.

Paw Care on Cold Ground

Frost and cold wet surfaces can irritate your dog's paw pads, causing cracking or redness. After walks:

  • Dry paws thoroughly with a towel, especially between the toes.
  • Check for grass seeds embedded in paw fur — these are a year-round hazard in NZ but harder to spot in winter when paws are muddy.
  • Apply a paw balm if pads look dry or cracked.

Indoor Enrichment on Wet Days

When it's genuinely too wet or cold for a walk, mental stimulation can partially replace physical exercise. Options include:

  • Puzzle feeders and snuffle mats
  • Scatter feeding kibble in the grass (works even in light rain)
  • Indoor training sessions (five minutes of new commands burns more energy than a ten-minute walk)
  • Tug-of-war and fetch in a long hallway
  • Frozen Kongs or lick mats filled with pet-safe treats
Tip: A mentally tired dog is a calm dog. On rainy winter days, twenty minutes of training or food puzzles can settle an otherwise restless dog better than a frustrated wet walk.

Winter Nutrition: Does Your Pet Need More Food?

Pets that spend time outdoors burn more calories in cold weather to maintain body temperature. However, for most NZ pet dogs and cats that live primarily indoors, caloric needs in winter are similar to the rest of the year.

The main winter nutrition considerations are:

Watch for Winter Weight Gain

Less exercise combined with the same food intake is a recipe for weight gain. If your dog is getting fewer walks in winter, reduce portions slightly or switch to a lower-calorie formula until activity levels return to normal.

Omega-3 Fatty Acids for Skin and Joints

Cold, dry air can make skin and coat issues worse in winter. A daily fish oil supplement (or a food rich in omega-3s) helps maintain coat quality and has anti-inflammatory benefits for dogs with joint issues.

Stay Hydrated

Pets drink less water in winter because they are less thirsty, but hydration is still essential. Ensure fresh water is always available and at room temperature — some pets will refuse very cold water. Cats especially benefit from a water fountain, which encourages drinking year-round.

Joint Health and Arthritis in Winter

If you have a senior dog or cat, or a breed prone to hip dysplasia (Labradors, German Shepherds, Golden Retrievers, French Bulldogs), winter is the hardest season on their joints. Cold temperatures genuinely do make arthritis worse — this is not just an old wives' tale.

Signs of joint pain worsening in winter include:

  • Stiffness after rest, especially first thing in the morning
  • Reluctance to climb stairs, jump onto furniture, or get in the car
  • Licking or chewing at specific joints
  • Limping that improves after gentle movement
  • Irritability when touched around the hips or lower back

Management options include:

  • Keeping sleeping areas warm and off cold floors
  • Maintaining regular gentle exercise — complete rest often makes stiffness worse
  • Omega-3 supplements (anti-inflammatory)
  • Joint supplements containing glucosamine and chondroitin
  • A vet consultation about pain management if symptoms are significant

Cats in Winter: Special Considerations

Cats are generally better at self-regulating body temperature than dogs, but they still need extra attention in winter.

Outdoor vs Indoor Time

If your cat usually goes outside, you may find they spend much more time indoors in winter — which is fine. Provide additional litter trays if needed (indoor accidents increase when cats avoid going outside in the cold), and ensure they have warm, elevated sleeping spots away from draughts.

Feline Lower Urinary Tract Disease (FLUTD)

Winter is a known risk period for FLUTD in cats, particularly males. Stress from being cooped up indoors, reduced water intake, and cold temperatures all contribute. Encourage water consumption (wet food, fountains) and watch for signs like straining to urinate, frequent small urinations, or blood in urine — these require urgent veterinary attention.

Lost Cats in Winter

Cats seeking warmth may crawl into car engine bays, outdoor sheds, or neighbouring garages. Before starting your car in winter, knock on the bonnet and check around the vehicle. Make sure your cat is microchipped and registered in case they wander further than usual.

Winter Hazards in the NZ Home and Garden

Warning: Several common winter household items are toxic to pets.
  • Rodenticides (rat poison): More commonly used in winter as rodents seek warmth indoors. Many NZ rodenticides are highly toxic to dogs and cats. Store securely and use bait stations that pets cannot access.
  • Slug and snail bait: Less of a winter issue but still present. Metaldehyde-based baits are extremely toxic to dogs. Use iron EDTA-based alternatives (pet-safe).
  • Antifreeze/coolant: While not widely used in NZ, it is used in high-altitude South Island areas. Ethylene glycol (found in some coolants) is fatally toxic to pets and has a sweet taste that attracts them.
  • Essential oil diffusers: Popular in winter for warm scents, but many essential oils (tea tree, eucalyptus, clove, cinnamon) are toxic to cats even when diffused. Ensure adequate ventilation and keep cats out of rooms where diffusers run.

Winter Vet Visits: What to Schedule

Autumn is the ideal time to get a few things sorted before winter arrives, but it is not too late to book now:

  • Flea and worm treatment: Fleas thrive in warm indoor environments in winter. Keep up year-round prevention even if your pet spends less time outside.
  • Dental check: Dental disease gets worse when pets eat more wet food or soft treats (common in winter). A scale and polish now prevents more expensive problems later.
  • Senior wellness check: Pets aged seven and over benefit from a winter blood panel to check organ function, thyroid levels, and joint inflammation markers.
  • Weight check: Ask your vet to body-condition-score your pet if you are unsure whether they are gaining winter weight.

Winter Pet Care Checklist

  • Pet bed raised off cold floor with warm bedding
  • Heater guards in place for open fires and bar heaters
  • Dog coat ready for cold-sensitive breeds
  • Paw balm and post-walk drying towel by the door
  • Indoor enrichment activities prepared for wet days
  • Fresh water available at room temperature
  • Flea and worm prevention up to date
  • Rodenticides stored securely or replaced with pet-safe alternatives
  • Cat microchipped and details current on NZ Companion Animal Register
  • Vet check booked for senior or arthritic pets
  • Pet sitter booked for July school holidays if travelling

Summary: Winter Is Manageable with a Little Preparation

New Zealand winters are rarely extreme, but they do require some adjustments for your pet's comfort and health. The key priorities are warmth (especially sleeping spots and heating safety), maintaining exercise with appropriate adjustments, monitoring joint health in older animals, and being aware of winter-specific toxins around the home. With a warm bed, regular meals, and a trusted pet sitter when you need to travel, your dog or cat can get through winter in great shape.
