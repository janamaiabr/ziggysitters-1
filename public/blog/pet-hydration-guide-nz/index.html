Pet Hydration Guide NZ: How Much Water Does Your Dog or Cat Really Need?

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  July 1, 2026  •  13 min read

Dehydration is one of the most common and easily preventable health problems in New Zealand pets. From scorching summer days in Central Otago to dry heated homes in a Wellington winter, your pet's water needs shift constantly. This guide covers exactly how much water dogs and cats need, how to spot dehydration early, and practical strategies to keep your pet hydrated — whether you are home or away with a sitter in charge.

How Much Water Does a Dog Need?

The general rule is 50 to 60 millilitres of water per kilogram of body weight per day. For a typical New Zealand dog, that looks like this:

Dog Size Weight Daily Water (Approx.)
Small (Chihuahua, Pug) 3–8 kg 150–480 ml
Medium (Cavoodle, Border Collie) 10–25 kg 500–1,500 ml
Large (Labrador, German Shepherd) 25–40 kg 1,250–2,400 ml
Giant (Great Dane, Mastiff) 40–70 kg 2,000–4,200 ml

However, water needs increase significantly with:

  • Exercise and heat — a dog that has been running at the beach or on a tramping track may need double or triple their normal intake
  • High-protein or dry kibble diets — wet food provides up to 70% moisture; dry kibble provides almost none
  • Illness — vomiting, diarrhoea, fever, and kidney disease all increase water requirements
  • Pregnancy and lactation — nursing bitches need substantially more water
  • Medications — some drugs increase thirst as a side effect
Quick test: Gently lift the skin on the back of your dog's neck. If it snaps back immediately, hydration is good. If it takes more than a second to flatten, your dog may be dehydrated. This is called the skin tent test.

How Much Water Does a Cat Need?

Cats are notoriously poor drinkers. In the wild, they get most of their moisture from prey. Domestic cats on dry kibble diets are at genuine risk of chronic low-grade dehydration, which contributes to kidney disease and urinary tract problems — two of the most common health issues in New Zealand cats.

The daily guideline is 40 to 60 ml per kilogram, but many cats drink far less than this on their own. A 4 kg cat needs roughly 200–240 ml per day, which is less than a standard cup.

Why Cats Do Not Drink Enough

  • Evolutionary instinct — cats evolved as desert animals that get water from food, not standing water
  • Whisker fatigue — narrow bowls irritate sensitive whiskers, making cats avoid drinking
  • Stale water aversion — cats prefer fresh, moving water and will avoid bowls that have been sitting out
  • Proximity to food — cats instinctively avoid water near their kill (food bowl) because of contamination risk
The single most impactful change: Switching a dry-food-only cat to a diet that includes wet food can increase their daily water intake by 50 to 100%. If your cat will not eat wet food, a pet water fountain is the next best investment.

Signs of Dehydration in Dogs and Cats

Dehydration can become dangerous quickly. Know the warning signs:

Early Signs (Mild Dehydration)

  • Dry or sticky gums
  • Sunken eyes
  • Skin tent (slow return when pinched)
  • Reduced energy or lethargy
  • Decreased urination (check the litter tray)
  • Panting more than usual (dogs)

Serious Signs (Require Immediate Vet Attention)

  • Refusal to drink for more than 12 hours
  • Vomiting or diarrhoea lasting more than 24 hours
  • Dark or concentrated urine
  • Rapid heart rate
  • Collapse or extreme lethargy
  • Dry, sunken eyes with no tear production
Emergency: Severe dehydration can cause organ failure and death within hours. If your pet shows serious signs — especially combined with vomiting or diarrhoea — take them to a vet immediately. New Zealand after-hours emergency vets are available in all major cities. Do not wait.

New Zealand-Specific Hydration Challenges

Summer Heat

New Zealand summers are intense, especially in Central Otago, Hawke's Bay, and Canterbury where temperatures regularly exceed 30 degrees. Dogs exercising in these conditions can dehydrate in under 30 minutes. Always carry water on walks, avoid midday exercise, and provide shade and water at all times.

Winter Heating

Kiwi homes with heat pumps and log burners create very dry indoor air in winter. This increases respiratory moisture loss for indoor pets. Cats and small dogs spending most of their time inside heated homes need access to water in every room they use.

Humidity in Auckland and Northland

High humidity reduces the effectiveness of panting (dogs' primary cooling mechanism). In muggy Auckland summers, dogs cannot cool themselves efficiently and need more water and more frequent rest breaks. A dog that appears fine can overheat surprisingly quickly in humid conditions.

Beach and River Water Risks

New Zealand dogs love the water, but not all water is safe to drink:

  • Salt water at beaches causes dehydration and diarrhoea — always bring fresh water to the beach
  • Farm runoff in rural streams can contain E. coli and other pathogens
  • Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) in stagnant lakes and slow rivers is lethal to dogs — check regional council warnings before letting your dog swim
  • Stock troughs may contain algae or chemical residues
Blue-green algae is a real killer in NZ. If you see green scum on a lake or slow-moving river, keep your dog out completely. Do not let them drink from or swim in affected waterways. Check lawa.org.nz (Land, Air, Water Aotearoa) for current water quality warnings in your region.

7 Strategies to Keep Your Pet Hydrated

1. Multiple Water Stations

Place water bowls in at least three locations around your home. Dogs and cats both drink more when water is convenient. For multi-level homes, have at least one bowl on every floor.

2. Use a Pet Water Fountain

Cats especially prefer flowing water. A good fountain can increase a cat's water intake by 20–40%. Recommended brands available in New Zealand include:

  • Catit Flower Fountain — affordable, quiet, good for cats
  • PetSafe Drinkwell — larger capacity, suitable for dogs and multi-pet homes
  • Dogit Fresh & Clear — large capacity for big dogs

Expect to spend $40–$100 on a quality fountain. Replace filters every 2–4 weeks and clean the unit weekly.

3. Upgrade Your Bowls

For cats, use wide, shallow bowls to prevent whisker fatigue. Ceramic or stainless steel is better than plastic, which can harbour bacteria and cause feline acne. For dogs, a heavy-bottomed bowl prevents tipping.

4. Add Water to Food

This is the easiest hydration hack. Add warm water to dry kibble (let it soak for 5 minutes) or switch to wet food for at least one meal per day. For cats, this single change can dramatically improve overall hydration and reduce urinary tract issues.

5. Ice Cubes and Frozen Treats

In summer, freeze low-sodium chicken broth in ice cube trays for dogs, or freeze diluted tuna water for cats. These provide both hydration and enrichment. Never use broth with onion or garlic, which are toxic to both species.

6. Keep Water Clean and Fresh

Wash water bowls daily with hot, soapy water. Refill with fresh water at least twice a day. Cats will refuse water that has been sitting for hours. A dirty bowl is one of the top reasons pets drink less than they should.

7. Monitor Intake

Measure how much water you put in your pet's bowl each morning and check how much is left at night. Sudden increases in water consumption can indicate diabetes, kidney disease, or Cushing's disease — all worth a vet visit.

Hydration for Puppies and Kittens

Young animals are more susceptible to dehydration than adults. Puppies and kittens have a higher body water percentage (about 80% vs 60% in adults) and smaller reserves to draw from.

  • Puppies need roughly 1 ml per gram of body weight per day (roughly 100 ml per 100 g of body weight for very young pups)
  • Kittens on wet food may get most of their water from food, but should always have fresh water available from 4 weeks of age
  • Both puppies and kittens should be monitored closely during hot weather — they dehydrate faster than adults
Pet sitter tip: If you have a puppy or kitten, include a specific note in your sitter instructions about how much water the animal should be drinking each day. Young animals cannot regulate their intake as well as adults, and a sitter may not notice a gradual decrease in drinking.

Senior Pet Hydration

Older pets face increased dehydration risk from:

  • Kidney disease — extremely common in senior cats, causing increased urination and water loss
  • Cognitive decline — senior pets may forget to drink or be unable to find their water bowl
  • Arthritis — pain may discourage trips to the water bowl, especially if it is on a different floor
  • Medication side effects — many common senior pet medications increase thirst or urination

For senior pets, place water bowls in every room they frequent, consider a fountain, and ask your vet about adding wet food or water supplements to their diet.

Hydration and Pet Sitters: A Critical Handover Point

When you leave your pet with a sitter, hydration management is one of the most important things to communicate clearly. Here is what to include in your sitter instructions:

  • Where every water bowl and fountain is located
  • How often to refill and clean each bowl
  • Your pet's approximate daily water intake
  • Any hydration strategies you use (water in food, fountains, ice treats)
  • Signs of dehydration to watch for
  • Vet contact details and written authorisation for emergency treatment
  • Whether your pet has any conditions that affect hydration (kidney disease, diabetes, medication)
Special note for cats: Cats are masters at hiding dehydration. A sitter who does not know your cat may not realise that a cat is drinking less than normal. If your cat is on a hydration protocol (wet food, fountain, water bowls in multiple rooms), make sure your sitter understands why it matters and exactly what to do.

Water Safety for NZ Pets

Beyond drinking water, New Zealand pets interact with water environments that carry specific risks:

Beaches

  • Salt water causes dehydration and gastrointestinal upset — always bring fresh water
  • Wash salt off your dog's coat after beach visits to prevent skin irritation
  • Watch for sharp shells, sea urchins, and jellyfish in shallow water
  • Be aware of rip currents — even strong-swimming dogs can get into trouble

Rivers and Lakes

  • Check for blue-green algae warnings before allowing swimming or drinking
  • Avoid stagnant water, especially in warm weather
  • River edges can be slippery and steep — dogs may struggle to get out

Pools and Spas

  • Not all dogs can swim — brachycephalic breeds (Pugs, French Bulldogs, Bulldogs) are especially at risk
  • Pool chemicals can irritate skin and eyes
  • Always supervise pets around pools; ensure there is a ramp or steps for easy exit
  • Never let pets drink pool or spa water (chlorine and salt levels are too high)

Frequently Asked Questions

Can my dog drink too much water?

Yes. Water intoxication (hyponatremia) is rare but dangerous, usually caused by a dog swallowing large amounts of water while swimming or playing with a hose. Symptoms include bloating, lethargy, pale gums, and loss of coordination. Breeds that love water (Labradors, Retrievers) are most at risk. Limit hose play and monitor swimming duration.

Should I add electrolytes to my pet's water?

Generally, no. Plain fresh water is all most pets need. Electrolyte supplements are only appropriate for dogs experiencing significant fluid loss from vomiting, diarrhoea, or extreme exercise — and only under veterinary guidance. Human sports drinks are NOT safe for pets due to sugar and artificial sweetener content.

My cat only drinks from the tap. Is that okay?

It is common but not ideal. Cats that only drink from dripping taps may not get enough water when no one is home. A pet water fountain provides the same running-water appeal without requiring you to leave the tap running — which wastes water and increases your bill.

How do I get my pet to drink more water when it is hot?

Try these: add water to food, offer ice cubes (or frozen broth cubes for dogs), use a fountain, place bowls in multiple cool locations, flavour water with a small amount of low-sodium chicken broth, and always offer water after exercise before allowing your pet to settle down.

What should I tell my pet sitter about hydration?

Be specific. Tell them where the water bowls are, how much your pet typically drinks, any medical conditions that affect hydration, and what to do if they notice decreased water intake. Leave your vet's number and authorisation for emergency treatment.
