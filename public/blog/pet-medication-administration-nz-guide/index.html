How to Give Your Pet Medicine at Home: A Practical NZ Guide

Anyone who has ever tried to push a pill down a cat's throat or coax a dog into swallowing a bitter liquid knows that administering pet medication can test the patience of even the most devoted owner. Yet medication compliance is one of the most important factors in whether your pet recovers from illness, manages a chronic condition, or stays comfortable in their senior years. Missing doses, incorrect dosing, or giving up entirely because it feels impossible can have real health consequences. This guide walks through every common medication format, species-specific strategies, and the practical tips that make the difference between a daily battle and a manageable routine.

Why Medication Compliance Matters

Veterinarians across New Zealand consistently report that medication non-compliance is one of the biggest barriers to successful treatment outcomes. A course of antibiotics that gets abandoned halfway through because the dog keeps spitting out the tablets can lead to resistant infections. A cat with hyperthyroidism that misses daily methimazole doses may continue losing weight and developing heart complications. Chronic pain medications skipped because administration is too stressful leave pets suffering unnecessarily.

The good news is that nearly every medication administration challenge has a workaround. The key is understanding why your pet resists, choosing the right technique for the right drug format, and building a consistent routine rather than relying on improvisation each time.

Oral Medications: Pills and Tablets

Dogs

Dogs are generally easier to medicate than cats, but that does not mean they cooperate willingly. The most common mistake owners make is trying to force a pill into a dog's mouth while the dog is already anxious. If your first attempt results in the dog clamping their jaw shut, backing away, or running to another room, you have already created a negative association that will make every subsequent attempt harder.

The hide-in-food method works for most dogs and should always be your first strategy. Soft foods are ideal because they mask the texture and can be shaped around the pill. Options available in any New Zealand supermarket include:

  • Canned dog food rolled into small balls around the pill
  • Soft cheese like tasty or camembert wrapped around the tablet
  • Peanut butter (xylitol-free; check the label as some brands contain it) smeared on a piece of bread with the pill hidden inside
  • Commercial pill pockets, available from vet clinics and pet stores throughout NZ
  • Small pieces of cooked chicken, sausage, or liver wrapped around the medication

The critical detail with food hiding is to offer the medicated treat alongside non-medicated treats. Give your dog two or three plain treats first, then the one with the pill, then another plain one immediately after. This sequence creates momentum. Your dog is already chewing and swallowing by the time the medicated treat arrives, and the follow-up treat ensures they swallow rather than pausing to examine what they just ate.

If your dog detects the pill and spits it out, try a different food. Some dogs are surprisingly skilled at eating the cheese and leaving the tablet on the floor. Switching to a stronger-smelling food like sardines or liver can overcome this. Crushing the tablet and mixing it thoroughly into a small portion of wet food is another option, but only if your vet confirms the medication is safe to crush. Some tablets have enteric coatings or are extended-release formulations that must remain intact.

Direct administration is the fallback when food hiding fails. Tilt your dog's head back gently, place the pill as far back on the tongue as possible, hold the mouth closed, and stroke the throat in a downward motion to encourage swallowing. A small syringe of water flushed into the side of the mouth after placing the pill helps ensure it goes down rather than lodging in the oesophagus.

Cats

Cats are the ultimate medication challenge. They are faster, more flexible, more suspicious, and far more willing to inflict damage on anyone trying to force something into their mouth. Food hiding works for some cats, but many will eat around a pill or simply refuse the food if they detect anything unusual.

For cats that accept food hiding, the same principle applies as with dogs: use strong-smelling, highly palatable food. Options that work well for NZ cats include:

  • Tinned tuna or salmon with a small amount of the liquid to mask the smell
  • Commercial cat pill pockets (available from most NZ vet clinics)
  • Small amounts of premium wet cat food like Whiskas or Fancy Feast
  • Butter or cream cheese smeared on the pill to make it slippery and palatable

When food hiding fails, you need a calm, efficient technique for direct administration. The towel wrap method is the gold standard for cats. Wrap your cat snugly in a thick towel like a burrito, leaving only their head exposed. This restrains their claws and reduces their ability to back away. Sit behind your cat so they cannot reverse away from you. Use one hand to grip the top of their skull and tilt the head back. With your other hand, open the lower jaw and place the pill as far back on the tongue as possible. Close the mouth and gently stroke the throat until you feel or see your cat swallow.

Pill guns, also called pill poppers or pill dispensers, are inexpensive tools available from any NZ vet clinic or pet supply store. They allow you to place the pill at the back of the throat without putting your fingers in your cat's mouth. For cats that bite or resist aggressively, a pill gun is not just convenient but essential for safety.

Pro tip: Always follow a pill with a small amount of water given via syringe. Tablets and capsules that lodge in a cat's oesophagus can cause severe inflammation and strictures. A 3-5 ml syringe of water flushed gently into the side of the mouth after each pill prevents this.

Liquid Medications

Liquid medications are often prescribed for cats and small dogs because they are easier to dose precisely and can be mixed with food. However, many liquid medications taste bitter, and a cat that gets a mouthful of foul-tasting liquid will develop a powerful aversion to the process.

The most effective technique for liquid medication is to insert the syringe tip into the side of the mouth, between the cheek and the teeth, and dispense slowly. Never squirt the entire dose at once. Give small amounts at a time and allow your pet to swallow between squirts. Rushing causes gagging, spitting, and aspiration, where medication enters the lungs instead of the stomach.

Mixing liquid medication with a small amount of strong-flavoured food works for some pets. The important rule is to use only a small portion so you can confirm the entire dose is consumed. If you mix the medication into a full bowl of food and your pet only eats half, you have no way of knowing how much medication they actually received. Use a tablespoon of food, mix the medication thoroughly, and watch your pet eat the entire portion before offering the rest of their meal.

Ask your vet about flavouring options. Many NZ vet clinics can compound medications with pet-friendly flavours like chicken, fish, or beef. This service adds a small cost but can transform a daily struggle into something your pet actually accepts willingly.

Eye Drops and Eye Ointments

Eye medications are common for conditions like conjunctivitis, corneal ulcers, and dry eye. They require a steady hand and a calm pet, which is easier said than done when your dog or cat sees you approaching with a bottle and starts backing away.

Position your pet with their back against a wall or corner so they cannot retreat. For dogs, gently place one hand on top of the head and use your thumb to pull the upper eyelid up. With your other hand, apply the drop or ointment strip to the lower conjunctival sac by pulling the lower eyelid down slightly. Avoid touching the tip of the bottle or tube to the eye itself, as this contaminates the medication and can cause injury.

For cats, the towel wrap is again your best friend. Wrap them securely, have someone hold them if possible, and use the same technique. Cats with eye conditions are often in pain and light-sensitive, so work in a dim room if possible to reduce their discomfort and squinting.

After applying eye drops, hold the eyelids closed gently for a few seconds to allow the medication to spread across the surface of the eye rather than blinking it out immediately. If you are applying both drops and ointment, always apply drops first and wait at least five minutes before applying the ointment, which forms a barrier that prevents the drops from being absorbed if applied in the wrong order.

Ear Medications

Ear medications are prescribed for infections, mites, and inflammatory conditions. The biggest challenge is not applying the medication but doing so without causing pain. Infected ears are painful, and pets will pull away, shake their head, or snap if the application hurts.

Warm the medication bottle in your hands for a few minutes before applying. Cold ear drops hitting an inflamed ear canal causes a startling, unpleasant sensation that makes pets resistant to future applications. Gently pull the ear flap upward and outward to straighten the ear canal. Apply the prescribed number of drops or amount of ointment. Massage the base of the ear gently; you should hear a squelching sound that tells you the medication has reached deep into the canal. Let your pet shake their head afterward; this is normal and helps distribute the medication.

Always clean the ear before applying medication if your vet has recommended cleaning. Debris and discharge prevent the medication from reaching the skin of the ear canal. Use only the cleaning solution recommended by your vet, never water, hydrogen peroxide, or cotton buds inserted into the canal.

Subcutaneous Injections

Some pets require subcutaneous (under the skin) injections, most commonly for conditions like diabetes requiring daily insulin, or chronic kidney disease requiring regular fluid therapy. Many owners are terrified at the prospect of injecting their pet, but the reality is far less dramatic than the fear.

Your vet will teach you the technique before sending you home. Subcutaneous injections go into the loose skin between the shoulder blades or along the back, not into muscle. The needle is tiny, typically 25 or 27 gauge, and most pets barely feel it. The key is confidence and speed. Hesitating while the needle hovers above the skin gives your pet time to notice something is happening and become anxious.

Tent the skin by pinching a fold between your thumb and fingers. Insert the needle firmly into the tented skin at a 45-degree angle. Push the plunger steadily. Withdraw the needle and apply gentle pressure to the site for a few seconds. Praise your pet throughout and follow with a treat to create a positive association.

Important: Never attempt to inject your pet without hands-on training from your veterinarian. Incorrect technique can cause injury, infection, or delivery of medication to the wrong tissue layer. If you are uncomfortable with injections, discuss alternatives with your vet or arrange for a medically trained pet sitter to handle this task.

Getting Your Pet Sitter Up to Speed

If your pet is on medication and you are planning to travel, ensuring your pet sitter can administer it correctly is critical. Do not assume that any pet sitter is comfortable or experienced with medication. This is a specific skill set, and you should confirm it during the booking process.

Prepare a written medication schedule that includes the name of each medication, the dose, the time it must be given, how it is administered, and any special instructions like giving with food or on an empty stomach. Leave this on the kitchen bench or another obvious location, not buried in a drawer.

Demonstrate the administration technique at your meet-and-greet. Have your sitter practice while you watch so you can correct any issues before you leave. This is especially important for injections, eye medications, and any technique that requires specific positioning of your pet.

Leave more medication than you think is needed, plus your vet's contact details and the address and phone number of the nearest after-hours emergency vet clinic. If your sitter drops and loses a pill or your pet spits one out, they need backup supplies rather than having to skip a dose.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Stopping medication early because your pet looks better. Antibiotics must be completed even after symptoms resolve. Stopping early encourages resistant infections that are harder and more expensive to treat.
  • Adjusting doses without veterinary advice. Giving half a pill because your pet seems improved is not the same as a vet adjusting the dose. Call your vet first.
  • Crushing tablets that should not be crushed. Extended-release, enteric-coated, and bitter-tasting tablets often become ineffective or harmful when crushed. Always check with your vet.
  • Using human medication. Paracetamol, ibuprofen, and many other common human medications are toxic to dogs and cats. Never give your pet anything not prescribed by your vet.
  • Storing medication incorrectly. Some medications require refrigeration. Others degrade in heat or sunlight. Check the label and store accordingly, especially during New Zealand summer when indoor temperatures can climb.

Building a Stress-Free Routine

The most effective long-term strategy is making medication time predictable and associated with positive outcomes. Give medication at the same time each day, in the same location, using the same technique. Consistency reduces anxiety because your pet learns what to expect. Always follow medication with something enjoyable: a treat, a walk, play time, or grooming. Over time, your pet may begin to approach you at medication time because they know the reward follows.

For pets with chronic conditions requiring long-term medication, discuss long-acting injectable alternatives with your vet. Some medications, including certain arthritis treatments and hormonal therapies, are available as depot injections that last weeks or months, eliminating the daily struggle entirely. These options are increasingly available in New Zealand and are worth asking about if daily oral medication is making both you and your pet miserable.

Medicating your pet is never fun, but it does not have to be a battle. The right technique, a consistent routine, and a calm approach make the process manageable for both of you.
