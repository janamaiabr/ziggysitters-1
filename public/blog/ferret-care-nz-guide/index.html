Ferret Care Guide NZ: Housing, Diet, Health & Enrichment for Pet Ferrets

Published 18 July 2026 · Exotic Pet Care

Ferrets are ridiculous, affectionate, endlessly curious, and surprisingly high-maintenance for an animal that sleeps 18 hours a day. Those 6 awake hours are chaos: climbing into furniture, stealing keys, hiding socks, and doing the weasel war dance across your lounge floor. They are not starter pets. They are not low-maintenance cage pets. They are intelligent predators that need space, stimulation, specialised vet care, and an owner who understands that a bored ferret is a destructive ferret. This guide covers everything you need to know about keeping ferrets in New Zealand, from the legal requirements to daily care, health problems, and what to tell your pet sitter.

Legal Requirements in New Zealand

Ferrets occupy a unique legal space in New Zealand. They are classified as unwanted organisms under the Biosecurity Act 1993 because feral ferrets (technically feral polecat-ferret hybrids) are significant predators of native wildlife, particularly kiwi, whio, and ground-nesting birds. However, desexed pet ferrets are permitted.

The law: You must desex any pet ferret. It is illegal to keep an entire (undesexed) ferret, import ferrets, or breed them. If you are buying a ferret, ensure it comes already desexed from a reputable source. Breeding or selling entire ferrets is a prosecutable offence under the Biosecurity Act.

Key legal points:

  • All pet ferrets must be desexed (spayed for jills, castrated for hobs).
  • You cannot import ferrets into New Zealand.
  • You cannot breed ferrets, even accidentally.
  • Pet ferrets must be kept in secure enclosures or indoors. They must not be allowed to roam free.
  • Walking a ferret on a harness in a controlled area is generally acceptable, but they must not be released to hunt or roam.
  • Some local councils have additional bylaws about keeping ferrets. Check with your council before getting one.

Bringing a Ferret Home

Ferrets are available from specialist breeders and rescue organisations in New Zealand. Because breeding is illegal, the supply is limited, and you may need to join a waitlist. The NZ Ferret Association and local ferret rescue groups are the best starting points.

What to Look For

  • Age: Kits (baby ferrets) are ready to leave their mother at 8-10 weeks. They should be desexed before you bring them home, or you must arrange desexing immediately.
  • Health signs: Clear eyes, clean ears, glossy coat, healthy weight (you should feel ribs but not see them), no discharge from nose or eyes, active and curious.
  • Temperament: A well-socialised kit will be curious, playful, and willing to approach you. A kit that hisses, bites hard, or cowers has not been properly handled.
  • Vaccination records: Ferrets in NZ should receive canine distemper vaccination. Ask for records.
One or two? Ferrets are highly social. A single ferret needs at least 4 hours of dedicated human interaction daily. Two ferrets keep each other company, play together, and are generally happier. If you work full-time, get two. They should be desexed regardless of sex combination since all pet ferrets must be desexed by law.

Housing and Cage Setup

The Cage

Ferrets are not hamsters. A small cage is not acceptable. The minimum cage size for one or two ferrets is 120cm wide x 60cm deep x 150cm tall, with multiple levels connected by ramps or tubes. Bigger is always better.

Cage requirements:

  • Wire bars: Horizontal bars on at least one side so ferrets can climb. Bar spacing must be 2.5cm or less to prevent escape. Ferrets can squeeze through gaps you would not believe.
  • Levels: At least 3 levels connected by ramps or tubes. Ferrets love vertical space.
  • Solid flooring: Wire floors cause foot injuries. Use solid shelves or cover wire with linoleum, carpet tiles, or fleece.
  • Doors: Large doors for easy cleaning. Ferrets are messy, and you will be cleaning the cage daily.
  • Location: Indoors, away from direct sunlight and draughts. Ferrets overheat easily and are sensitive to temperatures above 30 degrees Celsius.

Free-Roaming Time

Ferrets need at least 3-4 hours of supervised free-roaming time outside the cage daily. This is non-negotiable. A ferret kept in a cage 24/7 will become depressed, destructive, and aggressive.

Ferret-proofing your home:

  • Block gaps: Ferrets fit through gaps as small as 4cm. Check behind appliances, under cabinets, around pipes. Block everything.
  • Secure bins: Ferrets open cupboards, drawers, and bins. Use child locks.
  • Protect cables: Ferrets chew cables. Use cable covers or keep them out of reach.
  • Close appliances: Ferrets climb into washing machines, dryers, and dishwashers. Always check before turning on.
  • Remove small items: Rubber bands, coins, buttons, earplugs. Ferrets swallow things they should not.
  • Block recliners and sofa beds: Ferrets climb inside and get crushed. Block access or remove from ferret areas.
Dangerous items: Ferrets are attracted to rubber, foam, and soft plastics. They chew and swallow them, causing intestinal blockages that require emergency surgery. Remove rubber bands, foam earplugs, styrofoam, soft toy stuffing, and any small rubber items from ferret-accessible areas. This is the number one cause of emergency vet visits for ferrets.

Diet and Nutrition

Ferrets are obligate carnivores. Their digestive system is short and simple, designed to process meat and fat quickly. They cannot digest plant matter, grains, or fibre effectively. Feeding them inappropriate food is not a minor mistake. It causes illness, shortens their life, and is entirely preventable.

What to Feed

EggsOne raw or lightly cooked egg per week. A favourite treat and good source of protein.

What Not to Feed

  • Dog food: Too low in protein and fat, too high in carbohydrates.
  • Cat food with grains: Some grain-free cat foods are acceptable as a supplement, but most standard cat foods have too much plant matter.
  • Fruit and vegetables: Ferrets cannot digest these. They pass through undigested and can cause blockages.
  • Dairy: Ferrets are lactose intolerant. No milk, cheese, or yoghurt.
  • Chocolate, caffeine, alcohol, onions, garlic, grapes: All toxic. Same list as dogs and cats.
  • Sugary treats: Ferrets do not process sugar. It causes insulinoma, a common and life-threatening condition.
  • Bread, cereal, pasta, rice: All carbohydrates that ferrets cannot digest properly.
NZ ferret diet hack: If you cannot find commercial ferret kibble locally, high-protein grain-free kitten food (like Ziwi Peak or K9 Natural) meets the nutritional requirements and is widely available in NZ pet stores. Check the protein is above 35% and fat above 18% before switching.

Feeding Schedule

Ferrets have fast metabolisms and eat small amounts frequently. Leave dry kibble available at all times. Offer raw meat or bones once daily. Fresh water must always be available.

Common Health Problems

Ferrets are prone to several health conditions that every owner should know about. Many are age-related and appear after age 3-4. Having an exotics vet before you need one is essential.

Adrenal Gland Disease

The most common health problem in NZ ferrets over 3 years old. One or both adrenal glands produce excess hormones, causing hair loss (starting at the tail and progressing forward), itchy skin, enlarged vulva in females, difficulty urinating in males (from enlarged prostate), and muscle loss. Treatment options include surgery (adrenalectomy) or hormone implants (deslorelin), which are effective and less invasive.

Insulinoma

Tumours on the pancreas that produce excess insulin, causing low blood sugar. Symptoms include lethargy, wobbling, drooling, staring into space, and in severe cases, seizures and collapse. This is a medical emergency. If your ferret has a low blood sugar episode, rub honey or glucose syrup on their gums and get to a vet immediately. Long-term management involves a high-protein diet and medication.

Sugar kills: Never give ferrets sugary treats, not even as a one-off. Sugar spikes trigger insulin release and accelerate insulinoma. This is not an exaggeration. Ferrets are physiologically vulnerable to this disease.

Lymphoma

Cancer of the lymphatic system. Can affect ferrets of any age. Symptoms include swollen lymph nodes (feel under the jaw, armpits, and groin), weight loss, lethargy, and difficulty breathing. Treatment includes chemotherapy, which some NZ exotics vets offer.

Dental Disease

Ferrets develop tartar and gingivitis, especially on soft food diets. Feeding raw meaty bones 2-3 times per week is the best prevention. If your ferret has bad breath, discoloured teeth, or drools, see a vet for a dental check.

Ear Mites

Common in ferrets. Symptoms include head shaking, scratching at ears, dark discharge, and a distinctive odour. Easily treated with vet-prescribed ear drops.

Intestinal Blockages

Usually from swallowing rubber, foam, or fabric. Symptoms include lethargy, loss of appetite, vomiting, and straining to poo. This is a surgical emergency. If you suspect a blockage, do not wait. Get to a vet immediately.

Finding an Exotics Vet in NZ

Not all vets treat ferrets. Before you get a ferret, find a vet who is experienced with exotic animals. In New Zealand, start with:

  • Auckland: The Vet Hospital (Greenlane), VetCare (Grey Lynn), Unitec Veterinary Hospital
  • Wellington: The Animal Hospital (Island Bay), Wellington Bird and Exotics Vet
  • Christchurch: Southside Vet Clinic, The Vet Centre (Papanui)
  • Other areas: Ask the NZ Ferret Association for recommendations

Enrichment and Play

Ferrets are intelligent, curious, and prone to boredom. A bored ferret is a destructive ferret. Enrichment is not optional. It is a basic welfare requirement.

Physical Enrichment

  • Tubes and tunnels: The number one ferret toy. Dryer vent tubes from Bunnings ($15), flexible fabric tunnels, and PVC pipes. Change the layout regularly to keep it interesting.
  • Dig box: A large plastic container filled with rice, biodegradable packing peanuts, or shredded paper. Ferrets dig, burrow, and tunnel in it for hours.
  • Ball pits: A container filled with plastic ball-pit balls. They dive in, dig, and play hide-and-seek.
  • Paper bags and boxes: Free, disposable, and endlessly entertaining. Ferrets love the crinkle sound and the hiding places.
  • Tunnels under blankets: Lay a blanket on the floor and create tunnels under it. Ferrets will race through them for hours.

Mental Enrichment

  • Forage feeding: Hide kibble around the room or in tubes so your ferret has to hunt for it. This is more natural than bowl feeding.
  • Puzzle toys: Dog puzzle feeders work well for ferrets. Start simple and increase difficulty.
  • Training: Ferrets can learn their name, come when called, sit, spin, and even use a litter tray more reliably. Use high-value treats (raw meat, egg) and short sessions (2-3 minutes).
  • New environments: Rotate which rooms they explore. Set up obstacle courses. Introduce new cardboard boxes and tunnels weekly.

The Weasel War Dance

If your ferret arches its back, puffs its tail, bounces sideways, and makes a dooking sound, this is the weasel war dance. It means they are incredibly happy and want to play. Chase them, play tug, or bounce around with them. This is peak ferret joy.

Understanding Ferret Behaviour

Biting

Kits bite. It is how they explore the world and play with each other. They need to learn bite inhibition, which means you need to teach them that biting humans is not acceptable. When a kit bites too hard, say "no" firmly, stop playing, and walk away. Do not yell, hit, or flick their nose. Consistent redirection works within 2-4 weeks for most kits. Adult ferrets that bite hard are usually scared, in pain, or poorly socialised.

Scent Marking

Ferrets drag their belly on the floor to mark territory with scent glands. This is normal. Desexed ferrets have a milder scent. Do not over-bathe to try to eliminate the smell. Frequent bathing strips natural oils and makes the scent worse as the skin overcompensates.

Sleeping Patterns

Ferrets sleep 16-18 hours per day in bursts. They are crepuscular, meaning they are most active at dawn and dusk. Do not wake a sleeping ferret unless necessary. They sleep very deeply and can appear dead (this alarms new owners). If in doubt, wait 30 seconds. A sleeping ferret will twitch, breathe visibly, and respond to gentle touch.

Dead Sleep

Sometimes ferrets sleep so deeply they appear completely limp and unresponsive. This is normal and called "dead sleep." It can last 10-20 seconds. If your ferret is in a dead sleep, do not panic. Gently touch them or call their name. They will wake up groggy and annoyed.

Grooming and Hygiene

  • Nail trimming: Every 2-3 weeks. Use small animal nail clippers. Cut below the quick (the pink part). If you cut the quick, use styptic powder to stop bleeding. Have someone hold the ferret while you trim. Distraction with a treat on their belly works well.
  • Ear cleaning: Weekly. Use a ferret-safe ear cleaner on a cotton ball. Never insert anything into the ear canal. Check for dark discharge or odour (signs of mites).
  • Bathing: No more than once a month, ideally less. Over-bathing causes dry skin and increased oil production, making the smell worse. Use a ferret-specific or kitten shampoo.
  • Teeth: Check weekly. Raw meaty bones 2-3 times per week keep teeth clean. If tartar builds up, a vet dental may be needed.
  • Coat brushing: Weekly during seasonal coat changes (spring and autumn). Ferrets shed heavily twice a year.
  • Litter tray: Scoop daily, full change every 2-3 days. Ferrets are fastidious about cleanliness and may refuse to use a dirty tray.

What Pet Sitters Need to Know About Ferrets

If you are hiring a pet sitter for your ferrets, these are the non-negotiables they need to understand before you leave.

Essential Instructions for Sitters

  1. Feeding: Show the sitter exactly what to feed, how much, and where the food is stored. Ferrets on raw diets cannot have their food swapped without digestive consequences. Leave a written schedule.
  2. Litter tray: Must be scooped daily. Ferrets will refuse a dirty tray and toilet elsewhere. Show the sitter where the replacement litter is stored.
  3. Free-roaming time: Minimum 3-4 hours per day, supervised. The sitter must be in the room. Ferrets get into trouble fast. Show them the ferret-proofing and which rooms are safe.
  4. Escape risk: Ferrets are escape artists. Doors must be closed firmly. Windows must be screened. Any gap larger than 4cm is an escape route. Emphasise this to the sitter.
  5. Health warning signs: Leave a list of emergency contacts (your vet, a local exotics vet, your phone number). Tell the sitter to call you immediately if the ferret is lethargic, not eating, has diarrhoea, or is straining to urinate.
  6. Do not feed treats: Unless you have left specific, approved treats. Ferrets and inappropriate food do not mix. One wrong treat can trigger insulinoma symptoms.
  7. Biting: Warn the sitter if your ferret is nippy. Show them how to redirect biting to a toy. If the ferret draws blood, clean the wound thoroughly. Ferret bites can be deep and should be washed with soap and water immediately.
  8. Sleeping ferrets look dead: Warn the sitter about dead sleep. It is terrifying the first time you see it. Do not let the sitter panic and rush to the vet for what is actually just a deep sleep.
Ferret sitter checklist: Written feeding schedule, written health warning signs, emergency vet number, approved treats only, daily litter tray cleaning, supervised free roam 3-4 hours, and clear instructions about escape risks. Leave this on the fridge.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do ferrets get along with cats and dogs?

Some do, with careful, supervised introductions. Ferrets and cats can coexist peacefully, though interactions should always be supervised. Dogs are higher risk, especially terrier breeds with strong prey drives. Never leave ferrets and dogs unsupervised. Ferrets and rodents, birds, or rabbits should never interact. Ferrets are predators.

Can I walk my ferret outside?

Yes, with a ferret-specific harness (not a cat or small dog harness, which they slip out of) and in a safe, enclosed area. Avoid areas treated with pesticides, areas with dogs, and long grass (tick risk). Keep walks short (15-20 minutes) and watch for signs of overheating. Never let your ferret off the harness outside.

How do I litter train a ferret?

Place litter trays in corners (ferrets naturally toilet in corners). Put a small amount of soiled litter in the tray so they know it is a toilet. When you see a ferret backing into a corner, gently place them in the tray. Reward with a treat when they use it. Ferrets are not as reliable as cats with litter training, so expect some accidents, especially in new environments.

Why does my ferret smell?

All ferrets have a natural musky scent from skin glands. Desexed ferrets have a much milder scent. The smell gets worse if you over-bathe them (the skin produces more oil to compensate). Clean bedding weekly, scoop the litter tray daily, and bathe no more than once a month. Good ventilation and a clean cage matter more than bathing.

Ferrets are rewarding, hilarious, and demanding pets. They need specialised care, an exotics vet, daily supervised playtime, and an owner who understands that these are intelligent predators, not hamsters. If you can provide that, a ferret will reward you with more personality per kilogram than almost any other pet.
