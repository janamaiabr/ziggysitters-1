Greyhound Care in New Zealand: The Complete Owner's Guide

Greyhounds are one of the most misunderstood dog breeds in New Zealand. Most people see a dog built for speed and assume it needs hours of exercise. The reality? Greyhounds are the laziest athletes on the planet. They sprint for 30 seconds, then sleep for 16 hours. They are the quintessential couch potato in a cheetah costume.

New Zealand has a unique relationship with greyhounds. Our racing industry, while controversial and now winding down, has meant thousands of greyhounds have needed homes. The Greyhound Adoption Programme (GAP) NZ has been rehoming ex-racing greyhounds since 2006, and the demand for adoptable greyhounds has only grown as more Kiwis discover what genuinely lovely pets they make.

This guide covers everything you need to know about living with a greyhound in New Zealand — from adoption and exercise to health, grooming, and finding a pet sitter who understands the breed.

Adopting a Greyhound in NZ

The Greyhound Adoption Programme (GAP NZ)

GAP NZ is the primary pathway for adopting ex-racing greyhounds in New Zealand. Based in Christchurch with satellite operations across the country, GAP takes greyhounds directly from the racing industry and prepares them for life as pets.

The programme includes:

  • Temperament assessment — each dog is evaluated for suitability as a pet, including testing around children, other dogs, and small animals
  • Foster care period — greyhounds spend time in a foster home learning about stairs, glass doors, vacuum cleaners, and all the everyday things they have never encountered
  • Veterinary work — desexing, vaccination, dental work, microchipping, and registration
  • Muzzle training — all ex-racing greyhounds adopted through GAP come with a muzzle, which is a legal requirement in many NZ public spaces

Adoption costs typically range from $350 to $450, which is remarkable value given the veterinary work included.

Important: With the NZ greyhound racing industry set to ban racing by 2026, the number of greyhounds needing homes is expected to increase. If you are considering adoption, now is the time to get on the waitlist. GAP NZ and other rescue organisations like Hound Rescue NZ are your best starting points.

What to Expect From an Ex-Racer

A greyhound coming off the track has lived a highly structured life. They have never seen a staircase. They have never walked on polished floorboards. They have no idea what a glass door is (many walk straight into them). They have never been alone in a house or encountered a television.

This does not mean they are difficult. It means they are blank slates. Most ex-racers adapt within two to four weeks. The key is patience, consistency, and managing your own expectations.

Common adjustment challenges:

  • Stairs — teach them one step at a time, literally. Lead them up and down slowly. Most figure it out in a few days.
  • Glass doors put stickers or tape at eye level until they learn where the glass is
  • Slippery floors — lay down rugs or mats. Greyhounds have very low body fat and no traction on polished concrete or floorboards.
  • Separation anxiety — many ex-racers have never been alone. Build up alone time gradually: five minutes, then ten, then thirty.

Exercise: Less Than You Think

This is the biggest misconception about greyhounds. Yes, they can hit 70km/h. No, they do not want to do it very often.

A healthy adult greyhound needs 20 to 30 minutes of exercise per day. That is it. A walk around the block. A play in the backyard. A sprint at an off-leash area once or twice a week. The rest of the time, they are perfectly happy lying on the couch.

In New Zealand, this makes greyhounds genuinely suitable for apartment living, small sections, and owners who are not particularly athletic themselves. A greyhound in a central Wellington apartment is entirely feasible, provided they get a daily walk.

Off-Leash Considerations in NZ

This is where greyhounds differ significantly from other breeds. A greyhound off-leash is a risk. Their prey drive can kick in without warning — a rabbit, a cat, even a plastic bag blowing across a field — and they will be gone in a flash. Their speed means they can cover 100 metres in under six seconds. You will not catch them.

For this reason:

  • Always keep your greyhound on-leash in unfenced areas
  • Use fully fenced dog parks for off-leash exercise — check the fencing is intact before letting them off
  • Be cautious around small dogs — some greyhounds have a small-animal prey drive that can be triggered by tiny dogs running
  • Muzzle requirements — many NZ councils require greyhounds to be muzzled in public. Check your local council rules. It is not cruel; it is responsible ownership.

Coat and Skin Care

Greyhounds have incredibly thin skin and a short, single coat. This means two things: they are easy to groom, but they are vulnerable to the elements.

Grooming

A weekly brush with a soft bristle brush or a rubber grooming mitt is sufficient. They shed moderately. Their coat is short enough that you do not need professional grooming — ever. No clipping, no stripping, no expensive salon visits.

Skin Tears

This catches new owners off guard. Greyhound skin is paper-thin. A small knock against a coffee table corner can cause a tear that looks dramatic but is usually minor. Keep a basic first- aid kit at home, and do not panic if your greyhound comes back from a run with a small cut. Clean it, apply a pet-safe antiseptic, and monitor. If the wound does not stop bleeding within 10 to 15 minutes or is deep, see your vet.

Cold Weather

New Zealand winters, especially in the South Island and central North Island, are cold for greyhounds. Their single coat and low body fat offer zero insulation. Your greyhound needs:

  • A warm dog coat for any outdoor time below about 10 degrees
  • A draught-free sleeping spot indoors
  • Warm bedding — they love doonas and soft blankets
  • Shorter walks in cold weather and longer warm-up time

If you live in Queenstown, Wanaka, or anywhere that gets frost, your greyhound needs a wardrobe. Seriously. A rain coat, a warm winter coat, and possibly a fleece layer underneath. They will thank you for it.

Health Issues Common in Greyhounds

Greyhounds are generally healthy dogs, but the breed has some specific conditions to be aware of:

Critical vet tip: Greyhounds have different normal blood values than other breeds. Their haematocrit (red blood cell count) is naturally higher, and their platelet count is naturally lower. A vet unfamiliar with greyhounds may misread blood work. Make sure your vet knows the breed, or find one who does. The New Zealand Greyhound Association and GAP NZ can recommend breed-knowledgeable vets.

Feeding Your Greyhound

Greyhounds need a high-quality diet, but they are not big eaters. A typical adult greyhound weighs 25 to 35kg and eats about 250 to 350 grams of food per day, split across two meals.

Key feeding considerations:

  • Feed twice daily to reduce bloat risk. Never feed one large meal.
  • Use a raised bowl — greyhounds are tall, and eating from floor level is uncomfortable and can contribute to bloat
  • Avoid grain-heavy foods — many greyhounds do better on grain-free or low-grain diets. Discuss with your vet.
  • Watch the weight — you should be able to see the last two or three ribs and feel (but not see) the rest. A greyhound at correct weight has a visible hip point and a slight tuck-up at the waist. If you cannot feel the ribs at all, your greyhound is overweight — and that is dangerous for their joints.
  • Ex-racers may have food anxiety — some bolt their food because that was the norm in racing kennels. A slow feeder bowl can help.

Sleeping Habits: The 45-MPH Couch Potato

Greyhounds sleep between 16 and 18 hours per day. Not an exaggeration. They are genuinely that lazy. Your greyhound will find the softest spot in your home — usually the couch, your bed, or a doona they have claimed — and collapse there for hours.

They also have a specific sleeping quirk: sleep startle reflex. When a greyhound is woken suddenly, particularly from a deep sleep, they may snap instinctively before they are fully conscious. This is not aggression — it is a reflex. To manage it:

  • Call your greyhound\'s name before approaching when they are asleep
  • Never startle them awake by touching them
  • Teach children to approach sleeping greyhounds with voice first
  • Give your greyhound a safe, quiet sleeping space where they will not be disturbed

This is important information for pet sitters too. Make sure anyone caring for your greyhound knows about the sleep startle reflex.

Finding the Right Pet Sitter for Your Greyhound

Not every pet sitter is a good fit for a greyhound. The breed has specific needs that a general dog sitter might not anticipate. Here is what to look for:

  1. Breed experience. Has the sitter cared for greyhounds before? Do they know about the sleep startle reflex, the skin tear risk, the muzzle requirements, and the cold sensitivity?
  2. Understanding of prey drive. If your greyhound needs to be muzzled on walks, your sitter needs to be comfortable with that and know how to fit and use a muzzle correctly.
  3. Cat and small-animal awareness. If your greyhound is not cat-safe and your sitter also looks after cats, that is a conflict. Make sure your sitter understands what "not cat-safe" means in practice.
  4. Stair and floor confidence. If your home has stairs or polished floors, your sitter needs to know how to help your greyhound navigate them safely — especially for newly adopted dogs still learning.
  5. Emergency awareness. Your sitter should know the location of the nearest emergency vet, the signs of bloat, and that greyhounds react differently to certain medications.

Living With a Greyhound in NZ: What Makes It Special

Owning a greyhound in New Zealand comes with some unique advantages and considerations:

  • Low exercise needs make them perfect for NZ\'s increasingly compact urban living — small Wellington sections, Auckland apartments, and townhouses are all workable
  • They rarely bark — great for close-quarter living and maintaining neighbour relations
  • They are incredibly affectionate — greyhounds form strong bonds with their owners and often lean against you as a sign of trust
  • The NZ climate is mostly kind — except for South Island winters and high-country areas, most of NZ is mild enough that a coat is only needed for a few months
  • The adoption community is strong — greyhound owners in NZ are a passionate bunch. There are meet-up groups, Facebook communities, and charity events around the country

The biggest challenge? Explaining to people that your greyhound is tired. Again. Still.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are greyhounds good with children?

Generally, yes. Greyhounds are gentle and tolerant. However, because of their thin skin and sleep startle reflex, supervision with young children is essential. Teach kids to call the dog\'s name before approaching and never to disturb a sleeping greyhound.

Do greyhounds need a big backyard?

No. A small courtyard or even no outdoor space at all is fine, provided they get a daily walk and access to a fenced area for a weekly sprint. Many greyhounds in central Wellington and Auckland city live happily without yards.

Why does my greyhound stare at me?

Greyhounds are known for their intense staring. It usually means they want something — food, a walk, attention, or they are just checking in on you. It can be unsettling at first, but most owners come to love it. It is basically a very quiet way of saying "hello, I am here, please acknowledge me."

Can greyhounds swim?

Most greyhounds are not natural swimmers. Their low body fat and deep chest make buoyancy difficult. Some will paddle in shallow water, but do not assume your greyhound can swim. If you want them near water, use a dog life jacket. Never throw a greyhound into deep water.

How long do greyhounds live?

The average lifespan is 10 to 14 years. With good care, regular vet visits, and a healthy diet, many NZ greyhounds live well into their teens. They are a relatively long-lived breed for their size.
