Cat Vomiting & Diarrhoea in NZ: The Complete Guide to Causes, Treatment, and When to See a Vet

If your cat has thrown up or has runny stools, you are not alone. Vomiting and diarrhoea are among the most common reasons NZ cat owners contact their vet. Most cases are mild and resolve within 24 to 48 hours, but some are emergencies. This guide helps you tell the difference, treat mild cases at home, and know exactly when to seek professional help. It also covers what pet sitters should know if a cat falls ill while you are away.

Emergency Warning: Take your cat to the vet IMMEDIATELY if you see any of these signs: blood in vomit or stool, repeated vomiting (more than 3 times in a few hours), extreme lethargy, a hard or swollen abdomen, or if your cat has not eaten or drunk anything for over 24 hours. Do not wait. Find your nearest emergency vet clinic in NZ.

Common Causes of Cat Vomiting

Not all vomiting is the same. A single episode of bringing up a hairball is very different from persistent vomiting. Here are the most common causes in New Zealand cats:

Hairballs

The most common and least concerning cause. Cats groom themselves and ingest loose hair, which forms a wad in the stomach. When the wad gets too big to pass into the intestines, the cat vomits it up. You will usually see a cylindrical mass of hair. Occasional hairballs (once every week or two) are normal for long-haired breeds and during shedding season. If your cat is producing hairballs daily or vomiting food alongside hairballs, something else may be going on.

Dietary Indiscretion

Cats are curious eaters. They may raid the rubbish bin, eat something spoiled, or snack on a houseplant. In New Zealand, common culprits include:

  • Human food scraps: Dairy products (most cats are lactose intolerant), fatty trimmings, onions, garlic, and chocolate are all toxic
  • Houseplants: Lily species are extremely toxic to cats and can cause fatal kidney failure. Check our NZ toxic plants guide for a full list
  • Grass: Eating grass is normal cat behaviour and usually causes harmless vomiting. However, if grass-eating is accompanied by persistent vomiting or diarrhoea, it may indicate gastrointestinal irritation
  • String, wool, or thread: Extremely dangerous — linear foreign bodies can slice through the intestines. If you suspect your cat has swallowed string, go to the vet immediately. Do NOT pull on anything hanging from the mouth or rear end

Sudden Diet Change

Cats have sensitive digestive systems. Switching food brands or flavours too quickly is one of the most common causes of vomiting and diarrhoea. The rule of thumb is to transition over 7 to 10 days: start with 75% old food and 25% new food, then gradually shift the ratio. A sudden switch — even to a high-quality food — can cause several days of gastrointestinal upset.

Infections and Parasites

New Zealand cats can pick up several gut infections:

  • Roundworms and tapeworms: Common in outdoor cats. Regular deworming (every 3 months for outdoor cats, every 6 months for indoor cats) prevents most issues
  • Giardia: A protozoan parasite that causes foul-smelling diarrhoea. More common in multi-cat households and shelters. Requires veterinary treatment with fenbendazole or metronidazole
  • Feline panleukopenia: Rare in vaccinated cats but serious. Causes severe vomiting and bloody diarrhoea. Vaccination is critical — this is part of the standard NZ cat vaccination schedule
  • Salmonella and Campylobacter: More common in cats fed raw diets. If you feed raw, source from reputable NZ suppliers and handle with the same care as human food

Stress and Anxiety

Cats are notoriously stress-sensitive. Vomiting and diarrhoea are common physical responses to:

  • Moving house (one of the top triggers — see our moving with pets guide)
  • A new pet in the household
  • Building work or loud noises
  • Being left alone for extended periods (see our home alone guide)
  • Changes in routine — a different feeding schedule or a new person in the house

If your cat has vomiting or diarrhoea that coincides with a stressful event and no other symptoms (normal appetite, normal energy), stress is the likely culprit. See our full guide on understanding pet anxiety in NZ.

Chronic Conditions

If your cat vomits regularly (more than once a week over several weeks), it may indicate an underlying condition:

  • Kidney disease: Very common in older cats in NZ. Chronic vomiting, weight loss, and increased thirst are red flags. See our senior cat care guide
  • Hyperthyroidism: Another common condition in cats over 8. Weight loss despite a good appetite, plus vomiting, is the classic presentation
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD): Chronic vomiting and diarrhoea that comes and goes. Requires veterinary diagnosis, usually via biopsy
  • Dental disease: Bad teeth can cause cats to swallow food without chewing, leading to vomiting. Check our pet dental care guide

Common Causes of Cat Diarrhoea

Many of the same causes of vomiting also cause diarrhoea. Some additional triggers:

Food Intolerances

Some cats cannot tolerate certain ingredients. Common culprits include dairy (lactose), beef, fish, and artificial additives. If your cat has recurrent soft stools, your vet may recommend an elimination diet to identify the trigger.

Stress Colitis

Stress can inflame the large intestine, leading to mucous-covered or bloody diarrhoea. This is especially common when cats are boarded, moved, or have their routine disrupted. A pet sitter staying in your home causes far less stress than boarding, which is one reason in-home care is preferable for anxious cats.

Antibiotic-Associated Diarrhoea

If your cat has recently been on antibiotics, the medication may have killed off beneficial gut bacteria. Probiotics designed for cats (available from NZ vets and pet stores) can help restore gut flora.

Foreign Body Ingestion

Cats, especially kittens, may swallow objects that partially block the intestine — elastic bands, hair ties, bits of string, tinsel at Christmas, and hair accessories are common in NZ vet clinics. Partial blockages can cause chronic diarrhoea with occasional vomiting. This is a surgical emergency if the object does not pass.

Home Treatment: What You Can Do

Important: Home treatment is only appropriate for mild cases in otherwise healthy adult cats. Kittens, senior cats, and cats with existing health conditions should see a vet at the first sign of vomiting or diarrhoea. These animals dehydrate quickly.

Step 1: Withhold Food (Short Fast)

For adult cats with a single episode of vomiting or mild diarrhoea, withhold food for 12 to 24 hours. This gives the gastrointestinal tract time to rest and reset. Do NOT withhold water — dehydration is more dangerous than the original problem. Always ensure fresh water is available.

Step 2: Reintroduce Bland Food

After the fast, offer small amounts of bland, easily digestible food:

  • Boiled chicken (no skin, no bones, no seasoning): The gold standard for upset cat stomachs in NZ
  • Boiled white rice mixed with chicken: A small amount of rice provides fibre and helps firm stools
  • Cooked pumpkin (plain, not pie filling): Available in cans at NZ supermarkets. One teaspoon mixed with chicken can help both diarrhoea and constipation due to its fibre content
  • Prescription gastrointestinal food: If your vet has recommended a specific GI diet (such as Royal Canin Gastrointestinal or Hill's i/d), use that

Feed small meals — about a quarter of the normal portion — every 4 to 6 hours for the first day. If the cat keeps food down and stools start to firm up, gradually transition back to normal food over 3 to 5 days.

Step 3: Monitor Hydration

Dehydration is the most dangerous consequence of vomiting and diarrhoea. Check your cat's hydration status:

  • Skin tent test: Gently pinch the skin at the back of the neck. It should snap back immediately. If it takes more than 1 to 2 seconds to return to position, your cat is dehydrated
  • Gum check: Gums should be pink and moist. Dry, tacky, or pale gums indicate dehydration
  • Eyes: Sunken eyes are a sign of severe dehydration — this is an emergency
Encouraging Drinking: Cats are notoriously bad drinkers. Try these: place multiple water bowls around the house, use a pet water fountain (cats prefer running water), add a small amount of tuna water to the water bowl, or offer low-sodium chicken broth (no onion or garlic). Some NZ pet stores stock cat-specific rehydration solutions.

Step 4: Probiotics

Cat-specific probiotics can help restore healthy gut bacteria, especially after a bout of diarrhoea or following antibiotic treatment. Available from NZ vets and pet stores, look for products containing Enterococcus faecium or similar beneficial strains. Do not use human probiotics — they are formulated for human gut flora, not feline.

When to See a Vet

Knowing when to wait and when to act is critical. Here is a clear decision framework:

Situation Action Urgency
Single vomit, no other symptoms, cat acting normal Monitor at home for 24 hours Low
2 to 3 episodes of vomiting over 24 hours, cat still eating and drinking Withhold food for 12 hours, then bland diet. See vet if it continues Low — Moderate
Diarrhoea lasting more than 48 hours without other symptoms Vet visit within 24 hours Moderate
Vomiting and diarrhoea together Vet visit same day Moderate — High
Vomiting more than 3 times in a few hours See vet immediately High
Blood in vomit (red or looks like coffee grounds) See vet immediately Emergency
Blood in stool (red streaks or black, tarry stools) See vet immediately Emergency
Lethargy, hiding, or not responding to you See vet immediately Emergency
Swollen or hard abdomen, painful when touched See vet immediately — possible blockage Emergency
Not eating or drinking for more than 24 hours See vet same day High
Vomiting in a kitten, senior cat, or cat with known health issues See vet promptly — do not wait High

Special Cases That Require Faster Action

  • Kittens under 6 months: Dehydrate rapidly. Do not fast kittens. See a vet within hours of persistent vomiting or diarrhoea
  • Senior cats (over 10 years): Vomiting and diarrhoea may be the first sign of kidney disease, hyperthyroidism, or cancer. Do not dismiss these symptoms in older cats
  • Cats on medication: Some medications cause gastrointestinal side effects. Do not stop medication without consulting your vet
  • Potential toxin ingestion: If you suspect your cat has eaten a lily, rat bait, human medication, or cleaning product, go to the vet immediately. Do not wait for symptoms to appear

What Pet Sitters Need to Know

If you are leaving your cat with a pet sitter, gastrointestinal issues are one of the most common problems that arise. Here is what both owners and sitters should prepare:

For Owners: Before You Leave

  • Leave a care sheet: Include your cat's normal feeding schedule, food brand and quantity, any known sensitivities, and what constitutes "normal" stool for your cat. Use our pet care sheet guide to create one
  • Vet authorisation: Leave a signed letter authorising the sitter to take your cat to the vet, plus your vet's name, phone number, and address. Include your credit card details or a pre-arranged payment plan with your vet
  • Emergency contacts: Provide the sitter with two emergency contacts — your vet and a backup (a trusted friend or family member who can make decisions if you are unreachable)
  • Supply kit: Leave extra food, a small supply of bland diet ingredients (boiled chicken portion, plain pumpkin), and any medications your cat is currently taking

For Pet Sitters: If the Cat Gets Sick

  • Document everything: Note the time, appearance, and frequency of any vomit or diarrhoea. Take photos if the owner asks — it helps the vet diagnose
  • Do not fast the cat without permission: Only withhold food if the owner has explicitly authorised it. Kittens and senior cats should never be fasted without veterinary guidance
  • Contact the owner: Notify the owner at the first sign of illness. Even if it seems minor, the owner needs to know
  • Call the vet if in doubt: Most NZ vet clinics offer free phone advice. If you are unsure whether the situation is urgent, call and describe the symptoms. They will tell you whether to come in
  • Watch for dehydration: Check the skin tent and gum moisture every few hours. If you notice dehydration developing, take the cat to the vet — do not wait for the owner to respond

Prevention: Keeping Your Cat's Gut Healthy

Prevention is always better than treatment. Here are the key steps for NZ cat owners:

Diet

  • Feed a consistent, high-quality diet. If you change food, transition over 7 to 10 days
  • Avoid dairy — most cats are lactose intolerant, despite the stereotype
  • If feeding raw, source from reputable NZ suppliers and follow strict hygiene. Consider a probiotic supplement
  • Ensure fresh water is always available. Consider a water fountain if your cat does not drink enough

Parasite Prevention

  • Deworm every 3 months for outdoor cats, every 6 months for indoor cats
  • Use a quality flea treatment monthly — fleas carry tapeworm eggs
  • Check our cat parasite prevention guide for product recommendations available in NZ

Household Safety

  • Remove or secure toxic plants — especially lilies, which are fatal to cats even in small amounts. See our NZ toxic plants guide
  • Keep string, rubber bands, hair ties, and small objects out of reach — these are the most common foreign bodies in NZ cat emergency surgeries
  • Secure rubbish bins with lids — especially kitchen waste that may contain bones, onion, or garlic
  • Store cleaning products safely. See our pet-safe cleaning products guide

Stress Management

  • Maintain a consistent routine for feeding, play, and sleep
  • Introduce changes gradually — new pets, new people, or new environments should be introduced slowly
  • Use Feliway diffusers in multi-cat households or during stressful events (moving, renovations, new baby)
  • Ensure your cat has a safe retreat space — a high shelf, a covered bed, or a room they can escape to

Regular Vet Check-ups

Annual vet visits (twice yearly for cats over 10) catch problems early. Blood work can detect kidney disease and hyperthyroidism months before symptoms appear. Early detection means simpler, cheaper treatment and a better quality of life for your cat. Check our vaccination schedule to stay on track.

The Bottom Line

Most episodes of cat vomiting and diarrhoea in New Zealand are mild and self-limiting. A short fast followed by bland food, plenty of water, and close monitoring resolves the majority of cases within 48 hours. But knowing the red flags — blood, repeated vomiting, lethargy, or a cat that stops eating and drinking — is what separates a manageable stomach upset from a medical emergency.

If you are going away, prepare your pet sitter with a detailed care sheet, vet authorisation, and emergency contacts. Cats are masters at hiding illness, so a sitter who knows what to look for is invaluable.
