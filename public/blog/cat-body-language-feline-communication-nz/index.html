Cat Body Language: Understanding What Your Cat Is Telling You

2026-05-04 · 6 min read · ZiggySitters Team

Cats are famously subtle communicators. Unlike dogs, who wag their tails with obvious enthusiasm, cats use a complex system of ear positions, tail movements, eye signals, and vocalisations to express how they feel. For New Zealand cat owners — and there are plenty of us, with an estimated 1.2 million pet cats across Aotearoa — learning to read feline body language is one of the most valuable skills you can develop. It deepens the bond with your cat, helps you spot early signs of stress or illness, and makes life easier for anyone caring for your cat when you are away.

The Tail: Your Cat's Most Expressive Feature

A cat's tail is arguably the most readable part of their body language vocabulary. Here are the key positions and what they mean:

Tail held high and upright: Your cat is confident, content, and pleased to see you. A slight curve at the tip is an especially friendly greeting — the feline equivalent of a wave.

Tail tucked between the legs: Fear, anxiety, or submission. Your cat feels threatened or insecure. This is common when a cat is introduced to a new environment, such as a holiday home or an unfamiliar pet sitter's visit.

Tail swishing slowly from side to side: Your cat is focused and potentially overstimulated. This is not the same as a dog's wag — it often signals that your cat would prefer to be left alone. If the swishing becomes rapid or thumping, back off.

Puffed-up tail (bottlebrush): Your cat is startled or frightened. The fur stands on end to make the cat appear larger to potential threats. This is a clear stress signal — give your cat space and time to calm down.

Tail curled gently around your leg or another cat: Affection and trust. This is a warm, affiliative gesture that signals comfort and companionship.

Ears Forward, Ears Back: Reading Feline Ear Positions

Cat ears are remarkably mobile — they have over 30 muscles controlling each ear — and their position tells you a great deal about your cat's emotional state:

Ears facing forward and slightly upright: Alert, curious, and engaged. Your cat is interested in whatever is in front of them, whether it is a toy, a bird outside the window, or you walking through the door.

Ears turned to the side or slightly back: Your cat is feeling irritated, anxious, or overstimulated. This is an early warning sign that your cat may be approaching their threshold. Respect the signal and reduce interaction.

Ears flattened to the sides (airplane ears): Significant anxiety or annoyance. Your cat is uncomfortable and may lash out if pushed further. The SPCA NZ recommends giving cats a safe, quiet retreat space when they display this behaviour.

Ears pinned flat against the head: Extreme fear or defensive aggression. Your cat is preparing to defend itself. Do not attempt to pick up or restrain a cat in this state — provide an escape route instead.

Eye Signals: Slow Blinks, Dilated Pupils, and Stares

A cat's eyes communicate volumes, and understanding these signals can transform your relationship with your feline companion:

Slow blinking: This is one of the most important signals in cat body language. When a cat looks at you and slowly closes and opens their eyes, they are showing trust and affection. Research published in Scientific Reports (2020) confirmed that cats are more likely to approach humans who slow-blink at them. Try slow-blinking back — it is the closest thing to a cat saying "I love you."

Half-closed eyes: Relaxation and contentment. Your cat feels safe enough to let their guard down. You will often see this when a cat is sitting in a sunbeam on a warm New Zealand afternoon.

Dilated pupils (large, dark eyes): This can indicate excitement, playfulness, or fear. Context matters. If your cat is chasing a toy, dilated pupils mean they are in play mode. If they are hiding or crouching, dilated pupils signal fear or overstimulation.

Constricted pupils (narrow slits): Tension or potential aggression. Combined with a hard stare, this is a challenge or threat. Never stare directly back at a cat showing this signal — look away slowly to de-escalate.

Unblinking stare: In cat culture, a direct stare is confrontational. If your cat is staring unblinkingly at another animal or person, they are asserting dominance or feeling threatened. Breaking eye contact slowly signals that you are not a threat.

Purring: Not Always What You Think

Most people assume purring always means a cat is happy. While that is frequently true, the reality is more nuanced:

Contentment purring: A relaxed body, closed or half-closed eyes, and a soft, rhythmic purr typically indicate your cat is content. This is the purr most owners know and love.

Solicitation purring: Cats have a specific purr that combines a standard purr with a high-frequency cry (similar to a human baby's cry). This "solicitation purr" is designed to get your attention and is most commonly heard at feeding time. Studies have shown that humans find this particular purr difficult to ignore.

Stress or pain purring: Cats also purr when they are in pain, frightened, or unwell. Purring releases endorphins and can be a self-soothing mechanism. If your cat is purring but also hiding, not eating, or showing other signs of distress, consult your vet. The NZ Veterinary Association recommends that any sudden change in purring behaviour warrants a check-up.

Body Posture: The Big Picture

Reading your cat's overall posture ties all the individual signals together:

Relaxed, loose body: Your cat is comfortable. They may be lying on their side with exposed belly (a sign of trust, not necessarily an invitation for belly rubs), sitting with a soft posture, or sprawling in a patch of sun.

Arched back with fur raised: Startled or defensive. Your cat is trying to look bigger. This is the classic Halloween cat silhouette — back off and let them decompress.

Crouched low to the ground: Anxiety or fear. Your cat is trying to make themselves small and less visible. This posture is common when a cat is in a new environment or around unfamiliar people.

Rubbing against your legs or furniture: Scent marking and affection. Cats have scent glands on their cheeks, forehead, and flanks. When they rub against you, they are claiming you as part of their territory — and showing they like you.

Kneading (making biscuits): A behaviour from kittenhood that signals comfort and contentment. If your cat kneads your lap, consider it a high compliment.

Whisker Positions: Subtle but Telling

Whiskers are not just for measuring gaps — they are emotional indicators too:

Whiskers relaxed and fanned out: Your cat is calm and content.

Whiskers pushed forward: Curiosity or excitement. Your cat is interested in something — perhaps a treat, a toy, or a bird on the feeder.

Whiskers pulled back flat against the face: Fear, anxiety, or defensive tension. This is a subtle but reliable stress indicator that often accompanies flattened ears and a tucked tail.

Vocalisations: More Than Just Meowing

Cats have a surprisingly wide vocal range, and each sound carries meaning:

Short, high-pitched meow: A greeting or request for attention. Cats rarely meow at other adult cats — this sound is reserved almost exclusively for humans.

Long, drawn-out meow: A demand. Your cat wants something specific — food, an open door, or playtime.

Chirping or trilling: Excitement, often triggered by seeing prey (like a bird or insect) that they cannot reach. Many New Zealand indoor cats chirp at the sparrows and waxeyes outside the window.

Hissing or growling: Clear warnings. Your cat feels threatened and is asking you — or whatever is scaring them — to back off. Never punish a cat for hissing; it is communication, not misbehaviour.

Yowling: A prolonged, mournful cry that can indicate distress, pain, or (in unsterilised cats) mating behaviour. If your cat is yowling and is spayed or neutered, a vet visit is warranted. The SPCA NZ strongly advocates for desexing to reduce yowling and wandering behaviour.

Why Cat Body Language Matters for Pet Sitters

Understanding cat body language is not just an academic exercise — it has real practical value, especially when someone else is caring for your cat. Pet sitters who can read feline signals provide safer, more comfortable care. A sitter who recognises the difference between a slow blink (trust) and a hard stare (tension) will know whether to approach or give space. They will notice when a cat is hiding due to normal shyness versus showing signs of illness or distress.

This is particularly important in New Zealand, where many cats are indoor-outdoor and may be more territorial than cats in fully indoor environments. A sitter who understands that a swishing tail means "not now" or that airplane ears signal rising stress can adjust their approach, building trust gradually rather than forcing interaction.

Stress Signals vs Contentment: A Quick Reference

Signs of stress or fear: dilated pupils, flattened ears, tucked tail, hiding, hissing, growling, whiskers pulled back, crouched posture, overgrooming, refusing food.

Signs of contentment: slow blinking, relaxed ears, upright gently curving tail, kneading, purring with relaxed body, rubbing against you, half-closed eyes, exposed belly, normal eating and grooming habits.

If your cat shows prolonged stress signals — especially refusing food for more than 24 hours — consult your veterinarian. Cats are masters at hiding illness, and behavioural changes are often the first clue something is wrong.

Key Takeaways

  • A high, gently curved tail signals confidence and friendliness; a tucked tail means fear or anxiety
  • Ear positions range from forward (alert and curious) to pinned flat (extreme fear) — learn to read the gradient
  • Slow blinking is the feline equivalent of "I trust you" — try blinking back to strengthen the bond
  • Purring does not always mean happiness; cats also purr when in pain or stressed, so always check the context
  • Professional pet sitters who understand cat body language provide safer, more responsive care for your feline family member
