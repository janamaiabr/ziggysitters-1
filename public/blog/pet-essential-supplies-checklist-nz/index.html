Pet Essential Supplies Checklist for New Zealand Owners

Published 11 July 2026 · Pet Care Tips

Bringing a pet home is exciting, but showing up without the right supplies turns day one into a scramble. Whether you are picking up a puppy from a breeder in Auckland, adopting a rescue cat in Wellington, or fostering a senior dog in Christchurch, this checklist covers every essential your new companion needs from the moment they walk through the door. We have organised it by category, included NZ-specific recommendations, and flagged what can wait versus what you need on day one.

Day One Essentials

These are the items you need before your pet arrives. Do not pick up your new pet without having these ready at home.

Dog Day One

  • Food and water bowls (stainless steel or ceramic, not plastic)
  • Age-appropriate dog food (confirm with breeder or rescue what they have been eating)
  • Collar with ID tag (include your phone number)
  • Lead (1.2m to 1.8m for walking; longer lead for training)
  • Crate or carrier (large enough to stand up and turn around)
  • Bed or blanket for the crate
  • Poop bags (biodegradable preferred)
  • Enzyme cleaner for accidents (Nature\'s Miracle or similar)
  • A few toys (one chew toy, one soft toy, one ball)
  • Baby gate or play pen for containing puppies

Cat Day One

  • Food and water bowls (shallow ceramic preferred, placed away from litter)
  • Age-appropriate cat food (wet and dry)
  • Litter tray (at least one, preferably two for a new cat)
  • Cat litter (clumping clay is popular; pine or paper for kittens)
  • Scoop and liner bags
  • Scratching post or pad
  • A couple of toys (wand toy, catnip mouse)
  • Carrier for vet visits and transport
  • Hiding spot (a covered bed or cardboard box with a blanket)
  • Enzyme cleaner for accidents
NZ tip: Under the Dog Control Act 1996, all dogs in New Zealand must be registered with their local council by 3 months of age. Keep your registration tag on the collar at all times. Microchipping is also mandatory for all dogs registered after 1 July 2006. Cats do not have a national registration requirement, but microchipping is strongly recommended.

Food and Water Supplies

Choosing the right food

Your pet\'s food is the single biggest ongoing expense and the most important supply decision you will make. In New Zealand, pet food is regulated by the Ministry for Primary Industries (MPI), and all commercially sold food must meet certain safety standards. Look for products that meet AAFCO or FEDIAF standards for complete and balanced nutrition.

For dogs, premium brands widely available in NZ include Royal Canin, Hill\'s Science Diet, Ziwi Peak (NZ-made), and Addiction (NZ-made). For cats, the same brands offer feline-specific lines. If you are unsure, ask your vet for a recommendation based on your pet\'s age, breed, and health status.

Important: Transition food gradually over 7 to 10 days. Mix increasing amounts of the new food with decreasing amounts of the old food. A sudden switch causes stomach upset in almost every pet.

Food and water supplies checklist

  • Food (matching what the breeder, shelter, or rescue has been feeding)
  • Food storage container (airtight, to keep food fresh and pest-free)
  • Stainless steel or ceramic bowls (plastic scratches and harbours bacteria)
  • Water bowl or fountain (cats especially prefer running water)
  • Measuring scoop or kitchen scale for portion control
  • Treats for training (small, soft, and high-value)
  • Long-lasting chew or dental treat (for dogs)

Bowl placement matters

Dogs do best with bowls at a comfortable height. For large breeds, elevated bowls can help reduce gulping and bloating. For cats, place food and water bowls away from the litter tray. Cats instinctively avoid eating near where they eliminate. In multi-pet households, give each pet their own feeding station to reduce resource guarding.

Grooming and Hygiene

Grooming is not just about looks. Regular brushing removes dead hair and distributes natural oils, keeping the coat healthy and reducing the hair on your furniture. Nail trimming prevents painful overgrowth and gait problems. Dental care prevents periodontal disease, which affects over 80% of dogs and 70% of cats over age three.

Dog grooming supplies

  • Brush or comb appropriate for coat type (slicker brush for long coats, rubber curry for short coats)
  • Dog shampoo (oatmeal-based for sensitive skin)
  • Dog conditioner (for long or double-coated breeds)
  • Nail clippers or grinder (guillotine style for small dogs, pliers style for large dogs)
  • Styptic powder (stops bleeding if you cut a nail quick)
  • Toothbrush and dog toothpaste (never use human toothpaste)
  • Dental chews (supplementary, not a replacement for brushing)
  • Towels for bathing
  • Ear cleaning solution (especially for floppy-eared breeds)
  • Dog-safe wipes for muddy paws

Cat grooming supplies

  • Fine-toothed flea comb (useful even if your cat is indoor-only)
  • Soft bristle brush or deshedding tool (for longer coats)
  • Cat nail clippers (small scissor style)
  • Cat toothbrush and toothpaste
  • Ear cleaning solution
  • Cat-safe wipes
NZ grooming tip: Double-coated breeds like German Shepherds, Huskies, and Border Collies blow their coat twice a year, usually in spring and autumn. During these periods, daily brushing with a deshedding tool is essential. Never shave a double-coated dog in summer. Their undercoat provides insulation against both heat and cold, and shaving can permanently damage the coat.

Health and Safety

Vaccinations and registration

New Zealand has specific vaccination and registration requirements that you should sort early.

Requirement Dogs Cats
Core vaccinations Parvovirus, distemper, hepatitis, parainfluenza (DHP-P) Feline panleukopenia, calicivirus, herpesvirus (F3)
Non-core vaccinations Kennel cough, leptospirosis, canine influenza Feline leukaemia virus (FeLV), FIV
Microchipping Mandatory (Dog Control Act) Strongly recommended
Council registration Mandatory by 3 months Not required (but some councils encourage it)
Flea and worm treatment Monthly (flea), quarterly (worm) Monthly (flea), quarterly (worm for outdoor cats)
Desexing Recommended at 6-12 months Recommended at 4-6 months

Health supplies checklist

  • Vet clinic selected and contact details saved in your phone
  • After-hours emergency vet number (different from your regular vet)
  • Vaccination appointment booked (or completed if adopting from a shelter)
  • Flea and worm treatment (Bravecto, NexGard, or Advantage are popular in NZ)
  • Pet first aid kit (gauze, bandages, antiseptic wipes, thermometer, tweezers)
  • Tick remover (especially if you live in a tick-prone area like the upper North Island)
  • Pet insurance researched and ideally activated before any health issues arise
  • Microchip registered on the NZ Companion Animal Register (NZCAR)
Karaka berry warning: If you live in the upper North Island, be aware of karaka berries. These bright orange berries drop from January to April and are highly toxic to dogs. Even one berry can cause permanent neurological damage or death. If you walk your dog near karaka trees, keep them on lead during berry season and prevent them from eating anything on the ground.

Comfort and Environment

Creating a safe space

Every pet needs a place they can retreat to when they feel overwhelmed. For dogs, this is usually a crate with the door left open, a dog bed in a quiet corner, or a mat under a desk. For cats, it is a high perch, a covered bed, or a cardboard box in a low-traffic area. This space should always be accessible and never used as punishment.

Comfort supplies

  • Bed or crate pad (washable, appropriately sized)
  • Crate (for dogs) with a cover to create a den-like space
  • Blanket or towel that smells like home (especially for new arrivals)
  • Scratching post or cat tree (tall enough for full-body stretch, at least 90cm)
  • Window perch or cat shelf (cats love vertical territory)
  • Baby gates to manage access to rooms
  • Pet-safe houseplants or cat grass (see our guide on safe indoor plants for NZ pets)
  • Pheromone diffuser (Feliway for cats, Adaptil for dogs) to reduce stress

Pet-proofing your home

Before your pet arrives, walk through your home at their eye level. Look for electrical cords they might chew, small objects they could swallow, toxic plants on accessible shelves, cleaning products in low cupboards, and gaps in fencing they could squeeze through. See our full pet-proofing guide for a room-by-room walkthrough.

Travel and Transport

Even if you do not plan to travel with your pet regularly, you need a safe way to transport them. Vet visits, emergencies, and moving house all require secure transport. In New Zealand, it is illegal to have an unrestrained dog in a moving vehicle in some regions, and even where it is not legally required, an unrestrained pet is a danger to itself and everyone in the car.

Travel supplies

  • Pet carrier (airline-approved if you plan to fly; sturdy and well-ventilated)
  • Car harness and seatbelt tether (for dogs)
  • Travel bowl (collapsible silicone bowls are light and easy to pack)
  • Poop bags and portable litter tray (for cats on longer trips)
  • Travel water bottle with attached bowl
  • Calming spray or treats for anxious travellers
  • Car seat cover or boot liner (to protect your car)
  • Pet first aid kit in the car (separate from the home kit)
NZ travel tip: If you are travelling between the North and South Island with a pet, the Interislander ferry allows pets in approved carriers in the vehicle deck area or in designated kennels. Book early as pet spots are limited. See our full NZ pet travel guide for ferry and airline policies.

Emergency Preparedness

New Zealand is prone to earthquakes, floods, and severe weather. Your pet emergency kit should be packed, accessible, and checked every 6 months. This is separate from your everyday supplies and should be ready to grab at a moment\'s notice.

Pet emergency kit

  • 3-day supply of food and water (rotate every 3 months)
  • Copy of vaccination records and microchip registration
  • Recent photo of your pet (in case you get separated)
  • Medications and copies of prescriptions
  • Lead, collar with ID tag, and carrier
  • Comfort item (favourite toy or blanket)
  • Litter and litter tray (for cats)
  • Poop bags
  • Basic first aid supplies (bandages, antiseptic, tick remover)
  • List of emergency vet contacts (including after-hours)
  • Emergency sticker on your front window indicating number and type of pets inside
Earthquake and flood plan: Identify which rooms are safest in your home during an earthquake (internal walls, away from windows). Practice getting your pet into their carrier quickly. Know which local motels and holiday parks accept pets if you need to evacuate. Many NZ emergency shelters do not accept animals, so have a backup plan.

Where to Buy Pet Supplies in New Zealand

Physical stores

  • Animates — Largest pet retail chain in NZ. Wide range of food, toys, and accessories. Most stores have vet clinics and grooming services.
  • Petstock — Australian chain with growing NZ presence. Good range of premium brands and farm supplies.
  • Bargain Buy Pets — Budget-friendly option. Good for bulk food and basic supplies.
  • Kmart and The Warehouse — Surprisingly decent for basic bowls, beds, and toys at low prices. Not the place for premium food or specialty items.

Online stores

  • Pet.co.nz — Free shipping on orders over $49. Good range and competitive prices.
  • Ultra Pet — Subscription-based auto-delivery for food and flea treatment. Convenient if you tend to forget to reorder.
  • VetShop — Vet-run online store with clinical-grade products and advice.

Second-hand and community

  • Trade Me — Great for second-hand crates, carriers, and large items like cat trees. Always clean thoroughly before use.
  • Community Facebook groups — Many NZ pet communities run free or cheap supply exchanges. Check your local area.
  • Op shops — Good for blankets and towels that you do not mind getting dirty.
Money-saving tip: Buy food in bulk from online retailers during sale periods (Boxing Day, Click Frenzy). Set up auto-delivery for flea and worm treatment so you never miss a dose, and many online stores offer a small discount for subscriptions. For crates and large items, second-hand is often just as good at half the price.

Getting Ready for Your Pet

The most important thing on day one is not the supplies. It is patience. Your new pet is entering a strange environment with strange smells and strange people. Give them time to explore at their own pace. Set up their safe space before they arrive. Keep the house quiet for the first few hours. Let them come to you rather than chasing them around trying to bond.

Have the essentials ready, book the vet within the first week, register your dog with the council, and start building a routine. The rest you will figure out as you go. Every pet is different, and the supplies you thought were essential might turn out to be ignored while the cardboard box the toy came in becomes their favourite thing in the house.
