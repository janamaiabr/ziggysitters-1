Beginner Pet Grooming Guide NZ: First-Time Dog & Cat Grooming Tips

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  April 17, 2026  •  9 min read

You have got your first dog or cat. Congratulations! Now comes the part no one tells you about — the grooming. Between the fur, the nails, the occasional mud bath, and those mystery smells, keeping your pet fresh takes a bit of know-how. This guide walks NZ first-time pet owners through the basics of home grooming without the panic.

Many new NZ pet owners assume grooming means a trip to the professional groomer every few weeks. While that is useful for some breeds, most dogs and cats can go years between salon visits with just basic home care. The trick is knowing what to do, how often, and when to hand things over to someone with more experience.

Why Grooming Matters Beyond Looking Nice

Grooming is not vanity. It is health. Here is what regular brushing, cleaning, and nail trims actually prevent:

  • Mattted fur: Mats pull on the skin, cause discomfort, and can hide skin infections or parasites.
  • Hot spots and infections: Dirty ears, unbrushedcoats, and overgrown nails lead to bacterial and yeast problems.
  • Painful nails: Overgrown nails curve into the paw pad and cause chronic pain.
  • Tooth decay: Yes, dental care is part of grooming. Three-quarters of cats and dogs over three years old have dental disease.

The good news is that most of this takes fifteen minutes a week once you and your pet get into a routine.

Grooming Tools You Actually Need

You do not need a garage full of equipment. For a typical NZ dog or cat, these essentials cover 95% of what you need:

  • Slicker brush: Removes loose fur and prevents mats. Works for most coat types.
  • Wide-tooth metal comb: For after brushing and checking for tangles.
  • Nail clippers or grinder: Either scissor-style clippers for small pets or a grinder (dremel-style) for larger dogs.
  • Dog or cat shampoo: Use only pet-formulated products. Human shampoo damages pet skin pH.
  • Eyedropper or squirt bottle for rinsing
  • Styptic powder (for if you cut a nail too short and it bleeds)
  • Soft towels (NZ weather means you will use these constantly)
Tip: Start gathering these items before your pet arrives home. Having tools ready means you can begin building a grooming routine from day one, which makes future salon visits much easier.

How to Brush Your Dog: A Step-by-Step

Brushing is the single most important grooming task. It removes loose fur, distributes natural oils, lets you check for problems, and builds bonding time. Most dogs learn to love it.

Getting Started

  1. Choose a calm moment when your pet is relaxed, not right after a walk or play.
  2. Sit on the floor with them or place them on a non-slip surface.
  3. Start with gentle strokes in the direction of fur growth.
  4. Use short strokes, checking the skin as you go.
  5. If you hit a tangle, hold the fur at the base (to avoid pulling skin) and work through it with the comb.
  6. End with praise and a treat.

How Often to Brush

  • Short coats (Boxer, Staffy, Beagle): Once or twice a week
  • Double coats (Labrador, Shepherd, Husky): Two to three times a week, more during shedding season (spring and autumn)
  • Long or silky coats (Shih Tzu, Maltese, Afghan Hound): Every day or every second day
  • Wire or broken coats (Welsh Corgi, Wire Fox Terrier): Weekly brushing, with periodic hand-stripping for show dogs

The Mats Test

Run your fingers through your dog's coat after brushing. If you feel any resistance, you have missed a spot. Pay extra attention behind the ears, on the belly, and around the collar area — these are prime matting zones.

How to Brush Your Cat (Without Losing Blood)

Cats are generally better at grooming themselves than dogs, but they still benefit from brushing, particularly long-haired breeds and senior cats who cannot reach everywhere.

The Gentle Approach

  • Start when your cat is relaxed and possibly sleepy. Ideally, start as a kitten.
  • Use a soft bristle brush or rubber grooming mitt for sensitive cats.
  • Brush in the direction of fur growth, never against it.
  • If your cat tolerates it, upgrade to a slicker or wide-tooth comb.
  • If they wriggle, let them go. Forcing it creates negative associations.
  • Most cats prefer short sessions of two to three minutes.

High-Shed Alert

If you have a long-haired breed (Maine Coon, Ragdoll, Persian, Nebelung), brush every day during shedding season. Matting in long-haired cats can become severe enough to require a full shave at the vet, which is stressful for everyone.

Warning: Never use scissors to cut out mats near the skin. You can easily cut the skin underneath. If a mat is tight and close to the body, see a professional groomer or vet.

Bathing Your Dog: The Basics

Most NZ dogs do not need frequent baths. Over-bathing strips natural oils from their coat and can cause skin problems. Unless your dog has gotten into something genuinely filthy or smelly, a bath every four to six weeks is plenty for most dogs.

Before the Bath

  • Brush out all tangles first. Wet mats become impossible to remove.
  • Use lukewarm water — not hot, not cold. Dogs are more sensitive to temperature than you think.
  • Plug the ears loosely with cotton balls to keep water out (this prevents infections).

During the Bath

  1. Wet your dog thoroughly, avoiding the head and face.
  2. Apply shampoo diluted in water (concentrated shampoo straight from the bottle is harder to rinse).
  3. Massage from neck to tail, paying attention to the belly, legs, and paws.
  4. Rinse thoroughly. Leftover shampoo causes itching.
  5. Use a hand-held sprayer or pitcher for hard-to-reach areas.

After the Bath

  • Towel dry as much as possible.
  • Let them shake (this is their job, not yours).
  • Use a second towel or a blow dryer on low if your dog tolerates it.
  • Keep them warm until fully dry — especially in winter.
Tip: In NZ, the easiest time to bathe is in the shower or outside on a warm day. In winter, make sure your home is warm enough that your dog does not get cold after bathing.

Bathing Your Cat: Is It Necessary?

Short answer: usually not. Cats are meticulous self-groomers and rarely need baths. The exceptions include:

  • Senior cats who cannot clean themselves
  • Cats with medical conditions (skin infections, allergies)
  • Extremely dirty cats (paint, oil, chemicals)
  • Some long-haired breeds prone to grease buildup at the tail base

If you must bathe your cat:

  • Use a sink or basin, not the full bathtub.
  • Fill with only a few inches of lukewarm water.
  • Have a towel ready to wrap them immediately after.
  • Consider using dry shampoo or waterless pet foam instead.

Nail Trimming: The Part Everyone Avoids

nail trims are the grooming task pet owners dread most, but they are essential. Overgrown nails cause pain, alter gait, and can lead to joint problems.

How to Tell If Nails Are Too Long

When your dog or cat is standing on a hard floor, their nails should not touch the ground. If you hear clicking on hard floors, the nails are too long.

Trimming at Home

  1. Get your pet comfortable with having their paws handled. Do this for several days before attempting a trim.
  2. Use sharp, clean clippers. Dull clippers crush the nail and cause pain.
  3. Hold the paw firmly but gently.
  4. Cut at a 45-degree angle, avoiding the quick (the pink area inside the nail that contains blood vessels).
  5. For black nails where you cannot see the quick, trim small amounts at a time.
  6. Have styptic powder on hand in case you nick the quick.
If you cut the quick: It bleeds profusely but is not dangerous. Apply styptic powder or corn starch, hold pressure for one to two minutes, and comfort your pet. This happens to everyone at first.

How Often

  • Dogs: Every two to four weeks, depending on activity and surfaces walked on.
  • Cats: Every four to six weeks (mostly applies to indoor cats with no scratching posts).
Tip: Walking your dog on concrete naturally wears nails down. Dogs who walk daily on pavement often need fewer trims than dogs who mostly walk on grass or carpet.

Ear and Eye Cleaning

Ears

Check your pet's ears weekly. Signs of problems include smell, discharge, redness, or head shaking.

To clean:

  • Use a vet-approved ear cleaner (available from NZ pet stores and vets).
  • Apply a few drops to the ear canal.
  • Massage the base of the ear for ten to twenty seconds.
  • Let your pet shake out the excess.
  • Wipe the outer ear with a cotton ball. Never use cotton buds inside the ear canal.

Eyes

Gently wipe around your pet's eyes with a damp cotton ball to remove discharge. Use separate balls for each eye. If discharge is coloured, excessive, or the eye looks irritated, see a vet.

When to See a Professional Groomer

Some tasks are better left to the experts. See a professional groomer for:

  • Breed-specific clips: poodles, Shih Tzus, Bichon Frises, and show dogs need professional cuts.
  • Severe matting that brushing cannot fix
  • Nail grinding for dogs who are terrified of clippers
  • Full grooms (bath, blow dry, brush, nail trim, ear cleaning) for double-coated or long-haired breeds
  • Anal gland expression (if recommended by your vet)

In New Zealand, professional groomers charge approximately $40 to $80 depending on dog size, coat length, and location. Auckland Wellington, and Christchurch have the widest options.

Building a Simple Grooming Routine

You do not need to do everything at once. Build this weekly routine:

  • Every second day: Quick brush (two to five minutes)
  • Weekly: Full brush, ear check, eye wipe
  • Every two to four weeks: Nail trim (or as needed)
  • Every four to six weeks: Bath (dogs only, as needed)

Keep sessions short and positive, especially with kittens, puppies, and nervous pets. Five minutes of calm brushing beats twenty minutes of a stressed struggle.

Summary: Start Simple

Beginner grooming is about consistency, not perfection. Brush your pet regularly, keep nails trimmed, check ears and eyes, and bathe only when needed. Most pets stay healthy and happy with fifteen minutes of weekly home care and a trip to the professional groomer every few months for breed-specific needs. The bonding time is a bonus.
