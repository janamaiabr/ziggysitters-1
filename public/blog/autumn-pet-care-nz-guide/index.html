Autumn Pet Care Guide NZ 2026

By ZiggySitters NZ • March 2026 • 10 min read

Autumn in New Zealand — March through May — brings cooler mornings, shorter evenings, and a whole new set of challenges and opportunities for pet owners. From parasite season peaks to dietary adjustments and seasonal hazards in the garden, here is everything you need to keep your dogs and cats healthy as the leaves begin to turn.

What Autumn Means for NZ Pets

New Zealand’s autumn is mild compared to northern hemisphere standards, but the transition from summer still brings meaningful changes for your pets. Daytime temperatures across Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch drop noticeably from March into May, and rainfall increases across much of the country. Humidity remains high in the North Island, while the South Island cools quickly after Easter.

For pets, these changes affect everything from energy levels and coat condition to parasite load and appetite. A proactive approach to seasonal care means fewer vet visits and a happier, healthier animal all year round.

NZ Autumn At a Glance (March–May)
  • Temperatures: 12–22°C across most of NZ
  • Rainfall: Increasing, especially in the North Island
  • Daylight: Decreasing after the March equinox
  • Flea & tick activity: Still elevated from summer peak
  • Key hazard: Toxic mushrooms and fungi appearing in lawns and parks

Autumn Dog Care Tips

1. Adjust Exercise Routines as Days Shorten

With the clocks turning back at the end of April, many owners find their morning and evening walks shifting into darker conditions. This brings safety considerations that are easy to overlook until they become a problem.

  • Reflective gear: Invest in a reflective vest or collar for your dog and reflective ankle bands for yourself. Most NZ pet stores stock these affordably.
  • Leash or LED collar lights: A clip-on light lets drivers and cyclists see your dog even in low light.
  • Route awareness: Stick to well-lit paths, especially in Wellington and Auckland where traffic volume stays high into the evenings.
  • Mud management: Autumn rain means muddy trails. Keep a towel by the door and check paws after walks for cuts from hidden debris in wet grass.

2. Watch for Autumn Fungi and Mushrooms

This is one of the most underrated seasonal hazards for NZ dog owners. Autumn moisture triggers a surge in fungi growth across lawns, parks, and bushland from Northland to Southland. Many wild mushroom species are toxic to dogs — and curious dogs will investigate and sometimes eat them.

Urgent Warning: If your dog eats an unknown mushroom, contact your vet immediately. Symptoms of mushroom toxicity include vomiting, drooling, lethargy, jaundice, and in severe cases, seizures. Do not wait for symptoms to worsen.
  • Regularly check your garden and remove any mushrooms or fungi as soon as they appear
  • Supervise your dog closely in parks and on bush walks during March to May
  • Teach a reliable “leave it” command — it could save your dog’s life
  • If in doubt about what your dog ate, photograph the fungus and take it to the vet with you

3. Coat and Grooming Changes

Autumn triggers a seasonal moult in many dog breeds as their summer coat is shed and a thicker winter undercoat grows in. You may notice significantly more shedding from March through April. Regular brushing during this period:

  • Reduces the volume of hair on your floors, furniture, and clothing
  • Prevents matting, particularly in double-coated breeds like Border Collies, Golden Retrievers, and Huskies
  • Allows you to check the skin condition and spot any signs of irritation, fleas, or grass seed injuries from summer

Short-coated breeds like Staffies and Boxers may benefit from a dog coat or jacket on cold South Island mornings or on elevated Wellington days when the southerly bites.

4. Diet and Weight Management

As activity levels shift with shorter days and wetter weather, some dogs naturally reduce their exercise volume. This can lead to gradual weight gain if feeding quantities are not adjusted. Autumn is also a natural time to assess your dog’s body condition score:

  • You should be able to feel (but not prominently see) your dog’s ribs
  • There should be a visible waist when viewed from above
  • The abdomen should tuck up slightly when viewed from the side

If your dog is gaining weight, reduce treat quantities first, then consider slightly smaller meal portions. Consult your vet before making significant dietary changes.

Autumn Cat Care Tips

1. The Great Autumn Moult

Like dogs, cats undergo a seasonal coat change in autumn. Indoor cats may shed more throughout the year due to consistent artificial lighting, but outdoor and semi-outdoor cats will show a more pronounced autumn moult. Daily brushing during peak shedding weeks helps prevent hairballs — a common autumn complaint in longhaired breeds like Persians and Maine Coons.

Hairball Tip: Adding a small amount of pumpkin puree (plain, no additives) to your cat’s food can help hairballs pass naturally. Ask your vet about hairball prevention treats if the problem is recurring.

2. Outdoor Cats and Shorter Days

As daylight decreases, many outdoor cats naturally spend more time indoors. This is a good time to:

  • Ensure your cat has a warm, dry indoor sleeping spot away from draughts
  • Check that cat flaps are working properly and not letting in cold air when not in use
  • Review microchipping — autumn weather can disorient cats, particularly younger ones, and a registered microchip is their best chance of being reunited with you if they become lost
  • Provide additional indoor enrichment (climbing structures, puzzle feeders, interactive toys) as outdoor exploration time naturally reduces

3. Preparing for Cooler Nights

Even in Auckland and Hamilton, autumn nights drop to 12–14°C by May. Older cats, kittens, and cats with health conditions can feel the cold acutely. Consider:

  • A heated cat bed or a self-warming thermal mat (no electricity required)
  • Moving your cat’s sleeping area away from external walls or windows
  • For outdoor cats: providing a weatherproof outdoor shelter if they prefer to sleep outside

Parasite Control in Autumn

Many NZ pet owners make the mistake of thinking parasite prevention is only a summer concern. In reality, flea populations remain elevated well into May across the North Island, and some tick species are active throughout the year in coastal and bushy areas.

Fleas

Flea eggs that hatched in summer remain in your home environment and will continue to develop in the warmth of your heated home even as outdoor temperatures drop. If you stopped flea treatment in February, your pet may already be picking up fleas from the environment.

  • Continue year-round flea prevention on all pets in the household
  • Wash pet bedding weekly in hot water during peak shedding and flea season
  • Vacuum floors, furniture, and skirting boards frequently and empty the bag/canister outside immediately

Ticks

In NZ, the main tick concern is the paralysis tick (Ixodes holocyclus) which is less common here than in Australia but is present in some regions. The NZ cattle tick (Haemaphysalis longicornis) can also affect dogs. Check pets after walks in long grass or bush and consult your vet about appropriate tick prevention for your region.

Intestinal Worms

Autumn is a good time to worm your pets if you have not done so recently. Dogs and cats should generally be wormed every 3 months. Puppies and kittens require more frequent treatment — consult your vet for an appropriate schedule.

Autumn Gardening Hazards

The NZ autumn garden is full of seasonal hazards your pets could encounter. As you plant winter vegetables, mulch, or clear summer growth, be aware of the following:

Hazard Risk Action
Wild mushrooms and fungi High — potentially fatal Remove immediately, supervise pets outdoors
Slug and snail bait (metaldehyde) Very high — toxic to dogs and cats Use pet-safe alternatives (iron EDTA-based baits)
Compost bins Medium — mouldy food is toxic Secure lids, fence off compost areas
Autumn bulbs (daffodil, tulip) Medium — bulbs especially toxic Keep pets away during planting, store bulbs securely
Fallen stone fruit (plums, peaches) Low-medium — pits contain cyanide Clear fallen fruit regularly
Slug Bait Warning: Metaldehyde slug pellets are one of the most common causes of pet poisoning in NZ each year. Even a small amount can cause seizures and death. Switch to iron EDTA-based products (e.g., Quash or Tui Quash), which are effective against slugs and snails but much safer around pets.

Autumn Vet Check: What to Review

Autumn is an ideal time to schedule a general health check if your pet has not seen a vet recently. Key items to discuss include:

  • Vaccination status: Are your annual boosters up to date? Autumn is a common time to catch up before winter illness peaks.
  • Dental health: Dental disease worsens through winter when pets chew less on natural materials. A dental scale may be worth considering.
  • Weight check: Get a baseline weight before winter so you can monitor any seasonal changes.
  • Joint health: Cooler, damper weather aggravates arthritis in older pets. Discuss pain management options with your vet.
  • Coat condition: Dull or flaky coats can indicate nutritional deficiencies. Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation often helps.

Planning Autumn Pet Sitting and Holidays

ANZAC Day (25 April) is a popular long weekend for NZ families to take short breaks, and Easter (April) is one of the busiest travel periods of the year. If you’re planning to travel over any of these autumn holidays, booking pet care early is essential.

  • Book at least 4–6 weeks in advance for Easter and ANZAC Day, when demand for pet sitters spikes significantly
  • Hold a meet-and-greet: Introduce your sitter to your pet before your departure, especially if your pet has anxiety or special health needs
  • Leave a care sheet: Include feeding instructions, medication schedules, vet contact details, and any autumn-specific hazards to watch for in your garden
  • Share your pet’s routine: Autumn behavioural changes (more sleeping, reduced appetite) are normal but your sitter should know your pet’s baseline so they can identify anything out of the ordinary

Autumn Wellness Checklist for NZ Pet Owners

  • Continue flea and tick prevention through May (and year-round in the North Island)
  • Worm pets if not done in the past 3 months
  • Increase brushing frequency to manage the seasonal moult
  • Check and remove any mushrooms or fungi from your garden weekly
  • Replace metaldehyde slug bait with pet-safe alternatives before autumn rains
  • Add reflective gear to your dog’s walking kit as evenings darken
  • Prepare a warm, draft-free sleeping area for indoor cats and senior pets
  • Monitor your pet’s weight as exercise levels naturally shift
  • Book Easter and ANZAC Day pet sitting early
  • Schedule an autumn vet check if overdue for vaccinations or a health review

Final Thoughts

New Zealand’s autumn is a beautiful season — and with a few small adjustments, it can be just as enjoyable for your pets as it is for you. The key is staying ahead of seasonal changes rather than reacting to them. Regular grooming, smart parasite control, awareness of garden hazards, and a warm indoor retreat are the foundations of great autumn pet care.
