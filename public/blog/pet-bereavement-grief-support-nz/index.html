Pet Bereavement \& Grief Support in New Zealand: Coping with the Loss of Your Pet

Published 22 May 2026

Losing a pet is one of the most painful experiences a person can go through. For many New Zealanders, a pet is not just an animal \u2014 they are a family member, a daily companion, and a source of unconditional love. When that bond is broken, the grief can feel overwhelming and surprisingly isolating.

This guide is here to help you understand what you are feeling, find professional support in Aotearoa, and navigate the practical and emotional decisions that come with pet loss. You are not alone, and what you are feeling is valid.

Understanding Pet Grief: Why It Hits So Hard

Pet grief is real grief. Research shows that the loss of a pet can trigger the same psychological processes as losing a human loved one. The bond between humans and companion animals is deep \u2014 studies from the University of Auckland and Massey University have documented how pets reduce stress, lower blood pressure, and provide emotional stability. When that presence disappears, the void is enormous.

In New Zealand, where pets live in roughly 64% of households, the experience of pet loss is widespread but rarely discussed openly. Many people feel they should not grieve as intensely as they do, which compounds the pain with shame. The truth is straightforward: grief over a pet is normal, natural, and deserving of support.

The Stages of Pet Grief

While grief is not a tidy linear process, most people move through recognisable phases:

1. Shock and Denial

Immediately after loss, you may feel numb or struggle to accept that your pet is gone. This is your mind\u2019s way of protecting you from the full weight of reality all at once. You might catch yourself expecting to hear paws on the floor or see your pet in their usual spot. These moments are completely normal.

2. Anger and Guilt

Many pet owners experience intense guilt. \u201cCould I have done more?\u201d \u201cDid I wait too long?\u201d \u201cWas it the right decision?\u201d These questions are agonising but incredibly common. Anger can also surface \u2014 at the vet, at the illness, at the unfairness of it all. Neither guilt nor anger means you did something wrong. They are part of processing loss.

3. Bargaining

\u201cIf only I had noticed sooner.\u201d \u201cIf only we had tried that other treatment.\u201d Bargaining is the mind\u2019s attempt to regain control over an uncontrollable situation. It reflects how much you cared, not what you failed to do.

4. Depression and Sadness

This is often the longest phase. You may feel a persistent emptiness, cry unexpectedly, or lose interest in activities you usually enjoy. The house feels different \u2014 quieter, emptier, and full of reminders. Allow yourself to feel this sadness without rushing to \u201cmove on.\u201d

5. Acceptance and Integration

Acceptance does not mean forgetting or being fine with the loss. It means finding a way to carry the love you had for your pet forward while gradually re-engaging with life. The grief softens over time, even if it never fully disappears. Many people find they can eventually smile at memories rather than only feel pain.

The Euthanasia Decision: One of the Hardest Choices

For many NZ pet owners, the most agonising part of pet loss is the decision around euthanasia. Knowing when it is time is never simple, and the weight of that responsibility can feel crushing.

Signs It May Be Time

  • Chronic pain that medication cannot adequately manage
  • Loss of interest in food, water, or favourite activities
  • Inability to stand, walk, or get comfortable
  • Frequent accidents or inability to control bodily functions
  • More bad days than good days over a sustained period

Talking to Your Vet

New Zealand vets are trained to have these conversations compassionately. Ask your vet directly: \u201cIf this were your pet, what would you do?\u201d Many clinics across NZ offer quality-of-life assessments that can help you make a more informed decision. The SPCA and NZ Veterinary Association both emphasise that humane euthanasia, when timed appropriately, is an act of love.

What Happens During Euthanasia

Understanding the process can reduce anxiety. In NZ, euthanasia is typically performed by a vet using an intravenous injection. Most pets pass within seconds, peacefully and painlessly. You can usually choose to be present, hold your pet, and say goodbye. Some clinics offer home euthanasia services, allowing your pet to pass in a familiar, comfortable environment.

Aftercare Options in New Zealand

Cremation

Cremation is the most common option in NZ. You can choose:

  • Individual cremation: Your pet is cremated separately, and ashes are returned to you in an urn or container. Cost typically ranges from $200\u2013$500 depending on the pet\u2019s size.
  • Communal cremation: Your pet is cremated alongside others, and ashes are scattered by the crematorium. This is a more affordable option, usually $50\u2013$150.

Burial

In New Zealand, you can bury your pet on your own property as long as the burial does not contaminate water supplies and is at a reasonable depth (generally at least 60cm). Some pet cemeteries exist, such as those in Auckland and Christchurch, offering perpetual care plots starting around $400\u2013$800.

Memorial Options

  • Paw print jewellery: Several NZ companies create silver or gold pendants from your pet\u2019s paw print
  • Memorial stones: Engraved garden stones for your property
  • Tree planting: Planting a native tree as a living memorial \u2014 native species like p\u014dhutukawa or k\u014whai provide beautiful, enduring tributes
  • Donation: Many owners donate to the SPCA, local rescue, or vet school in their pet\u2019s name

Professional Grief Support in New Zealand

You do not have to navigate this alone. Several NZ-specific resources exist:

Pet Loss Counselling

  • NZ Pet Loss Support \u2014 Offers phone and online counselling specifically for pet bereavement
  • Lifeline Aotearoa (0800 543 354) \u2014 Available 24/7 for anyone in emotional distress, including grief
  • Your local vet clinic \u2014 Many NZ vet clinics can refer you to counsellors experienced in pet loss
  • Need to Talk? (1737) \u2014 Free text or call service for mental health support

Online Communities

  • The Rainbow Bridge online community has active NZ members sharing stories and support
  • Facebook groups like Pet Loss Support New Zealand provide peer support from people who understand
  • The Association for Pet Loss and Bereavement offers free chat rooms and resources

Helping Children Cope with Pet Loss

Children process grief differently from adults. Their understanding of death evolves with age, and they need honest, age-appropriate communication.

Age-Appropriate Approaches

  • Under 5: Keep explanations simple and concrete. Avoid euphemisms like \u201cwent to sleep\u201d, which can cause fear of sleeping. Say: \u201cTheir body stopped working and they died. We will always love them.\u201d
  • 6\u201310: Children this age understand death is permanent. They may have many questions. Answer honestly, and let them express sadness through drawing, writing, or creating a memory box.
  • 11+: Pre-teens and teenagers experience grief similarly to adults. They may withdraw, become angry, or seek peer support. Respect their need for space while ensuring they know support is available.

What to Avoid

  • Do not say the pet \u201cran away\u201d \u2014 this breeds false hope and trust issues
  • Do not rush to replace the pet \u2014 each animal has their own unique relationship with the family
  • Do not minimise their feelings with phrases like \u201cit was just a pet\u201d

When Other Pets Grieve

If you have other pets, they may also show signs of grief. Dogs may pace, whine, or lose appetite. Cats may search for their companion, vocalise more, or become withdrawn. To support surviving pets:

  • Maintain their routine as much as possible
  • Give them extra attention without overwhelming them
  • Allow them to see and sniff their companion after death if possible \u2014 this can help them understand
  • Be patient with changes in behaviour; most pets adjust within a few weeks

Self-Care During Pet Grief

Grief takes a physical toll. Prioritising your own wellbeing is not selfish \u2014 it is necessary.

  • Rest: Grief is exhausting. Allow yourself more sleep than usual
  • Nutrition: Eat regular meals even if your appetite is low
  • Movement: A walk in a NZ regional park or along the beach can help process emotions
  • Connection: Talk to people who understand \u2014 friends with pets, support groups, or a counsellor
  • Creative expression: Journaling, art, or creating a photo album can be therapeutic
  • Avoid major decisions: Grief clouds judgement. Wait before making big life changes

When to Consider Another Pet

There is no right timeline. Some people need months or years; others find comfort in a new companion sooner. What matters is that you are getting a new pet because you are ready to love again, not because you are trying to replace the one you lost. Every pet is irreplaceable. A new animal will have their own personality and will build their own relationship with you.

When you are ready, consider adopting from the SPCA or a local rescue. Giving a home to an animal in need can be a beautiful way to honour the love you gave your previous pet.
