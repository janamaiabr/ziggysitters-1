Kitten Care Guide NZ: Everything New Cat Owners Need to Know

By ZiggySitters • March 3, 2026 • 12 min read

Bringing home a new kitten is one of life's great joys — and also one of its biggest responsibilities. New Zealand has some specific considerations for kitten owners, from local vaccination schedules to the unique plants in your garden that could harm your new furry family member. This comprehensive kitten care guide covers everything you need to raise a healthy, happy cat from day one.

Preparing Your Home Before Your Kitten Arrives

Kittens are curious, fearless, and remarkably good at getting into trouble. Before you bring your kitten home, do a thorough kitten-proofing inspection of every room they'll have access to.

Essential Safety Checks

  • Secure loose electrical cords and cables (kittens chew)
  • Remove or fence off toxic NZ houseplants (see our plant guide below)
  • Check for small gaps behind appliances where a kitten can get trapped
  • Install childproof latches on low cupboards containing cleaning products
  • Ensure all windows have secure fly screens — kittens will jump
  • Keep the toilet lid down; small kittens can fall in and drown
  • Store rubber bands, string, and hair ties safely out of reach
⚠ NZ-Specific Hazard: Toxic Plants Many common New Zealand garden and houseplants are toxic to cats. Remove or restrict access to agapanthus, lilies (all species), oleander, wandering dew, and kowhai. Even small amounts of lily pollen can cause fatal kidney failure in cats.

What to Buy Before Kitten Arrives

Set up a complete kitten station before bringing your new pet home:

  • Food and water bowls — ceramic or stainless steel, not plastic (plastic can cause chin acne)
  • Litter tray — at least one per cat plus one extra
  • Quality kitten food — both wet and dry
  • Cat carrier — essential for vet visits from day one
  • Scratching post — tall enough for a full stretch
  • Safe toys — avoid small pieces that can be swallowed
  • Cosy bed or blanket — ideally something with the breeder's scent

First Week: Settling In

The first week is all about letting your kitten build confidence at their own pace. Resist the urge to invite everyone over for a cuddle — give your kitten time to explore their new environment before introducing more people and experiences.

Set up a “base camp” room: a single room with their litter tray, food, water, bed, and a few toys. Once your kitten is confidently using this space, gradually open up more of the house. This approach reduces overwhelm and helps them learn where the litter tray is.

Pro Tip: The Towel Trick Ask the breeder or shelter to send home a towel or blanket with your kitten's mother's scent. Place it in their bed for the first few nights. The familiar smell reduces stress significantly during the transition.

Feeding Your Kitten: NZ Guide

Nutrition is the foundation of your kitten's long-term health. Kittens have very different nutritional needs from adult cats — they need roughly twice the calories per kilogram and much higher levels of protein, fat, and certain vitamins.

How Much to Feed at Each Age

Age Meals Per Day Food Type Notes
8–12 weeks 4 meals Kitten wet + dry Small stomach; feed little and often
3–6 months 3 meals Kitten wet + dry Growth phase; do not restrict food
6–12 months 2–3 meals Kitten food Transition to adult food at 12 months
12 months+ 2 meals Adult cat food Adjust to healthy weight

In New Zealand, several quality local and imported brands are widely available including Royal Canin, Hills Science Diet, and Advance. Wet food is important for hydration; cats have a naturally low thirst drive and rely on food moisture to meet their water needs.

Foods to Strictly Avoid

  • Onions and garlic (all forms — toxic, cause anaemia)
  • Grapes and raisins (kidney failure)
  • Chocolate and caffeine
  • Raw fish fed regularly (destroys vitamin B1)
  • Cow's milk (most cats are lactose intolerant)
  • Any cooked bones (splintering hazard)

Vaccinations and Vet Care in NZ

New Zealand has a specific core vaccination schedule for cats. Understanding what's required — and what's optional — helps you have informed conversations with your vet.

Core Cat Vaccinations in NZ

The standard F3 vaccine (also called the tricat) protects against three diseases:

  • Feline panleukopenia (feline parvovirus) — highly contagious and potentially fatal
  • Feline herpesvirus (cat flu) — causes respiratory illness
  • Feline calicivirus (cat flu) — second major cause of cat flu

The typical vaccination schedule starts at 8 weeks, with boosters at 12 weeks and 16 weeks, then an annual or triennial booster depending on your vet's protocol.

🔴 Note on FeLV in NZ Feline leukaemia virus (FeLV) vaccination is not considered core in New Zealand as the disease prevalence is relatively low. However, if your cat goes outdoors or mixes with unknown cats, your vet may recommend it. Discuss your individual situation.

Desexing

Desexing (neutering/spaying) is strongly recommended in New Zealand and is legally required in some council areas. Most vets recommend desexing at 4–6 months, before sexual maturity. Benefits include preventing unwanted litters, reducing roaming behaviour, eliminating the risk of certain cancers, and reducing fighting in male cats.

Microchipping

Microchipping is compulsory for cats in several New Zealand councils and highly recommended everywhere. The chip is no larger than a grain of rice and is implanted under the skin between the shoulder blades. Register your details on the New Zealand Companion Animal Register (NZCAR) after chipping.

Litter Training

Most kittens learn to use a litter tray remarkably quickly — especially if their mother used one. The key is placement, cleanliness, and choosing a litter your kitten accepts.

  • Location: Quiet, private, and easy to access at all times. Never near the food bowl.
  • Litter type: Start with fine, unscented clumping litter. Avoid strongly scented litters; cats often reject them.
  • Cleaning: Scoop daily, full change weekly. Cats refuse to use a dirty tray and will find alternatives on your floor.
  • Number of trays: One per cat plus one extra is the golden rule. Two kittens need three trays.
  • Size: The tray should be at least 1.5 times the length of your cat. Many commercial trays are too small.

Socialisation: The Critical Window

The socialisation window for cats is 2–9 weeks of age. During this period, kittens form lasting impressions about what is safe and normal in the world. If your kitten was well-socialised by the breeder or shelter during this time, they'll be far more adaptable as adults.

Once your kitten is home and settled, gently expose them to:

  • Different people, including children and people with hats or glasses
  • Gentle handling of paws, ears, and mouth (important for vet visits and grooming)
  • Common household sounds like vacuum cleaners and washing machines
  • The cat carrier (make it a positive space with treats, not just a vet trip vehicle)
  • Other pets in a controlled, positive way
Tip: Carrier Training from Day One Leave the cat carrier out permanently as a cosy sleep spot. Feed treats inside it. Kittens who associate the carrier with safety travel far less stressfully throughout their lives — something pet sitters and vets deeply appreciate.

Indoor vs Outdoor: The NZ Debate

New Zealand has one of the most passionate debates about cat containment in the world, and with good reason. Native birds like the tui, fantail, and kereru have not evolved alongside feline predators and are highly vulnerable. Several New Zealand councils are introducing cat containment bylaws.

Options to consider:

  • Fully indoor: Safest for both cat and wildlife. Requires environmental enrichment to prevent boredom.
  • Contained garden: Cat-proof fencing or a purpose-built outdoor enclosure (catio) allows fresh air safely.
  • Leash trained: Some cats adapt well to harness and lead walks.
  • Dusk to dawn indoors: If you allow outdoor access, keeping cats in at night significantly reduces bird predation and traffic risk.

Common Health Issues to Watch For

Knowing the signs of illness in kittens means you'll catch problems early when they're most treatable:

  • Upper respiratory infection (cat flu): Sneezing, runny nose, watery eyes. Common in kittens from shelters; needs prompt vet attention.
  • Fleas: Scratching, tiny black specks in the coat. Use vet-recommended flea treatment — many over-the-counter products are ineffective or unsafe for kittens.
  • Worms: Pot belly, scooting, visible segments in faeces. Worm every 2 weeks until 12 weeks, then monthly until 6 months, then quarterly.
  • Ringworm: Despite the name, it's a fungal infection causing circular patches of hair loss. It's zoonotic, meaning humans can catch it.
  • Diarrhoea: Common with diet changes or stress. Persistent diarrhoea needs vet attention as kittens dehydrate quickly.

When You Travel: Finding a Kitten Sitter in NZ

Kittens should not be left alone for long periods. While adult cats can manage overnight with appropriate food and water, kittens under 6 months need someone checking in at least twice daily. Young kittens are prone to health issues that can deteriorate rapidly if unnoticed.

When looking for a cat sitter for your kitten, prioritise:

  • Experience with kittens specifically, not just adult cats
  • Knowledge of early warning signs of illness
  • Willingness to follow your kitten's specific routine exactly
  • Clear communication — daily photo updates are a reasonable expectation
  • Familiarity with your vet and emergency vet locations
ZiggySitters Tip: Build the Sitter Relationship Early Introduce your kitten to their pet sitter while you are still home. Have the sitter visit 2–3 times before your first trip away so your kitten knows and trusts them. This dramatically reduces separation stress — for both the kitten and you.

Your First-Year Kitten Checklist

  • Vet check within 48 hours of bringing kitten home
  • F3 vaccination series complete by 16 weeks
  • Microchipped and registered on NZCAR
  • Desexed by 4–6 months
  • Flea, tick, and worm treatment plan established
  • Pet insurance arranged (before any pre-existing conditions)
  • Kitten-proof home confirmed
  • Trusted cat sitter identified and introduced
  • Emergency vet contact saved in your phone
  • Transitioned to adult food at 12 months

Final Thoughts

Raising a kitten in New Zealand comes with some unique considerations — from our native wildlife and cat containment laws to local vaccination protocols — but the core of good kitten care is universal: good nutrition, early vet care, gentle socialisation, and a safe, enriched environment. Invest time and attention in these first months and you'll be rewarded with a confident, affectionate companion for the next 15–20 years.
