Birds are among New Zealand\'s most popular pets after cats and dogs, and for good reason. They are intelligent, social, and deeply rewarding companions. But bird care is fundamentally different from caring for a cat or dog. Their dietary needs are more precise, their environmental requirements are more delicate, and their emotional needs are more complex than many first-time bird owners expect. Whether you have a chatty budgie, a whistling cockatiel, or a demanding African Grey parrot, understanding how to care for your feathered companion, and how to find the right person to care for them when you cannot, is essential. This guide covers the specific needs of common NZ pet bird species, how to prepare your bird for a sitter, and what to look for when hiring someone to mind your bird.

Budgerigar (Budgie) Care in NZ

Budgerigars, commonly called budgies, are the most popular pet bird in New Zealand. They are small, affordable, relatively easy to care for, and capable of learning to talk and perform tricks. A healthy budgie can live 8 to 15 years, which makes them a significant long-term commitment despite their low purchase price. In NZ, budgies are widely available from breeders, pet shops, and rescue organisations, and they adapt well to indoor living in most New Zealand homes.

Diet is where most budgie owners go wrong. A seed-only diet is deficient in vitamins A, D, and calcium, and leads to obesity, liver disease, and shortened lifespan. A proper budgie diet should be based on a high-quality pellet formulated for small parrots, supplemented with fresh vegetables including dark leafy greens like silverbeet and kale, broccoli florets, grated carrot, and small amounts of fruit as a treat. Seeds should be offered as a supplement or training reward, not as the primary food. Clean fresh water must be changed daily, and a cuttlebone or mineral block should always be available for calcium and beak maintenance.

Budgies are highly social animals that need daily interaction. A single budgie bonds strongly with its human and requires at least 30 to 60 minutes of direct interaction per day, including talking, training, and out-of-cage time in a bird-safe room. A pair of budgies will keep each other company, but they may become less tame and less interested in interacting with humans. If you want a talkative, tame companion, a single budgie with plenty of human attention is the better path. If you work long hours, a second budgie is a kindness that prevents loneliness, but accept that the birds will be more interested in each other than in you.

Cage size matters more than most new owners expect. The minimum for a single budgie is 45 cm wide by 45 cm deep by 60 cm high, but bigger is always better. Horizontal bar spacing of 12 mm or less prevents escape attempts and injuries. The cage should be placed in a living area where the bird can see and hear family activity, but away from the kitchen, where cooking fumes from non-stick pans (containing PTFE) can be fatal to birds. NZ homes with open-plan living need to think carefully about cage placement relative to the cooking area.

Cockatiel Care in New Zealand

Cockatiels are the second most popular pet bird in NZ after budgies, and they offer a wonderful middle ground between small and large parrots. They are larger than budgies, roughly 30 cm from beak to tail, and can live 15 to 25 years with good care. Their defining characteristic is their expressive crest, which rises when they are alert or excited and flattens when they are relaxed or unwell. Cockatiels are renowned for their whistling ability, with males in particular learning complex tunes and being more vocal than females.

Cockatiel diet mirrors budgie diet in principle but with larger pellets and slightly different proportions. A pellet-based diet (approximately 60 to 70 percent of daily intake) supplemented with vegetables, small amounts of seed, and occasional fruit is the foundation. Cockatiels are prone to fatty liver disease and obesity, so high-fat seeds like sunflower and safflower should be strictly limited. Fresh water in a clean dish is essential, and many cockatiels enjoy bathing, so offering a shallow dish of water for splashing a few times a week provides both hygiene and enrichment.

One health issue that disproportionately affects cockatiels in New Zealand is night frights. Also called night thrashing, this is a panic response where the bird thrashes around the cage in the dark, often injuring itself on bars or toys. It is more common in NZ because many homes are dark and quiet at night, and sudden noises from outside, such as possums on the roof, can trigger an episode. To reduce the risk, provide a night light near the cage, cover the cage partially rather than completely so the bird can orient itself, and avoid placing the cage near windows where outside movement or reflections might startle the bird. If your cockatiel does experience a night fright, check for injuries, especially broken blood feathers on the wings, which can bleed significantly and require veterinary attention.

Cockatiels also produce a fine powder down that helps waterproof their feathers. This powder is normal and healthy, but it can be a respiratory irritant for people with asthma or allergies. If anyone in your household has respiratory issues, consider an air purifier near the cage and ensure good ventilation. Bathing the cockatiel regularly also reduces airborne powder.

Parrot Care for NZ Owners

The term "parrot" covers a wide range of species, from small conures and lovebirds to medium-sized Indian Ringnecks and Alexandrines, all the way up to large cockatoos and macaws. In New Zealand, the most commonly kept medium-to-large parrots are Indian Ringnecks, Alexandrines, Eclectus parrots, and various cockatoo species. Each has distinct care requirements, but some principles apply across the board.

Diet

All parrots need a pellet-based diet as their nutritional foundation, supplemented with fresh vegetables, some fruit, and limited seed. The exact proportions vary by species, but as a general rule, pellets should make up 60 to 80 percent of daily intake, vegetables 20 to 30 percent, and seeds and fruit the remaining 5 to 10 percent. Avoid avocado, chocolate, caffeine, alcohol, onion, garlic, and fruit seeds and pits, all of which are toxic to birds. In NZ, seasonal fruits like feijoas, kiwifruit, and tamarillos can be offered in small amounts as treats.

Enrichment and Mental Stimulation

Parrots are among the most intelligent animals kept as pets, and their need for mental stimulation cannot be overstated. An under-stimulated parrot will develop behavioural problems including screaming, feather plucking, aggression, and self-harm. Enrichment for parrots includes foraging toys that require problem-solving to access food, shredding toys that satisfy natural destructive instincts, rotating perches and toys to prevent boredom, training sessions that challenge the bird to learn new behaviours, and supervised out-of-cage time for flight and exploration. Plan to replace destructible toys regularly. A parrot that destroys its toys is a healthy, engaged parrot. A parrot that ignores its toys for days may be depressed, ill, or under-stimulated.

Social Needs

Large parrots, especially cockatoos and African Greys, form intense bonds with their human companions and can develop severe separation anxiety when that bond is disrupted. This makes them challenging pets for people who travel frequently or work long hours outside the home. Before acquiring a large parrot, honestly assess whether you can provide 3 to 4 hours of direct interaction daily for the next 30 to 80 years, which is the potential lifespan of many large parrot species. If you cannot, a smaller, more independent species is a more ethical choice.

Finding a Bird Sitter in New Zealand

Bird sitting is fundamentally different from dog or cat sitting, and finding the right person requires more care. Birds hide illness as a survival instinct, so a sitter who does not know what subtle signs to watch for may miss the early indicators of a health crisis. A dog sitter who "also has birds" is not the same as an experienced bird sitter. Here is what to look for and what to prepare.

What to Look for in a Bird Sitter

  • Direct bird experience: Your sitter should have hands-on experience with your species of bird, not just general pet experience. A person who has kept budgies is not necessarily equipped to handle a cockatoo, and vice versa. Ask specific questions about what birds they have owned or cared for, and for how long.
  • Knowledge of bird body language: A good bird sitter can read the difference between a relaxed bird and a stressed one. They should know that a budgie grinding its beak is content, that a cockatiel with a flattened crest and hissing is defensive, and that a parrot with dilated pupils and raised foot feathers is overstimulated and may bite.
  • Understanding of bird-safe environments: Bird sitters need to know about common household hazards: non-stick cookware fumes, scented candles and essential oils, ceiling fans, open windows, other pets, and toxic houseplants. If your sitter is staying in your home, they must be willing to follow your bird-safety rules without exception.
  • Medication experience: If your bird requires medication, make sure the sitter is comfortable administering it. Birds are notoriously difficult to medicate, and a sitter who has never given oral medication to a squirming cockatiel may not be able to do it effectively.
  • Emergency preparedness: Ask your sitter what they would do if your bird stopped eating, started vomiting, or injured itself. The correct answer involves calling you immediately and contacting your avian vet. A sitter who says "I would just keep an eye on it" does not understand how quickly birds can decline.

Preparing Your Bird for a Sitter

Birds are creatures of routine, and any disruption to their daily schedule can cause stress. The more you can do to make the transition smooth, the better your bird will cope with your absence. Here is a preparation checklist that applies to all bird species.

  1. Introduce the sitter before you leave. Arrange at least one meet-and-greet where the sitter visits your home and interacts with your bird while you are present. For parrots and other intelligent species, multiple short visits over several days work better than one long introduction. The goal is for the sitter to become a familiar face, not a stranger who suddenly appears when you are gone.
  2. Write a detailed care sheet. Include feeding amounts and schedule, fresh food preparation instructions, cage cleaning routine and products used, medication administration if applicable, your bird\'s normal behaviour patterns so the sitter can spot changes, and emergency contact information including your vet\'s number and a backup contact if you are unreachable.
  3. Pre-portion food and medications. Measure out daily portions of pellets and seeds into separate containers for each day you will be away. This removes any ambiguity about how much to feed and prevents overfeeding, which is a common mistake with birds. If your bird takes medication, pre-measure doses and label them clearly by day and time.
  4. Maintain the routine. Birds notice changes in timing. If your bird is usually uncovered at 7 AM, fed at 7:30 AM, and given out-of-cage time at 6 PM, ask your sitter to follow the same schedule. Even 30 minutes of deviation can cause anxiety in sensitive species.
  5. Prepare the environment. Ensure the cage is clean before you leave, with fresh perches, clean water dishes, and new or rotated toys. Stock up on supplies including extra food, clean cage liners, and any medications so the sitter does not need to go shopping. Leave a list of where everything is stored.

Home Boarding vs In-Home Sitting for Birds

Birds should almost always be cared for in their own home rather than being transported to a sitter\'s house. The stress of transport, a new environment, and the presence of other animals in the sitter\'s home can be overwhelming for a bird, particularly small species like budgies and canaries that are naturally prey animals. In-home sitting eliminates transport stress, keeps the bird in familiar surroundings, and reduces the risk of disease exposure from other animals.

The exception is when a sitter operates a dedicated bird boarding facility with proper quarantine protocols, appropriate housing, and no other animals on site. These are rare in New Zealand but do exist in larger centres like Auckland and Wellington. If you choose this option, verify that the facility requires proof of health checks, maintains separate air spaces between boarded birds, and has an avian vet on call.

Common NZ Pet Bird Health Issues

Understanding the health issues that commonly affect pet birds in New Zealand helps you and your sitter spot problems early. Birds are masters at hiding illness, so subtle changes in behaviour are often the only warning signs.

Respiratory Infections

NZ\'s variable climate, with cold damp winters and warm humid summers, creates conditions where respiratory infections thrive. Symptoms include sneezing, nasal discharge, laboured breathing, tail bobbing, and decreased activity. If your sitter reports any of these signs, contact your vet immediately. Birds can die from respiratory infections within 24 to 48 hours if untreated.

Feather Plucking and Self-Mutilation

Feather plucking is a behavioural issue that can also have medical causes including skin infections, nutritional deficiencies, and hormonal imbalances. In NZ, low humidity during winter when homes are heated can contribute to dry, itchy skin that triggers plucking. A humidifier near the cage and regular bathing opportunities help prevent this. If your bird starts plucking while you are away, your sitter should notify you immediately, as early intervention has a much better prognosis than chronic plucking.

Psittacosis (Chlamydiosis)

Psittacosis is a bacterial infection that can affect all bird species and is zoonotic, meaning it can be transmitted to humans. It is present in NZ wild bird populations, particularly in feral pigeons and some parrot species. Symptoms in birds include lethargy, loss of appetite, green or yellow droppings, and respiratory distress. If your sitter develops flu-like symptoms while caring for your bird, they should seek medical attention and mention psittacosis exposure to their doctor.

Bird-Safe and Bird-Toxic Plants in NZ

Many common NZ garden and house plants are toxic to birds. If your bird has out-of-cage time, or if your sitter allows your bird to fly in the house, ensure the following toxic plants are removed or inaccessible: avocado (all parts), kowhai (seeds are toxic to birds despite being native), oleander, poinsettia, lily (all parts), daffodil bulbs, and rhododendron. Bird-safe plants that can be placed near or in the cage include spider plant, Boston fern, bamboo palm, and sword fern. Always verify plant safety before introducing anything new to your bird\'s environment.

Avian Vets in New Zealand

Not all veterinarians in NZ have experience with birds. Before you need one, find an avian vet or a vet with significant bird experience in your area. In Auckland, the Avian & Exotic Animal Clinic and several veterinary centres offer bird-specific services. In Wellington, the Wellington Zoo Veterinary Centre and selected private clinics provide avian care. In Christchurch and Dunedin, options are more limited, so establish a relationship with a vet who has bird experience before an emergency arises. Leave your avian vet\'s contact details with your sitter, along with a backup after-hours vet, and authorise your sitter to make treatment decisions up to a specified dollar amount in case you cannot be reached.
