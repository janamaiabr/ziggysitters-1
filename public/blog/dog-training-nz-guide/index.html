Essential Dog Training Commands in NZ: A Beginner\'s Guide

Published April 12, 2026 · 14 min read

Teaching your dog basic commands is one of the most rewarding things you can do as a dog owner. Beyond the convenience of a well-behaved pet, training builds a deep bond of communication and trust between you and your furry companion.

New Zealand dog owners have a particular responsibility - our outdoor lifestyle means many dogs enjoy off-leash freedom in beautiful spaces like beaches, forests, and regional parks. Earning that privilege starts with solid foundational training.

This guide walks you through the five essential commands every NZ dog should know, using positive reinforcement methods backed by veterinary science and recommended by the New Zealand Veterinary Association.

Why Positive Reinforcement Works Best

Positive reinforcement means marking the exact moment your dog does something right and immediately rewarding them. This creates a clear mental connection: "When I do X, Y happens" - and your dog wants to repeat the behaviour to earn more rewards.

The training formula: Command + Correct Response + Reward = Learned Behaviour.

Rewards can be treats, praise, play, or access to something your dog wants (like going outside). Mix it up to keep your dog engaged and motivated.

Avoid punishment-based methods. Yelling, alpha rolls, or corrective collars can cause fear, anxiety, and aggression - and they simply do not teach your dog what you actually want them to do.

Command #1: Sit

Why it matters: Sit is the foundation of all other training. It calms your dog, gives you control in tricky situations, and is the basis for polite greetings.

How to teach sit:

  1. Hold a treat near your dog\'s nose - let them smell it but not grab it.
  2. Slowly move the treat up and slightly back over their head.
  3. As their head goes up, their bottom naturally lowers.
  4. The moment their bottom hits the floor, say "Sit" clearly and give the treat.
  5. Celebrate! Praise them enthusiastically.
  6. Repeat 5-10 times, then take a break.

Puppies can learn sit in just one session. Adult dogs new to training typically grasp it within a few days. Once your dog understands the word, start asking for sit without the lure - just the verbal command.

Command #2: Stay

Why it matters: Stay impulse controls. It keeps your dog safe at the vet, lets you walk through doors first, and prevents boundary breaches.

How to teach stay:

  1. Ask your dog to sit (they should already know this).
  2. Hold your palm out like a stop sign and say "Stay."
  3. Take one step back. If they stay, immediately return and reward.
  4. Gradually increase distance and duration over sessions.
  5. Always release with a specific word like "OK" or "Free."

Build duration and distance separately - don\'t practise long stays at long distances until each is solid on its own.

Command #3: Come (Recall)

Why it matters: Recall could save your dog\'s life. In NZ, dogs with reliable recall can enjoy off-leash freedom - without it, you\'re limited to leash-only areas.

NZ context: Under the Dog Control Act, owners can be prosecuted if their dog attacks or chases livestock. Reliable recall keeps both your dog and farm animals safe.

How to teach recall:

  1. Start indoors in a low-distraction environment.
  2. Say your dog\'s name and "Come" in an excited, happy voice.
  3. When they come, give a high-value treat and make a fuss.
  4. Never call your dog to punish them - they must associate coming to you with good things.
  5. Practise randomly throughout the day so coming becomes a fun game.

Once recall is solid at home, gradually increase distraction levels - first with toys, then with other dogs, then in outdoor environments. This is where NZ parks and beaches become your training ground.

NZ recall tip: Carry high-value treats on walks. Randomly call your dog and reward - they never know when the "game" will start!

Command #4: Down (Lie Down)

Why it matters: Down is essential for vet visits, polite restaurant behaviour, and settling. It is also the foundation for the useful "place" command.

How to teach down:

  1. With your dog in sit, hold the treat to their nose.
  2. Slowly lower the treat straight down to the floor between their paws.
  3. As they follow it into lying position, say "Down" and reward.
  4. Some dogs find this harder than sit - be patient and break it into smaller steps if needed.

Command #5: Leave It

Why it matters: Leave it could save your dog from eating something toxic (like chocolate or rat poison), a sharp object, or another animal.

How to teach leave it:

  1. Place a treat in both hands - show one fist with treat, one flat palm without.
  2. Say "Leave it" while presenting the flat palm.
  3. When they stop trying to get it (even slight hesitation), reward from the other hand.
  4. Progress to treating from the floor - "leave it" means do not touch until released.
  5. Practise with items on the floor - progressively harder distractions.

Practising Training in NZ Environments

Training at home is just the start. For commands to be reliable in real-world situations, you must generalise the learning:

  • Start easy: Indoor to backyard, quiet street, calm park
  • Add distractions gradually: Other dogs, people, squirrels, food, play
  • Practise everywhere: Different surfaces, weather, times of day
  • NZ-specific locations: Beaches, dog parks, walking tracks, cafes with outdoor areas

The key is to set your dog up for success. If your dog is struggling in a distracting environment, you have asked too much too soon - go back to an easier level and build up again.

Common Training Mistakes to Avoid

  • Training too long: Puppies have 5-minute attention spans. Shorter, more frequent sessions beat long, occasional ones.
  • Inconsistency: Everyone in the household must use the same commands and rules.
  • Repeating commands: Say it once. Repeating teaches your dog they do not need to listen the first time.
  • Rewarding for nothing: Always mark the exact behaviour you want before treating.
  • Giving up too soon: Reliability takes weeks or months, not days.

When to Get Professional Help

Some situations benefit from professional guidance:

  • Aggression or significant fear
  • No progress despite consistent effort
  • Specific behavioural issues (biting, severe anxiety)
  • Your dog is a breed with strong working instincts (need experienced handling)

NZ has excellent positive reinforcement trainers. Look for those certified by the Institute of Modern Dog Trainers (IMDT) or recommended by your vet.

Training Makes Life Better

Beyond the practical benefits, training your dog creates a language you share. Your dog learns what you expect, feels confident in their environment, and genuinely enjoys performing trained behaviours because they have been rewarded for doing so.

Training is also mental exercise - a tired dog is a well-behaved dog, and mentally stimulated dogs are calmer and happier than those without enough to think about.

Whether you are preparing for off-leash beach adventures in Whangarei, cafe hopping in Wellington, or simply enjoying quiet evenings at home, the investment you put into training now pays dividends for your entire life together.
