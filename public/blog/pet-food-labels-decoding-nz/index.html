How to Read Pet Food Labels in NZ: A Complete Guide

Published 10 June 2026 • 12 min read • ZiggySitters NZ

Standing in the pet food aisle can feel overwhelming. With bold claims like "grain-free," "natural," and "premium" staring back at you, how do you know what you are actually feeding your pet? This guide breaks down every part of a pet food label sold in New Zealand so you can make informed, confident choices for your dog or cat.

Why Pet Food Labels Matter

Your pet eats the same food every day. Unlike humans, who variety-eat across food groups, your dog or cat relies on that single bag or tin for complete nutrition. A label that looks impressive at first glance might hide cheap fillers, ambiguous protein sources, or nutritional gaps that only become apparent over months or years.

In New Zealand, pet food is regulated under the Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code and the Agricultural Compounds and Veterinary Medicines (ACVM) Act. However, the rules around labelling are less strict than human food standards, which means manufacturers have significant room for creative marketing. Understanding how to read past the marketing spin is essential.

The Key Sections of a Pet Food Label

1. Product Name and Naming Rules

The product name is not just branding. It actually tells you how much of the named ingredient is in the food, thanks to internationally recognised naming conventions:

Naming PatternWhat It MeansExample
"Beef" (95% rule)At least 95% of the product must be the named ingredient (excluding water for processing)Beef Dinner for Dogs
"Beef Dinner" or "Beef Platter" (25% rule)At least 25% of the named ingredient, but less than 95%Beef Dinner with Vegetables
"With Beef" (3% rule)As little as 3% of the named ingredientDog Food with Beef
"Beef Flavour" (0% rule)Only needs to be detectable by a pet — no minimum percentage requiredBeef Flavour Biscuits
Watch out: "With beef" sounds similar to "beef dinner" but could contain 22% less of the named ingredient. Always check the ingredient list to confirm.

2. Ingredient List

Ingredients are listed by weight before processing, from heaviest to lightest. This is the single most important part of the label. Here is what to look for:

  • Named animal protein first: "Chicken," "lamb," or "salmon" is better than "meat" or "animal by-products." A named protein tells you exactly what your pet is eating.
  • Whole meat vs. meal: "Chicken meal" is actually more protein-dense than "chicken" because whole chicken is roughly 70% water that evaporates during processing. Both are acceptable, but meal provides more protein per gram.
  • Splitting: If you see "rice, rice flour, rice bran" listed separately, the manufacturer may be splitting a single filler ingredient to push a meat ingredient higher on the list. Combined, the rice content could exceed the meat content.
  • By-products: Not all by-products are bad. Organ meats like liver and heart are nutritionally rich. However, vague terms like "animal by-products" without naming the species are a red flag.
Quick check: The first two ingredients should be named animal proteins. If grains or fillers dominate the top five, the food is likely carbohydrate-heavy with modest protein quality.

3. Guaranteed Analysis

The guaranteed analysis panel shows minimum or maximum levels of key nutrients:

NutrientWhat It MeansTypical Range (Dry Food)
Crude Protein (min)Minimum protein contentDogs: 18-30%, Cats: 26-40%
Crude Fat (min)Minimum fat content8-20%
Crude Fibre (max)Maximum fibre (indigestible plant material)2-6%
Moisture (max)Maximum water contentDry: 10-12%, Wet: 70-85%

These numbers are on an "as-fed" basis, meaning they include water. To compare dry and wet foods fairly, you need to convert to a dry matter basis:

Dry Matter Conversion:
Dry Matter % = 100% - Moisture %
Dry Matter Protein = (Crude Protein % ÷ Dry Matter %) × 100

Example: A wet food with 10% protein and 78% moisture:
Dry Matter = 100 - 78 = 22%
Dry Matter Protein = (10 ÷ 22) × 100 = 45.5%

That same protein looks like only 10% until you do the math. Always convert before comparing.

4. Nutritional Adequacy Statement

This is the statement that tells you the food is complete and balanced for a specific life stage. Look for one of two formats:

  • AAFCO statement: "Formulated to meet the nutritional levels established by the AAFCO Dog/Cat Food Nutrient Profiles for [life stage]." This means the food was formulated on paper but not necessarily tested on animals.
  • Feeding trial statement: "Animal feeding tests using AAFCO procedures substantiate that [Product] provides complete and balanced nutrition for [life stage]." This is stronger — the food was actually fed to animals and the results measured.
Best choice: Foods with feeding trial statements have been tested in real conditions. Formulated-only foods meet the minimum requirements on paper but have not been verified through actual feeding.

5. Feeding Guidelines

The feeding guide tells you how much to feed based on your pet\'s weight. However, these are starting estimates. Your pet\'s actual needs depend on activity level, age, metabolism, and whether they are neutered. An active working dog on a lifestyle block in the Waikato needs significantly more calories than a sedentary apartment cat in Wellington.

Use the guidelines as a baseline, then adjust based on your pet\'s body condition. You should be able to feel — but not see — the ribs on a dog, and a cat should have a visible waist from above.

Decoding Marketing Claims

"Grain-Free"

Grain-free does not mean low-carb or higher-quality. Most grain-free foods replace wheat, corn, and rice with peas, lentils, potatoes, or sweet potato — ingredients that are still carbohydrate sources. The FDA in the United States has been investigating a potential link between grain-free diets and dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) in dogs, though the evidence remains inconclusive.

If your dog has a diagnosed grain allergy (which is rare — most food allergies in dogs are protein-based, not grain-based), a grain-free diet makes sense. Otherwise, whole grains like brown rice and oats can be excellent, affordable sources of fibre and nutrients.

"Natural" and "Holistic"

Neither term has a strict legal definition in New Zealand pet food regulation. "Natural" typically means the food does not contain chemically synthesised ingredients, but enforcement is loose. "Holistic" is pure marketing — it means nothing from a nutritional standpoint.

"Premium," "Super Premium," "Ultra Premium"

These are self-assigned labels with no regulatory threshold. A "premium" food may contain the same ingredients as a mid-range product. Always check the ingredient list and guaranteed analysis rather than trusting the branding.

"Human-Grade"

This claim is meaningful only if it is verified. In New Zealand, true human-grade pet food must be manufactured in facilities that meet human food safety standards. If the label says "made with human-grade ingredients," only some components may meet that standard. Look for "100% human-grade" for the whole product.

"Made in New Zealand"

NZ-made pet food benefits from strict agricultural standards and high-quality local ingredients (particularly lamb, beef, and seafood). However, "made in" does not always mean "sourced in." The ingredients may still be imported. If supporting local producers matters to you, look for "made in NZ with NZ ingredients" or "100% NZ sourced."

New Zealand-Specific Regulations

Pet food in New Zealand is regulated under several frameworks:

  • ACVM Act 1997: Covers veterinary medicines and some functional pet food claims (e.g., dental health, joint support). Products making therapeutic claims may need registration.
  • Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code: Applies to pet food safety and labelling standards shared with Australia.
  • NZ Pet Food Manufacturers Association (NZPFMA): A voluntary industry body. Members commit to quality and safety standards, but membership is not mandatory.
NZ-specific tip: Look for the NZPFMA logo on packaging. While not a guarantee of quality, it signals the manufacturer follows industry safety standards. Major NZ brands like Ziwi Peak, K9 Natural, and Addiction Pet Foods are all NZPFMA members.

Red Flags on a Pet Food Label

  • "Meat" or "animal protein" without naming the species — you deserve to know what species your pet is eating
  • Artificial colours (Red 40, Yellow 5, etc.) — these are for human appeal, not pet nutrition
  • Sugar or sweeteners (sorbitol, corn syrup) — unnecessary and potentially harmful
  • Propylene glycol — banned in cat food in many countries; still appears in some dog food
  • BHA, BHT, or ethoxyquin — synthetic preservatives with controversial safety profiles
  • No AAFCO statement — the food may not be nutritionally complete
  • Vague life stage claims — "for all life stages" often means it meets minimum adult requirements but may not be ideal for puppies, kittens, or seniors
Critical for cats: Cats are obligate carnivores. A food that lists plant proteins (soy, corn gluten meal) ahead of animal proteins is fundamentally unsuitable for feline nutrition. Cats also require taurine, arachidonic acid, and preformed vitamin A — nutrients only found in animal tissue.

For cats especially, wet food provides crucial hydration. Cats evolved in arid environments and have a low thirst drive. A cat eating only dry food is likely chronically under-hydrated, which contributes to kidney disease and urinary tract issues — both common problems in NZ veterinary clinics.

Practical Label-Reading Checklist

Next time you shop for pet food, run through this list:

  1. Is the first ingredient a named animal protein? (chicken, lamb, salmon — not "meat")
  2. Are the first two ingredients animal-based? (protein-dense foods should lead with animal ingredients)
  3. Is there an AAFCO adequacy statement? (preferably with feeding trials)
  4. Does the life stage match your pet? (growth, maintenance, or all life stages)
  5. Is the guaranteed analysis appropriate? (compare on a dry matter basis)
  6. Are there unnecessary artificial colours or preservatives?
  7. Does the feeding guide match your vet\'s recommendation for your pet\'s weight and activity?
  8. If making a claim (grain-free, human-grade, NZ-made), is it specific and verifiable?

When Your Pet Sitter Needs to Know

If you use a pet sitter, make sure they understand your pet\'s dietary requirements. A common mistake is overfeeding or mixing foods that should not be combined. Write down:

  • The exact brand and formula name
  • Portion size per meal (weighed, not estimated)
  • How many meals per day
  • Any supplements or toppers added
  • Foods that are strictly off-limits

Frequently Asked Questions

Is expensive pet food always better?

Not always. Price reflects ingredient quality, manufacturing processes, and marketing spend. A mid-priced food with named proteins and an AAFCO feeding trial statement may outperform a "super premium" product that relies on formulation alone. Read the label, not the price tag.

Should I rotate my pet\'s food?

Moderate rotation (switching between two or three high-quality foods) can broaden nutrient intake and reduce the risk of developing food sensitivities. Always transition gradually over 7-10 days to avoid digestive upset.

Are NZ pet food standards the same as Australia\'s?

Largely yes. Both countries operate under the joint Food Standards Code. However, enforcement varies. The NZPFMA provides voluntary guidelines that go beyond minimum standards for member companies.

Can I trust online reviews of pet food?

With caution. Many reviews focus on palatability (whether the pet ate it) rather than nutritional completeness. A food your dog loves eating is not necessarily a food that meets their long-term health needs. Prioritise the ingredient list and guaranteed analysis over star ratings.
