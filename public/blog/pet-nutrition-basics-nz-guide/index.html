Pet Nutrition Basics: A New Zealand Guide to Feeding Dogs and Cats

Published 20 February 2026 · 7 min read

Getting your pet\'s nutrition right is one of the most important things you can do as a pet owner — or as a trusted pet sitter in New Zealand. Whether you\'re caring for a bouncy Labrador in Auckland or a pampered Persian cat in Wellington, understanding the fundamentals of pet nutrition ensures your furry friend stays healthy, energetic, and happy.

New Zealand has unique considerations when it comes to pet food. From locally made brands to seasonal produce that can supplement your pet\'s diet, this guide covers everything you need to know about feeding dogs and cats in Aotearoa.

Why Nutrition Matters for Your Pet

Proper nutrition is the foundation of your pet\'s overall health. A well-fed pet will have a shiny coat, strong teeth, healthy weight, and plenty of energy for walks around the neighbourhood or play sessions in the garden. Poor nutrition, on the other hand, can lead to obesity, dental problems, dull fur, digestive issues, and even shortened lifespan.

According to New Zealand veterinary professionals, the most common health issues they treat — including obesity, diabetes, and skin conditions — are often directly linked to diet. Investing time in understanding what your pet needs nutritionally can save significant vet bills down the track.

Understanding Dog Nutrition in New Zealand

Essential Nutrients for Dogs

Dogs are omnivores, which means they thrive on a balanced diet of protein, carbohydrates, fats, vitamins, and minerals. Here\'s a breakdown of what your dog needs:

  • Protein: The building block of muscles, skin, and coat. Look for dog foods where real meat (lamb, chicken, beef, or fish) is the first ingredient. New Zealand lamb is an excellent protein source that many NZ-made dog foods feature prominently.
  • Fats: Essential for energy and a healthy coat. Omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids, often sourced from New Zealand fish oils, support brain function and reduce inflammation.
  • Carbohydrates: Provide energy and fibre for digestive health. Sweet potato, brown rice, and pumpkin are common in quality dog foods available across New Zealand.
  • Vitamins and minerals: Support immune function, bone health, and overall wellbeing. A complete commercial dog food should provide all essential micronutrients.
  • Water: Always ensure fresh, clean water is available. This is especially important during New Zealand\'s warm summers.

Choosing the Right Dog Food

New Zealand is home to several excellent pet food manufacturers. When selecting dog food, look for products that meet AAFCO (Association of American Feed Control Officials) or equivalent standards. Many NZ brands use locally sourced ingredients, including grass-fed lamb, wild-caught fish, and free-range poultry.

Consider your dog\'s age, size, and activity level when choosing food. Puppies need calorie-dense food with higher protein content, while senior dogs often benefit from lower-calorie formulas with joint-supporting supplements like glucosamine.

Portion Control

Overfeeding is one of the biggest nutritional mistakes pet owners make. Follow the feeding guidelines on your chosen food\'s packaging, but remember these are starting points. An active working dog on a Canterbury farm will need more calories than a lapdog living in a Ponsonby apartment. Your vet can help you determine the ideal portion size for your specific dog.

Understanding Cat Nutrition in New Zealand

Essential Nutrients for Cats

Unlike dogs, cats are obligate carnivores — they require a meat-based diet to survive and thrive. This is a critical distinction that every cat owner and pet sitter should understand.

  • Animal protein: Cats need significantly more protein than dogs. Chicken, fish, and lamb are excellent sources readily available in NZ cat foods.
  • Taurine: An essential amino acid that cats cannot produce on their own. Deficiency can cause serious heart and eye problems. All quality commercial cat foods include adequate taurine.
  • Fats: Arachidonic acid, found only in animal fats, is essential for cats. This is another reason why vegetarian diets are not suitable for felines.
  • Water: Cats are notorious for not drinking enough water. Wet food can help increase their hydration, which is particularly important for urinary tract health.

Wet Food vs Dry Food

The wet versus dry debate is ongoing among New Zealand cat owners. The truth is that both have their place. Dry food (biscuits) is convenient, helps with dental health, and is more economical. Wet food provides hydration, is more palatable for fussy eaters, and is closer to a cat\'s natural diet.

Many NZ vets recommend a combination of both — primarily wet food with some dry biscuits available for grazing. This approach gives your cat the hydration benefits of wet food while still providing the dental advantages of dry kibble.

Foods That Are Dangerous for Pets in New Zealand

Some common foods found in New Zealand homes can be toxic to pets. Make sure both pet owners and pet sitters are aware of these hazards:

  • Chocolate: Contains theobromine, which is toxic to both dogs and cats. Dark chocolate is the most dangerous.
  • Grapes and raisins: Can cause kidney failure in dogs, even in small quantities.
  • Onions and garlic: Damage red blood cells in both dogs and cats. This includes cooked onions in leftovers.
  • Macadamia nuts: Particularly relevant in New Zealand where macadamias are grown commercially. They can cause vomiting, tremors, and weakness in dogs.
  • Avocado: Contains persin, which can cause vomiting and diarrhoea in dogs and cats.
  • Xylitol: Found in sugar-free gum and some peanut butters. Extremely toxic to dogs.
  • Cooked bones: Can splinter and cause internal damage. Raw bones are generally safer but should always be supervised.

NZ-Specific Nutrition Considerations

Seasonal Feeding Adjustments

New Zealand\'s climate varies significantly from Northland to Southland, and seasonal changes should influence your pet\'s diet. During colder months (June to August), outdoor pets may need slightly more calories to maintain body temperature. In summer (December to February), ensure pets have constant access to fresh water and consider lighter meals during the hottest parts of the day.

Raw and Fresh Food Diets

Raw feeding has become increasingly popular in New Zealand, with several local companies offering raw pet food made from NZ-sourced meats. If you choose to feed raw, ensure the diet is nutritionally complete. A common mistake is feeding only muscle meat without organs, bones, and supplements.

Consult with your vet before switching to a raw diet, and always handle raw pet food with the same hygiene precautions you\'d use for raw meat intended for human consumption.

Local Produce Safe for Pets

New Zealand gardens and markets offer several pet-safe fruits and vegetables that make excellent treats and supplements:

  • Blueberries: Rich in antioxidants, great as training treats for dogs.
  • Pumpkin: Excellent for digestive health, easy to grow in NZ gardens.
  • Carrots: Low calorie, good for dental health, and dogs love the crunch.
  • Green-lipped mussels: A New Zealand specialty rich in omega-3s, excellent for joint health in older dogs.
  • Kumara: A Kiwi staple that\'s safe and nutritious for dogs when cooked.

Nutrition Tips for Pet Sitters

If you\'re a pet sitter in New Zealand, nutrition is a key part of providing excellent care. Here are some essential tips:

  • Follow the owner\'s instructions exactly. Stick to the specified food, portions, and feeding schedule. Sudden diet changes can cause digestive upset.
  • Ask about allergies and sensitivities. Many pets have food intolerances. Document these before the owner leaves.
  • Know where the food is stored. Dry food should be kept in an airtight container. Wet food needs refrigeration after opening.
  • Monitor appetite. A pet that suddenly refuses food could be stressed or unwell. Note any changes and inform the owner.
  • Resist the urge to give extra treats. It\'s tempting to win a pet over with treats, but overfeeding — even with treats — can cause problems.
  • Fresh water always. Check water bowls multiple times daily, especially in summer.

When to See a Vet About Nutrition

Consult a New Zealand veterinarian if your pet shows any of these signs that may be nutrition-related:

  • Unexplained weight gain or loss
  • Dull, flaky coat or excessive shedding
  • Chronic digestive issues (vomiting, diarrhoea, constipation)
  • Low energy or lethargy
  • Dental problems or bad breath
  • Skin irritation or itching that doesn\'t resolve

Many vet clinics across New Zealand now offer nutritional consultations where they can create a tailored feeding plan for your pet based on their specific needs, breed, age, and health conditions.
