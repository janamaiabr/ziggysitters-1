Pet Poisoning Prevention in NZ: Protecting Your Furry Friends

New Zealand is a paradise for pets—with beaches, parks, and endless adventures. But our homes and gardens can hide dangers that would surprise many pet owners. From everyday foods to common garden plants, knowing what threatens your furry friend could save their life.

Toxic Foods: The Kitchen Dangers

Chocolate and Caffeine

You\'ve likely heard this one before, but it bears repeating. Chocolate contains theobromine, which dogs metabolize very slowly. Dark chocolate is the most dangerous, but all chocolate should be kept well out of reach. The same goes for coffee, coffee grounds, and energy drinks.

Xylitol: The Hidden Danger

This artificial sweetener is increasingly common in sugar-free products, including some peanut butters, chewing gum, and baked goods. Xylitol causes a rapid insulin release in dogs, leading to dangerous drops in blood sugar and possible liver failure. Always check labels before sharing any human food.

Onions, Garlic, and Chives

All members of the allium family—onions, garlic, chives, leeks, and shallots—can cause damage to red blood cells in dogs and cats. While a small amount might only cause mild stomach upset, significant quantities can lead to anemia. This includes cooked, raw, and powdered forms.

Grapes and Raisins

Despite being a healthy snack for humans, grapes and raisins can cause acute kidney failure in dogs. Even small amounts should be treated as a veterinary emergency. Currants, which some New Zealand gardeners grow, are equally dangerous.

Alcohol and Raw Dough

Even small amounts of alcohol can cause vomiting, diarrhea, difficulty breathing, and death in pets. Raw bread dough containing yeast expands in the stomach and produces alcohol as it ferments—a double danger.

Toxic Plants in NZ Gardens

Common Garden Dangers

  • Lily species - Easter lilies, peace lilies, and calla lilies are extremely toxic to cats, causing kidney failure
  • Azalea and rhododendron - Found in many NZ gardens, causes vomiting, diarrhea, and potentially death
  • Oleander - Popular in warmer NZ areas, every part is highly toxic
  • Foxglove (digitalis) - Beautiful but deadly, affects the heart
  • Taro/caladium - Common houseplants, cause oral irritation and swelling
  • Agapanthus - Popular NZ garden plant, toxic to pets

Native Plants to Be Aware Of

While many native plants cause only mild upset, some taupoka (karo) and putaputaweta (marble leaf) can cause problems if large amounts are ingested. If your pet is a known plant chewer, research any native species in your garden.

Everyday Household Hazards

Human Medications

  • Anti-inflammatory drugs - Ibuprofen and naproxen are extremely dangerous to pets
  • Paracetamol/acetaminophen - Particularly toxic to cats, can be fatal
  • Antidepressants - Can cause sedation or agitation, seizures
  • Blood pressure medications - Cause dangerous drops in blood pressure

Cleaning Products and Chemicals

  • Antifreeze (ethylene glycol) - Sweet taste attracts pets, extremely deadly
  • Rodent poison - Even small amounts require immediate veterinary care
  • Industrial cleaners - Bleach, ammonia, and other chemicals
  • Pesticides and herbicides - Lawn and garden chemicals

Recognizing Poisoning Signs

Symptoms vary depending on the toxin, but watch for:

  • Vomiting or diarrhea
  • Excessive drooling
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Lethargy or collapse
  • Seizures or tremors
  • Bleeding from gums or nose
  • Changes in urination

What To Do If Poisoning Occurs

Immediate Steps

  1. Remove your pet from the source of poison
  2. Try to identify what was ingested and how much
  3. Don\'t induce vomiting unless specifically directed by a vet
  4. Call your vet or the Emergency Vet Clinic immediately
  5. Bring a sample or photo of what was ingested

NZ Emergency Vet Contacts

Keep these numbers saved:

  • Your regular vet\'s after-hours number
  • Nearest 24-hour emergency clinic
  • Animal Poisons Helpline: 0800 869 628 (ASPCA-affiliated)

Prevention Strategies

  • Store all medications in secure cabinets out of reach
  • Keep chocolate and toxic foods locked away
  • Research plants before adding them to your garden
  • Use pet-safe cleaning products when possible
  • Secure chemical storage in locked garages or sheds
  • Teach commands like "leave it" to prevent scavenging
  • Supervise pets in gardens and outdoor spaces

Regular Garden Checks

If your pet has access to outdoor areas, regularly check for:

  • Fallen fruit or berries they might eat
  • Mushrooms or fungi (particularly after rain)
  • Dead wildlife that might have been poisoned
  • Spilled chemicals or antifreeze in driveways

Prevention is far easier than treatment. By understanding the dangers in your home and garden, you can create a safe environment for your pet to thrive in—keeping those vet visits happy ones instead.
