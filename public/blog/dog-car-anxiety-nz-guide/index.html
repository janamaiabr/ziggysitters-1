Dog Car Anxiety: Help Your Dog Enjoy Car Travel in NZ

Published 1 June 2026 · ZiggySitters NZ

If your dog trembles, pants, whines, or refuses to get in the car, you are not alone. Car anxiety is one of the most common behavioural issues New Zealand dog owners face — and it can make everything from vet visits to weekend road trips a stressful ordeal. The good news is that car anxiety is treatable. With patience, the right approach, and consistent training, most dogs can learn to tolerate and even enjoy car travel.

This guide explains why dogs develop car anxiety, how to recognise the signs, and walks you through a step-by-step desensitisation plan that works.

Recognising Car Anxiety in Dogs

Dog car anxiety shows up in different ways. Some dogs are vocal about it; others suffer silently. Watch for these signs:

Physical Signs

  • Pacing, panting, or drooling excessively
  • Trembling or shaking
  • Vomiting or lip-licking (motion sickness indicators)
  • Dilated pupils or whale eye (showing the whites of their eyes)
  • Skin tremors or shedding more than usual

Behavioural Signs

  • Refusing to get in the car — pulling away, planting feet, hiding
  • Whining, barking, or howling during the drive
  • Clinging to a specific person or trying to climb into the front seat
  • Escaping from the car at any opportunity
  • Destructive behaviour — chewing seatbelts or scratching at doors
  • Freezing — becoming completely still and unresponsive
Tip: If your dog vomits in the car, start with motion sickness as the primary suspect. Car sickness and car anxiety often coexist — the nausea makes the car a negative experience, which then creates anxiety about getting in the car. Treat the sickness first, and the anxiety often improves.

Why Dogs Develop Car Anxiety

Understanding the root cause helps you choose the right approach:

Motion Sickness

Particularly common in puppies and young dogs whose inner ear has not fully developed. The dog feels nauseous in the car, associates the car with feeling sick, and becomes anxious before they even get in.

Negative Associations

If the only time your dog gets in the car is to go to the vet or the kennel, the car becomes a predictor of unpleasant experiences. This is one of the most common causes of car anxiety.

Lack of Control

Dogs that are not properly restrained in the car slide around on seats or get thrown by sudden stops. This lack of stability is genuinely frightening.

Generalised Anxiety

Some dogs are anxious travellers because they are anxious in new situations generally. These dogs may also show anxiety in other contexts — new environments, loud noises, unfamiliar people.

Past Trauma

Rescue dogs may have had a traumatic car experience in their past — being dumped, abandoned at a shelter, or involved in an accident.

The Desensitisation Plan: Step by Step

Desensitisation is the gold standard treatment for car anxiety. The principle is simple: expose your dog to the car in tiny, non-threatening increments, building positive associations at each step. Never rush. If your dog shows anxiety, go back to the previous step.

Week 1: The Car Is Just a Place

  1. Park the car in the driveway with the engine off and all doors open
  2. Feed your dog meals near the car — start a few metres away and gradually move closer over several days
  3. Scatter treats around and inside the car on the floor
  4. Let your dog explore the car at their own pace — no pressure, no commands
  5. Once your dog willingly jumps in, start feeding meals inside the car with the engine off

Week 2: Settling in the Car

  1. Close the doors (not all at once — start with one) while your dog eats
  2. Practice sitting in the car with your dog for 5-10 minutes, giving treats and calm praise
  3. Give your dog a long-lasting chew or stuffed Kong inside the parked car
  4. Do not turn the engine on yet — just build positive time in the car

Week 3: Engine On, Stationary

  1. Start the engine while your dog is comfortably settled with a chew or Kong
  2. If your dog shows anxiety, turn the engine off and go back to Week 2
  3. Gradually increase engine-on time from 1 minute to 10 minutes
  4. Keep sessions short and always end on a positive note

Week 4: Short Trips

  1. Drive to the end of the street and back — literally 2 minutes
  2. Do 3-5 short trips per day if possible
  3. Always end the trip somewhere fun — the local park, a walking track, or just home for a treat
  4. Never drive to the vet or other stressful places during this phase

Week 5 and Beyond: Gradual Increases

  1. Slowly extend trip duration — 5 minutes, then 10, then 15
  2. Vary your destinations so the car does not always mean the same thing
  3. Continue rewarding calm behaviour in the car
  4. If your dog regresses, go back to the last successful step
Important: Never force your dog into the car, never scold them for being anxious, and never drag them in. Force creates more fear. Patience is the only approach that works.

Car Setup for Anxious Dogs

How you set up the car makes a big difference to an anxious dog:

Proper Restraint

A dog harness attached to a seatbelt anchor or a crash-tested travel crate gives your dog stability. Sliding around on seats is terrifying for an already-anxious dog. Options in New Zealand include:

  • Travel harnesses — clip to the seatbelt, allow some movement but keep the dog secure
  • Travel crates — the safest option, and the enclosed space can actually reduce anxiety for some dogs
  • Boot barriers — keep your dog in the boot area with a dog guard, giving them more floor space
Tip: In New Zealand, dogs must be properly restrained in vehicles. An unrestrained dog is a safety hazard and can result in a fine. More importantly, a dog that feels secure in the car is less anxious.

Comfort Items

  • A familiar blanket or dog bed that smells like home
  • A favourite toy (not a squeaky one — you do not need the noise while driving)
  • An item of your clothing — your scent is reassuring
  • A stuffed Kong or long-lasting chew for distraction

Environment

  • Keep the car cool — air conditioning or open windows for ventilation
  • Play calm music or leave the radio on at low volume (classical music has been shown to reduce dog anxiety)
  • Limit visual stimuli — some dogs do better when they cannot see out the window. A crate cover or window shade can help
  • Avoid strong air fresheners or cleaning products in the car

When to See Your Vet

Sometimes training alone is not enough. Consult your NZ vet if:

  • Your dog vomits on every car trip, even short ones — medication for motion sickness can help
  • Anxiety is severe — trembling, panic, or self-injury in the car
  • The anxiety appeared suddenly in a previously calm traveller (could indicate pain or illness)
  • You have been training consistently for 4-6 weeks with no improvement

Medication Options

Your vet may recommend:

  • Cerenia (maropitant) — specifically for motion sickness in dogs, very effective for car sickness
  • Acepromazine — a sedative for severe anxiety, short-term use only
  • Fluoxetine (Prozac) — for generalised anxiety, taken daily, takes 4-6 weeks to take effect
  • Pheromone sprays — Adaptil (dog-appeasing pheromone) can be sprayed in the car before trips

Never give your dog human medication without veterinary advice. Dosages and drug safety differ significantly between species.

NZ Road Trip Tips for Anxious Dogs

New Zealand is a road trip country. If you want to explore with your dog, here is how to make longer journeys manageable:

Before the Trip

  • Practice with short trips in the weeks leading up to the road trip
  • Book pet-friendly accommodation in advance — NZ has many dog-friendly holiday parks and motels
  • Pack a travel kit: water bowl, treats, comfort items, poo bags, towels, and any medication
  • Plan your route with regular stops every 1-2 hours

During the Trip

  • Stop every 60-90 minutes for a short walk and a drink
  • Never leave your dog in a parked car — NZ summer temperatures can be fatal within minutes
  • Keep the car well-ventilated and at a comfortable temperature
  • Play calming music or leave the radio on
  • Avoid feeding a large meal right before travel — a small snack is fine

Good Stop Options Around NZ

New Zealand has many dog-friendly rest stops along major routes:

  • Auckland to Whangarei (SH1): Warkworth Domain, Wellsford rest area
  • Auckland to Hamilton (SH1): Mercer rest area, Rangiriri Domain
  • Wellington to Palmerston North (SH1): Otaki Beach, Levin Domain
  • Christchurch to Queenstown (SH1/SH6): Numerous dog-friendly reserves in Geraldine, Twizel, and Cromwell

Most NZ holiday parks allow dogs for a small fee, and many have dog exercise areas. Book ahead during summer and school holidays.

Pet Sitters and Car Anxiety

If your dog has car anxiety, you might wonder whether a pet sitter can help. Absolutely — in several ways:

  • Walks instead of drives: A local pet sitter can walk your dog from your home, eliminating the need for car travel entirely
  • Vet trips: Some pet sitters offer transport to vet appointments, providing a calm, experienced handler during the drive
  • Consistent routine: Regular visits from a familiar sitter reduce general anxiety, which can improve car anxiety over time
  • Holiday care: If you are going on a trip and cannot take your anxious dog, in-home pet sitting avoids the double stress of car travel and a new environment

Summary: The Path Forward

Car anxiety takes time and patience to overcome. The keys are:

  1. Rule out motion sickness first — talk to your vet
  2. Build positive associations with the car gradually
  3. Create a secure, comfortable setup in the car
  4. Never force or rush — that only makes it worse
  5. Seek professional help if you are not making progress after a month of consistent training

Most dogs can improve significantly with the right approach. It may take weeks or even months, but the result — a dog who can hop in the car and enjoy a trip to the beach — is absolutely worth the effort.
