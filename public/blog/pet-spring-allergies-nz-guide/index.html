Spring Allergies in NZ Pets: Symptoms, Triggers & Treatment

Published 24 July 2026 — ZiggySitters NZ

Spring in New Zealand is spectacular — blossoming kowhai, lush pastures, and warmer days. But for many dogs and cats, September through November brings relentless itching, sneezing, and skin flare-ups. If your pet starts scratching the moment the pollen count rises, this guide will help you understand what is happening and what to do about it.

Why Spring Is the Toughest Season for NZ Pets

New Zealand has one of the highest rates of seasonal pet allergies in the world, and spring is peak season. The combination of native and introduced plant species, our long grass pollen window (September through January), and the prevalence of fleas that thrive in spring warmth creates a perfect storm for allergic pets. Unlike humans, who mostly get hay fever (respiratory symptoms), dogs and cats primarily express allergies through their skin — a condition veterinarians call atopic dermatitis.

Common Spring Allergy Triggers in New Zealand

Grass Pollen

Ryegrass and tall fescue dominate New Zealand farmland and roadside verges. Their pollen season runs from September through December, and it is the single biggest trigger for canine atopic dermatitis in NZ. Dogs that roll in grass, walk through paddocks, or simply spend time outdoors during spring are exposed to massive pollen loads.

Native Plant Pollen

Kowhai, flax (harakeke), and rewarewa flower in spring and produce significant pollen loads. While these native species are less commonly implicated in pet allergies than introduced grasses, they can still affect sensitive animals, especially in native bush areas and coastal gardens.

Wandering Willie (Tradescantia fluminensis)

This invasive ground cover is notorious in New Zealand. It grows in shady, damp areas — exactly the spots dogs love to sniff through. Wandering Willie causes contact allergic dermatitis: red, inflamed skin on the belly, armpits, and groin. If your dog comes back from a walk with a sudden rash underneath, this plant is a prime suspect.

Tree Pollen

Birch, pine, and macrocarpa release large pollen loads in spring. Pine pollen is particularly visible — the yellow dust that coats cars and windowsills in October is inhaled and absorbed through your pet's skin as well. Macrocarpa hedges, common on rural NZ properties, are another strong allergen source.

Mould Spores

Spring rain and warming temperatures create ideal conditions for mould growth in leaf litter, compost heaps, and damp soil. Aspergillus and other mould spores become airborne and can trigger both respiratory and skin allergies in pets.

Flea Allergy Dermatitis

Spring is flea season. Even a single flea bite can set off intense itching in a flea-allergic pet. This is the most common allergy in NZ dogs and cats, and it peaks precisely when spring temperatures allow flea populations to explode. If your pet is scratching at the base of the tail or along the lower back, flea allergy is the first thing to rule out.

How to Recognise Spring Allergies in Your Pet

In Dogs

  • Persistent scratching, licking, or chewing — especially paws, belly, and armpits
  • Red or inflamed skin, often in patches
  • Recurrent ear infections (head shaking, smelly ears)
  • Rubbing face on furniture or carpet
  • Hot spots (moist, raw patches of skin)
  • Watery or red eyes
  • Sneezing or reverse sneezing

In Cats

  • Over-grooming — thinning fur or bald patches, especially on the belly and inner thighs
  • Excessive scratching around the head and neck
  • Miliary dermatitis (small crusty bumps along the back)
  • Face rubbing against furniture
  • Sneezing or nasal discharge
  • Red, inflamed ears
Important: If your pet develops open sores, hair loss over large areas, or seems lethargic alongside itching, see your vet immediately. These can indicate secondary infections or more serious conditions that mimic allergies.

Diagnosis: What Your Vet Will Check

Diagnosing spring allergies is a process of elimination. Your vet will typically:

  1. Rule out fleas — thorough flea comb check and trial of rigorous flea prevention
  2. Rule out food allergies — an 8-12 week elimination diet if skin issues persist year-round
  3. Rule out contact irritants — review shampoos, cleaning products, and lawn chemicals
  4. Intradermal or blood allergy testing — identifies specific environmental allergens (pollen, mould, dust mites)

Most NZ vets will start with flea control and symptomatic treatment before investing in allergy testing, because the majority of spring itch cases involve fleas or are manageable without immunotherapy.

Treatment and Management Strategies

1. Flea Prevention (Non-Negotiable in Spring)

Every dog and cat in NZ should be on year-round flea prevention, but it is especially critical in spring. Use a vet-recommended product (NexGard, Bravecto, Advocate, or Revolution) and treat all pets in the household simultaneously. Treat the environment too — wash bedding in hot water and vacuum frequently.

2. Pollen Reduction

Practical tips to reduce pollen exposure:
  • Wipe your dog's paws and belly with a damp cloth after every walk
  • Rinse your pet with cool water after outdoor play during high-pollen days
  • Walk dogs early morning or after rain when pollen counts are lower
  • Avoid walking through long grass or recently mown fields
  • Keep windows closed on windy, dry spring days
  • Wash pet bedding weekly in hot water

3. Medicated Baths and Rinses

Regular bathing with a hypoallergenic or oatmeal-based shampoo soothes inflamed skin and physically removes pollen. For dogs with secondary bacterial infections (common with chronic scratching), your vet may prescribe a chlorhexidine-based shampoo. Cool water is essential — warm water worsens itching.

4. Veterinary Medications

When lifestyle changes are not enough, your vet has several effective options:

  • Apoquel (oclacitinib): Fast-acting, targets itch signalling. Widely used in NZ and highly effective for seasonal flares.
  • Cytopoint: An injection given every 4-8 weeks that blocks the itch signal at the nerve level. Excellent for dogs with seasonal allergies.
  • Antihistamines: Less effective in pets than humans, but some dogs respond to cetirizine or chlorphenamine. Only use under vet guidance.
  • Topical corticosteroids: Short-term relief for hot spots. Not suitable for long-term use.
  • Immunotherapy (desensitisation): Allergy shots or drops based on test results. Takes 6-12 months to work but can significantly reduce long-term symptoms.

5. Dietary Support

Omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil) reduce skin inflammation and strengthen the skin barrier. Look for a vet-quality supplement with EPA and DHA. Probiotics can also modulate the immune system and reduce allergic responses. If your vet suspects food allergies are contributing, they may recommend a hydrolysed protein diet.

How a Pet Sitter Can Help During Spring

If you are travelling during spring, your allergic pet needs extra care. A ZiggySitters pet sitter can:

  • Wipe paws and belly after every outdoor visit
  • Administer prescribed allergy medications on schedule
  • Apply medicated rinses or spot treatments as directed
  • Monitor for hot spots, ear infections, or skin flare-ups
  • Keep your pet indoors during high-pollen periods
  • Communicate any changes in symptoms so you can consult your vet promptly
Spring Allergy Checklist for Pet Owners:
  • Start flea prevention before spring (August at the latest)
  • Book a spring vet check to discuss allergy management
  • Stock up on medicated shampoo and paw wipes
  • Identify and remove Wandering Willie from your garden
  • Brief your pet sitter on allergy symptoms and medication schedules
  • Keep an antihistamine supply (vet-approved) on hand
  • Plan indoor enrichment for high-pollen days

When to See Your Vet Urgently

Book an urgent appointment if your pet shows any of these signs:

  • Open, oozing sores or raw skin patches
  • Swelling of the face, muzzle, or eyes
  • Difficulty breathing or persistent coughing
  • Sudden, intense scratching that will not stop
  • Lethargy, loss of appetite, or fever alongside skin symptoms
  • Ear discharge or foul smell from ears

FAQ

Can I give my pet human antihistamines for spring allergies?

Only under veterinary guidance. Some human antihistamines are safe for dogs at specific doses, but others are toxic. Never medicate your pet without consulting your vet first.

Are some breeds more prone to spring allergies?

Yes. Labrador Retrievers, Golden Retrievers, West Highland White Terriers, Bulldogs, German Shepherds, and Shar-Peis are all predisposed to atopic dermatitis. Among cats, Siamese and Abyssinian breeds show higher rates.

Will my pet grow out of spring allergies?

Unfortunately, no. Allergies tend to worsen with each exposure. However, they can be managed very effectively with a combination of avoidance, medication, and desensitisation therapy. Early intervention in the first season gives the best long-term outcome.

Is honey effective for pet allergies?

There is no evidence that local honey helps pet allergies. This is a myth based on the human hay fever theory, and even in humans the evidence is weak. Do not give honey to pets, especially those with diabetes or weight issues.

How long does spring allergy season last in NZ?

Typically September through January, with peak pollen in October and November. However, pets sensitised to multiple allergens may show symptoms year-round. In warmer regions like Northland and Auckland, the season can start earlier and finish later.
