Pet-Safe Cleaning Products in New Zealand: What Every Pet Owner Needs to Know

2026-05-28 · 10 min read · ZiggySitters Team

Most NZ households clean weekly, if not more often. Sprays on benchtops, mopping solutions on floors, bleach in the bathroom, detergent in the laundry. But if you share your home with a cat or dog, the products you use to keep things spotless could be putting your pets at risk. Dogs lick floors. Cats walk on surfaces and then groom their paws. Both species are lower to the ground than humans, closer to where residues settle, and far more susceptible to chemical toxicity at smaller doses. This guide covers which cleaning products are genuinely pet-safe, which ingredients to avoid at all costs, and how to keep your home clean without compromising your pet's health.

Why Pet-Safe Cleaning Matters More Than You Think

New Zealanders love clean homes. But the products that make surfaces sparkle can be silently harmful to pets. Dogs and cats process chemicals differently from humans. Their livers are less efficient at breaking down certain toxins, their body weight is significantly lower, and their behaviour patterns, walking bare-pawed on freshly mopped floors, then licking their paws or grooming themselves, creates a direct ingestion pathway that humans simply don\'t have.

A study by the Veterinary Poisons Centre found that household cleaning products consistently rank among the top ten causes of pet poisoning calls. In New Zealand, the National Poisons Centre receives hundreds of calls annually about pets exposed to cleaning chemicals. The symptoms range from mild, vomiting, drooling, and paw irritation, to severe, chemical burns to the mouth and oesophagus, respiratory distress, kidney failure, and in the worst cases, death.

The risk is not limited to pets that directly drink from bottles or chew on sponges. Residual film on floors, surfaces, and even air-borne particles from sprays can accumulate in a pet\'s system over time. A dog sleeping on a freshly bleached floor is absorbing that chemical through its skin and paw pads. A cat grooming itself after walking across a bench sprayed with disinfectant is ingesting whatever was on that bench. This is not theoretical. It is happening in homes across the country every day.

The Dangerous Ingredients: What to Avoid

Reading product labels is the single most important habit you can develop as a pet owner. The following ingredients are red flags. If you see them, find an alternative.

Bleach (Sodium Hypochlorite)

The most common household disinfectant in New Zealand and one of the most dangerous for pets. Bleach fumes irritate respiratory passages, and contact with skin or paws causes chemical burns. Pets that walk on bleached surfaces and then lick their paws ingest a concentrated dose. Even diluted bleach residues on floors can cause vomiting, drooling, and abdominal pain. If you must use bleach, keep pets out of the room until surfaces are completely dry and well-ventilated, which can take several hours.

Ammonia

Found in many glass cleaners, floor cleaners, and multipurpose sprays. Ammonia fumes irritate the respiratory tract and eyes of both dogs and cats. Cats are particularly sensitive because their respiratory systems are more delicate. In enclosed spaces, ammonia can cause coughing, wheezing, and in severe cases, respiratory distress. Never mix ammonia-based products with bleach; the combination produces chloramine gas, which is lethal.

Phenols and Pine Oil

These are the ingredients that give some disinfectants their distinctive hospital smell. Phenols are particularly toxic to cats because cats lack the liver enzyme (glucuronyl transferase) needed to metabolise them. Even small amounts absorbed through the skin or ingested during grooming can cause liver damage, neurological symptoms, and death in cats. Products containing pine oil, phenol, or cresol should never be used in a home with cats, full stop.

⚠️ Critical Warning for Cat Owners

Cats are dramatically more sensitive to phenols, essential oils, and many disinfectants than dogs. A product labelled "pet safe" for dogs may still be dangerous for cats. Always verify that a product is specifically safe for feline companions. If a label does not explicitly mention cats, assume it is not safe for them.

Formaldehyde

Sometimes listed as formalin or methyl aldehyde, this chemical is a preservative and disinfectant found in some heavy-duty cleaners. It is a known carcinogen and respiratory irritant. Pets exposed to formaldehyde fumes can experience coughing, breathing difficulty, and eye irritation. Long-term exposure increases cancer risk. There is no safe level of formaldehyde exposure for pets.

Glycol Ethers

Found in many glass cleaners, spot removers, and carpet cleaners. Glycol ethers, including 2-butoxyethanol and ethylene glycol, are toxic when ingested and can be absorbed through the skin. They cause anaemia, kidney damage, and liver damage in pets. Carpet cleaning products are a particular concern because pets lie on carpets and groom themselves afterwards.

Quaternary Ammonium Compounds (Quats)

Commonly found in antibacterial wipes, disinfectant sprays, and fabric softeners. Quats are effective at killing bacteria but they are also toxic to pets, causing skin irritation, respiratory issues, and gastrointestinal problems if ingested. Antibacterial wipes are one of the most common sources of pet exposure because owners wipe surfaces and leave residue that pets then contact.

Essential Oils

This surprises many people because essential oils are often marketed as natural and safe. Tea tree oil, eucalyptus, peppermint, citrus, pine, clove, and ylang-ylang are all toxic to pets, especially cats. Cats lack the liver enzymes to metabolise these compounds. Even diffused essential oils can cause respiratory distress, drooling, vomiting, and in severe cases, liver failure. A few drops of tea tree oil applied to a dog\'s skin can cause toxicity. Do not assume "natural" means "pet safe."

Pet-Safe Cleaning Alternatives

Switching to pet-safe products does not mean sacrificing cleanliness. These alternatives are effective, affordable, and available in NZ supermarkets or online.

✅ Safe and Effective Alternatives

  • White vinegar: Excellent for cutting grease, removing hard water stains, and deodorising. Mix equal parts vinegar and water for a general-purpose cleaner. Safe for floors, benchtops, and bathrooms. The smell dissipates within an hour.
  • Baking soda: A gentle abrasive for scrubbing sinks, baths, and ovens. Also works as a carpet deodoriser. Sprinkle on, leave for 15 minutes, then vacuum. Completely non-toxic to pets.
  • Castile soap: A plant-based soap that cleans effectively without harsh chemicals. Dr Bronner\'s is widely available in NZ. Dilute with water for dishwashing, floor mopping, and surface cleaning.
  • Lemon juice: Natural degreaser and deodoriser. Mix with baking soda for a paste that tackles stubborn stains. Safe for pets in small quantities.
  • Hydrogen peroxide (3%): Effective disinfectant for cutting boards and bathroom surfaces. Breaks down into water and oxygen, leaving no harmful residue. Keep higher concentrations away from pets.
  • Enzyme cleaners: Specifically designed to break down organic stains and odours (urine, vomit, faeces). Products like Urine Off and Simple Solution are available in NZ pet stores and are both effective and pet safe.

DIY Pet-Safe All-Purpose Cleaner Recipe

Making your own pet-safe cleaner takes two minutes and costs a fraction of commercial alternatives. Combine the following in a spray bottle:

  • 1 cup white vinegar
  • 1 cup water
  • 10 drops lavender essential oil (optional, but only if you don\'t have cats, as lavender can be toxic to felines in concentrated form)

Shake before each use. This works on benchtops, stovetops, bathroom surfaces, and glass. For stubborn stains, sprinkle baking soda on the surface first, then spray the vinegar solution. The fizzing action lifts grime effectively.

Cleaning Safely Around Pets: Room by Room

Kitchen

The kitchen is ground zero for cleaning product exposure. Benchtops are cleaned frequently, floors are mopped often, and pets, especially cats, love to hop up on surfaces. Use vinegar-based cleaners on benchtops and food preparation areas. Avoid antibacterial sprays unless they are specifically labelled pet safe, and even then, wipe surfaces with a damp cloth after applying to remove residue. For the floor, a vinegar and hot water mop is effective and leaves no harmful residue once dry. Keep bin areas clean with baking soda rather than chemical deodorisers.

Bathroom

Bathrooms are where the strongest cleaning products tend to live. Toilet bowl cleaners, mould removers, and shower sprays are often the most chemically intense products in the house. Close the bathroom door while cleaning and keep it closed until surfaces are completely dry. Close the toilet lid after cleaning; dogs in particular will drink from a freshly cleaned toilet. Switch toilet cleaners to a vinegar and baking soda combination. For mould, use a 3% hydrogen peroxide solution sprayed directly on the affected area and left for 15 minutes before scrubbing.

Laundry

Pet bedding should be washed regularly, but the detergent you choose matters. Many mainstream detergents contain optical brighteners, artificial fragrances, and surfactants that can irritate pet skin. Choose fragrance-free, plant-based detergents. Skip fabric softeners entirely; they coat fibres in chemicals that transfer directly to pet skin. If your pet has skin sensitivities, add half a cup of baking soda to the wash cycle as a natural deodoriser instead.

Floors

Floor cleaning is the single highest-risk cleaning activity for pets because dogs and cats spend significant time on floors and then groom their paws. Never use bleach, ammonia, or commercial floor cleaners containing quats on floors where pets walk. A hot water and vinegar mop is effective for most hard floors. For vinyl and lino, add a small amount of castile soap. For tile and grout, a baking soda paste works well. Ensure floors are completely dry before allowing pets back onto them.

Commercial Pet-Safe Cleaning Brands Available in NZ

If DIY is not your preference, several brands available in New Zealand offer genuinely pet-safe cleaning products. Look for:

  • Ecostore: NZ-made, widely available in supermarkets. Their plant-based surface sprays, dish liquids, and laundry products are free from nasties and safe for homes with pets. Available at Countdown, New World, and Woolworths.
  • Earthwise: Another NZ brand using plant-based ingredients. Their floor cleaners, surface sprays, and laundry liquids are biodegradable and pet safe.
  • Good Change: NZ company producing compostable cleaning wipes and eco-friendly surface sprays. No harsh chemicals.
  • Method: Plant-based, biodegradable cleaners with naturally derived fragrances. Their all-purpose sprays and floor cleaners are a good option, though check labels if you have cats, as some fragrances may not be feline-safe.
  • Seventh Generation: Available online and in some specialty stores. Fragrance-free versions are the safest choice for pet households.

Always read the label, even on "eco" or "natural" products. "Natural" does not automatically mean pet safe. Check the ingredients list against the dangerous ingredients above and look for specific claims about pet safety.

Special Considerations for Pet Sitters

If you are hiring a pet sitter, or if you are a sitter yourself, cleaning product safety should be part of the conversation. Here is what to cover:

  • Before the sit: Walk your sitter through which cleaning products are safe to use and which areas to avoid. Point out where your pet-safe products are stored. If you have specific products you don\'t want used in your home, make this explicit in your care sheet.
  • During the sit: If a mess needs cleaning, the sitter should use only the products you have designated as safe. For pet accidents, enzyme cleaners are the gold standard. Keep a bottle accessible and show your sitter where it is.
  • After the sit: If the sitter needs to clean up before leaving, vinegar and water is the safest bet for a quick surface wipe. No need to deep clean with chemicals.

What to Do If Your Pet Is Exposed to Cleaning Products

Despite precautions, accidents happen. If you suspect your pet has ingested or been exposed to a toxic cleaning product, act immediately:

  • Do not induce vomiting unless specifically instructed by a vet or the Poisons Centre. Some products cause more damage coming back up.
  • Remove your pet from the contaminated area immediately. Get them to fresh air if fumes are present.
  • Rinse skin or paws with copious amounts of water if the product is on their body.
  • Call the NZ National Poisons Centre on 0800 764 766 (0800 POISON). They operate 24/7 and can advise on next steps for specific products.
  • Contact your vet or the nearest after-hours emergency clinic. Have the product label ready so the vet can identify the active ingredients.

Keep the phone number for your nearest 24-hour emergency vet saved in your phone. Time matters in poisoning cases. Do not wait to see if symptoms develop before calling for help.

The Bottom Line

Cleaning your home and keeping your pets safe are not competing priorities. With the right products and a few simple habits, keeping surfaces hygienic, rinsing thoroughly, drying floors before pets return, and storing products securely, you can maintain a spotless home without putting your animals at risk. Switch to vinegar, baking soda, and pet-safe commercial brands for everyday cleaning. Reserve heavy-duty chemicals for situations where you can fully ventilate and isolate the area, and always keep the Poisons Centre number handy. Your pets will be healthier for it, and you will have one less thing to worry about when you head out the door or leave them in the care of a sitter.
