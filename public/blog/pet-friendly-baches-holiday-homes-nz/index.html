Pet-Friendly Baches & Holiday Homes in NZ: The Complete Guide

Nothing says summer in Aotearoa quite like loading up the car, heading to the coast, and settling into a classic Kiwi bach for a week of salt air and slow mornings. And for many New Zealand families, a holiday does not feel right unless the dog comes along too. This guide covers everything you need to know about finding, booking, and enjoying pet-friendly baches and holiday homes across New Zealand.

What Makes a Bach Pet-Friendly?

Not every holiday home that claims to be pet-friendly actually is. A truly pet-friendly bach goes beyond simply allowing animals. It actively welcomes them. Here is what to look for:

  • Fully fenced section with secure latches on all gates. Check gate height, as some smaller baches have low fences that determined dogs can scale.
  • Durable flooring such as polished concrete, timber, or tile. Carpeted baches and muddy paws do not mix.
  • Outdoor hose or shower for rinsing off sandy, salty dogs after beach walks.
  • Proximity to off-leash areas or beaches where dogs are permitted.
  • Escape-proof design: no gaps under the house, secure decking, and no easily accessible toxic plants in the garden.
  • Understanding hosts who do not treat your pet as a nuisance. The best pet-friendly bach owners are pet owners themselves.
Check the fine print: Many listings say "pet-friendly" but charge a surcharge of $50–$150 per stay, restrict pets to certain rooms, or exclude certain breeds. Read the full pet policy before booking.

Where to Find Pet-Friendly Baches in NZ

Bookabach

New Zealand\'s original holiday home platform remains one of the best for pet-friendly listings. Use the "Pets Allowed" filter under amenities. Listings often include detailed pet policies and photos of the outdoor space. Many bach owners on Bookabach are dog owners themselves and are genuinely welcoming.

Stayz (formerly HomeAway)

Stayz has a solid selection of pet-friendly holiday homes across NZ. The platform lists many larger properties with big sections, which suits families travelling with dogs. Filter by "Pets Allowed" and always message the host to confirm specifics before booking.

Bring Your Pet NZ

A dedicated New Zealand directory for pet-friendly travel, Bring Your Pet NZ lists baches, holiday homes, motels, and camping grounds that welcome animals. The site is smaller but focused, which means every listing has been vetted for genuine pet-friendliness.

Local Facebook Groups

Search for "pet-friendly bach" plus your destination region on Facebook. Local community groups often have bach owners who do not list on commercial platforms but rent their properties informally. These can be excellent deals, though you will not have the same booking protections.

Best Regions for Pet-Friendly Bach Holidays

Coromandel Peninsula

The Coromandel is bach country. Dozens of pet-friendly baches sit within walking distance of dog-friendly beaches like Hot Water Beach (dogs on leash) and the stretch of sand at Matarangi. The region offers a classic Kiwi holiday experience: fishing, swimming, bush walks, and fish and chips on the beach. Most Coromandel baches have large decks and fenced sections perfect for dogs.

Bay of Plenty

Mount Maunganui, Papamoa, and Waihi Beach all have pet-friendly bach options with easy beach access. The Mount\'s base track is a fantastic dog walk, and many cafes in the area are dog-friendly. Papamoa\'s long, wide beach is ideal for off-leash play during permitted hours.

Northland

Mangawhai, Waipu, and the Tutukaka Coast offer secluded baches where your dog can run free on near-empty beaches. Northland\'s warmer climate makes it a year-round destination. The region is less developed than the Coromandel, which means more space and fewer restrictions, but also fewer fenced properties, so check carefully.

Marlborough Sounds

For South Island travellers, the Sounds offer water-access-only baches that are as close to paradise as it gets. Many are dog-friendly, and the walking tracks around the bays make for excellent exploration. The catch: you need a boat or water taxi to reach most of them, which limits what you can bring.

Central Otago / Wanaka

If mountains appeal more than beaches, the Central Otago region has holiday homes with fenced sections, mountain views, and walking tracks right from the doorstep. Wanaka has an active dog community and several off-leash areas. Lake swimming is available but bracing, even in summer.

Packing List: What to Bring for Your Pet

A bach holiday with pets requires a bit more planning than a solo trip. Here is a comprehensive checklist:

Essentials

  • Enough food for the entire stay plus two extra days (in case of delays)
  • Food and water bowls (collapsible bowls for the beach)
  • Medications and copy of prescriptions
  • Vaccination records and microchip details
  • Lead, harness, and a long lead for beach walks
  • Poo bags (bring extra; rural bins are not always available)
  • Flea and tick prevention (applied before the trip)
  • Pet first-aid kit
  • Familiar bedding or blanket (reduces anxiety in a new space)
  • 2–3 favourite toys

Bach-Specific Items

  • Towels specifically for the dog (beach sand and salt water get everywhere)
  • Portable water bowl for walks and day trips
  • Dog-safe sunscreen for light-nosed breeds
  • Tie-out cable or stake if the section is not fully fenced
  • Baby gates to block off restricted areas inside the bach
  • Enzyme cleaner for accidents on carpets or furniture
  • Lint roller or pet hair remover for soft furnishings

Cat-Specific Extras

  • Portable litter box and enough litter for the stay
  • Carrier for safe transport and as a hiding spot at the bach
  • Feliway diffuser or spray to reduce stress in the new environment
  • Window screens or mesh (many baches have windows that open wide with no screens)

Travelling to Your Bach with Pets

By Car

Most Kiwi bach trips are road trips, which is the easiest way to travel with pets. Ensure your dog is properly restrained in the vehicle (a harness attached to a seatbelt or a travel crate). Cats should always travel in a secure carrier. Plan for stops every two hours for exercise, water, and toilet breaks. New Zealand state highways have rest areas with grass patches that work well for dog breaks.

By Ferry (Cook Strait)

Pets travel in the car deck on the Interislander and Bluebridge ferries. You cannot stay with your pet during the crossing, but crew check on animals regularly. Book early for summer sailings as pet spaces fill up. Bring a water bowl that attaches to the crate and ensure your pet has adequate ventilation. The crossing takes roughly 3.5 hours, so a comfort break before boarding is essential.

By Air

If your bach destination requires a flight, Air New Zealand and Jetstar both accept pets as cargo on most domestic routes. Book well in advance for summer. Cats and small dogs travel in airline-approved crates in the hold. The cost is typically $50–$120 per pet per flight, depending on size. Snub-nosed breeds (pugs, French bulldogs, persian cats) face restrictions due to breathing risk.

Common Bach Hazards for Pets

Holiday homes come with hazards that urban homes do not. Be aware of these common risks:

  • Open decks and staircases: Many baches have elevated decks with wide gaps. Small dogs and cats can fall through. Bring baby gates or netting if needed.
  • Rat bait and pest control: Some bach owners leave bait stations under the house or in sheds. Check on arrival and keep pets away.
  • Sea life: Dead fish, jellyfish, and sea slugs on the beach can be toxic if eaten. Monitor your dog closely on beach walks.
  • Unfenced drops: Coastal baches on cliffs may have unfenced edges. Keep dogs on lead near drops.
  • Hot decks and sand: If you cannot hold the back of your hand on the deck for five seconds, it is too hot for your dog\'s paws.
  • Ticks and sandflies: Coastal and bush areas have more insect pressure. Apply tick prevention before your trip and check your dog daily.
  • Septic tanks: Many baches use septic systems. Keep dogs away from tank covers and drainage fields.

When You Cannot Bring Your Pet

Sometimes the bach simply is not suitable for your pet, or your pet does not travel well. In that case, leaving them in good hands at home is often the kinder choice. A professional pet sitter who stays in your home maintains your pet\'s routine, avoids the stress of travel, and keeps your house occupied, which is a security benefit while you are away.

Key Takeaways

  • True pet-friendly baches have secure fencing, durable flooring, and outdoor wash-down areas. Always confirm the full pet policy before booking.
  • Book early. Pet-friendly baches represent a small fraction of holiday homes and sell out fast for the December to February peak.
  • Check local council dog bylaws for your destination. Summer beach restrictions vary by region and can limit off-leash access.
  • Pack for your pet as carefully as you pack for yourself. Bring food, medications, familiar bedding, and bach-specific items like tie-out cables and beach towels.
  • Watch for bach-specific hazards: open decks, rat bait, sea life on beaches, and hot surfaces.
  • If the bach is not right for your pet, a home pet sitter is often the best alternative for your animal\'s comfort and routine.

A Kiwi bach holiday with your pet is one of the best experiences New Zealand has to offer. With a bit of planning, the right property, and realistic expectations, you and your dog (or cat, if they are the adventurous type) can create the kind of memories that make the drive home feel way too short.
