Catios & Outdoor Cat Enclosures in New Zealand

Published by ZiggySitters • May 2026 • 12 min read

New Zealand loves its cats — but we also love our native wildlife. Catios and outdoor cat enclosures solve the oldest tension in Kiwi cat ownership: how to give your feline the fresh air and stimulation they crave without putting birds, lizards, and the cats themselves at risk. This guide covers everything from council rules to DIY builds to what your pet sitter needs to know when you have a catio.

Why Catios Matter in New Zealand

New Zealand has one of the highest cat ownership rates in the world, with roughly 41% of Kiwi households sharing their home with at least one cat. But our native wildlife — kiwi, kakariki, geckos, weta, and countless other species — evolved without mammalian predators and remain devastatingly vulnerable to free-roaming cats.

A well-designed catio gives your cat the best of both worlds: the sights, sounds, and smells of the outdoors, without the risks of fights, cars, dog attacks, toxins, or predation on native species. It also keeps your cat safer from diseases spread by stray and feral cats.

The Case for Catios in NZ
  • Wildlife protection: Domestic cats kill an estimated 1.1 million native birds per year in NZ
  • Cat safety: Indoor-outdoor cats have significantly shorter lifespans than indoor-only cats
  • Council compliance: Some NZ councils now require cats to be contained on their owner's property
  • Mental health: Cats with outdoor access show fewer behavioural problems than fully indoor cats
  • Neighbour relations: Contained cats don't dig in neighbouring gardens or fight with other pets

NZ Council Rules on Cat Containment

Cat containment regulations vary across New Zealand and are evolving rapidly. As of 2026, several councils have introduced or are consulting on cat containment bylaws:

Even where cat containment is not mandatory, building a catio demonstrates responsible pet ownership and protects you from potential neighbour complaints. The trend across New Zealand is clearly moving toward greater containment requirements.

Tip: Before building, check your local council's website for cat bylaws and any resource consent requirements for structures. Most small catios attached to a house wall are exempt from building consent, but freestanding enclosures over a certain size may require approval.

Types of Catios and Outdoor Enclosures

1. Window Box Catio

The simplest and most affordable option. A wire mesh box that attaches to the outside of a window, giving your cat a small shelf to sit on and watch the world. Ideal for apartments or rental properties.

  • Size: 60–120 cm wide, 40–80 cm deep
  • Cost: $150–$400 (DIY) or $400–$800 (professional)
  • Best for: Single cats, apartments, renters
  • Access: Through an open window or cat door insert

2. Balcony Catio

Encloses an existing balcony with cat-safe mesh, transforming it into a secure outdoor space. Popular in Wellington and Auckland apartments where outdoor space is limited.

  • Size: Depends on balcony dimensions
  • Cost: $500–$2,000 (DIY) or $1,500–$4,000 (professional)
  • Best for: Apartment cats, multi-cat households
  • Key consideration: Body corporate or landlord approval may be needed

3. Porch Catio (Cat Patio)

The classic catio: an enclosure attached to the side of your house, accessible through a cat door or window. This is the most popular style in New Zealand, offering a good balance of space, cost, and integration with the home.

  • Size: Typically 2m × 2m to 4m × 3m
  • Cost: $800–$3,000 (DIY) or $2,500–$8,000 (professional)
  • Best for: Most Kiwi homes with a garden or side yard
  • Access: Cat door in wall or window, tunnel from house

4. Freestanding Cat Run

A standalone enclosure in the garden, connected to the house by a tunnel. These offer the most space and can include trees, climbing walls, and multiple levels.

  • Size: 2m × 3m up to 6m × 6m or larger
  • Cost: $1,500–$5,000 (DIY) or $4,000–$15,000+ (professional)
  • Best for: Lifestyle blocks, larger sections, multi-cat households
  • Key consideration: May need building consent if over 10m² in some council areas

5. Cat Tunnel System

A network of enclosed tunnels connecting different areas of your property. Some Kiwi cat owners have created spectacular tunnel systems that run along fence lines, up walls, and through trees, giving their cats an entire adventure playground.

  • Cost: $200–$500 per metre of tunnel (materials only)
  • Best for: Creative cat owners with larger sections
  • Key consideration: Neighbours may need to be consulted if tunnels run along boundary fences

Essential Features Every Catio Needs

Shelter and Shade

New Zealand's UV index regularly reaches extreme levels (12+) during summer. Your catio needs solid roofing over at least part of the enclosure and shade cloth or plants over the rest. Cats need to be able to retreat from both rain and direct sun at any time of day.

Vertical Space

Cats are three-dimensional creatures — they think vertically. Include shelves at multiple heights, a tall climbing post, and if possible, a cat tree that reaches near the top of the enclosure. Wall-mounted timber shelves are inexpensive and easy to install.

Safe Flooring

Avoid bare wire mesh floors, which can injure paw pads. Good options include:

  • Timber decking (treated pine is affordable and weather-resistant)
  • Outdoor carpet tiles (replaceable when worn)
  • Artificial turf (easy to clean, soft on paws)
  • Pavers with gaps planted with cat-safe grass

Cat-Safe Plants

Add enrichment with plants, but choose carefully. Many common garden plants are toxic to cats. Safe options for NZ catios include:

  • Cat grass (Dactylis glomerata) — the classic
  • Catnip (Nepeta cataria) — perennial favourite
  • Lemon balm (Melissa officinalis) — many cats love the scent
  • Spider plant (Chlorophytum comosum) — safe and easy to grow
  • New Zealand flax (Phormium) — native, hardy, and non-toxic
  • Carex grasses — NZ native sedges, great texture for cats
Warning: Avoid lilies (all parts are highly toxic and can cause fatal kidney failure), as well as kowhai seeds, poinsettia, aloe vera, and ivy. The SPCA maintains a full list of toxic plants for NZ cat owners on their website.

Weather Protection

New Zealand weather changes fast. Include a windbreak on at least one side, a waterproof roof, and a small insulated shelter box for colder days. In areas prone to strong winds (Wellington, we are looking at you), use marine-grade stainless steel mesh and heavy-duty fixings.

DIY vs Professional Catio Builds

DIY Catio

If you are handy with tools, building your own catio can save significant money and let you customise every detail. The DIY route is best for window boxes, simple porch enclosures, and tunnel systems.

  • Mesh: Use 25mm × 25mm welded mesh (2.5mm wire diameter) — this is strong enough to resist a determined cat and keeps out predators
  • Timber: H3.2 treated pine for framing (weather-resistant, affordable)
  • Fixings: Stainless steel screws and staples (galvanised will rust in NZ conditions)
  • Cat door: Install a quality cat door in the wall or window panel — magnetic or microchip-activated models prevent strays entering

Professional Catio Builders in NZ

Several New Zealand companies specialise in custom cat enclosures:

  • Cat Pens NZ (Auckland) — Custom enclosures and tunnels, nationwide shipping of DIY kits
  • Catnets (Australia, ships to NZ) — Modular cat netting systems, popular for balcony and verandah enclosures
  • Local builders — Many fencing and landscaping companies will build catios to spec; get three quotes
Tip: Whether DIY or professional, always use mesh that a cat cannot push through or chew through. Chicken wire is NOT adequate — cats can push through it and it rusts quickly in NZ conditions. Welded mesh at 2.5mm gauge is the minimum recommended.

Catio Enrichment Ideas

A catio is only as good as what is inside it. Here are enrichment features that Kiwi cats love:

  • Multiple shelf levels — Cats prefer to observe from above; aim for at least 3 levels
  • Scratching posts — Sisal rope on timber posts last longer than carpet
  • Hammocks and beds — Outdoor-rated cat beds with waterproof liners
  • Interactive toys — Rotate wand toys, puzzle feeders, and balls weekly
  • Water feature — A small cat fountain provides fresh water and entertainment
  • Bird-watching perch — Position a shelf facing trees or a bird feeder (outside the mesh, naturally)
  • Cat grass planter — A tray of growing grass cats can nibble and roll in
  • Tunnel tubes — PVC drain pipes make excellent cat tunnels; cut holes for entry/exit
  • Weatherproof box — A small insulated shelter gives your cat a cosy retreat on cold or wet days

Keeping Your Cat Safe in the Catio

Even in a secure enclosure, there are safety considerations:

  • Escape-proof access points: Ensure all doors, windows, and cat doors close securely. Microchip cat doors are the gold standard
  • No gaps: Check regularly for mesh damage, loose fittings, or gaps near the house wall
  • Predator protection: In rural areas, consider double-layer mesh or a roof to protect cats from dogs, hawks, or stoats
  • Heat management: In summer, a catio in full sun can become dangerously hot. Ensure shade and ventilation, and consider a misting system for very hot days
  • Water supply: Always have fresh water available in the catio, and check it twice daily in summer
  • Plant safety: Only use cat-safe plants inside the enclosure; check the SPCA toxic plant list before adding anything new
  • Regular inspection: Check mesh, fixings, and structural integrity monthly — NZ weather is hard on outdoor structures
Important: Never leave a cat in a catio during extreme weather events (cyclones, severe storms, or heatwaves). Ensure there is always a way back into the house, and consider a cat door that locks in both directions so you can control access.

Catios and Pet Sitters: What You Need to Know

If you have a catio and are hiring a pet sitter, there are a few key things to communicate:

  1. Show the sitter how the cat door works — demonstrate locking and unlocking, and explain any microchip settings
  2. Explain the catio routine: Is your cat allowed out at night? Do you close the cat door at dusk? What are the house rules?
  3. Point out escape routes — Show the sitter every access point and how to secure them
  4. Emergency contacts — Provide your vet's details and the nearest after-hours clinic
  5. Weather protocols: What should the sitter do if a storm is forecast? Where are the keys to the catio door?
  6. Enrichment rotation: Leave instructions for rotating toys and topping up cat grass

A good pet sitter will appreciate knowing the catio setup in advance. Many sitters on ZiggySitters are experienced with cats who have outdoor access and understand the importance of containment and routine.

Seasonal Catio Tips

Summer (December–February)

  • Add extra shade cloth or sail coverage for UV protection
  • Place a frozen water bowl in the catio on very hot days
  • Check mesh for heat expansion — fixings can loosen in extreme temperatures
  • Consider a small fan for enclosed catios with limited airflow
  • Watch for flies and insects; avoid toxic sprays near the catio

Autumn (March–May)

  • Clean out summer plants and refresh cat grass
  • Check weatherproofing before winter — replace any rotting timber or rusting mesh
  • Add a cosy insulated shelter box if you don't already have one
  • Autumn leaves make great natural enrichment — rake a pile into the catio for your cat to play in

Winter (June–August)

  • Add a self-heating cat pad or microwavable heat disc to the shelter box
  • Ensure water doesn't freeze — use a heated water bowl or check more frequently
  • Wind chill can be severe in southern NZ; consider closing the cat door on the coldest nights
  • If your catio has a clear roof panel, clean it to maximise winter sunlight

Spring (September–November)

  • Deep clean the catio — scrub shelves, replace worn bedding, refresh the substrate
  • Replant cat grass and catnip after winter die-back
  • Check mesh and fixings after winter storms — Wellington cats, we feel your pain
  • Spring is prime hunting season for outdoor cats; keeping your cat contained protects native birds nesting

Rental-Friendly Catio Options

If you are renting, you can still give your cat outdoor access. Here are landlord-friendly options:

  • Free-standing window boxes that don't require permanent fixtures — some use suction cups or clamps
  • Pop-up mesh enclosures that fold away when not in use, similar to outdoor playpens
  • Cat enclosures on wheels that can be moved around the yard and stored in a garage
  • Balcony netting systems that clip to existing railings without drilling — Catnets products are popular for this
  • Window cat doors that fit into sliding window tracks without permanent modification

Always get written landlord approval before making any modifications, even temporary ones. Most landlords are receptive when you explain that a contained cat won't damage the property or bother neighbours.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Using chicken wire instead of welded mesh — Cats can push through it, and it rusts quickly in NZ conditions
  2. No shade or shelter — A catio in full sun is a sauna in summer; always include covered areas
  3. Forgetting about vertical space — Cats need to climb; a flat enclosure is boring
  4. No escape route back inside — Always ensure your cat can get back into the house, especially in bad weather
  5. Using treated timber without sealing — CCA-treated timber can be harmful if cats chew it; seal with non-toxic paint or use untreated macrocarpa
  6. Ignoring council rules — Check before you build; some structures need consent
  7. Not introducing the catio gradually — Let your cat explore the catio at their own pace; don't force them outside

Introducing Your Cat to the Catio

Some cats take to a catio immediately; others need time. Here is a step-by-step approach:

  1. Leave the cat door open for a few days with the catio door closed so your cat can investigate the smell
  2. Place treats and favourite toys inside the catio to create positive associations
  3. Sit in the catio with your cat for the first few visits — your presence provides reassurance
  4. Start with short sessions (10–15 minutes) and gradually increase the time
  5. Never force your cat outside — some cats, especially older indoor cats, may never want to use a catio, and that is okay
  6. Use catnip strategically — a sprinkle of catnip on a shelf can tempt reluctant explorers
