Cat Separation Anxiety: Signs, Causes & Solutions in NZ

By Juliano Ramos | Updated 16 April 2026 | 12 min read

It might surprise many New Zealand cat owners to learn that cats can suffer from separation anxiety just like dogs do. While dogs often show their distress overtly through barking, destructive behavior, or constant attention-seeking, cats express separation anxiety in more subtle ways that are easy to miss or misinterpret.

If you have noticed your cat behaving differently when you are about to leave the house, or returning home to find unexpected messes, it might not be mischief - it could be anxiety. Understanding the signs of separation anxiety in cats is the first step toward helping your feline friend feel more comfortable spending time alone.

What Is Separation Anxiety in Cats?

Separation anxiety in cats is a behavioural condition that occurs when a cat becomes excessively stressed when left alone or separated from their primary caregiver. Unlike dogs, cats are often seen as more independent creatures, which has led to the misconception that they do not form strong attachment bonds. However, cats can develop deep bonds with their owners and can experience significant distress when these bonds are disrupted.

The condition typically manifests when cats have not been properly conditioned to spending time alone, or when there have been changes in their routine or environment. In New Zealand, where many cat owners work full-time jobs, the issue has become increasingly common as cats are left alone for extended periods during the day.

Signs of Separation Anxiety in Cats

Recognising separation anxiety in cats requires careful observation of your cat's behaviour, both when you are home and when you are away. Here are the most common signs to watch for:

Inappropriate Elimination

One of the most common signs of separation anxiety in cats is urinating or defecating outside the litter box, particularly on items that carry your scent, such as your bed, clothing, or personal belongings. This behaviour is not spite or revenge - it is a coping mechanism where the cat is trying to mix their scent with yours to feel more secure.

Excessive Vocalisation

Cats with separation anxiety may become unusually vocal, meowing, yowling, or crying excessively, particularly immediately after you leave or just before you are expected to return. This loud behaviour is often a way of calling you back or expressing distress.

Destructive Behaviour

Your cat might scratch furniture, doors, windows, or other surfaces excessively, particularly around exit points like doors and windows. This behaviour can be an attempt to escape and find you, or it can be a way of releasing pent-up anxiety and stress.

Excessive Grooming

Cats that are anxious may over-groom themselves, pulling out fur or licking themselves raw. This behaviour, known as psychogenic alopecia, can lead to bald patches and skin infections if left untreated.

Following You Constantly

If your cat follows you from room to room, becomes distressed when you head toward the door, or seems unable to settle when you are at home, these can all be signs of attachment issues that may escalate when you leave.

Changes in Appetite

Some cats with separation anxiety will refuse food while you are away, while others may overeat as a coping mechanism. Both behaviours can indicate underlying stress that needs to be addressed.

What Causes Separation Anxiety in Cats?

Understanding the root causes of separation anxiety can help you address the problem more effectively. Several factors can contribute to the development of this condition:

Early Weaning or Separation

Cats that were separated from their mother and littermates too early, anywhere between birth and twelve weeks, may be more prone to developing attachment issues later in life. This is because they missed important developmental stages where they would have learned independence and coping skills.

Change in Routine

Cats thrive on routine, and any significant change can trigger anxiety. This might include a new work schedule that means you are away for longer periods, moving to a new home, the addition or loss of a family member (human or animal), or even changes in your daily routine such as feeding times.

Sudden Absence

If you have suddenly had to leave for an extended trip without proper preparation, or if there has been a traumatic event associated with your departure, your cat may develop anxiety about you leaving again.

Learned Behaviour

Sometimes, separation anxiety can be inadvertently reinforced. If your cat is anxious when you leave and you make a big fuss saying goodbye, or if you return home and comfort them excessively when they are distressed, you may be reinforcing the anxiety cycle.

Lack of Environmental Enrichment

Cats that are not mentally stimulated while alone can become bored and anxious. A lack of appropriate toys, climbing spaces, and hiding spots can contribute to stress and anxiety.

How to Help Your Cat with Separation Anxiety

Fortunately, there are many strategies you can employ to help your cat feel more comfortable when left alone. The key is to be patient and consistent in your approach.

Create a Safe Space

Designate a specific area in your home where your cat feels safe and secure. This might include a quiet room with their bed, favourite toys, scratching post, and items that smell like you. Make sure they have access to this space at all times, especially when you are leaving.

Establish a Departure Routine

Cats are experts at reading your body language and can detect subtle cues that you are about to leave. Try to make your departure routine as low-key as possible. Avoid making a big production of saying goodbye, and do not punish your cat for any anxious behaviour - this will only increase their stress.

Leave Under for Comfort

Consider leaving an item of clothing that smells like you with your cat when you leave. This can provide comfort and help your cat feel less alone. You might also consider leaving the radio or television on at low volume for background noise.

Environmental Enrichment

Invest in puzzle feeders, interactive toys, and climbing structures to keep your cat mentally stimulated while you are away. Cat trees, scratching posts, and window perches can all help to create a stimulating environment.

Practice Short Departures

Start by leaving your cat alone for very short periods - just a few minutes - and gradually increase the duration over time. This desensitisation process can help your cat learn that you always return.

Consider a Companion

In some cases, adopting a second cat can provide companionship and reduce loneliness. However, this is not always the right solution, particularly if your cat has shown aggression toward other animals.

Consult Your Vet

If your cat's separation anxiety is severe, it is worth consulting with your veterinarian. They may recommend behavioural therapy, pheromone treatments, or in some cases, medication to help manage the anxiety.

Conclusion

Separation anxiety in cats is a real and treatable condition. By understanding the signs and causes, you can take proactive steps to help your feline companion feel more comfortable when left alone. Remember to be patient - behavioural changes take time, and what works for one cat may not work for another.

If your cat's anxiety is impacting their quality of life or your ability to care for them properly, do not hesitate to seek professional help. With the right approach and support, most cats can learn to manage their anxiety and enjoy peaceful time alone.
