British Shorthair Care Guide for New Zealand Owners

Published 1 April 2026 • 10 min read • By ZiggySitters NZ

The British Shorthair is one of the world’s most beloved cat breeds — and for good reason. With their round faces, plush coats, and wonderfully calm temperaments, they make outstanding companions for New Zealand families, apartment dwellers, and retirees alike. If you share your home with one of these dignified felines, or you’re thinking about adding one to the family, this comprehensive care guide covers everything you need to know.

British Shorthair Breed Overview

Originally developed in Britain from Roman-era cats, the British Shorthair is one of the oldest recognised cat breeds. They are stocky, muscular cats with a characteristic “teddy bear” appearance: broad chest, round head, thick neck, and a short, dense plush coat that almost springs back when touched.

In New Zealand, British Shorthairs are increasingly popular thanks to their adaptability to apartment living and their generally undemanding nature. They tend to be quiet, not particularly vocal, and very content to spend hours observing the household from a comfortable perch.

Temperament and Personality

British Shorthairs are often described as “dog-like” in their loyalty. They will follow their owners from room to room, but they are not typically lap cats — they prefer to sit close rather than be constantly picked up. This makes them ideal for owners who want companionship without clinginess.

Key personality traits:
  • Calm and unflappable — rarely startled by noise or new situations
  • Adaptable — adjusts well to new environments when introduced carefully
  • Gentle and tolerant — generally good with children and other pets
  • Independent — can handle alone time better than many breeds
  • Playful but not hyperactive — enjoys interactive toys without demanding constant stimulation

Their independent streak means British Shorthairs cope relatively well when left alone during a working day, though they still benefit enormously from regular interaction and enrichment. If you work long hours, consider arranging a midday visit from a pet sitter to break up your cat’s day.

Feeding Your British Shorthair in NZ

British Shorthairs are prone to weight gain — one of their most common health challenges. Because they are not particularly active cats by nature, it is easy for them to become overweight, which puts pressure on joints and increases cardiovascular risk over time.

Feeding Guidelines

  • High-protein, lower-carbohydrate diet: British Shorthairs thrive on quality wet or raw food rather than carbohydrate-heavy dry kibble alone. Look for cat foods where meat or fish is the first listed ingredient.
  • Portion control: Use the guidelines on your cat’s food packaging as a starting point, then adjust based on body condition. You should be able to feel (but not easily see) your cat’s ribs.
  • Structured mealtimes: Two meals per day works well for most adult British Shorthairs. Avoid free-feeding, which makes it harder to monitor intake.
  • Fresh water: Always provide fresh water. Many British Shorthairs prefer a running water fountain, which also supports kidney health.
  • Treats: Keep treats to less than 10% of daily caloric intake. Opt for single-ingredient options like small pieces of cooked chicken.
NZ Tip: Several quality New Zealand and Australian-made raw and freeze-dried cat foods are excellent choices for British Shorthairs. Look for options from brands like K9 Natural (NZ) or Ziwi Peak (NZ) — both use locally sourced, high-quality proteins that align well with this breed’s dietary needs.

Grooming British Shorthairs

Despite their lush, plush coats, British Shorthairs are surprisingly low-maintenance in the grooming department compared to longhaired breeds. Their coats do not mat easily, but they do shed year-round and especially in spring and autumn as the seasons change in New Zealand.

Grooming Routine

  • Brushing: Once or twice a week with a soft slicker brush or rubber grooming mitt is usually sufficient. During heavy shedding periods, daily brushing helps reduce hairballs and keeps the coat looking its best.
  • Bathing: Rarely necessary unless your cat gets into something. Their dense coat takes a long time to dry thoroughly, so this is best left to a professional groomer if needed.
  • Nail trimming: Every 3–4 weeks. Provide a sturdy scratching post to allow natural nail maintenance between trims.
  • Ear cleaning: Check weekly. Clean gently with a veterinary ear cleaner and cotton wool if you notice debris. Do not insert anything into the ear canal.
  • Dental care: Daily tooth brushing is ideal. At minimum, use dental treats and schedule professional dental cleans with your vet.

Health Considerations for British Shorthairs in NZ

British Shorthairs are generally a robust and healthy breed, but there are a few conditions that owners and pet sitters should be aware of.

Common Health Issues

  • Obesity: The most common issue. Regular weigh-ins and portion discipline are essential preventive measures.
  • Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM): A genetic heart condition that can affect this breed. Annual cardiac screening by a vet is recommended from age three onwards.
  • Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD): Reputable breeders DNA test for this. Ask your breeder for certification.
  • Dental disease: Their somewhat compressed jaw means teeth can be crowded. Regular dental care is important.
  • Joint issues: Overweight individuals are at higher risk of arthritis, particularly in the hips.
Vet visits: Annual health checks are recommended for adult British Shorthairs. Keep vaccinations up to date with your NZ vet (core feline vaccines: herpesvirus, calicivirus, panleukopaenia). Discuss whether your indoor or outdoor cat requires additional coverage.

Exercise and Enrichment

While British Shorthairs are not the most energetic of cats, they still need daily mental and physical stimulation to stay healthy and prevent boredom-related behaviours.

  • Interactive play: 10–15 minutes of wand or feather toy play twice a day is sufficient for most British Shorthairs. They enjoy a good hunt-and-pounce session.
  • Puzzle feeders: Slow feeders and puzzle toys serve double duty — they slow down eating and provide mental stimulation.
  • Window perches: British Shorthairs love to watch the world. A secure window perch or catio view is excellent enrichment, particularly for indoor cats.
  • Cat trees: A sturdy cat tree gives them a safe vantage point. Choose one with wide platforms to accommodate their solid build.

British Shorthairs and Indoor vs Outdoor Living in NZ

New Zealand’s native wildlife is particularly vulnerable to domestic cats. Many councils and conservation organisations recommend keeping cats indoors or contained. The good news is that British Shorthairs adapt extremely well to full indoor living, provided they have adequate enrichment.

If you choose to give your British Shorthair outdoor access, consider a cat enclosure or “catio” — a contained outdoor space that allows fresh air and stimulation without allowing hunting of native birds or lizards. Leash-trained British Shorthairs can also enjoy supervised outdoor time; their calm temperament makes them one of the more leash-friendly breeds.

British Shorthairs with Children and Other Pets

British Shorthairs are notably tolerant cats. Their calm disposition means they tend to handle the unpredictable energy of young children better than more highly strung breeds. They are unlikely to scratch or bite unless seriously provoked, but they will typically remove themselves from a situation before reacting defensively.

They generally coexist well with dogs and other cats, particularly when introductions are managed gradually. Use scent swapping before direct meetings, keep initial interactions brief, and give your British Shorthair plenty of high perches and escape routes.

Finding a Pet Sitter for Your British Shorthair in NZ

British Shorthairs handle short periods of solitude reasonably well compared to more social breeds, but they still need someone to check in when you are away for more than a day. A good pet sitter will:

  • Provide at least one visit per day for feeding, fresh water, and litter box cleaning
  • Spend 15–20 minutes of interaction and play per visit
  • Monitor food intake (important given their weight tendencies)
  • Check for any signs of illness and contact you or an emergency vet if concerned
  • Follow your cat’s routine as closely as possible — British Shorthairs are creatures of habit
Leaving care notes: Before heading away, create a care sheet for your pet sitter that includes your cat’s daily feeding schedule and portions, favourite toys, any medical conditions or medications, your vet’s contact details, and any quirks to be aware of. The more information your sitter has, the more confident and effective they can be.

What to Tell Your Pet Sitter About British Shorthairs

If your sitter is not familiar with the breed, a quick briefing helps enormously. Let them know that British Shorthairs may seem aloof or standoffish at first — this is normal. They are not unfriendly, just dignified. A sitter who respects the cat’s space and moves calmly will win trust within a visit or two. Encouraging gentle play with a wand toy is often the quickest way to build rapport.

Also mention that these cats can be subtle about showing pain or discomfort. Changes in appetite, hiding more than usual, or changes in litter box habits are the most common early warning signs that something is off.

  • ✓ High-protein, portion-controlled diet — no free-feeding
  • ✓ Fresh water available at all times (fountain preferred)
  • ✓ Twice-weekly brushing, daily during shedding season

  • ✓ Enrichment: window perch, cat tree, puzzle feeder
  • ✓ Indoor or contained outdoor access to protect NZ wildlife
  • ✓ Regular nail trims and dental care
  • ✓ Trusted pet sitter for overnight or multi-day absences
