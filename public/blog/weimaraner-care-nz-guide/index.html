Weimaraner Care Guide NZ: Exercise, Diet & Pet Sitting Tips for the Grey Ghost

By ZiggySitters • March 31, 2026 • 8 min read

The Weimaraner — affectionately called the “Grey Ghost” — is one of the most striking and energetic dog breeds in New Zealand. These sleek, silver-coated hunting dogs are fiercely loyal, brilliantly athletic, and genuinely demanding. If you own one, you already know they are not your average pet. This guide covers everything you need to keep a Weimaraner thriving in the NZ climate, from exercise routines to diet and what to look for in a trusted pet sitter.

About the Weimaraner

Originally bred in 19th-century Germany as an all-purpose hunting dog, the Weimaraner was used to track large game such as deer and bears before transitioning to bird hunting. Their speed, stamina, and extraordinary sense of smell made them prized by German nobility.

Today, Weimaraners are popular in New Zealand as working dogs, sporting companions, and devoted family pets. They come in two coat varieties — smooth (short) and longhaired — though the smooth coat is by far the most common in NZ. Both are low-shedding relative to many other breeds, which apartment and allergy-conscious owners appreciate.

Exercise Requirements in New Zealand

There is no breed that illustrates “working dog energy” quite like the Weimaraner. Under-exercised Weimaraners become destructive, anxious, and difficult to manage. In a New Zealand context, the good news is that this country has remarkable natural resources for high-energy dogs.

Daily Exercise Minimum

A healthy adult Weimaraner requires a minimum of 2 hours of vigorous exercise per day. This is not a leisurely stroll — it should include running, off-lead play, and ideally some form of mentally engaging activity such as fetch, tracking, or agility.

  • Morning run (45–60 min): Ideal with a cyclist or jogger, or off-lead in a fenced park
  • Midday mental stimulation (20–30 min): Training drills, sniff walks, puzzle feeders
  • Evening walk or play (30–45 min): Lower intensity winding-down activity
NZ Tip: Many New Zealand beaches allow dogs off-lead in off-peak hours. Weimaraners love water and are strong swimmers. Beaches near Auckland (Orewa, Muriwai), Wellington (Plimmerton), and Christchurch (Sumner) are excellent options for a morning sprint and swim session.

Puppies and Young Dogs

For Weimaraner puppies under 12 months, avoid high-impact exercise that can damage developing joints. Follow the 5-minute rule: 5 minutes of exercise per month of age, twice a day. A 4-month-old puppy needs no more than 20 minutes per session. Keep exercise on soft surfaces like grass rather than concrete.

Senior Weimaraners

Older Weimaraners (8+ years) still need daily activity but benefit from shorter, lower-impact sessions. Swimming is excellent for maintaining muscle tone without stressing joints. Watch for signs of hip dysplasia, which is more common in older large breeds.

Diet and Nutrition

Weimaraners are deep-chested dogs, which means they are at elevated risk of gastric dilatation-volvulus (GDV), commonly known as bloat. This can be fatal if not treated immediately and is directly influenced by feeding habits.

Feeding Guidelines

  • Feed 2–3 smaller meals per day rather than one large meal
  • Never exercise your Weimaraner within 1 hour before or after eating
  • Use a raised food bowl to reduce air intake
  • Choose a premium kibble with a named meat protein as the first ingredient (e.g., chicken, lamb, venison)
  • Adult Weimaraners typically need 450–600g of dry kibble per day depending on activity level
Bloat Warning: If your Weimaraner appears restless, attempts to vomit without producing anything, or has a distended abdomen, seek emergency veterinary care immediately. Bloat can be fatal within hours.

Foods to Avoid

All the standard canine toxins apply: no chocolate, grapes, raisins, onions, garlic, macadamia nuts, xylitol, or cooked bones. New Zealand-specific hazards include tuatua and pipi shells on beaches (sharp, can cause internal injury if ingested) and certain native plants such as ngaio, karaka berries, and tutu.

Health and Grooming

Common Health Issues in NZ Weimaraners

  • Hip and elbow dysplasia: Screen breeding dogs. Look for hip-scored parents when buying a puppy.
  • Hypothyroidism: Symptoms include weight gain, lethargy, and coat changes. Manageable with daily medication.
  • Entropion: Eyelid rolling inward, causing corneal irritation. Can require surgery.
  • Gastric dilatation-volvulus (bloat): As above — the most acute risk in this breed.
  • Von Willebrand disease: A clotting disorder. Test breeding dogs before mating.

Grooming Needs

One of the Weimaraner’s practical advantages is their low-maintenance coat. Short-haired Weimaraners need only a weekly wipe-down with a damp cloth or rubber grooming mitt to remove loose hair and keep their coat gleaming. Bathing once a month (or when genuinely dirty) is typically sufficient. Longhaired Weimaraners require brushing every 2–3 days to prevent tangles.

Pay attention to ears — Weimaraners have floppy ears that trap moisture. Clean weekly with a vet-approved ear cleaner, especially after swimming. Nails should be trimmed every 3–4 weeks.

Training and Mental Stimulation

Weimaraners are highly intelligent but notorious for testing boundaries. Early, consistent, positive reinforcement-based training is essential. They excel at obedience, agility, tracking, and hunt tests — all popular dog sports in New Zealand.

Key Training Principles

  • Begin obedience training at 8–10 weeks of age
  • Enrol in puppy school as early as possible (many NZ vets and SPCA branches run programmes)
  • Use high-value treats — Weimaraners respond best to food rewards
  • Keep sessions short (10–15 minutes) and varied to maintain focus
  • Avoid punishment-based methods, which can trigger anxiety and aggression in sensitive dogs
NZ Dog Sports: Dog NZ (formerly the New Zealand Kennel Club) runs agility, obedience, and tracking competitions across the country. Weimaraners are competitive in all three disciplines.

Separation Anxiety: The Weimaraner’s Biggest Challenge

More than almost any other breed, Weimaraners form intense bonds with their humans. This “velcro dog” quality is endearing at home but can become a serious problem when owners go to work. Weimaraners with untreated separation anxiety will bark continuously, destroy furniture, self-harm (licking paws until raw), and attempt to escape.

If you work full-time, a Weimaraner requires either a dog-friendly workplace, doggy daycare, or a dedicated pet sitter who can spend meaningful time — not just a quick check-in — with your dog during the day.

Finding a Pet Sitter for Your Weimaraner in NZ

Not every pet sitter is equipped to handle a high-drive, large breed like a Weimaraner. When searching for care, look for sitters who:

  • Have experience with large, energetic breeds — ideally working dogs or sporting breeds
  • Can commit to 2+ hours of outdoor exercise per day
  • Have secure, fully fenced outdoor space (Weimaraners can jump 1.8m fences)
  • Are comfortable with dogs that test boundaries — the sitter needs confidence
  • Can provide regular photo/video updates so you can see your dog is settled
  • Know the signs of bloat and have a nearby vet contact

Weimaraners and NZ Wildlife

New Zealand’s native wildlife presents unique hazards for a breed with strong hunting instincts. Weimaraners have a high prey drive and will chase birds, lizards, and small mammals instinctively. Always keep your dog on lead near known kiwi habitat, wetlands, and coastal reserves. Many New Zealand reserves require dogs to be on lead or prohibit them entirely to protect threatened native species. Check the local council or Department of Conservation rules before visiting any reserve with your Weimaraner.

Summary: Is a Weimaraner Right for Your NZ Lifestyle?

Weimaraners are extraordinary companions for the right owner — active, outdoorsy, and present. They are not ideal for apartment living, long working hours without support, or first-time dog owners who are unprepared for a high-maintenance breed. With the right investment in exercise, training, and quality care, a Weimaraner will reward you with a decade of fierce loyalty and endless energy.

Whether you’re heading overseas, working late, or simply need reliable daily support, connecting with an experienced Weimaraner-friendly pet sitter is one of the best investments you can make for your dog’s wellbeing.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much exercise does a Weimaraner need in NZ?

A healthy adult Weimaraner needs at least 2 hours of vigorous exercise daily. New Zealand’s off-lead parks and beaches are ideal for this breed’s running and swimming needs.

Can Weimaraners be left alone during the day?

Weimaraners are prone to severe separation anxiety and should not be left alone for extended periods. A professional pet sitter or doggy daycare is strongly recommended for full-time working owners.

Are Weimaraners good family dogs in New Zealand?

Yes — they are affectionate, loyal, and great with children when properly socialised. They thrive in active NZ households with access to open space and regular exercise.

What should I feed a Weimaraner in NZ?

A premium kibble with named meat protein as the first ingredient, fed in 2–3 smaller meals per day. Avoid exercising before or after meals to reduce the risk of bloat.
