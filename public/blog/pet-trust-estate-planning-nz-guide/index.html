What Happens to Your Pet If Something Happens to You?

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  July 2, 2026  •  13 min read

It is not a comfortable topic, but it is an essential one. Over 60% of New Zealand households include a pet, yet most owners have no formal plan for what happens to their animal companion if they die, become incapacitated, or face an emergency. This guide walks you through every option available under New Zealand law, from simple arrangements to formal trusts, so your pet is never left without care.

The Uncomfortable Truth

When a pet owner dies or is hospitalised unexpectedly in New Zealand, the pet\'s fate depends entirely on whether someone else knows about the animal and is willing to step in. Without a plan:

  • Pets may be discovered days later, alone and without food or water
  • Family members may assume someone else is taking responsibility
  • Animals can end up at SPCA shelters where, if not claimed quickly, they may be rehomed or euthanised
  • Multiple family members may dispute who takes the pet, causing stress for the animal and delays in care

The SPCA takes in over 30,000 animals annually across New Zealand. Many of those are pets whose owners experienced an unexpected crisis with no care plan in place. You can change that outcome for your pet with a few hours of planning.

Option 1: Informal Arrangements (Start Here)

The simplest step is also the most commonly overlooked: talk to someone you trust and make sure they know your wishes.

Choose a Pet Guardian

A pet guardian is the person who would take your animal if you could not care for them. This person should:

  • Know your pet and have a good relationship with them
  • Have a suitable home environment (space, yard, other pets compatibility)
  • Be financially stable enough to manage ongoing care costs
  • Live close enough to respond quickly in an emergency
  • Genuinely want the responsibility (do not assume this; ask directly)
Ziggy Tip: Always have a backup guardian. Your first choice may move overseas, change circumstances, or be unable to help when the time comes. Having a second and third option means your pet always has someone.

Create a Pet Care Information Sheet

Write down everything a caregiver would need to know. Keep this in an obvious place and give copies to your guardians:

  • Pet\'s full name and any nicknames they respond to
  • Microchip number and registration details
  • Registration details (NZ dog registration for dogs)
  • Vet name, clinic address, and phone number
  • Vaccination records and due dates
  • Medication names, dosages, and schedule
  • Diet: exact food brand, portion size, and feeding times
  • Allergies or food sensitivities
  • Behavioural quirks, fears, and triggers
  • Daily routine: walk times, play preferences, bedtime habits
  • Insurance policy number and provider
  • Emergency after-hours vet clinic address and phone
  • Preferences for end-of-life care (if applicable)

The Window Card

Place a card in your front window or on your fridge that says: "Animals inside. In emergency, contact [name] on [phone]." Emergency responders and neighbours will see this and know to check on your pet. The SPCA and several NZ pet organisations offer free window cards for this purpose.

Option 2: Your Will (Essential for Every Pet Owner)

In New Zealand, pets are legally classified as property. This means you cannot leave money directly to an animal in a will. However, you can specify who receives your pet and leave a gift of money to that person on the condition that they care for the animal.

What to Include in Your Will

  • A specific bequest of your pet to a named individual, along with a backup person if the first is unable or unwilling
  • A monetary gift to the guardian to cover care costs. This can be a set amount or a percentage of your estate
  • A letter of wishes (kept with the will) detailing your pet\'s care preferences, routine, and veterinary history. This is not legally binding but provides essential guidance
  • A directive that if no guardian is available, your pet should go to a specific rescue organisation rather than a shelter
Important legal note: Under NZ law, a gift with conditions (like "to Sarah, provided she cares for my dog Buster") can be challenged if Sarah stops caring for Buster. The money becomes hers once she accepts the bequest. To create enforceable conditions, you need a trust (see Option 3). Talk to a lawyer about the right structure for your situation.

What Happens Without a Will

If you die intestate (without a will) in New Zealand, the Administration Act 1969 determines who gets your property, including your pet. Your pet goes to whoever inherits your personal property, which may not be the person best suited to care for them. If no family member claims the animal, they become the responsibility of the estate executor, who may surrender them to a shelter.

Option 3: Pet Trusts (The Gold Standard)

New Zealand does not have a specific "pet trust" statute like some US states, but you can create a trust for the benefit of your pet under the Trusts Act 2019. This is the most robust legal arrangement available.

How a Pet Trust Works in NZ

A pet trust is a legal arrangement where you (the settlor) transfer assets to a trustee, who is legally obligated to use those assets for the care of your pet. The key parties are:

  • Settlor: You, the pet owner creating the trust
  • Trustee: The person or organisation managing the trust assets (can be a lawyer, trusted friend, or trust company)
  • Guardian/caregiver: The person physically caring for the pet
  • Enforcer: Someone designated to ensure the trustee fulfils their obligations (a friend or family member who can take legal action if the trust terms are breached)

What a Pet Trust Covers

Trust Provision Details
Guardian designation Names who will physically care for the pet, with backup guardians
Care instructions Detailed standards of care: food, vet, grooming, exercise, living conditions
Financial provisions Amount held in trust, how funds are disbursed, and for what purposes
Veterinary care Authority and budget for medical treatment, including end-of-life decisions
Trust duration Remains active for the lifetime of your pet, then remaining assets distributed per your instructions
Enforcement Named enforcer can take legal action if trustee or guardian neglects duties
Remainder beneficiaries Who receives any leftover funds after your pet passes
Ziggy Tip: A pet trust does not have to be expensive. If your pet has modest care needs, a simple trust through a community law centre or a solicitor can cost as little as $500-$1,500 to set up. The peace of mind is worth far more.

Option 4: Enduring Power of Attorney (EPOA)

A will only takes effect after death. What if you are temporarily or permanently incapacitated? An Enduring Power of Attorney (EPOA) under the Protection of Personal and Property Rights Act 1988 allows you to designate someone to make decisions on your behalf, including for your pet.

Two Types of EPOA Relevant to Pet Care

  • Property EPOA: Allows your attorney to access your funds to pay for pet care, vet bills, and related expenses while you are unable to.
  • Personal Care and Welfare EPOA: Allows your attorney to make decisions about your living situation, which can include ensuring your pet stays with you or is appropriately cared for.
Do not assume your EPOA covers pets automatically. Standard EPOA forms rarely mention animals. Include a specific clause directing your attorney to arrange and fund your pet\'s care, naming your preferred guardian and vet. Without this, your attorney may have no clear authority to spend your money on your pet.

Option 5: Emergency Short-Term Arrangements

Not every crisis involves death or long-term incapacity. Sometimes you need someone to step in for a few days or weeks. Here is how to prepare.

The Emergency Contact Card

Keep a card in your wallet and on your phone that lists:

  • Number of pets at home and their species/breed
  • Emergency caregiver name and phone number
  • Vet clinic name and phone
  • Any critical medical needs (medication, allergies)

If you are in an accident, first responders or hospital staff can use this to ensure your pet is not left alone.

Neighbour and Friend Network

Give a trusted neighbour or nearby friend a key to your home and brief them on your pet\'s basic needs. Even if they only check in for 24 hours, that bridge can save your pet from distress until your designated guardian arrives.

Special Considerations

Multiple Pets

If you have more than one pet, specify in your will and trust whether they should stay together. Bonded pairs and multi-pet households suffer significantly when separated. However, also be realistic about whether one guardian can manage all your animals. Sometimes the kindest arrangement is different guardians for different pets, with provisions for visitation.

Senior and Special Needs Pets

Elderly or medically complex pets need guardians who can handle medication schedules, vet visits, and end-of-life decisions. Be explicit about what level of veterinary intervention you want. Document your preferences for:

  • Maximum amount you would spend on emergency vet care
  • Whether you want heroic measures or palliative comfort care
  • Quality of life thresholds that would trigger end-of-life decisions
  • Preferred vet clinic and specialist contacts

Rental Properties and Pets

New Zealand\'s tenancy laws do not guarantee that your guardian will be able to keep your pet in a rental property. If your guardian rents, discuss this with them. Consider including in your trust a provision for pet-related housing costs, such as a pet bond or temporary accommodation.

Step-by-Step: Your Pet Protection Action Plan

  • Talk to 2-3 people about becoming your pet\'s guardian and get their agreement
  • Create a detailed Pet Care Information Sheet with all medical, dietary, and behavioural details
  • Place a window card or fridge card with emergency contact details
  • Add your pet to your will with a specific bequest and guardian designation
  • Consider setting up a pet trust if your pet has significant care needs or a long expected lifespan
  • Update your Enduring Power of Attorney to include pet care provisions
  • Keep an emergency contact card in your wallet and phone
  • Give a trusted neighbour or friend a spare key and basic care instructions
  • Register with a pet sitting service for emergency backup
  • Review and update all arrangements annually or when circumstances change

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I leave money directly to my pet in NZ?

No. Under New Zealand law, pets are property and cannot inherit. You must leave money to a person (guardian or trustee) with instructions to use it for the pet\'s care. A trust provides the strongest legal mechanism for this.

What if my designated guardian cannot take my pet when the time comes?

Always name at least two backup guardians. If all named guardians are unable, your will or trust should specify a rescue organisation to contact. SPCA and breed-specific rescues can often assist with rehoming.

How much does it cost to set up a pet trust in NZ?

A simple pet trust through a solicitor typically costs $500 to $2,000 in legal fees, plus the amount you fund the trust with. More complex trusts involving significant assets will cost more.

Does pet insurance help with this?

Pet insurance covers veterinary costs while you are alive and paying premiums. It does not cover ongoing care after your death. However, it is worth ensuring your trust or guardian can continue the policy, as a pre-existing condition not covered by insurance could create significant costs.

What about relationship property?

If you separate from a partner, pet custody falls under the relationship property regime if the pet was acquired during the relationship. The court treats the pet as property, not as a child. Specifying pet ownership in a relationship property agreement (pre-nup) is the best way to avoid disputes. See our pet custody and separation guide for more details.

  • In NZ, pets are property. You cannot leave money directly to them — use a will bequest with a named guardian as a minimum.
  • A pet trust provides the strongest legal protection, ensuring funds are actually spent on your pet\'s care.
  • Update your Enduring Power of Attorney to include pet care provisions for incapacity situations.
  • Create a Pet Care Information Sheet and window card so emergency responders know about your animal.
  • Register with a pet sitting service for emergency backup care.
  • Review all arrangements annually or whenever your circumstances change.
