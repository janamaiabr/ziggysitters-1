Dog Body Language Guide: Reading What Your Dog Is Telling You

2 April 2026 · 10 min read · ZiggySitters Team

Dogs communicate constantly — through the angle of their tail, the tension in their shoulders, the width of their eyes. The challenge is that most of us were never taught how to listen. Misreading a dog can turn a simple greeting into a bite, or cause a stressed animal to escalate unnecessarily. Whether you are a dog owner in Auckland, a pet sitter in Wellington, or just someone who encounters dogs regularly in your daily New Zealand life, understanding canine body language is one of the most valuable skills you can develop.

This guide walks you through the core signals dogs use, how to read them in context, and what to do when you spot stress or discomfort in a dog in your care.

Why Body Language Matters More Than You Think

Dogs do not have the words to say "I am uncomfortable" or "I am about to snap." Instead, they use a sequence of increasingly obvious signals called a bite ladder or stress escalation sequence. A bite is almost never the first message — it is the last resort after quieter signals have been ignored.

Studies in veterinary behaviour consistently show that most dog bites in household settings occur because people missed or dismissed earlier warning signals. By learning to recognise the early signs, you give both yourself and the dog the chance to de-escalate before anything goes wrong. This is especially important for pet sitters and dog walkers who are working with unfamiliar dogs, and for children who may not naturally read canine cues.

A note on context

No single signal tells the full story. A wagging tail does not always mean a happy dog. A yawn is not always tiredness. Always read the whole dog in the whole situation — body posture, facial expression, environment, and recent history all matter.

The Relaxed, Happy Dog

Before learning to spot stress, it helps to have a clear picture of what a calm, content dog looks like:

  • Tail: Held in a neutral, relaxed position (varies by breed) and wagging with loose, full-body engagement. The wag involves hips and sometimes the whole rear end.
  • Ears: Held loosely in their natural position — neither pinned back nor rigidly forward.
  • Eyes: Soft, almond-shaped, with no visible white. Blinking at a normal rate.
  • Mouth: Slightly open, relaxed. May hang open with tongue lolling — the classic "happy dog" look.
  • Body: Weight evenly distributed. Muscles loose. No tension along the shoulders, neck, or hindquarters.
  • Movement: Wiggly and bouncy with a loose, fluid gait.

This is your baseline. Any departure from this relaxed state is worth noticing.

Early Stress Signals: The Subtle Signs

Early stress signs are easy to miss because they look innocuous in isolation. Dogs use these calming signals — a term coined by Norwegian trainer Turid Rugaas — to manage their own anxiety and to communicate peaceful intentions to others:

  • Yawning when not tired, especially in response to a person approaching
  • Lip licking or nose licking without food present
  • Turning away or offering a side view rather than facing you directly
  • Sniffing the ground intensely during a social interaction
  • Shaking off as if wet, when the dog is dry
  • Blinking slowly or looking away briefly
  • Sitting or lying down when approached — not always submission, sometimes a calming signal

When you see these signals, the appropriate response is to give the dog more space, slow your movements, and avoid direct eye contact. In a pet-sitting context, these signals are important to notice during a first meet with a new dog — they tell you the dog needs time and a calm, patient approach before they feel safe.

Moderate Stress: Escalating Signals

If early signals are ignored, dogs move to clearer, harder-to-miss communications:

  • Whale eye: The white of the eye (sclera) becomes visible as the dog turns their head slightly but keeps their gaze fixed on something. The eye looks white and tense.
  • Stiff tail: The tail rises and becomes rigid. A stiff, upright wag is not always friendly — it can signal high arousal or tension.
  • Ears pinned back flat against the head (fear) or pricked rigidly forward (threat assessment).
  • Hackles raised: The fur along the spine, especially at the shoulders and base of the tail, stands up. This is an involuntary stress response.
  • Freezing: The dog goes completely still. This is a serious warning — the dog is deciding what to do next.
  • Low growl with closed or tight mouth.
  • Weight shifting back with head low — fear posture indicating the dog wants to flee.
  • Weight forward with stiff legs — the dog may be considering advancing.

Freezing is a serious signal

If a dog freezes — goes completely still with a rigid body — back away slowly and calmly. Do not reach toward the dog, do not make direct eye contact, and do not run. Give the dog space immediately.

Tail Position: A Detailed Breakdown

The tail is one of the most expressive parts of a dog, but it requires careful interpretation because tail carriage varies significantly between breeds. A Greyhound naturally carries its tail low; a Husky naturally carries it high and curled. Always assess tail signals relative to the individual dog's neutral position:

  • Tail tucked between legs: Fear, submission, or pain. Combined with lowered body posture, this dog is very uncomfortable.
  • Tail low and slow wag: Uncertainty or mild insecurity. The dog is not confident in the situation.
  • Tail neutral and broad wag: Generally relaxed and friendly. The whole rear moves with the wag.
  • Tail high and fast wag: High arousal — can be excitement or aggression depending on context. A stiff, rapid wag from a still body is not necessarily friendly.
  • Tail raised and rigid: Alertness, potential threat behaviour. Take note of the rest of the dog.

Facial Expressions

Dogs have a surprisingly complex range of facial expressions. Key ones to know:

  • Relaxed mouth: Slightly open, tongue visible, no tension in the lips or jaw.
  • Closed mouth / tight lips: Tension. The dog is on alert or uncomfortable.
  • Lip curl: The upper lip raises to expose teeth. This is a warning — do not dismiss it.
  • Snarl with wrinkled muzzle: Clear warning signal. The dog is prepared to escalate.
  • Submissive grin: Some dogs pull back their lips horizontally in a greeting that can look like a snarl but is accompanied by squinting eyes, low body posture, and a wagging tail. This is friendly, not threatening.

Eye contact is also worth understanding. Dogs avoid prolonged direct eye contact as a calming signal. Hard, unwavering eye contact from a dog is a challenge, not an invitation to connect. When greeting an unfamiliar dog, avoid sustained eye contact and let the dog approach you.

Body Posture and What It Communicates

Whole-body posture gives you the big picture:

  • Play bow (front end down, rear up, often bouncing): Clear invitation to play. This is almost always friendly.
  • Rolling onto back: Can be submission or a request for belly rubs from a trusted person. Context matters — if combined with a tense body and whale eye, it may be appeasement in discomfort rather than an invitation.
  • Leaning into you: Generally affectionate. Some dogs do this to request reassurance.
  • Making themselves small: Lowered body, tucked tail, flattened ears — the dog is trying to appear non-threatening because they are afraid.
  • Standing tall and forward: Confident or assertive posture. Combined with a tense face and stiff tail, this can indicate a readiness to confront.

Reading Dogs in a Pet-Sitting Context

For pet sitters in New Zealand, reading body language correctly can make the difference between a smooth stay and a stressful one. Here is how to apply these skills practically:

At the initial meet-and-greet

Ask the owner to let the dog approach you, rather than you approaching the dog. Crouch down sideways (not facing the dog directly), avoid eye contact, and let the dog sniff at their own pace. Watch for early calming signals that indicate the dog needs more time. Do not rush this process — first impressions have a lasting effect on how a dog relates to their sitter.

During the first day alone with a new dog

Watch for stress signals that indicate the dog is not comfortable: excessive yawning, inability to settle, scanning the room, pacing, or refusing food. These are normal in the first few hours but should begin to ease. If stress signals persist, keep your own energy calm and predictable, maintain routines, and avoid introducing anything new or exciting.

On walks with unfamiliar dogs

If you encounter another dog, watch both dogs for early stress signals before allowing them to approach. Many dog-on-dog incidents happen when a stressed or reactive dog is approached head-on by an exuberant stranger. When in doubt, give the other dog space by crossing the road or creating distance.

Signs Your Dog Is Uncomfortable With Their Sitter

Before leaving your dog with a new sitter, watch for these signals during the introduction:

  • Dog moves away from the sitter and seeks you out repeatedly
  • Dog will not take treats from the sitter (especially if normally food-motivated)
  • Dog shows calming signals (lip licking, yawning, turning away) when the sitter approaches
  • Stiff body or freezing when the sitter reaches toward them

If you notice these signals, take more time at the introduction. Have the sitter toss treats toward the dog without reaching for them, and allow the dog to choose their own level of engagement. If a dog is consistently uncomfortable despite a calm approach, trust that signal — not every sitter-dog pairing is right, and finding a better fit is always an option.

Teaching Children to Read Dogs

Children are disproportionately represented in dog bite statistics, largely because they miss or ignore stress signals that adults would catch. In New Zealand, the Dog Control Act places responsibility on owners to prevent incidents, but education is the most powerful prevention tool.

Teach children these simple rules: ask before touching any dog, let the dog sniff your closed hand first, watch for the dog turning away or licking their lips (they need space), never approach a dog who is eating or sleeping, and never chase a dog who moves away. Even young children can learn to recognise a happy bouncy dog versus a tense or scared one if you make it a game.

Building a Stronger Bond Through Better Communication

Learning to read your dog is not just about safety — it is about connection. When you notice that your dog is uncomfortable in a crowded space and advocate for them, they learn to trust you. When you respond to their calming signals by backing off rather than pushing, they learn that communication works and that escalation is unnecessary.

Dogs who feel heard tend to be calmer, more confident, and more relaxed in new situations — including time with a pet sitter. The investment in learning their language pays off every day, in every interaction, and makes life better for both of you.
