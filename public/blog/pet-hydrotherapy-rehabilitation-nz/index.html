What Is Pet Hydrotherapy?

Hydrotherapy literally means “water therapy.” For pets, it involves controlled exercise in water — either swimming in a purpose-built pool or walking on an underwater treadmill. The water provides buoyancy (reducing weight-bearing on joints by up to 60%), resistance (building muscle without impact), and warmth (relaxing muscles and increasing blood flow to injured tissues).

The key difference between “swimming at the beach” and hydrotherapy is control. A hydrotherapy session is planned, supervised, and progressive. The therapist controls water temperature, depth, duration, and intensity based on your pet\'s specific condition and recovery stage. Beach swimming is great for fit dogs, but for a post-surgical patient, uncontrolled swimming can actually cause harm.

Three key benefits of water: Buoyancy reduces joint loading, resistance builds muscle safely, and warmth promotes circulation and healing. No land-based exercise offers all three simultaneously.

Conditions Treated with Hydrotherapy

Hydrotherapy is used for a wide range of conditions in dogs, and increasingly for cats as well. The most common include:

Orthopaedic Surgery Recovery

  • Cruciate ligament repair (TPLO, TTA, extracapsular): The most common reason dogs are referred to hydrotherapy in NZ. Post-surgical rehabilitation typically begins 2–4 weeks after surgery (once incisions have healed) and continues for 8–16 weeks.
  • Hip dysplasia surgery: Including femoral head ostectomy (FHO) and total hip replacement. Hydrotherapy helps rebuild the muscle mass that supports the joint and prevents compensatory strain on healthy limbs.
  • Fracture repair: Once the fracture is stable and the surgical site is healed, hydrotherapy helps restore range of motion and rebuild muscle lost during immobilisation.
  • Patellar luxation surgery: Rehabilitation focuses on strengthening the quadriceps and stabilising the knee.

Chronic Conditions

  • Osteoarthritis: Perhaps the single biggest beneficiary of ongoing hydrotherapy. Regular sessions maintain muscle mass, improve joint mobility, reduce pain, and slow the progression of arthritis. For many older NZ dogs, weekly hydrotherapy is the difference between a comfortable life and constant pain.
  • Elbow dysplasia: Common in larger breeds (Labradors, German Shepherds, Retrievers). Hydrotherapy maintains fitness while reducing impact on affected joints.
  • Intervertebral disc disease (IVDD): Particularly in dachshunds, corgis, and other long-backed breeds. Hydrotherapy is a key part of post-surgical and conservative management.
  • Degenerative myelopathy: While progressive, hydrotherapy helps maintain mobility and muscle mass for as long as possible.

Weight Management

Obesity is a significant problem in NZ dogs and cats. Hydrotherapy provides high-calorie-burning exercise with minimal joint stress, making it ideal for overweight pets who cannot tolerate normal land exercise. A 30-minute hydrotherapy session can burn as many calories as a 60-minute walk for a medium-sized dog.

Neurological Conditions

  • Post-stroke rehabilitation: Water supports the body while the pet relearns motor patterns.
  • Fibrocartilaginous embolism (FCE): Recovery varies, but hydrotherapy aids in regaining proprioception and strength.
  • Peripheral nerve injuries: Water provides a safe environment for gait retraining.
Important: Hydrotherapy should only begin with veterinary approval. Your vet will determine when your pet is ready (usually after surgical wounds have healed and sutures are removed) and may refer you to a qualified canine rehabilitation practitioner. Never start hydrotherapy without veterinary clearance.

Types of Hydrotherapy

Swimming Pools (Canine Hydrotherapy Pools)

Purpose-built heated pools (typically 28–32°C) designed for dogs to swim under controlled conditions. The therapist may swim alongside the dog, guide them with a harness, or use a pool jet to create current resistance. Pools are excellent for:

  • Non-weight-bearing exercise (ideal for early post-surgical stages)
  • Cardiovascular fitness
  • Building overall body condition and endurance
  • Dogs that enjoy swimming (most Labradors, Retrievers, Spaniels)

Pools are less suitable for dogs that are nervous in water or need very precise gait retraining, as the swimming motion differs from normal walking.

Underwater Treadmills

An underwater treadmill is exactly what it sounds like: a treadmill inside a water-filled chamber. The water level can be adjusted to control buoyancy, and the belt speed controls exercise intensity. Underwater treadmills are particularly effective for:

  • Gait retraining — the dog walks in a normal pattern, with water supporting their weight
  • Targeted muscle strengthening — the therapist can focus on specific limbs or muscle groups
  • Proprioceptive retraining — helping the dog regain awareness of limb position after neurological injury
  • Dogs that are nervous about swimming — they feel more secure with their feet on a surface
Which Is Better? For most rehabilitation cases, the underwater treadmill is the gold standard because it reproduces functional walking patterns with reduced load. Swimming pools are excellent for general conditioning and dogs that love water. Many NZ centres offer both, and your therapist may recommend a combination.

What a Hydrotherapy Session Looks Like

Understanding what happens in a session helps you prepare your pet (and yourself). A typical hydrotherapy appointment follows this structure:

  1. Assessment (5–10 minutes): The therapist observes your pet standing, walking, and (if relevant) sitting and rising. They check range of motion, muscle symmetry, gait abnormalities, and pain responses. This assessment determines the session plan.
  2. Warm-up (5 minutes): Passive range-of-motion exercises, gentle massage, or a short walk to increase blood flow and prepare tissues for exercise.
  3. Water session (15–30 minutes): The main event. Your pet swims or walks in the pool or treadmill under close supervision. The therapist adjusts intensity, duration, and water depth based on your pet\'s response and recovery stage. For early post-surgical patients, this may start at just 5–10 minutes and gradually increase.
  4. Cool-down (5 minutes): Gentle massage, stretching, and drying. Most centres use warm air dryers. Your pet should not be sent home wet and cold, especially in winter.
  5. Home exercise plan: Your therapist provides exercises to do at home between sessions. These are essential — hydrotherapy once a week is not enough on its own to achieve rehabilitation goals.

Finding a Hydrotherapy Centre in NZ

The number of canine hydrotherapy centres in New Zealand has grown significantly, and most major cities now have at least one facility. Here are some pointers for finding a reputable centre:

  • Veterinary referral: Ask your vet first. They will know the best local facilities and can provide a referral, which is required by most reputable centres before starting treatment.
  • Qualifications matter: Look for therapists with qualifications from recognised bodies such as the Canine Rehabilitation Institute (CRI), the Academy of Canine Physiotherapy, or equivalent NZ-recognised qualifications. In New Zealand, canine physiotherapists should be registered with the Physiotherapy Board of NZ.
  • Facility standards: The pool or treadmill should be clean, well-maintained, and heated. Water should be tested and treated (typically with chlorine or saltwater systems). Ask about their water testing regime and infection control protocols.
  • Individual assessment: A good centre will not start treatment without reviewing your vet\'s referral, doing their own assessment, and creating a written treatment plan with goals.

How Pet Sitters Support Rehabilitation

If your pet is undergoing hydrotherapy and rehabilitation, you will likely need pet sitting support — whether for transport to appointments, medication administration, exercise compliance, or simply being there when you cannot. Here is how a good pet sitter helps:

  1. Transport to appointments: If your pet has hydrotherapy sessions two or three times per week, and you work full-time, getting them there can be challenging. A pet sitter experienced with rehab transport can pick up your dog, take them to their session, wait, and bring them home.
  2. Home exercise compliance: The exercises your therapist sends home are critical to recovery. A pet sitter who understands the rehab plan can ensure exercises are done correctly and consistently — not skipped because “the dog did not seem keen.”
  3. Post-surgical wound monitoring: If your pet is recovering from surgery, a sitter can check incision sites for redness, swelling, discharge, or excessive licking. They can also manage the “cone of shame” (Elizabethan collar) routine if your pet is wearing one.
  4. Medication administration: Post-surgical pets are often on pain medication, anti-inflammatories, and sometimes antibiotics. A sitter can ensure medication is given on time and at the correct dose.
  5. Restricted activity enforcement: One of the hardest parts of pet rehabilitation is keeping them calm. No running, no jumping on furniture, no stairs (or limited stairs), no off-leash walks. A sitter enforces these restrictions even when your pet gives you those big eyes.
  6. Progress reporting: A good sitter documents your pet\'s daily function — weight-bearing on the affected limb, appetite, energy levels, pain indicators — and reports back to you and your vet or therapist.

Home Care During Rehabilitation

Hydrotherapy sessions are only one piece of the recovery puzzle. What happens at home between sessions is equally important. Here is a practical home care guide for pets in rehabilitation:

Environment Modifications

  • Non-slip surfaces: Place yoga mats or rubber-backed rugs on slippery floors (hardwood, tile, laminate). Slipping is a major setback for recovering pets.
  • Ramps: If your dog normally gets on the couch or bed, provide a ramp with good traction. Jumping is one of the most common causes of re-injury.
  • Block stairs: Use baby gates to restrict stair access during the early recovery phase. Carry small dogs up and down stairs if needed.
  • Raised food bowls: For dogs with neck or spinal issues, elevated food and water bowls reduce strain.
  • Comfortable bedding: Orthopaedic dog beds with memory foam support joints and prevent pressure sores in dogs that are resting more than usual.

Exercise Between Sessions

  • Follow the plan: Your therapist will give you specific exercises — do exactly what they prescribe. More is not better in rehab; overdoing it causes setbacks.
  • Short, controlled walks: On-leash walks on flat ground, starting at 5 minutes and gradually increasing. Avoid hills, uneven terrain, and off-leash areas until your vet gives the all-clear.
  • Passive range of motion: Gently moving your pet\'s affected joint through its full range of motion (as demonstrated by your therapist) prevents stiffness and maintains flexibility.
  • Rest is exercise: In the early stages of recovery, rest IS the treatment. Do not feel guilty for keeping your pet quiet. You are helping them heal.

Pain Monitoring

Pets are masters at hiding pain. Watch for these subtle signs that your pet may be uncomfortable:

  • Reluctance to put weight on the affected limb
  • Slow to rise after resting
  • Decreased appetite
  • Licking or chewing at the surgical site or a specific joint
  • Restlessness, panting, or inability to settle
  • Withdrawal or hiding behaviour
  • Grumbling or growling when touched in a specific area

If you notice any of these signs, contact your vet. Pain management is a critical part of rehabilitation — your pet cannot exercise effectively if they are in pain.

Hydrotherapy for Cats

While hydrotherapy is far more common for dogs, cats can also benefit — particularly for post-surgical rehabilitation and arthritis management. Yes, most cats dislike water, but the approach is different:

  • Underwater treadmill, not swimming: Cats walk on the treadmill with water supporting their weight. They do not need to swim. Most cats tolerate this surprisingly well once they realise they are safe and stable.
  • Gradual introduction: The first session may involve simply standing in the empty treadmill chamber with treats and praise. Water is introduced slowly over subsequent sessions.
  • Shorter sessions: 5–10 minutes of actual exercise is typical for a cat session, compared to 15–30 for dogs.
  • Warming up matters more: Cold muscles are more injury-prone. Cats benefit from warm-up massage before getting into the water.
Cat Hydrotherapy in NZ: Fewer centres offer feline hydrotherapy than canine, and you may need to travel to a larger city. Ask your vet for a referral to a practice that works with cats specifically. The New Zealand Veterinary Nursing Association maintains a list of rehabilitation providers.

Signs Hydrotherapy Is Working

How do you know if your pet\'s rehabilitation programme is effective? Look for these positive signs over the course of treatment:

  • Improved weight-bearing on the affected limb (measured by your therapist)
  • Increased willingness to walk, play, and engage
  • Better range of motion in the affected joint
  • Reduced reliance on pain medication (as guided by your vet)
  • Improved muscle mass on the affected side
  • More even gait pattern — less limping
  • Quicker recovery after exercise (less stiffness the following day)

Most rehabilitation programmes show noticeable improvement within 4–6 sessions. If you see no improvement after 8–10 sessions, discuss the programme with your therapist and vet — the approach may need adjustment.

Working with Your Vet and Therapist

Successful rehabilitation is a team effort. Your vet diagnoses the condition and performs surgery if needed. Your hydrotherapist designs and delivers the exercise programme. Your pet sitter ensures consistency at home. And you are the coordinator who makes sure everyone is communicating.

  • Get a written referral: Most hydrotherapy centres require a vet referral. This ensures the therapist knows your pet\'s full medical history and current status.
  • Share progress reports: Ask your therapist to send progress notes to your vet after every few sessions. This keeps your vet in the loop and allows them to adjust pain medication or exercise restrictions as recovery progresses.
  • Be honest about compliance: If you have not done the home exercises, say so. Your therapist cannot adjust the programme accurately without honest feedback.
  • Attend sessions when you can: Watching your pet\'s sessions gives you a better understanding of their progress and the exercises involved. It also helps your pet feel more confident.
