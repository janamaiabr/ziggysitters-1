Apartment Living with Pets in New Zealand: The Complete Guide

Published 7 March 2026 • 9 min read • ZiggySitters NZ

Urban New Zealand is changing fast. As apartment living grows across Auckland, Wellington, Christchurch, and Tauranga, more and more Kiwis are navigating what it means to share a smaller space with a dog, cat, or other companion animal. The good news: apartment life and pet ownership are absolutely compatible — but they require more intentional planning than a house with a backyard.

This guide covers everything from choosing the right pet for apartment living, to managing your lease, to building a care routine that keeps both you and your pet happy. We also cover what happens when you travel: finding a pet sitter who understands apartment-dwelling animals is its own skill.

Is Apartment Life Fair on Pets?

This is the question most urban pet owners ask themselves. The honest answer is: it depends entirely on the pet and the owner, not the square footage.

A Greyhound in a small Auckland apartment with two hour-long walks per day will be far happier and healthier than a Border Collie in a large suburban home who gets a 10-minute walk and is left alone for 10 hours. Research consistently shows that exercise quantity, mental stimulation, and quality time with their owner matter far more to dogs than the size of the space they sleep in.

Cats, in many ways, are naturally suited to apartment life. They are territorial animals who map and master their environment, and a well-enriched apartment — with vertical space, window perches, and regular play — meets a cat's core needs without any outdoor access.

The challenge is not apartment size. The challenge is owner commitment.

New Zealand Tenancy Law and Pets

Before you bring a pet into a rental apartment, you need to understand your legal position.

Under the Residential Tenancies Act 1986 (amended 2021), landlords can include no-pets clauses in tenancy agreements. However, the law now requires landlords to have reasonable grounds to decline a pet request made by a tenant. Blanket pet bans are increasingly difficult for landlords to enforce, particularly for small or low-risk pets.

Practical Steps

  • Ask before you sign: Raise the pet question at the application stage, not after you move in. Surprising a landlord with a dog after signing is a lease violation.
  • Get it in writing: Any approval should be a written addendum to your tenancy agreement, not a verbal conversation.
  • Pet bond: Landlords in NZ cannot charge a separate pet bond on top of the standard rental bond (which is capped at four weeks' rent). Be wary of any landlord trying to impose one.
  • Body Corporate rules: In apartment buildings managed by a Body Corporate, pet rules may be set at the Body Corporate level rather than by individual landlords. Check the Body Corporate rules before assuming your landlord's approval is sufficient.

Important: Always check Body Corporate rules separately from your tenancy agreement. A landlord may allow pets but the building's Body Corporate rules may prohibit dogs or set weight limits. Both sets of rules apply to you.

Best Dog Breeds for NZ Apartments

Not every dog thrives in a smaller space. High-energy working breeds like Border Collies, Australian Cattle Dogs, and Siberian Huskies need significant physical and mental work each day — work that is genuinely difficult to provide from an apartment without extraordinary commitment.

The following breeds tend to adapt well to apartment life:

Cavalier King Charles Spaniel

Calm, affectionate, minimal exercise needs. Loves cuddles and indoor time. Popular in NZ cities.

Greyhound / Italian Greyhound

Surprising apartment dogs — sprinters not marathoners. Sleep most of the day, need a daily run.

French Bulldog

Very popular in Auckland. Low exercise, affectionate, quiet. Watch for breathing issues in heat.

Shih Tzu

Bred as a companion dog for indoor living. Minimal shedding, easy going, suits apartment life well.

Maltese

Tiny, low-shedding, and gentle. Suits small spaces and older apartment residents.

Basset Hound

Laid-back and quiet indoors. Needs daily walks but not intense exercise. Surprisingly calm apartment dog.

Medium and larger dogs are not automatically ruled out — many Labradors, Boxers, and even Standard Poodles thrive in apartments with committed owners. The key question is always: can you realistically meet this dog's exercise and stimulation needs from your building?

Best Cat Breeds for Apartment Life

Most cats do perfectly well in apartments. Some breeds are even better suited to indoor-only living:

  • Ragdoll: Famous for going limp when picked up, Ragdolls are gentle, quiet, and tend to stay close to their humans. Excellent indoor cats.
  • British Shorthair: Independent and calm, this breed does not demand constant attention and adapts easily to apartment routines.
  • Persian: Laid-back and not particularly active — Persians are content with a comfortable indoor space and regular grooming.
  • Devon Rex / Cornish Rex: Playful and dog-like in personality, these breeds love interactive play and indoor enrichment activities.
  • Burmese: Affectionate and sociable, Burmese cats bond strongly with their owners and do well in apartments where they get regular attention.

Enrichment tip: For indoor cats, vertical space matters as much as floor space. Install a tall cat tree, wall-mounted shelves, or a window perch near a bird feeder. The visual stimulation of watching birds can keep a cat entertained for hours — and it costs almost nothing.

Building a Daily Routine for an Apartment Pet

Routine is the foundation of a well-adjusted apartment pet. Without a yard to burn off energy freely, you become your pet's primary source of exercise, stimulation, and routine — which means your schedule needs to be deliberate.

For Dogs

  • Morning walk: At least 30–45 minutes, ideally before you leave for work. This is not optional.
  • Midday visit: If you work full time, a midday dog walker or lunchtime drop-in visit makes a significant difference to your dog's wellbeing. Dogs should not be left alone for more than 4–5 hours without a break.
  • Evening walk or play: Another 30+ minutes of activity, plus training or enrichment exercises indoors.
  • Weekend adventures: Longer hikes, beach visits, and dog parks compensate for weekday constraints and provide important socialisation.

For Cats

  • Morning play session: 10–15 minutes with a wand toy or interactive puzzle before you leave. Simulates the dawn hunting period cats are wired for.
  • Environmental enrichment: Rotate toys weekly so your cat doesn't become bored with the same objects. Food puzzles and treat-dispensing balls keep them problem-solving.
  • Evening play: Another short session before bed. This helps cats settle overnight rather than becoming active at 3am.

Managing Noise and Neighbours

One of the real challenges of apartment pets — especially dogs — is noise. A dog who barks during the day while you're at work is a neighbourly crisis waiting to happen.

Prevention Strategies

  • Ensure your dog is adequately exercised before you leave for work — a tired dog is a quiet dog.
  • Use white noise machines or calming music (there are specific playlists designed for dogs) to mask triggering sounds like corridor footsteps or lift dings.
  • Consider a dog trainer who specialises in separation anxiety if the barking is stress-based rather than boredom-based — these require different approaches.
  • Ask a neighbour honestly if they hear your dog during the day. It is better to know than to assume.

Pet Sitting for Apartment Dwellers: What to Know

When you travel, your apartment pet has specific needs that differ from pets in houses with yards. Here is what to look for in a pet sitter when you live in an apartment:

In-Home Sitters Are Usually Best

For apartment cats especially, keeping the pet in their own familiar environment is almost always the right call. An in-home sitter (or a sitter who does multiple daily drop-ins) maintains your cat's routine with minimal disruption.

For dogs in apartments, an in-home stay is also excellent — your sitter stays in your apartment overnight, maintains the walk schedule, and your dog does not have the added stress of navigating a new environment.

Choose a Sitter Who Understands Building Access

Apartments have security systems, intercoms, and sometimes complex access arrangements. Your sitter needs a key, fob, or code. Brief them thoroughly on how to enter and exit, where to park if they drive, and any building rules about pets in lifts or common areas.

Midday Dog Walking During Your Trip

If you book a sitter to do drop-in visits rather than overnight stays, ensure the schedule includes a midday walk — not just morning and evening. An apartment dog alone from 8am to 6pm with no midday visit will be uncomfortable and potentially destructive.

Before you travel: Leave your sitter a written guide that includes: how to operate the intercom, parking instructions, building rules for pets, your vet's address and phone number, and a list of trusted neighbours they can contact if something unexpected happens.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can landlords ban pets in NZ apartments?

Under New Zealand tenancy law, landlords can include no-pets clauses in residential tenancy agreements. However, the Residential Tenancies Act was amended in 2021 to make it harder to refuse pets without reasonable grounds. Always get written consent before bringing a pet into a rental property.

What are the best dog breeds for NZ apartments?

Great apartment dog breeds in NZ include the Cavalier King Charles Spaniel, French Bulldog, Pug, Maltese, Shih Tzu, Greyhound, and Basset Hound. These breeds tend to be calm indoors and do not require extreme amounts of exercise. The key is consistent daily walks regardless of apartment size.

How do I find a pet sitter for my apartment in NZ?

Look for sitters who have experience with apartment pets and are comfortable doing drop-in visits or in-home stays in high-rise buildings. ZiggySitters lists verified pet sitters across New Zealand including in apartment-heavy cities like Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch.

Do I need a midday dog walker if I work full time?

For most dogs, yes — especially in apartments where they cannot self-regulate by spending time in a garden. Dogs should not be left alone for more than 4–5 hours without a break, exercise, and bathroom opportunity. A midday dog walker is one of the most impactful investments you can make in your apartment dog's wellbeing.

Apartment living with a pet in New Zealand is not just possible — for many people, it is genuinely great. The discipline required to provide structured exercise and enrichment often results in a closer, more attuned relationship with your animal than you might ever develop with a yard to defer to. Do the work, choose your pet thoughtfully, and find the right support network (vet, walker, sitter). Your pet will thrive.
