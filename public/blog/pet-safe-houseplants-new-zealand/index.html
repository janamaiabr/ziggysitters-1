Pet-Safe Houseplants in New Zealand: The Complete Guide

New Zealanders love their indoor plants. From the ubiquitous monstera in every Auckland apartment to the trailing pothos in Wellington living rooms, houseplants have become as essential as a good coffee machine. But if you share your home with a cat or dog, that lush indoor jungle might be hiding some dangerous secrets. Many popular houseplants sold in NZ garden centres are toxic to pets — and some can cause serious harm with just a single bite.

This guide covers everything you need to know about pet-safe houseplants in New Zealand, which common plants to avoid, and how to create an indoor garden that keeps your furry family members safe.

Why Plant Safety Matters for NZ Pet Owners

New Zealand\'s warm, humid climate makes indoor plant growing easy, which means many Kiwi homes are filled with greenery. But cats are notorious nibblers — they chew on leaves out of boredom, curiosity, or even to help with digestion. Dogs are less discriminating and may eat entire plants, soil and all. The SPCA and veterinary clinics across New Zealand treat hundreds of plant poisoning cases every year, and some are fatal.

The tricky part? Many toxic plants don\'t look dangerous. A peaceful lily sitting on your windowsill won\'t give you any warning that it could cause kidney failure in your cat within 48 hours of ingestion. Knowing which plants are safe before you bring them home is the only reliable way to protect your pets.

The Danger List: Common Toxic Houseplants in NZ

These plants are regularly sold in New Zealand garden centres and are toxic to cats, dogs, or both. If you already own any of these and have pets, consider rehoming them to a pet-free household or placing them in a room your animals cannot access.

Extremely Dangerous — Can Be Fatal

  • Lilies (all varieties) — Every part of the lily is highly toxic to cats. Even licking pollen off their fur or drinking water from a lily vase can cause acute kidney failure. This includes peace lilies, Easter lilies, tiger lilies, and stargazer lilies. There is no safe amount of lily exposure for a cat.
  • Oleander — All parts are highly toxic to both cats and dogs. Ingestion affects the heart and can be fatal.
  • Cyclamen — The tubers are particularly toxic and can cause seizures, heart abnormalities, and death.
  • Sago palm — Seeds and leaves contain cycasin, which causes liver failure. Just one or two seeds can be lethal to a small dog.

Moderately Toxic — Cause Illness and Discomfort

  • Monstera deliciosa (Swiss cheese plant) — Contains insoluble calcium oxalates that cause oral irritation, swelling, drooling, and difficulty swallowing.
  • Philodendron — Same mechanism as monstera. Painful but rarely fatal.
  • Pothos / Devil\'s ivy — One of the most common NZ houseplants and one of the most commonly ingested by pets. Causes oral irritation, vomiting, and difficulty swallowing.
  • Aloe vera — While great for human skin, ingestion causes vomiting, diarrhoea, and lethargy in cats and dogs.
  • Dumb cane (Dieffenbachia) — Intense oral burning, swelling that can block airways in severe cases.
  • ZZ plant (Zamioculcas zamiifolia) — Contains calcium oxalates. Causes similar symptoms to philodendron and monstera.
  • Snake plant (Sansevieria) — Mildly toxic. Causes nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea.
  • Jade plant (Crassula ovata) — Causes vomiting, depression, and incoordination in cats and dogs.
  • English ivy — Leaves and berries cause gastrointestinal distress, breathing difficulty, and in severe cases, coma.
  • Dracaena species — Vomiting, drooling, and dilated pupils in cats. Can be more severe in dogs.

The Safe List: Pet-Friendly Houseplants for NZ Homes

Good news — you don\'t have to choose between a beautiful home and a safe pet. These plants are non-toxic to cats and dogs and thrive in New Zealand\'s indoor conditions.

Statement Plants

  • Parlour palm (Chamaedorea elegans) — Lush, feathery, and completely safe. Grows well in medium light. Perfect for Auckland and Wellington apartments.
  • Boston fern (Nephrolepis exaltata) — Loves NZ\'s humidity. Hang it high and let it trail. Non-toxic and excellent at purifying air.
  • Areca palm (Dypsis lutescens) — A larger statement palm that is completely pet-safe. Needs bright, indirect light.
  • Prayer plant (Maranta leuconeura) — Stunning leaf patterns and completely non-toxic. Loves humidity, making it perfect for bathrooms.
  • Calathea species — Beautiful patterned leaves, non-toxic, and loves the same humidity that makes NZ homes great for growing.

Trailing and Hanging Plants

  • Spider plant (Chlorophytum comosum) — The ultimate easy, pet-safe plant. Produces baby plants that you can propagate. Cats love playing with the dangling babies, and it\'s completely harmless.
  • String of pearls (Senecio rowleyanus) — Wait — this one is actually toxic. Skip it and choose string of hearts instead.
  • String of hearts (Ceropegia woodii) — Beautiful trailing vine, completely non-toxic. Safe alternative to string of pearls.
  • Peperomia species — Compact, varied, and entirely pet-safe. Great for desks and shelves.
  • Swedish ivy (Plectranthus verticillatus) — Fast-growing trailer, non-toxic, and thrives in NZ conditions.

Low-Maintenance Options

  • Peperomia obtusifolia — Thick, glossy leaves, tolerates low light, completely safe.
  • Friendship plant (Pilea involucrata) — Textured leaves, non-toxic, easy to propagate and share.
  • UFO plant (Pilea peperomioides) — Also called the Chinese money plant. Trendy, easy to grow, and pet-safe.
  • Haworthia species — Small spiky succulents that look like aloe but are completely non-toxic. The perfect safe alternative.
  • Echeveria species — Most echeverias are safe for pets and easy to find in NZ garden centres.
  • Burro\'s tail (Sedum morganianum) — Trailing succulent, non-toxic, and very low maintenance.

Herbs You Can Grow Safely

  • Cat grass (wheatgrass or oat grass) — Not just safe but beneficial. Gives cats a healthy outlet for their urge to chew greens.
  • Basil, rosemary, and thyme — All pet-safe and useful in the kitchen.
  • Mint — Safe for dogs and cats in small amounts. Catnip is a type of mint and is perfectly safe.
  • Parsley — Non-toxic and easy to grow on a sunny windowsill.

Practical Tips for a Pet-Safe Indoor Garden

Placement Strategy

Even safe plants can cause problems if your pet digs in the soil or tips pots over. Place heavier pots on the floor where they\'re stable, and use hanging planters for trailing varieties. Keep all toxic plants in rooms that are completely off-limits to pets — a closed door is the only reliable barrier for determined cats.

Deterrent Methods

If your cat won\'t stop nibbling even safe plants, try placing citrus peels on the soil surface (cats dislike the smell), using a light spray of water to create a negative association, or providing a dedicated pot of cat grass so they have an approved chewing target.

Soil and Fertiliser Safety

Some potting mixes contain fertiliser granules that can be harmful if ingested. Choose organic, pet-safe potting mixes when possible, and avoid leaving slow-release fertiliser spikes where curious pets can dig them up. Cocoa mulch — sometimes used as a top dressing — is toxic to dogs and should never be used in a pet-owning household.

What to Do If Your Pet Eats a Plant

If you suspect your pet has eaten a toxic plant, don\'t wait for symptoms to appear. Contact your vet immediately or call the NZ Veterinary Emergency Service. For lily ingestion in cats, every hour counts — kidney damage can begin within hours and becomes irreversible quickly. Bring a sample or photo of the plant to help the vet identify the toxin and treat your pet faster.

Building Your Pet-Safe Plant Collection

Start with the easy wins: a spider plant for your bathroom, a parlour palm for your living room, and a pot of cat grass by your cat\'s favourite window. Add calatheas and prayer plants for colour, and pepper some peperomias on your shelves. Before buying any new plant, check it against the toxic plant lists above or look it up on the SPCA\'s resources. Most NZ garden centres don\'t label plants with pet safety information, so the responsibility falls on you as a pet owner.

A pet-safe home doesn\'t mean a plant-free home. With the right choices, you can have a lush, beautiful indoor garden that your pets can safely explore — even if they do occasionally take a nibble.

Quick Reference: Swap These Toxic Plants for Safe Alternatives

  • Monstera → Parlour palm or areca palm
  • Pothos → Spider plant or Swedish ivy
  • Aloe vera → Haworthia
  • ZZ plant → Peperomia or friendship plant
  • Snake plant → Calathea or prayer plant
  • Jade plant → Echeveria or burro\'s tail
  • String of pearls → String of hearts
  • Any lily → Orchid (also safe!) or artificial lilies
