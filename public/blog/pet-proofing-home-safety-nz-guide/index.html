Pet-Proofing Your Home in NZ: Complete Safety Guide & Checklist

Your home should be the safest place in your pet\'s world. But ordinary New Zealand homes often contain hazards that are easy for humans to miss: chocolate in a school bag, Pestoff rat bait in the garage, a bunch of lilies on the dining table, karaka berries dropped across a garden path, or a balcony window left open on a warm Auckland afternoon.

This pet proofing home NZ guide gives you a practical, room-by-room pet safety home checklist for dogs, cats, puppies, kittens, rabbits, and curious small pets. Use it before bringing home a new animal, before leaving pets with a sitter, and whenever your household routine changes.

Room-by-Room Pet-Proofing Checklist

Kitchen

The kitchen is the biggest source of household hazards pets New Zealand families encounter. Keep chocolate, coffee, grapes, raisins, onions, garlic, macadamia nuts, avocado, alcohol, and xylitol products in sealed cupboards. Use a bin with a secure lid, especially if it contains bones, food wrappers, cooked corn cobs, skewers, or fatty leftovers. If your pet counter-surfs, keep benches clear and cool stovetops covered until completely safe.

Bathroom

Medicines are one of the most serious home hazards. Paracetamol, ibuprofen, antidepressants, ADHD medication, cold and flu tablets, and human supplements can be dangerous or fatal. Store them in a high cupboard, not beside the sink or in a handbag on the floor. Keep toilet lids closed if you use cleaning blocks, and move razors, dental floss, cotton buds, and hair ties out of reach.

Laundry

Laundry pods, stain removers, bleach, disinfectants, and essential oils can irritate mouths, skin, and stomachs. Keep washing powder and liquids in closed containers. Check the washing machine and dryer before every use, especially in cat households. Cats love warm dark spaces, and a half-open dryer can look like a perfect nap spot.

Lounge and Bedrooms

Living areas hide small, swallowable objects: batteries from remotes, Lego, coins, earrings, needles, thread, wool, ribbons, and Christmas decorations. String is especially dangerous for cats because it can anchor around the tongue and damage the intestines. Keep bags, school backpacks, and gym bags off the floor; they often contain food, medications, or chewing gum. Place candles, diffusers, wax melts, and incense where pets cannot knock them over.

Garage and Shed

Garages often contain the most dangerous chemicals: antifreeze, fuel, paint, solvents, fertilisers, pool chemicals, rat bait, slug pellets, and sharp tools. Antifreeze is particularly concerning because ethylene glycol can taste sweet and cause kidney failure. In NZ, rat baits such as Pestoff and other anticoagulant products are common around homes, farms, and lifestyle blocks. They must be locked away, not just pushed to the back of a shelf.

Garden and Outdoor Areas

Walk your fence line and look for gaps under gates, compost access, sharp wire, stagnant water, and toxic plants. Slug pellets, weed killers, blood and bone fertiliser, cocoa mulch, and insecticides should be stored securely and used only according to label instructions.

Toxic Foods Dogs and Cats in NZ Commonly Encounter

Many poisoning cases start with everyday food. Keep these away from pets:

  • Chocolate and cocoa: dark chocolate, baking chocolate, and cocoa powder are highest risk.
  • Xylitol: found in some NZ sugar-free gum, mints, dental products, and diet foods; extremely dangerous for dogs.
  • Grapes, raisins, and currants: can cause kidney failure in dogs, even in small amounts.
  • Onions, garlic, chives, and leeks: toxic raw, cooked, powdered, or in leftovers.
  • Avocado: can cause stomach upset; the stone is also a choking or obstruction risk.
  • Caffeine: coffee grounds, tea bags, energy drinks, and caffeine tablets are unsafe.
  • Macadamia nuts: can cause weakness, vomiting, tremors, and pain in dogs.

If a pet eats a known toxin, do not wait for symptoms. Call your vet or an animal poison service immediately with the product name, amount eaten, time eaten, and your pet\'s weight.

NZ-Specific Plant Hazards

New Zealand gardens are beautiful, but some plants are unsafe for pets. Karaka berries are one of the big local risks: the orange berries and kernels can be deadly to dogs and are often found in coastal and suburban areas. Poroporo, a native shrub with purple flowers and yellow-orange berries, can also be toxic if eaten.

Other common hazards include foxglove, oleander, lilies, rhododendron, azalea, arum lily, and some bulb plants. Lilies deserve special mention: true lilies can cause acute kidney failure in cats, and exposure to pollen or vase water can be enough to matter.

For a deeper list of local plant risks, read our full NZ toxic plants guide for pets. If you are renting or buying a new home, checking the garden should be part of keeping pets safe at home NZ-wide.

Household Chemicals and Pest Control

Cleaning products, disinfectants, drain cleaners, oven cleaners, toilet cleaners, antifreeze, paint, rat bait, and slug pellets should be treated like medication: locked away and never accessible. In many NZ homes, pest control products are seasonal, appearing during wet weather, mouse activity, or before holidays. That makes them easy to forget.

If you use Pestoff, Talon, Storm, Racumin, slug bait, or any professional pest control product, keep the original packaging so a vet can identify the active ingredient. Different poisons need different treatment, and guessing wastes critical time.

Small Objects and Choking Hazards

Puppies and kittens investigate the world with their mouths. So do bored adult pets. Common choking or obstruction hazards include children\'s toys, socks, underwear, hair ties, corn cobs, cooked bones, bottle caps, coins, batteries, ear plugs, craft supplies, fishing hooks, string, wool, and ribbon.

Button batteries are an emergency because they can burn tissue. Fishing gear is another NZ-specific concern for coastal families: hooks, bait, and line should be stored in closed tackle boxes, never left on a garage floor or beach bag.

Electrical Cords and Cables

Chewed cords can cause burns, electric shock, or house fires. Hide cables behind furniture, use cord covers, unplug chargers when not in use, and block access to power boards. Rabbits, puppies, and kittens are particularly likely to chew thin cables. If your pet has chewed a live cord, call a vet even if they seem fine; mouth burns and breathing problems can develop later.

Windows, Balconies, and Escape Routes

Many New Zealand homes have ranch sliders, deck doors, louvred windows, cat doors, and indoor-outdoor flow. Great for humans, risky for pets. Check that fly screens are secure, balcony gaps are too small for pets to squeeze through, and upstairs windows cannot be pushed open by a cat.

High-rise cat safety matters in apartments around Auckland, Wellington, Christchurch, and central city developments. Cats do fall from balconies and windows; the myth that they always land safely is dangerous. Use secure screens, keep balcony doors closed unless supervised, and never rely on a loose chair or pot plant as a barrier.

For dogs, check gates after storms, gardening, or tradesperson visits. A gate that latched yesterday may not latch today.

Emergency Preparedness in New Zealand

Put emergency contacts somewhere visible and share them with every pet sitter. Useful NZ contacts include:

  • Your regular vet: save the clinic number and after-hours instructions.
  • NZ Animal Poisons Helpline: 0800 TOX PET / 0800 869 738.
  • Pet Poison Helpline: international service, available online and by phone; fees may apply.
  • Auckland: Animal Emergency Centre, 09 849 2121.
  • Wellington: Wellington After Hours Veterinary Clinic, 04 473 7545.
  • Christchurch: After Hours Veterinary Clinic, 03 366 1052.

Emergency numbers can change, so confirm your closest 24-hour clinic before you need it. Keep your pet\'s microchip number, medication list, insurance details, and recent photo in one easy-to-find place.

Preparing for a Pet Sitter

Pet-proof before your sitter arrives, not five minutes before you leave for the airport. A sitter can follow your instructions beautifully, but they cannot know every hidden risk in your home. Put away food, medicines, cleaning products, plant cuttings, pest baits, cables, and small objects. Show the sitter which rooms are off-limits, where the pet sleeps, how doors and gates lock, and what behaviour is normal for your pet.

Leave written notes covering feeding, walks, medication, hiding places, emergency contacts, and vet approval. If your dog is a bin-raider, your cat bolts through doors, or your rabbit chews cords, say so clearly. Good information prevents bad surprises.
