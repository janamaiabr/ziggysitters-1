Why Background Checks Matter for Pet Sitting

A pet sitter does not just feed your cat or walk your dog. They enter your home, access your private spaces, handle your belongings, and make daily decisions about the health and safety of a living creature you love. The trust involved is substantial, and the consequences of getting it wrong range from minor property issues to genuine animal welfare concerns.

New Zealand has no mandatory licensing or registration scheme for pet sitters. Anyone can list themselves as a pet sitter on social media or classified sites without any verification whatsoever. This is not inherently dangerous; many honest, capable people offer pet sitting services informally. But it does mean the responsibility for vetting falls entirely on you, the pet owner, and that makes a systematic approach to background checking essential rather than optional.

The stakes are real. A pet sitter who fails to notice early signs of illness can allow a treatable condition to become an emergency. A sitter who does not follow feeding instructions can trigger gastrointestinal crises in animals with dietary sensitivities. A sitter who leaves doors unlocked or windows open can put your pet at risk of escape or injury. And on the property side, a sitter who does not respect your home can create costs and stress that linger well after your holiday ends.

Identity Verification: The First Gate

The most fundamental check is confirming that your sitter is who they claim to be. In New Zealand, this starts with a few straightforward steps.

Ask for a New Zealand driver licence or passport. These are the primary forms of photo identification in NZ, and they carry significant weight because obtaining them requires proof of identity through the issuing authority. A sitter who is reluctant to show ID is a red flag, not because they are necessarily dishonest, but because transparency about identity is the bare minimum for someone you are inviting into your home.

Cross-reference the name on their ID with their bank account details. This matters for payment integrity and also confirms the identity chain. If the name on the ID, the name on the profile, and the name on the bank account all match, you have a strong identity signal. If they do not match, ask why before proceeding.

If you are booking through a platform, check whether the platform performs identity verification. ZiggySitters, for example, requires sitters to complete identity checks before they can accept bookings. Platforms that skip this step are leaving a gap that you must fill yourself.

Police Checks: What They Cover and What They Miss

A New Zealand police vet, sometimes called a criminal history check, reveals convictions recorded against an individual. You can request one through the Ministry of Justice, which provides a free criminal record check, or through the New Zealand Police, which offers a more detailed vetting service commonly used for roles involving vulnerable people.

For pet sitters, a police vet is a useful signal but not a complete safety guarantee. It tells you whether someone has been convicted of offences relevant to trust and safety, including theft, fraud, and violence. It does not tell you whether someone is kind to animals, reliable in an emergency, or respectful of other people is homes. A clean police check is a good starting point, not a conclusion.

When hiring independently, ask the sitter directly whether they are willing to provide a police check. Most reputable sitters will already have one, or will obtain one promptly upon request. If a sitter resists this request, consider it a significant warning sign. The cost of a police check in New Zealand is modest, typically under $50, and the process takes only a few days. Anyone serious about pet sitting as a profession should view this as a routine professional requirement rather than an imposition.

References: Past Clients Are Your Best Evidence

References from previous pet owners carry more practical weight than any other single verification method. A person can pass a police check and still be careless with animals. A person can have a spotless ID and still lack judgement in an emergency. But a pattern of positive reviews from real pet owners who have entrusted their animals and their keys to this person is difficult to fake at scale.

When evaluating references, look for specificity. Generic praise like, quote, great sitter, unquote, is less informative than detailed accounts: quote, Sarah noticed our elderly cat was not eating and took her to the vet immediately, unquote, or, quote, Tom sent us photos every day and even watered our vegetable garden, unquote. The more specific the review, the more likely it reflects an actual experience rather than a friendly favour.

Ask for references that include contact information you can verify. A phone number or email address that you can reach out to directly provides much stronger assurance than a written testimonial that cannot be authenticated. Most genuine pet sitters with experience will have multiple clients willing to vouch for them.

On platforms like ZiggySitters, reviews are tied to completed bookings and cannot be fabricated at volume. This makes platform reviews significantly more reliable than testimonials a sitter provides directly, which could theoretically be written by friends or family members.

Insurance: Professional Sitters Carry It

Professional pet sitters in New Zealand should carry public liability insurance at minimum. This protects both parties in the event that something goes wrong during the sit. If your pet causes damage to a third party is property while in the sitter is care, or if the sitter themselves is injured while walking your dog on a public path, liability insurance ensures there is a financial backstop.

Ask specifically about the scope of their insurance. Does it cover veterinary expenses if your pet is injured while in their care? Does it extend to property damage within your home? Does it cover key loss or lock replacement if keys are lost? The more comprehensive the cover, the more seriously the sitter takes their professional obligations.

Pet sitter insurance in New Zealand is available through several specialist providers and through general business insurance policies. Costs are reasonable, typically ranging from $300 to $800 per year depending on coverage levels, and any sitter who treats pet sitting as a profession rather than a casual side gig should view this as a non-negotiable business expense.

Red Flags: When to Walk Away

Certain behaviours should cause you to pause or step back entirely from a booking. None of these individually guarantees a problem, but they are warning signs worth taking seriously.

  • Reluctance to provide identification. A trustworthy sitter understands why you need to verify who they are and will not treat this request as an inconvenience.
  • No verifiable reviews. If a sitter cannot point to a single completed booking with a real pet owner, you are taking a risk that no amount of charm can offset.
  • Unwillingness to meet first. A meet-and-greet is standard practice. A sitter who pushes to skip it is either disorganised or avoiding scrutiny.
  • Vague or inconsistent answers about experience. If a sitter claims five years of experience but cannot name a single breed they have worked with, or claims experience with medication but cannot describe how they would administer a typical pill, the experience claim is hollow.
  • Pressure to bypass platform protections. If a sitter on a platform suggests taking the booking privately to avoid fees, they are also asking you to give up the dispute resolution, insurance, and review mechanisms the platform provides. This is not just about saving money; it is about removing accountability.
  • Poor communication before the sit. If a sitter is slow to respond, vague about logistics, or unclear about their availability during the booking period, their communication during the sit is unlikely to improve.

The Meet-and-Greet: Your Most Important Tool

No amount of online vetting replaces an in-person meeting. The meet-and-greet is where you assess the things that no background check, reference, or insurance policy can reveal: how your sitter carries themselves, how they interact with your pet, and whether your instincts tell you this is someone you can trust in your home.

Schedule the meet-and-greet at least a few days before your booking begins. This gives you time to find an alternative if something feels wrong. During the visit, watch how your sitter approaches your pet. Do they move calmly? Do they read your pet is body language? Do they ask thoughtful questions about routine, diet, medical needs, and preferences? A sitter who focuses only on logistics like key handover and payment, without asking about your pet is personality and needs, may not be prioritising the animal side of the job.

Use this meeting to walk through your home and point out anything the sitter needs to know: where the water main is, which doors must stay closed, where the emergency vet contact is posted, and where you keep the pet first aid supplies. A good sitter will take notes or photos. A disinterested sitter will rush through.

What a Pet Sitting Agreement Should Cover

A written agreement between you and your sitter is not about distrust; it is about clarity. When expectations are documented, both parties know exactly what is required, and there is less room for misunderstandings that could affect your pet is care.

Your agreement should include the following elements:

  • Pet care instructions: Feeding schedule, portion sizes, dietary restrictions, medication details, exercise requirements, and any behavioural quirks the sitter should know about.
  • Emergency contacts: Your phone number, your vet is phone number, the nearest after-hours emergency vet, and a local contact who can make decisions if you are unreachable.
  • Veterinary authorisation: A signed letter authorising your sitter to seek veterinary treatment for your pet up to a specified dollar amount, so they are not paralysed by indecision if something goes wrong at 2 AM.
  • Home access details: Key location, alarm codes, which doors to lock, and any home security considerations.
  • Communication expectations: How often you want updates, preferred method (text, photo, video call), and what to report immediately versus what can wait for the daily check-in.
  • House rules: Whether the sitter may have guests, which rooms are off-limits, and any other boundaries relevant to your home.

Platform Trust Features Versus Independent Hiring

Hiring through a platform like ZiggySitters and hiring independently both have merits, but they offer different trust mechanisms.

Platforms provide structured verification, including identity checks, review systems tied to actual bookings, insurance or guarantee funds that cover disputes, and a channel for communication that creates a record of your interactions. The trade-off is that you are working within the platform is framework, which may limit flexibility on pricing or scheduling.

Independent hiring gives you direct negotiation with the sitter and potentially lower costs, but the trust infrastructure is entirely your responsibility. You must conduct the identity checks, request and verify references, confirm insurance, draft or review the care agreement, and bear the full risk if something goes wrong. For many pet owners, the convenience and safety net of a platform is worth the modest service fee.

Whichever route you choose, the minimum standards should not change. Your sitter should have verifiable identity, genuine references, appropriate insurance, and a willingness to meet your pet before the booking begins.

Checking Your Sitter During the Sit

Vetting does not end once the booking starts. The first 24 hours of a sit are the most critical period for confirming that your sitter is following instructions and that your pet is settling well. Expect at least one update during this window, preferably including a photo of your pet in their normal environment.

If you have arranged a pet camera in your home, you can do a quick visual check without being intrusive. Many NZ pet owners now use affordable indoor cameras to monitor their pets and their sitter simultaneously. This is not about surveillance; it is about confirming that your pet is being fed, walked, and attended to as agreed.

If your sitter goes quiet for an extended period, especially during the first day, reach out directly. A brief message asking how things are going is entirely reasonable and should elicit a prompt response. If your sitter does not respond within a few hours during waking time, consider contacting your emergency backup or a neighbour to check on your pet.

Leaving a Review: Building Trust for the Next Pet Owner

After every sit, leave an honest review. This is not just a courtesy to your sitter; it is a contribution to the trust infrastructure that protects the next pet owner who is deciding whether to book. Be specific about what went well and what could improve. Mention whether the sitter communicated well, followed instructions, handled any issues that arose, and how your pet seemed when you returned.

Honest reviews, both positive and constructively critical, are the backbone of any trust-based marketplace. They allow good sitters to build reputations that attract more bookings, and they flag problems before they become patterns.
