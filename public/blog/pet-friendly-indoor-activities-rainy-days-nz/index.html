Rainy Day Pet Activities: Indoor Fun for NZ Dogs and Cats

Published 11 July 2026 · Pet Activities & Enrichment

New Zealand weather does not ask permission. Auckland gets 1,200mm of rain a year. Wellington averages 170 rainy days. Invercargill can feel like it rains sideways. When the forecast says stay inside, your dog is still staring at you with that walk-expectation face, and your cat has decided 3am is the perfect time to practice parkour off the furniture. This guide has 30+ indoor activities, games, and enrichment ideas to keep your pets happy, tired, and not plotting revenge against your curtains.

Why Indoor Enrichment Matters

A dog that does not get enough mental stimulation does not just get bored. They get destructive. Chewing furniture, barking at nothing, digging at carpets, pacing, and whining are not bad behaviour. They are a dog\'s way of saying, \"I have energy and nothing to do with it.\" Cats show boredom differently: overgrooming, aggression toward other pets, excessive vocalisation, or just lying around with flat eyes and zero interest in anything.

Mental stimulation tires a pet out more effectively than physical exercise alone. A 15-minute sniffari (scattering treats in the garden for your dog to find) can be as tiring as a 45-minute walk. A 10-minute clicker training session with your cat can leave them panting. The trick is not to do more. It is to do different.

The enrichment rule: Aim for a mix of food-based enrichment (puzzle feeders, snuffle mats), interactive play (tug, wand toys, fetch down the hallway), training (new tricks, obedience refreshers), and rest (calm time in a comfortable space). A balanced day looks like: active play, settle, food enrichment, settle, training, settle, calm time, bed.

Food-Based Enrichment for Dogs

Food-based enrichment is the easiest place to start because every dog eats, and most dogs love food. Instead of feeding from a bowl, turn mealtime into a problem-solving activity.

Frozen Kong

Setup time: 2 minutes (plus freezing). Duration: 20-45 minutes.

Stuff a Kong with wet food, plain yoghurt, mashed banana, or kibble mixed with a little water or low-sodium broth. Freeze overnight. For a quick version, 30 minutes in the freezer works. This is the single most recommended indoor enrichment tool by NZ veterinarians and behaviourists because it combines licking (which calms dogs) with problem-solving (which tires them out).

NZ tip: Kongs are available at Animates, Petstock, and online at Pet.co.nz. Start with a classic red Kong for medium chewers, or an extreme black Kong for heavy chewers.

Snuffle Mat

Setup time: 1 minute. Duration: 10-20 minutes.

A snuffle mat is a fabric mat with lots of hiding places for kibble or treats. Scatter dry food into the mat and let your dog sniff it out. It engages their nose, which is their most powerful sense, and sniffing is naturally calming. You can buy snuffle mats or make one by tying fleece strips to a rubber mat.

Puzzle Feeders and Treat Balls

Setup time: 1 minute. Duration: 10-30 minutes depending on difficulty.

Brands like Nina Ottosson, Outward Hound, and West Paw make puzzle feeders at different difficulty levels. Start with level 1 (slide a cover to reveal food) and work up to level 3 or 4 (multi-step puzzles). Treat balls that dispense kibble as the dog rolls them are great for independent play.

Scatter Feeding (Sniffari)

Setup time: 30 seconds. Duration: 10-15 minutes.

Throw a handful of kibble or small treats across the lounge floor or hallway. Let your dog sniff them out. This is the simplest form of enrichment and it works. For more challenge, scatter treats in a room with the lights dimmed, or hide them under cushions and behind furniture.

Cardboard Box Destruction

Setup time: 30 seconds. Duration: 5-15 minutes.

Take an old delivery box, put a few treats inside, fold it shut, and let your dog tear it open. Yes, it makes a mess. No, it is not elegant. But dogs love it, and it gives them a safe outlet for destructive energy. Clean up the pieces afterward to prevent ingestion.

Safety note: Remove any staples, tape, or plastic windows from boxes before giving them to your dog. Supervise and take the box away once it is destroyed.

Interactive Games for Dogs

Hide and Seek

Setup time: None. Duration: 10-20 minutes.

Ask your dog to stay, go hide in another room, and call them. Start easy (just around the corner) and increase difficulty as they get the idea. This game rewards recall, builds confidence, and uses their nose. It is also genuinely fun for both of you.

Which Hand?

Setup time: 5 seconds. Duration: 5-10 minutes.

Hide a treat in one hand and present both closed fists. Let your dog sniff and choose. When they nudge the correct hand, open it and give the treat. This is a simple game you can play anywhere, anytime, with zero equipment.

Indoor Obstacle Course

Setup time: 5 minutes. Duration: 15-20 minutes.

Use cushions to jump over, a blanket tunnel between chairs, a broomstick balanced on books to step over, and a cardboard box to crawl through. Guide your dog through with treats. This is especially good for high-energy breeds that need to move even when stuck inside.

Tug of War

Setup time: None. Duration: 5-10 minutes.

Tug is one of the best indoor games because it is physical, it teaches impulse control (you control when the game starts and stops), and it requires almost no space. Use a rope toy or an old towel. The rules: you start the game, you end the game, and if teeth touch skin, the game stops immediately.

Name That Toy

Setup time: 5 minutes initially. Duration: 10-15 minutes.

Teach your dog the names of their toys. Start with two: put one on the floor, say its name, and reward when they touch it. Add a second toy. Ask for one by name. Gradually add more. Dogs can learn 50+ object names with consistent training. This is a brilliant rainy-day project because it takes days to build up and each session is short.

Indoor Training Ideas for Dogs

Rainy days are perfect for training because there are no outdoor distractions and sessions are short (5-10 minutes). Here are tricks and skills to work on when you cannot go outside.

Foundation skills worth refreshing

  • Sit, down, stand — practise position changes with a lure
  • Stay with distractions — increase duration and distance gradually
  • Recall practice — call your dog from one room to another and reward heavily
  • Leave it — place a treat under a cup, tell your dog to leave it, then reward from your other hand
  • Place/mat training — send your dog to their mat and reward for staying there

Fun tricks for rainy days

  • Spin — lure in a circle, add the cue \"spin\"
  • Shake / high five — tap the back of their paw, mark and reward when they lift it
  • Roll over — from a down position, lure their nose toward their shoulder
  • Speak / quiet — capture a bark, mark it, add the cue. Then teach quiet by waiting for silence
  • Crawl — from down, lure forward along the floor without standing up
  • Back up — walk toward your dog and mark when they take a step backward
  • Figure eight between your legs — lure in a figure-eight pattern while you stand still
Training tip: Keep sessions to 5 minutes maximum. End on a success. If your dog is struggling, make the task easier rather than repeating the same thing. Three 5-minute sessions spread through the day are far more effective than one 15-minute session.

Food-Based Enrichment for Cats

Cats in the wild spend a significant portion of their waking hours hunting. When we put food in a bowl twice a day, we eliminate that entire behavioural repertoire. Food-based enrichment gives it back.

Puzzle Feeders for Cats

Setup time: 1 minute. Duration: 10-20 minutes.

Cat puzzle feeders range from simple (a ball with a hole that dispenses kibble as the cat bats it) to complex (multi-level mazes). Start easy and increase difficulty as your cat figures it out. Brands available in NZ include Catit, Trixie, and PetSafe. A muffin tin with treats under tennis balls is a free DIY version.

Treat Scavenger Hunt

Setup time: 2 minutes. Duration: 10-15 minutes.

Hide small treats around a room at different heights. On the cat tree, under a cushion, on a windowsill, behind a door. Let your cat sniff them out. This activates their hunting instinct and gets them moving around the house.

Lick Mat for Cats

Setup time: 2 minutes. Duration: 10-20 minutes.

Spread wet cat food, plain yoghurt, or a small amount of tuna paste on a silicone lick mat. Licking is calming for cats and the mat forces them to eat slowly. Freeze for a longer-lasting version.

Cardboard Box Feeding

Setup time: 1 minute. Duration: 5-15 minutes.

Cut holes in a cardboard box, put treats inside, and let your cat fish them out through the holes. This combines two things cats love: puzzles and cardboard boxes. Vary the size and number of holes to change the difficulty.

Interactive Play for Cats

Wand Toy Play

Duration: 10-15 minutes, 2-3 times daily.

Wand toys (feather wands, ribbon wands, fabric strips on a stick) mimic prey movement and activate the full hunting sequence: stalk, chase, pounce, catch, bite. Move the toy like a mouse (darting, hiding) or a bird (flying, landing). Let your cat catch it at the end of each session. A cat that never catches the toy gets frustrated and may stop playing.

Important: Always put wand toys away after play. The string is a strangulation risk and the small parts are choking hazards if left out unsupervised.

Laser Pointer (Done Right)

Duration: 5-10 minutes.

Laser pointers get cats moving, but the problem is that there is nothing to catch. End every laser session by pointing the laser at a real toy or treat so your cat can complete the hunt sequence with a physical catch. Without this, laser play can cause frustration and obsessive behaviour.

Paper Bag and Box Village

Setup time: 2 minutes. Duration: 20-60 minutes of independent play.

Place paper bags (handles removed) and cardboard boxes around the room. Cut a few holes in the boxes for doorways. Toss a treat or catnip toy into one of them. Cats will explore, hide, ambush each other, and generally treat your lounge like their personal adventure playground. This costs nothing and cats love it.

Under the Blanket Game

Setup time: 5 seconds. Duration: 5-10 minutes.

Drag a toy or your hand under a blanket on the bed or couch. Your cat will stalk, pounce, and try to catch whatever is moving under there. This is one of the easiest interactive cat games and it works for almost every cat.

Training and Tricks for Cats

Yes, you can train cats. They are not less intelligent than dogs. They are just more selective about when they feel like cooperating. Use high-value treats (freeze-dried chicken, tuna flakes, small bits of ham) and keep sessions to 3-5 minutes.

Tricks cats can learn

  • Touch target — hold a chopstick or pen near their nose, mark and reward when they touch it with their nose. This is the foundation for everything else.
  • Sit — hold a treat above their head and slightly back. When their bottom hits the floor, mark and reward.
  • High five — hold a treat above a paw. When they reach for it, mark the paw lift and reward.
  • Spin — lure in a circle with a treat on the target stick.
  • Come when called — say their name, mark and reward when they look at you. Gradually increase distance.
  • Go to mat — lure to a mat and reward for staying there. Build duration over time.
Clicker training for cats: A clicker marks the exact moment your cat does the right thing. Click, then treat. The click means \"yes, that is exactly what I wanted.\" Clickers cost $5 at any pet store and are worth every cent. Start by clicking and treating 10 times in a row so your cat learns that click means treat is coming.

DIY Indoor Activities (Free or Almost Free)

Not every enrichment activity requires a shopping trip. Here are activities that cost nothing or use things you already have at home.

For dogs

  • Muffin tin puzzle: Put treats in a muffin tin, cover each hole with a tennis ball. Let your dog figure out how to remove the balls to get the treats.
  • Towel roll: Lay a towel flat, place treats along one edge, and roll it up. Let your dog unroll the towel to find the treats. For more challenge, roll treats into multiple layers.
  • Bottle spin: Cut holes in a clean plastic bottle, put kibble inside, and let your dog bat it around to get the food out. Supervise to prevent chewing the plastic.
  • Cup shuffle: Place three cups upside down. Put a treat under one. Shuffle the cups. Let your dog sniff and choose. This is the dog version of the shell game.
  • Stair fetch: If you have carpeted stairs and a small ball, sit at the top and roll it down. Your dog runs down, grabs it, and brings it back. Moderate use only, especially for puppies and senior dogs.

For cats

  • Toilet roll treat dispenser: Fold one end of a toilet roll tube, put treats inside, fold the other end, and poke a few holes. Your cat bats it around until treats fall out.
  • Egg carton feeder: Put small treats or kibble in the cups of an egg carton and close it. Your cat has to figure out how to open it.
  • Ice cube treats: Freeze small treats or kibble in an ice cube tray. Place the ice cube on a plate or in the bathtub and let your cat lick it to get the treats out. Good for a hot day too.
  • Ping pong ball in the bathtub: Put a ping pong ball in the bathtub and let your cat bat it around. The enclosed space makes the ball bounce unpredictably. Line the tub with a towel if the noise bothers you.
  • Paper tunnel: Tape several paper bags together end to end with the bottoms cut out to create a tunnel. Most cats cannot resist running through it.

Breed-Specific Indoor Needs

Not all dogs and cats need the same amount or type of stimulation. Here is a quick guide by breed energy level.

Energy Level Dog Breeds (NZ popular) Best Indoor Activities Minutes/Day
Very High Border Collie, Australian Shepherd, Husky, Working Kelpie Puzzle feeders (level 3-4), training sessions, indoor obstacle course, tug, name-that-toy 60-90
High Labrador, German Shepherd, Groodle, Spoodle Frozen Kong, snuffle mat, hide-and-seek, trick training, tug 45-60
Medium Cavalier King Charles, French Bulldog, Pug, Beagle Treat ball, scatter feeding, short training sessions, tug, which-hand game 30-45
Low Greyhound, Bulldog, Basset Hound, older dogs Sniffari, lick mat, gentle training, stuffed chew toy 15-30

Cats by activity level

Activity Level Cat Types Best Indoor Activities Minutes/Day
Very Active Bengal, Siamese, Oriental breeds, young kittens Wand toy play, laser (with catch), training, box villages, puzzle feeders 30-45
Active Domestic shorthair, Ragdoll, Maine Coon (yes, they are playful), young adults Wand toy, treat hunt, under-blanket game, lick mat, paper bag play 20-30
Moderate British Shorthair, Persian, middle-aged cats Puzzle feeder, treat scatter, gentle wand play, cardboard box 15-20
Low Senior cats, cats with health issues Lick mat, sniffing treats on the floor, gentle petting, catnip toy 10-15
Puppy and kitten note: Puppies and kittens have shorter attention spans but more energy. Do 3-5 minute sessions with breaks in between. Over-exercising growing joints (especially in large-breed puppies) can cause long-term damage. Focus on short, gentle activities and plenty of sleep.

What to Tell Your Pet Sitter

If you are leaving your pet with a sitter during rainy season, give them an indoor enrichment toolkit. Write down which activities your pet enjoys, where the puzzle feeders and toys are stored, and how long each activity should last. A sitter who knows how to entertain your pet indoors is worth their weight in gold when the weather turns.

Sitter enrichment checklist

  • Where the puzzle feeders, Kongs, and treat toys are stored
  • Which games your pet enjoys (fetch, tug, wand toy, etc.)
  • How to prepare frozen Kongs (what to fill them with, where they go in the freezer)
  • Treat limits (how many treats per day, which treats are approved)
  • Which rooms are OK for indoor play and which are off-limits
  • Whether your cat is allowed on benches and tables
  • Any toys that should be put away after supervised play (strings, wand toys, small parts)
  • Signs your pet is getting overstimulated and needs a break

When You Can Still Go Outside

Not all rainy days mean staying indoors. A light drizzle is fine for most dogs, and some cats (particularly those used to going outside) still want outdoor access. See our guide on walking your dog in mud and rain for tips on wet-weather walks, including gear recommendations and safety considerations.

But when the rain is horizontal, the wind is howling, or you are in the middle of a Wellington southerly, indoor enrichment is the way to go. Your pet does not need a walk every single day. They do need engagement every single day. And with the right activities, a rainy day indoors can be just as enriching as a walk around the block.
