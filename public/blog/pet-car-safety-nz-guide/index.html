Pet Car Safety in NZ: Travel Safely with Your Dog or Cat

Published: April 21, 2026 | Reading time: 7 min

New Zealand is a beautiful country to explore with your pet. From the beaches of the Coromandel to the mountains of the South Island, there is no shortage of incredible destinations accessible by car. But before you strap in and hit the road, it is worth asking: is your pet actually safe in your vehicle?

Every year in New Zealand, unrestrained pets cause accidents, suffer injuries in crashes, or become projectiles during sudden braking. In a 60 km/h crash, an unsecured medium-sized dog becomes a projectile with a force equivalent to over half a tonne. That is not a risk worth taking.

This guide covers everything New Zealand pet owners need to know about travelling safely with pets in cars.

Why Pet Car Safety Matters in NZ

New Zealand has a unique driving environment. Our winding rural roads, variable weather, and long distances between destinations mean more time on the road with pets. Whether you are commuting to the vet, heading to a holiday home, or embarking on a South Island road trip, your pet deserves to be protected.

Research from the AA and other road safety organisations consistently shows that unrestrained pets are a distraction and a danger. They can:

  • Impede your ability to brake or steer in an emergency
  • Become seriously injured or killed in a crash
  • Cause injury to human passengers if thrown forward in a collision
  • Escape through a window or door in a crash and become a road hazard

Under New Zealand law, drivers have a responsibility to ensure their vehicle is safely loaded, and this includes pets. While specific rules about pet restraints in vehicles are still evolving, you can be fined if your pet causes you to drive unsafely.

Choosing the Right Restraint for Your Pet

Dog Car Harnesses

A crash-tested dog car harness is one of the most effective ways to keep your dog safe. Look for harnesses that meet international crash-test standards, not just "seat belt compatible" labels. Brands like Sleepypod, Truffleskin, and All Sound barriers have undergone genuine crash testing.

The harness should attach to your car is seat belt system. Your dog wears the harness, and a lead clips from the harness to the seat belt. This prevents your dog from moving around the cabin while still allowing them some comfort and flexibility.

Note: Never attach a lead to a collar in the car. In a crash, the forces on the neck from a collar can cause serious injury. Always use a harness.

Pet Carriers and Crates

For cats and small dogs, a well-ventilated, appropriately sized carrier is the safest option. The carrier should be secured with a seatbelt or placed in the cargo area of an SUV with the cargo barrier in place.

Soft-sided carriers can be appropriate for small pets in certain situations, but in a crash, rigid carriers offer better protection. For cats, introduce the carrier well before travel to reduce stress. Spray it with Feliway (available from NZ pet stores and vets) 20 minutes before travel to help calm anxiety.

SUV Cargo Barriers and Pet Nets

For larger dogs in SUVs and wagons, a cargo barrier prevents your dog from being thrown forward in a crash. These are especially important for dogs who ride in the cargo area. Metal or heavy-duty mesh barriers rated for pet use are widely available in New Zealand from brands like Lazybone and Pet Buckle.

Seat Belt Harnesses for Small Dogs

Small dogs can be safely transported using a harness that threads through the seat belt, keeping them secure in their designated seat. Never place a small dog on your lap while driving.

Preparing Your Pet for Car Travel

Get Them Comfortable Before the Trip

If your pet is not used to car travel, start slowly. First, let them explore the parked car with the engine off. Then progress to short trips around the block, gradually increasing duration. Reward calm behaviour in the car with treats and praise.

Some pets experience motion sickness. If your dog or cat vomits or drools in the car, speak to your vet. Medication like Cerenia (prescription anti-nausea) can help for longer journeys. Ginger-based treats can also help mild nausea.

What to Bring

For any car journey with your pet, pack:

  • Fresh water and a bowl
  • Any medications your pet needs
  • Poop bags
  • Your pet is favourite blanket or toy for comfort
  • their regular food for longer trips
  • Feliway or Adaptil spray for anxiety
  • First aid kit for pets

Feeding Before Travel

Feed a light meal a few hours before travel to avoid an upset stomach, but do not feed a large meal immediately before a long car journey. Always have water available.

Road Rules and Best Practices

Where Should Your Pet Sit?

For safety and to avoid distraction, the best position depends on your vehicle and your pet:

  • Back seat (seat belt harness): Safest for most dogs. Attach to the seat belt in the back seat.
  • Cargo area (with barrier): Appropriate for larger dogs in SUVs and wagons.
  • Front seat: Not recommended for dogs. Airbags pose a risk, and dogs can distract the driver.
  • On your lap: Never safe, even for small dogs. In a crash, they become a projectile.

Keep Windows Safe

Do not let your dog put their head out of a moving vehicle window. Debris, tree branches, and other objects can cause serious injury at speed. Keep window openings limited to a safe level where your pet cannot jump or fall out.

Take Breaks on Long Journeys

New Zealand is a long country, and driving between major cities can take hours. On trips over two hours, stop every one to two hours so your pet can relieve themselves, stretch their legs, and have a drink of water. Never leave your pet unattended in a parked car, especially in summer. Even on mild days, cars heat up quickly.

Keep the Car Cool

Use air conditioning or keep windows open enough to maintain airflow in warm weather. Never leave your pet in a hot car, even briefly. On hot New Zealand summer days, even parked cars can reach dangerous temperatures within minutes.

Travel Sickness and Anxiety

Motion sickness is common in young dogs and cats, but it can be managed. Tips for travel-sick pets:

  • Face pets forward, not sideways or backward
  • Keep the car cool and well-ventilated
  • Feed a light meal well before travel
  • Ask your vet about anti-nausea medication
  • Consider calming supplements or pheromones

For anxious pets, practice short trips before longer journeys. Calming music or white noise can help. Some pets benefit from Thundershirts or similar pressure wraps. In persistent cases, speak to your vet about prescription options.

After the Journey

Once you arrive at your destination, give your pet time to adjust. Unpack their familiar items first: bed, toys, and bowls. Let them explore at their own pace. If you have arrived at a new location like a holiday home or camping site, ensure the area is pet-safe and secure before letting them off-lead.

Finding the Right Equipment in New Zealand

In New Zealand, pet car safety products are available from:

  • PETstock and Animates retail stores
  • Online retailers like Grabone and TradeMe for specific brands
  • Specialist pet travel retailers
  • Your local veterinary clinic

Look for products that meet international safety standards. If a harness claims to be "crash tested", check for actual certification, not just marketing language.

Some New Zealand pet insurance policies may cover costs related to transport safety equipment or travel-related vet visits. Check with your provider.

Conclusion

Every journey, whether to the vet or to the beaches of Abel Tasman, deserves the same attention to pet safety. A properly restrained pet is safer, more comfortable, and less likely to distract you while driving. New Zealand is one of the most beautiful countries in the world to road trip with your best friend. Make sure you do it safely.

Invest in a quality harness or carrier, take the time to accustom your pet to car travel, and make safety a non-negotiable part of every journey. Your pet is counting on you to bring them home.
