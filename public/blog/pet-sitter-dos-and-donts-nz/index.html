Pet Sitter Do\'s and Don\'ts in New Zealand: The Complete Guide

Published 26 June 2026 · For Pet Owners & Pet Sitters

Whether you are hiring a pet sitter for the first time or you have been sitting for years, there are unwritten rules that separate a good experience from a great one. Small mistakes can cause big problems: a missed medication, an unsecured gate, a treat the vet never approved. This guide covers the essential do\'s and don\'ts for both pet owners and pet sitters in New Zealand, so every sit goes smoothly.

Do\'s and Don\'ts for Pet Owners

Do: Arrange a meet-and-greet first

A meet-and-greet is non-negotiable for a first-time sit. You need to see how the sitter interacts with your pet, and your pet needs a chance to size them up in a safe environment. Even if your pet is friendly with everyone, the sitter needs to know where the food is, how the alarm works, and which doors must stay closed. A 30-minute visit saves days of confusion later.

Do: Leave a written care sheet

Verbal instructions get forgotten. Write down your pet\'s daily routine, feeding times and portions, medication schedules, vet contact details, your emergency contact, and any quirks like \"she hides under the bed during thunderstorms\" or \"he barks at the postie but is all bluff.\" Leave it on the kitchen bench where the sitter will see it every morning.

Do: Be honest about your pet\'s behaviour

If your dog pulls on the lead, say so. If your cat scratches furniture, say so. If your elderly dog has accidents indoors, say so. Sitters are not judging you. They are trying to keep your pet safe and your home intact. Undisclosed behavioural issues are the number one cause of stressful sits.

Don\'t: Say your dog is \"fine off-lead\" when they are not. If your dog has unreliable recall, the sitter needs to know before they take them to a park. A lost dog is not a minor inconvenience. It is an emergency.

Don\'t: Hide medical issues

If your pet is on medication, has a chronic condition, or is recovering from surgery, tell the sitter before the booking is confirmed. A sitter who is not comfortable administering insulin or managing an epileptic dog should have the chance to say so before you are halfway through your holiday.

Don\'t: Overbook your sitter

If you have three dogs, two cats, a rabbit, and a fish tank, be upfront about the workload. Sitters price and plan based on what they know. Surprise pets mean surprise work, and that leads to burnout and resentment. If the scope changes, the conversation about compensation should happen before the sit, not after.

Don\'t: Micromanage from a distance

Daily updates are reasonable. Hourly photo requests are not. You hired a sitter because you trust them to care for your pet. Let them do their job. If you need more communication than the sitter provides, that is a conversation to have during the meet-and-greet, not a demand to text mid-sit.

Do\'s and Don\'ts for Pet Sitters

Do: Follow the routine exactly

Pets thrive on predictability. If the owner says breakfast at 7am, walk at 8am, and dinner at 6pm, do exactly that. Dogs do not understand weekends. Cats do not understand \"I slept in.\" A consistent routine keeps anxious pets calm and prevents digestive upsets from irregular feeding.

Do: Send updates without being asked

A quick morning message saying \"Breakfast eaten, walk done, all good\" takes 30 seconds and prevents the owner from worrying. A photo of their pet looking happy on the couch is worth more than any testimonial you will ever write. Owners who feel informed are owners who leave five-star reviews.

Do: Ask questions during the meet-and-greet

Where is the vet? Where are the bins? Which plants are toxic? Does the cat go outside? Is the fence secure? Can the dog be trusted off-lead? Where is the fuse box? What is the Wi-Fi password? These seem obvious until you are standing in a stranger\'s kitchen at 10pm with a dog that just ate something from the garden and you have no idea what it was or who to call.

Pro tip: Take photos of the care sheet and any important information during the meet-and-greet. If the original gets lost, damaged, or your phone dies, you will have a backup.

Don\'t: Give treats the owner has not approved

This is one of the most common mistakes new sitters make. You want to bond with the pet, so you give them a treat from your bag or a scrap from the fridge. But many pets have allergies, sensitivities, or strict diets. A dog on an elimination diet for skin issues can have a flare-up from a single unapproved treat. Always check first.

Don\'t: Invite friends over

You are staying in someone\'s home. It is not your flat. Inviting friends round for drinks, bringing your partner to stay, or throwing a dinner party is a hard no unless the owner has explicitly said it is fine. Even then, keep it low-key. Their home, their rules.

Don\'t: Let pets off-lead without explicit permission

New Zealand has strict dog control laws. If a dog rushes or attacks a person or animal, the owner can face a $3,000 fine and the dog can be classified as dangerous or even destroyed. As a sitter, you are responsible. Only let a dog off-lead if the owner has told you it is safe and the dog has reliable recall. When in doubt, keep the lead on.

Communication Rules That Matter

Do: Establish communication preferences upfront

Some owners want daily photo updates. Others prefer a single \"all good\" message. Ask during the meet-and-greet. Write it down. And then do what you agreed. Over-communicating can be as stressful as under-communicating if the owner is on holiday trying to disconnect.

Don\'t: Ghost the owner

Even if you agreed on minimal contact, send at least one daily check-in. Silence breeds anxiety. If you are busy, a single emoji or a one-line message is enough. But silence for 48 hours while the owner is in Fiji wondering if their cat is alive is not acceptable.

Do: Report problems immediately

If the dog is not eating, the cat is hiding and has not used the litter tray in 24 hours, or something in the house breaks, tell the owner straight away. Small problems become big problems when they are hidden. The owner would rather hear \"the dog vomited once\" immediately than \"the dog has been vomiting for three days\" when they get home.

House-Sitting-Specific Etiquette

Do: Treat the home with respect

This means: clean up after yourself, do not rearrange the furniture, leave things where you found them, and if you use something, replace it or note it for the owner. You are a guest, not a tenant. Act like you would want someone to act in your home.

Don\'t: Use the owner\'s personal things

Their wine, their fancy skincare, their spare bedroom linens, their garage tools. Unless the owner specifically says \"help yourself,\" treat everything as off-limits. Using someone\'s expensive face cream because \"they will not notice\" is a great way to ensure you never sit for them again.

Do: Manage household tasks

House-sitting usually includes basic tasks: collecting mail, putting out bins, watering plants, and keeping the place tidy. These are not optional extras. They are part of the job. A home that looks lived-in is a home that does not get burgled. Bins left out for two weeks scream \"nobody is home\" to every passerby.

NZ bins tip: Bin day varies by region. In Auckland it is often Tuesday or Wednesday. In Wellington it varies by suburb. In Christchurch it is usually Thursday. Check the local council website or ask the owner during the meet-and-greet.

Safety Do\'s and Don\'ts

Do: Know the nearest after-hours vet

New Zealand has excellent after-hours vet services in most cities, but in rural areas the nearest emergency clinic might be 40 minutes away. Find out before the sit begins. Write the address and phone number on the care sheet. If you are sitting in a small town like Whanganui or Timaru, the after-hours options are more limited. Know where you would go before you need to go.

Do: Secure the property

Close gates. Lock doors. Check fences. If the owner told you the side gate blows open in the wind, prop it shut. If the dog is an escape artist, double-check every time you go through a door. One open gate and one squirrel later, you are chasing a dog through a stranger\'s neighbourhood at 6am.

Don\'t: Take pets to off-leash dog parks without checking

Not every dog enjoys the dog park. Some are reactive, some are shy, some have health issues that make rough play dangerous. Ask the owner if the dog park is appropriate for their pet. Some dogs prefer a quiet walk along the Avon River in Christchurch or a stroll through the Wellington Botanic Garden over a chaotic play session at the local off-leash area.

Don\'t: Leave pets unattended in cars

New Zealand summers can hit 30 degrees Celsius. A car interior reaches 50 degrees in minutes. Even with windows cracked, it is dangerous and illegal. If you need to run errands, leave the pet at home. It is not worth the risk.

Money and Boundaries

Do: Agree on price and scope before the sit

Are you watering 2 pot plants or an entire greenhouse? Walking the dog once or three times a day? Feeding one cat or three cats and a dog? Scope creep is real. Agree on what the sit includes and what counts as extra work. If the owner adds tasks mid-sit, the conversation about additional pay should happen then, not when the sit is over.

Don\'t: Nickel-and-dime over small things

If the owner asks you to water a couple of extra pot plants, that is not worth a negotiation. But if you came to sit one dog and now you are caring for three dogs, a cat, and feeding the neighbour\'s fish, that is a different scope. Use your judgement. Small additions are part of being flexible. Big additions are worth a conversation.

What Makes a Great Pet Sitting Relationship

The best pet sits are built on three things: clear expectations, honest communication, and mutual respect. The owner trusts the sitter with their pet and their home. The sitter trusts the owner to be upfront about what they are walking into. When both sides hold up their end, the pet gets consistent care, the owner gets peace of mind, and the sitter gets a positive experience they would happily repeat.

New Zealand pet owners are increasingly choosing in-home pet sitting over kennels and catteries because pets stay in their own environment, maintain their routine, and experience less stress. But that only works when both parties follow the basics covered in this guide.

Key Takeaways

  • Meet-and-greets are non-negotiable for first-time sits, even if your pet is easygoing.
  • Write everything down. Verbal instructions get lost. A care sheet on the kitchen bench is the single most useful thing an owner can provide.
  • Honesty goes both ways. Owners must disclose behavioural and medical issues. Sitters must disclose their experience level.
  • Communicate on day one. Set expectations about update frequency before the sit begins.
  • Follow the routine. Pets are creatures of habit. The sitter\'s job is to maintain normality, not reinvent it.
  • Report problems early. Small issues become crises when hidden.
  • Use the platform. Payments, reviews, and dispute resolution exist for a reason.
