How Pet Boarding Works in New Zealand

Pet boarding in NZ falls into two categories: kennels for dogs and catteries for cats. Some facilities offer both, but many specialise in one or the other. The basic model is straightforward: you drop your pet off, they stay in an enclosure for the duration of your trip, and you pick them up when you return. Staff feed, clean, and monitor your pet, and most facilities offer exercise or play time as part of their standard service.

In New Zealand, boarding facilities are not required to hold a specific licence to operate, unlike in some countries. The Animal Welfare Act 1999 sets minimum standards for the care of animals, but there is no mandatory inspection regime for boarding kennels or catteries. This means the responsibility for vetting a facility falls entirely on you as the pet owner. Some facilities voluntarily register with organisations like the New Zealand Kennel Club or seek accreditation, but this is not compulsory.

How to Choose a Good Kennel or Cattery

Because New Zealand does not require boarding facilities to be licensed or inspected, you need to do your own due diligence. Here is a step-by-step approach:

1. Visit Before You Book

This is the single most important step. Any reputable facility should allow and encourage inspections. If a kennel or cattery refuses a visit, that is an immediate red flag. When you visit, pay attention to:

  • Cleanliness: Does the facility smell clean, not masked with disinfectant? Are enclosures free of waste?
  • Space: Are enclosures large enough for the animals inside? Dogs should have room to stand, turn around, and lie down comfortably. Cats need vertical space as well as floor space.
  • Ventilation and temperature: Is there adequate airflow? Are indoor areas temperature-controlled for extremes?
  • Security: Are fences and enclosures secure? Could a determined dog dig under or jump over? Are cat enclosures fully enclosed to prevent escape?
  • Noise levels: Constant barking suggests stress or poor management. Some noise is normal; a wall of sound is not.

2. Ask About Daily Routine

A good facility has a structured daily routine that includes feeding, exercise, rest, and social interaction appropriate to each animal. Ask specifically:

  • How often are dogs walked or exercised?
  • Are dogs exercised individually or in groups? If in groups, how are temperaments assessed?
  • How often are cats checked and interacted with?
  • What happens if a pet refuses to eat?
  • How are medications administered, and what happens if a pet needs veterinary care?

3. Check Veterinary Arrangements

Every boarding facility should have a relationship with a local veterinarian and a clear protocol for medical emergencies. Ask which vet they use, what their policy is for seeking treatment, and whether they contact you before taking your pet to the vet (unless it is a life-threatening emergency where delay would cause harm).

4. Read Reviews and Get Recommendations

Google reviews, Facebook recommendations, and word-of-mouth from other pet owners are valuable. Look for patterns in reviews rather than individual complaints. If multiple people mention the same issue, such as pets returning home with kennel cough or appearing stressed, take that seriously.

Red Flags to Avoid

Walk away from any facility that shows these warning signs:

  • Refuses inspection or allows only a partial tour
  • Requires no vaccination records or health information
  • Has visibly dirty enclosures or strong odours
  • Mixes dogs of vastly different sizes or temperaments without supervision
  • Cannot tell you which vet they use
  • Has no clear emergency protocol
  • Staff seem disengaged or unable to answer basic questions about animal care
  • Enclosures are overcrowded or too small for the animals inside
  • No contract or written agreement outlining responsibilities and liabilities

Preparing Your Pet for Boarding

Good preparation makes a significant difference to how your pet copes with boarding. Here are the key steps:

Vaccinations

All reputable facilities require current vaccinations. For dogs, this typically means C5 or C7 coverage (parvovirus, distemper, hepatitis, bordetella, parainfluenza, and sometimes coronavirus and leptospirosis). For cats, F3 or F4 vaccination (feline enteritis, calicivirus, herpesvirus, and sometimes chlamydia) is standard. Vaccinations must be completed at least 10 to 14 days before boarding to be effective. Do not leave this to the last minute.

Flea and Worm Treatment

Most facilities require proof of current flea and worm treatment. Apply flea prevention and give worm treatment before the boarding stay, following your vet's recommended schedule.

Trial Stay

If your pet has never been boarded before, book a short trial stay of one or two nights before committing to a longer period. This gives you a chance to see how your pet responds and allows the facility to get to know your pet. Some pets adapt quickly; others show significant stress. A trial stay helps you make an informed decision before a longer trip.

Bring Familiar Items

Most facilities allow you to bring a blanket, toy, or item of clothing that smells like home. This simple step can reduce anxiety significantly. Label everything clearly with your name and your pet's name.

Provide Detailed Instructions

Write a clear care sheet covering feeding amounts and times, medication schedules, behavioural quirks, and emergency contacts. The more information you provide, the better the care your pet will receive.

When In-Home Pet Sitting Is the Better Choice

Boarding is not the only option, and for many pets, it is not the best one. In-home pet sitting, where a sitter cares for your pet in your own home, offers several advantages:

For Cats

Cats are territorial animals. Removing them from their home and placing them in a cattery, even a good one, is inherently stressful. In-home cat sitting keeps your cat in familiar surroundings with their own territory, smells, and routine. For anxious cats, elderly cats, or cats with health conditions, this can make the difference between a stressful experience and a calm one.

For Anxious or Reactive Dogs

Some dogs do not cope well in kennel environments. The noise of other dogs, the confinement, and the disruption of routine can trigger anxiety, stress-related illness, or behavioural regression. If your dog is anxious around other dogs, reactive on leash, or prone to separation anxiety, in-home pet sitting is almost always the better option.

For Senior Pets

Older pets with arthritis, cognitive decline, or chronic health conditions benefit from staying in a familiar environment where they know the layout, the temperature, and the routine. A boarding facility, no matter how well-run, introduces variables that can exacerbate existing conditions.

For Multi-Pet Households

If you have multiple pets, boarding each one separately is expensive and logistically complex. An in-home pet sitter can care for all your pets in one place, maintaining their existing social dynamics and shared routines.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do kennels require vaccinations in New Zealand?

Yes. Every reputable boarding facility in NZ requires current vaccination records. Dogs typically need C5 or C7 vaccination; cats need F3 or F4. Vaccinations should be completed at least 10 to 14 days before boarding.

What is kennel cough and should I be worried?

Kennel cough (canine infectious tracheobronchitis) is a highly contagious respiratory infection spread through airborne droplets. It is common in environments where many dogs are housed together. Vaccination reduces but does not eliminate the risk. If your dog boards frequently, discuss the kennel cough vaccine with your vet and consider whether in-home sitting might be a lower-risk alternative.

Can I board my puppy?

Most kennels require puppies to have completed their full vaccination course, which typically finishes at around 16 weeks. Very young puppies that have not been fully vaccinated should not be boarded due to their incomplete immunity. In-home pet sitting with a sitter experienced with puppies is a safer option for young dogs.

How far in advance should I book?

For peak periods (Christmas, Easter, school holidays), book at least 4 to 6 weeks in advance. Good facilities fill up quickly during these periods. Outside peak times, 1 to 2 weeks is usually sufficient, but earlier is always better.

Making the Right Choice for Your Pet

There is no universally right answer when it comes to pet care while you travel. The best choice depends on your pet's temperament, health, age, and social nature. Some confident, social dogs genuinely enjoy the stimulation of a well-run kennel. Most cats and many dogs are better off at home with a sitter who maintains their routine and provides one-on-one attention.

Whatever you choose, do your research, visit the facility or meet the sitter in advance, and prepare your pet thoroughly. Your pet depends on you to make the right call, and a little effort upfront can prevent a lot of stress later.
