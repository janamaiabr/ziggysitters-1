French Bulldog Care Guide NZ 2026

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  March 9, 2026  •  11 min read

French Bulldogs have become one of New Zealand’s most popular dog breeds, and it’s easy to see why. They’re affectionate, adaptable, endlessly entertaining, and ideal for apartment life. But Frenchies are also one of the most medically complex breeds you can own. Understanding their specific needs — particularly around breathing, heat, diet, and exercise — is essential for every NZ owner. This guide covers everything you need to know.

1. Understanding the Brachycephalic Breed

The French Bulldog is a brachycephalic breed, meaning it has been selectively bred for a flattened face and shortened muzzle. While this gives Frenchies their distinctive, expressive look, it also creates a collection of anatomical challenges that affect breathing, temperature regulation, and overall health.

The term “brachycephalic obstructive airway syndrome” (BOAS) covers several related conditions common in Frenchies:

  • Stenotic nares — narrowed nostrils that restrict airflow
  • Elongated soft palate — excess tissue at the back of the throat that partially blocks the airway
  • Hypoplastic trachea — a narrower-than-normal windpipe
  • Everted laryngeal saccules — tissue that gets sucked into the airway under respiratory stress

Many Frenchies live comfortably with mild BOAS. Others require corrective surgery to breathe properly. If your dog snores loudly, breathes heavily at rest, gags frequently, or seems distressed after moderate activity, have them assessed by a vet with experience in brachycephalic breeds.

NZ context: Many NZ vets now offer BOAS grading assessments. Early intervention through nares widening surgery or palate correction can significantly improve quality of life for affected dogs and reduce long-term health costs. Ask your vet if your Frenchie would benefit from an airway assessment.

2. Exercise: Less is More (and Timing is Everything)

One of the most critical care considerations for French Bulldogs in New Zealand is exercise management. Because Frenchies struggle to cool themselves efficiently through panting — which requires drawing air rapidly through the nose and mouth — they overheat far more quickly than most other breeds.

How Much Exercise Does a Frenchie Need?

Adult French Bulldogs need around 20 to 30 minutes of moderate exercise per day. This should be split into two short sessions rather than one long walk. Puppies under 12 months should have exercise limited to five minutes per month of age, twice daily — so a four-month-old Frenchie should walk no more than 20 minutes total per day.

French Bulldogs are not distance runners, and they should never be pushed to keep up on long hikes or jogs. Watch for these signs that your Frenchie has had enough:

  • Heavy or laboured breathing that doesn’t settle quickly
  • Excessive drooling or foamy saliva
  • Slowing down or refusing to move
  • Bright red gums (a sign of overheating)
  • Stumbling or appearing uncoordinated
Heat danger: French Bulldogs can collapse and die from heatstroke remarkably quickly. Never walk your Frenchie between 10am and 5pm during NZ summer and spring. Early morning or evening walks are essential. If the air temperature is above 22°C, keep exercise very brief or skip it entirely.

Safe Exercise Options

  • Short, leisurely morning or evening walks in shaded areas
  • Sniff walks where the dog sets the pace and takes time to explore
  • Indoor play sessions with puzzle toys and games
  • Gentle swimming (with supervision — many Frenchies cannot swim unaided due to their heavy front end and short limbs)
  • Mental stimulation through training, scent work, and food puzzles

3. Keeping Your Frenchie Cool in NZ

New Zealand’s increasingly warm summers and mild springs mean heat management is a year-round consideration for Frenchie owners, particularly in Auckland, Wellington, and Hawke’s Bay. French Bulldogs cannot pant effectively enough to cool themselves, which means external cooling measures are essential.

Cooling Strategies That Work

  • Keep air conditioning or a fan running indoors during warm days
  • Provide a cooling mat or damp towel for your Frenchie to lie on
  • Offer ice cubes in their water bowl or a frozen Kong as a treat
  • Never leave a Frenchie in a car, even with windows cracked
  • Use a cooling vest or damp bandana around the neck during outdoor time
  • Place fresh cool water in multiple locations around the home
Car safety: A car interior can reach 40°C within 10 minutes on a 25°C day. French Bulldogs can suffer fatal heatstroke within minutes in these conditions. Never leave your Frenchie in a parked car, not even to run a quick errand.

4. Feeding Your French Bulldog

French Bulldogs are known for two things around food: they love it, and they are prone to gaining weight easily. Obesity places additional strain on their already compromised airways and joints, so maintaining a healthy weight is genuinely life-extending for this breed.

Choosing the Right Food

Look for a high-quality dog food with a named protein source (chicken, beef, lamb, fish) as the first ingredient. Frenchies are prone to food allergies and sensitivities — common triggers include chicken, wheat, corn, and soy. If your dog develops recurring skin issues, ear infections, or gastrointestinal problems, a food trial with a novel protein (such as venison, duck, or rabbit) may be worthwhile under vet guidance.

Many NZ Frenchie owners have success with:

  • Premium dry kibble designed for small to medium breeds with sensitive digestion
  • Raw feeding or partially raw diets (consult your vet for balanced ratios)
  • Limited-ingredient diets when allergies are confirmed

Portion Control and Treats

Follow the feeding guidelines on your chosen food and adjust based on body condition rather than the chart alone. You should be able to feel your Frenchie’s ribs easily without pressing hard, and they should have a visible waist when viewed from above. Treats should make up no more than 10% of daily caloric intake. Use high-value, low-calorie options like small pieces of cooked chicken or carrot for training.

Bloat prevention: French Bulldogs can be prone to gas and bloating due to their anatomy and enthusiastic eating habits. Use a slow-feeder bowl, avoid exercise for 30 minutes before and after meals, and feed two smaller meals per day rather than one large one.

Foods to Avoid

The following are toxic to French Bulldogs and must be kept strictly out of reach:

  • Grapes and raisins (kidney failure)
  • Xylitol (found in sugar-free gum, some peanut butters — causes liver failure)
  • Onions and garlic (toxic to red blood cells)
  • Macadamia nuts (neurological effects)
  • Chocolate and caffeine
  • Alcohol and cooked bones

5. Skin Fold and Facial Care

The deep skin folds around a French Bulldog’s nose, face, and tail area are a feature of the breed but require regular cleaning to prevent infection. Moisture, warmth, and skin-to-skin contact in these folds create ideal conditions for bacterial and yeast overgrowth.

Cleaning Routine

  • Clean facial folds 2 to 3 times per week using a damp cloth or specifically formulated dog fold wipes
  • Dry the folds thoroughly after cleaning — moisture left behind worsens rather than helps
  • Check the tail pocket (the small indentation above the screw tail) weekly for redness or discharge
  • Use a veterinary-recommended cream or powder if redness persists
  • Wipe around the eyes daily to remove discharge and prevent tear staining

Signs of fold dermatitis include redness, a sour or musty smell, discharge, and your dog rubbing their face on furniture or carpet. Left untreated, fold infections become painful and can spread. Contact your vet if cleaning alone doesn’t resolve the issue within a few days.

6. Common Health Issues in NZ French Bulldogs

Condition Signs Management
BOAS (Breathing issues) Loud snoring, laboured breathing, gagging Vet assessment, possible corrective surgery
Skin allergies (atopic dermatitis) Itching, paw licking, red skin, ear infections Diet trial, antihistamines, Apoquel, medicated shampoo
Intervertebral disc disease (IVDD) Back pain, weakness, paralysis Avoid jumping from heights, maintain healthy weight
Heatstroke Collapse, red gums, excessive drooling Cool immediately, emergency vet visit
Hip dysplasia Reluctance to exercise, stiffness, bunny hopping gait Joint supplements, weight management, physiotherapy
Conjunctivitis / cherry eye Red, weeping eyes, visible pink mass in corner of eye Vet treatment, possible surgical correction
Fold dermatitis Smell, discharge, redness in facial or tail folds Regular cleaning, veterinary antifungal cream
Pet insurance: Given the range of health conditions Frenchies are prone to, pet insurance is strongly recommended for NZ owners. Surgical corrections for BOAS, IVDD, and other conditions can run to several thousand dollars. Compare policies early — most insurers require enrolment before conditions are diagnosed to cover them.

7. Socialisation and Temperament

French Bulldogs are naturally social, people-oriented dogs. They bond strongly with their families and generally get along well with children and other pets when properly introduced. However, they can develop separation anxiety if left alone for long periods, particularly if they were raised with constant companionship.

Socialisation Tips

  • Expose puppies to a wide range of people, environments, sounds, and animals between 3 and 14 weeks of age — this critical window shapes their lifelong temperament
  • Puppy classes are excellent for socialisation and basic obedience — choose one held indoors during warm weather
  • Frenchies can be stubborn; short, positive training sessions with high-value rewards work far better than repetitive drilling
  • Build tolerance to alone time gradually from puppyhood to prevent separation anxiety developing

Frenchies and Other Pets

Most French Bulldogs coexist happily with cats and other dogs, particularly when raised together. Their moderate prey drive and relaxed energy make them less likely to chase small animals than higher-drive breeds. Introductions should always be calm and controlled, and early positive experiences set the tone for lifelong harmony.

8. Choosing the Right Pet Sitter for Your French Bulldog

Finding a pet sitter for a French Bulldog requires more care than it might for other breeds. Not all sitters are familiar with the specific needs and risks of brachycephalic dogs. The wrong approach to exercise, heat, or feeding can result in a medical emergency.

What to Look for in a Frenchie Sitter

  • Breed awareness — the sitter should know that Frenchies cannot handle heat, long walks, or exercise in warm conditions
  • Emergency preparedness — they should know the signs of heatstroke and have a plan to act quickly
  • Experience with short-nosed breeds — previous experience with Frenchies, Pugs, English Bulldogs, or similar breeds is a strong indicator of suitability
  • Air-conditioned environment — if your Frenchie will spend any time at the sitter’s home, confirm they have air conditioning or a reliably cool space
  • No group dog parks — Frenchies can be bullied by larger, more energetic dogs and should not be taken to high-energy group settings

Questions to Ask a Potential Sitter

  1. Have you cared for French Bulldogs or other flat-faced breeds before?
  2. What would you do if my dog seemed to be struggling to breathe or showed signs of overheating?
  3. Do you have air conditioning at your home?
  4. How do you manage walks in warm weather?
  5. Are you comfortable with their fold cleaning routine?

9. Frenchie Ownership Costs in New Zealand

French Bulldogs are one of the more expensive breeds to own in New Zealand, both upfront and in ongoing costs. Setting realistic expectations helps owners plan and avoid financial stress if health issues arise.

Final Thoughts

French Bulldogs are deeply rewarding companions. Their personalities — playful, loving, stubborn in the best way, and endlessly funny — make them perfect for families, singles, apartment dwellers, and retirees alike. But they are also a breed that asks their owners to be informed, proactive, and attentive.

Understanding their breathing limitations, respecting their heat sensitivity, maintaining their skin folds, and managing their weight are the pillars of excellent Frenchie care. Do these things well, and you’ll have a healthy, happy companion who will bring joy to your household for a decade or more.

And when you need a break or have to travel, trust your Frenchie only with someone who truly understands what makes this breed special — and what makes them vulnerable.
