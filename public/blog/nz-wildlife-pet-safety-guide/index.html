NZ Wildlife & Pet Safety: Protecting Your Pet From Native Encounters

23 May 2026 | ZiggySitters Team

New Zealand is one of the most wildlife-rich countries on Earth. From the iconic kiwi to the more common possum, hedgehog, and native bird species, our backyards are full of creatures that your dog or cat will inevitably encounter. But those encounters are not always harmless. Understanding how New Zealand wildlife interacts with pets and knowing how to keep both your pet and native species safe is a responsibility every Kiwi pet owner shares.

Why Wildlife Awareness Matters for NZ Pet Owners

New Zealand has no native land mammals except for two species of bat. Every other mammal, including possums, hedgehogs, rats, mice, stoats, ferrets, and even our domestic cats and dogs, was introduced by humans. This means native wildlife evolved without mammalian predators, making them incredibly vulnerable. At the same time, encounters with wildlife can harm your pet too, through disease, injury, or toxic substances.

Responsible pet ownership in New Zealand means understanding both sides of this equation: protecting your pet and protecting our unique native species from the animals we love.

Common NZ Wildlife Encounters and Risks

Kiwi

The kiwi is New Zealand's most treasured native bird and also one of the most vulnerable. Kiwi are ground-dwelling, flightless, and have a strong scent that makes them easy for dogs to track. Even well-behaved dogs can kill a kiwi in seconds, and a single dog can wipe out an entire local kiwi population. If you live in or visit areas where kiwi are present, including Northland, the Coromandel, and parts of the Bay of Plenty, keeping your dog on a lead is not just good practice, it is the law in some areas. Dogs caught killing kiwi can be destroyed, and owners can face prosecution and fines up to $20,000.

If you are visiting bush areas with your dog, check with the Department of Conservation (DOC) first. Many reserves and tracks prohibit dogs entirely, and those that allow them often require leads. Possums

The brushtail possum is one of New Zealand's most destructive introduced pests. They carry bovine tuberculosis, which can be transmitted to cattle and potentially to other mammals. If your dog tangles with a possum, there is a risk of TB exposure, bite wounds that can become infected, and parasites like fleas and mites. Possums also carry a risk of leptospirosis.

Cats that hunt possums are at risk of similar diseases, plus fighting injuries. Possums are surprisingly aggressive when cornered and have sharp claws and teeth. If you find a possum in your yard, do not let your pet approach it. Secure your pet indoors and contact a local pest control service if possums are becoming a regular problem.

Hedgehogs

Hedgehogs might seem cute and harmless, but they are an introduced pest in New Zealand and pose real risks to pets. Dogs that bite or chew on hedgehogs can get quills lodged in their mouth, gums, and throat, requiring veterinary attention. Hedgehogs also carry salmonella and can transmit ringworm and other fungal infections. They predate on native insects, snails, and ground-nesting birds, making them a conservation threat as well.

If your dog is fascinated by hedgehogs, discourage the behaviour firmly. A hedgehog encounter might seem like harmless fun, but the vet bill for quill removal tells a different story. A knowledgeable pet sitter can help manage these encounters if your dog tends to investigate the garden at night.

Native Birds: Tui, Kereru, Fantails and More

New Zealand's native birds are a joy to have in the garden, but cats are their biggest predator. A single domestic cat can kill hundreds of native birds in its lifetime. Studies in New Zealand have shown that even well-fed cats hunt birds instinctively, and bells on collars reduce hunting success by only about a third.

For dog owners, the risk is different. Dogs that chase birds can disturb nesting kereru or flush ground-dwelling species. The New Zealand pigeon, or kereru, is particularly vulnerable during breeding season when it sits on low branches and can be startled into injury by an enthusiastic dog.

Stoats, Ferrets, and Weasels

These mustelids are serious predators of native wildlife and can also pose risks to small pets. Stoats and ferrets will attack rabbits, guinea pigs, and even small cats and dogs. If you keep small pets outdoors, ensure their enclosures are mustelid-proof with mesh small enough to prevent entry and buried into the ground to prevent digging.

Seal and Sea Lion Encounters

If you walk your dog on New Zealand beaches, you may encounter seals or sea lions. These animals can be aggressive when approached, especially during breeding season, and a seal bite or slap can cause serious injury to a dog. DOC recommends keeping dogs at least 20 metres away from any marine mammal. This applies on beaches throughout New Zealand, particularly in Southland, Otago, and the Catlins where sea lions are common.

Practical Steps to Protect Your Pet and Wildlife

1. Keep Dogs on Lead in Bush and Wildlife Areas

This is the single most important thing you can do. Even if your dog has perfect recall, instinct can override training in a heartbeat when a kiwi, weka, or possum crosses the path. Check DOC signage before entering any reserve or track and respect all dog restrictions.

2. Manage Your Cat's Hunting Behaviour

Keep your cat indoors at night, when native birds and insects are most vulnerable. Consider a cat enclosure or "catio" that allows outdoor access without hunting opportunities. Keep cats inside during dawn and dusk when bird activity peaks. If you have a ZiggySitters cat sitter, make sure they follow these same guidelines while you are away.

3. Secure Your Property

Fencing helps keep wildlife out and pets in. This reduces encounters with possums, hedgehogs, and stray animals. Remove food sources that attract wildlife, including open compost bins, fallen fruit, and pet food left outside overnight.

4. Supervise Outdoor Time

Do not leave your dog unattended in the yard at night, especially in areas with possum and hedgehog activity. For cats, supervised outdoor time is much safer than free roaming. A pet sitter who stays in your home can provide this supervision when you are away.

5. Vaccinate and Prevent Parasites

Wildlife encounters increase the risk of disease transmission. Ensure your pet is up to date on vaccinations, including leptospirosis for dogs in rural areas. Use regular flea, tick, and worming treatments. Discuss wildlife-specific risks with your vet, especially if you live near bush or farmland.

What to Do If Your Pet Encounters Wildlife

Call your dog back immediately and secure them. Do not let them investigate, chase, or interact with any wild animal. If a physical encounter has already occurred, check your pet for injuries, bites, or quills. Even small wounds from possums or hedgehogs can become infected quickly. Contact your vet if there are any puncture wounds, or if your pet seems lethargic or off-colour in the days following an encounter.

For native bird incidents, contact your local DOC office or the Department of Conservation hotline. Report kiwi encounters immediately, even if your dog just found one, as tracking dogs can help monitor at-risk populations.

Seasonal Wildlife Considerations

Wildlife activity in New Zealand varies through the year. Spring (September to November) is breeding season for many native birds, meaning kereru and tui are more active and vulnerable. Summer brings more possum and hedgehog activity as nights warm up. Autumn is the season for fawning and bird migration, while winter sees more wildlife seeking shelter near human habitation, increasing the chance of encounters in your own backyard.

Adjust your pet management accordingly. More supervision in spring and summer, more vigilance about what might be sheltering under your deck in winter. Your vet or local council can provide area-specific advice about seasonal wildlife patterns.

The Bigger Picture

New Zealand's wildlife is unlike anywhere else on the planet. The choices we make as pet owners directly affect the survival of species that exist nowhere else. Keeping your dog on a lead in kiwi country, keeping your cat in at night, and being mindful of wildlife on beaches and in the bush are small actions with enormous consequences. Combined with good pet care and responsible management, these actions ensure that our pets and our native species can coexist safely.
