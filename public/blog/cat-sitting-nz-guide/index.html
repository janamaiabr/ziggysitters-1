Why Cat Sitting Beats Boarding for Most Cats

Unlike dogs, most cats are deeply territorial animals. Their sense of security is tied to their environment, not their companions. Removing a cat from their home and placing them in a boarding cattery introduces multiple stressors at once: unfamiliar smells, unfamiliar sounds, the presence of other animals, and the complete absence of their established territory. For many cats, especially anxious or older animals, this combination can trigger serious behavioural and health problems, including appetite loss, upper respiratory infections, and stress-induced illness.

In-home cat sitting, where a sitter visits your home one or more times daily, keeps your cat in their familiar environment. Your cat sleeps in their own bed, scratches their own post, and patrols their usual territory. The only variable is that a friendly human shows up to top up the food bowl, refresh the water, clean the litter tray, and spend some quality time. For most cats, this is far less disruptive than any boarding alternative.

The exception is cats that are genuinely social and actively seek human companionship throughout the day. These cats, rare though they are in the feline world, may actually fare better in an environment where a sitter is present full-time rather than popping in twice daily. Knowing your individual cat is the starting point for choosing the right care arrangement.

What Cat Sitting Services Typically Cover

Most cat sitters in New Zealand offer either drop-in visits or full in-home pet sitting where they stay at your property overnight. Drop-in visits typically last 30 to 60 minutes and include feeding, water refresh, litter tray cleaning, brief play or grooming, and a welfare check to make sure your cat is behaving normally. Some sitters offer two visits daily; others can accommodate three for cats that need more attention or medication monitoring.

Full in-home pet sitting means the sitter stays at your home, giving your cat consistent human presence throughout the day and overnight. This option suits cats that are unwell, elderly, on complex medication schedules, or simply very attached to human company. It also provides the bonus of someone present in your home while you are away, which many homeowners appreciate for security reasons.

Additional services vary by sitter but may include brushing and grooming, administering oral or injectable medications, photographing your cat and sending daily updates, collecting mail, watering plants, and putting bins out. Discuss your specific needs before booking to ensure your sitter is comfortable with everything required.

How Much Does Cat Sitting Cost in New Zealand?

Cat sitting costs in NZ vary depending on location, frequency of visits, duration, and whether overnight care is involved. As a general guide, drop-in visits typically range from $20 to $40 per visit in major centres like Auckland and Wellington, with slightly lower rates in smaller cities and regional towns. Overnight in-home pet sitting generally costs between $60 and $120 per night depending on the number of cats, additional services required, and the sitter's experience level.

Public holidays attract surcharges from most sitters, typically 20 to 50 percent above standard rates. If you are planning to travel over Christmas, Easter, or school holiday periods, budget accordingly and book well in advance. These periods see heavy demand for cat sitters across all NZ cities, and leaving your search too late often means limited availability and less choice.

Cat sitting is generally more affordable than cattery boarding when you factor in the reduced stress on your cat and the additional home-monitoring benefits. Compare the full cost including the wellbeing value, not just the dollar figure per night.

What to Look for in a Cat Sitter

Not every pet sitter who lists cats as a species they work with has genuine experience with feline behaviour and needs. Cats communicate very differently from dogs, and a sitter who is mainly a dog person may misread feline body language, push interaction too hard, or fail to notice subtle signs of illness or stress. When interviewing potential sitters, look for the following qualities.

Genuine enthusiasm for cats is the starting point. A good cat sitter will ask specific questions about your individual cat, their personality, their quirks, and their routine. They will want to know whether your cat is confident or shy, sociable or reclusive, and whether they have any health conditions requiring monitoring. Sitters who ask detailed questions before the meet-and-greet demonstrate the kind of attentiveness that translates into quality care.

Experience with cats of similar temperament to your own is valuable. If your cat is elderly and on medication, seek out a sitter who has experience with senior cats and medication administration. If your cat is a highly active young breed like a Bengal or an Abyssinian, look for someone with energy to match who understands the enrichment needs of intelligent, high-drive cats. Specific experience matters more than general pet care history.

Solid communication habits are non-negotiable. Your sitter should commit to sending daily updates during your absence, ideally with photos. NZ cat owners abroad want to know their cat is eating, using the litter tray normally, and not hiding under the bed for the entire duration of the visit. A sitter who communicates proactively provides real peace of mind.

The Meet-and-Greet: Why It Matters for Cats

Always arrange a meet-and-greet before your first booking. This in-person introduction serves multiple purposes. It gives your sitter a chance to locate everything they will need, including the food storage, the litter supplies, the carrier location, and your cat's preferred hiding spots. It gives you a chance to observe how your sitter interacts with your cat, and it gives your cat an opportunity to register the sitter as a non-threatening presence in their territory before you disappear.

Cats notice patterns. A sitter who has visited the house once before you leave is significantly less alarming than a complete stranger who appears after your cat has been alone for a day. For shy or anxious cats, multiple introductory visits before your departure can make a meaningful difference to how well they cope during the sit.

Use the meet-and-greet to go through your care sheet together. Walk the sitter through feeding locations, portion sizes, and any dietary restrictions. Show them how to clean the litter tray and where replacement litter is stored. Demonstrate any medication administration if required. The more hands-on this session is, the more confident your sitter will feel when they arrive for their first solo visit.

Creating an Effective Cat Care Sheet

A well-prepared care sheet is your cat sitter's primary reference document. Include your cat's full name and any nicknames they respond to, their age, breed, and any known health conditions. List all medications with exact dosages, timing, and administration instructions. Note feeding schedule, portion sizes, and the specific food your cat eats, since switching brands mid-sit can cause digestive upset in sensitive cats.

Describe your cat's typical behaviour so the sitter knows what is normal. If your cat usually hides when strangers first arrive but comes out within an hour, note that. If your cat vocalises a lot around feeding time but is otherwise quiet, mention it. Sitters who understand your cat's baseline are far better positioned to notice genuine problems like illness or injury.

Include your regular vet's name, address, and phone number, plus the closest after-hours veterinary clinic. Authorise your sitter in writing to seek emergency veterinary care if needed, and specify whether you want to be contacted before treatment or whether they should proceed and inform you afterwards. Clear guidance on this point prevents delays in emergencies.

Special Considerations for Multi-Cat Households

Households with multiple cats require a sitter who understands group dynamics. If your cats have an established hierarchy, stress caused by owner absence can disrupt those dynamics temporarily, leading to increased tension or resource guarding around food, water, and litter trays. A good multi-cat sitter ensures adequate resources are available, positions feeding stations to reduce competition, and monitors group interactions during visits.

If one cat in your household has a health condition or special dietary needs, be explicit with your sitter about preventing the other cats from accessing prescription food. Automatic feeders programmed to individual cats can help, but your sitter should understand the setup and how to troubleshoot it if something goes wrong.

For bonded pairs that have never been separated, in-home sitting is particularly important. Sending one cat to a cattery while the other stays home, or separating bonded cats unnecessarily, adds an additional layer of stress that in-home care entirely avoids.

Booking Tips for Peak Periods

Cat sitting demand in New Zealand spikes predictably around school holidays, Christmas, Easter, and long weekends. Auckland Anniversary Weekend, Wellington Anniversary Weekend, and Waitangi Day all see elevated booking volumes in their respective regions. If you know your travel dates in advance, book your cat sitter as early as possible. Many experienced sitters fill their calendars weeks or even months ahead during peak periods.

Building an ongoing relationship with a reliable cat sitter pays dividends over time. A sitter who knows your cat already requires far less briefing for repeat sits and can often accommodate last-minute bookings when your usual availability window is not possible. Think of finding your cat sitter as an ongoing relationship, not a one-off transaction.

Your cat deserves care that respects their nature: calm, consistent, and on their own terms. Finding the right cat sitter in New Zealand is one of the best things you can do for their wellbeing every time you need to be away.
