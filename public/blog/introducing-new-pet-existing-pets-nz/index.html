Introducing a New Pet to Your Existing Pets in New Zealand

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  March 29, 2026  •  9 min read

Bringing a new dog or cat into a home that already has resident pets is one of the most exciting — and potentially stressful — moments in a pet owner's life. Done well, it sets the foundation for a lifelong bond. Done poorly, it can create anxiety, conflict, and a home where animals are competing rather than coexisting. This guide walks you through every stage of the introduction process, from the day before your new pet arrives to the weeks that follow.

Why Introductions Matter So Much

Animals are territorial by nature. Your existing pet — whether a dog, cat, or both — has established this home as their space. Every corner carries their scent, every routine reinforces their sense of security. A new animal entering that world does not just disrupt the peace; it signals a potential threat to resources, attention, and safety.

The good news is that most animals, when given time and a structured introduction process, will adapt. What they cannot do is adapt instantly. The single biggest mistake New Zealand pet owners make when adding a second animal is moving too fast — expecting a same-day friendship, skipping the gradual steps, and being surprised when conflict follows.

A slow, patient introduction is not optional. It is the foundation everything else rests on.

Before Your New Pet Arrives: Prepare the Environment

The work begins before your new pet sets foot in the house.

Create Separate Spaces

Your new pet needs their own safe zone — a room or area that is theirs alone for the first few days. This should contain their bed, food and water bowls, litter tray if applicable, and ideally a worn item of your clothing for comfort. Keep this space separate from your existing pet's usual areas.

Your resident pet should not lose access to their favourite spaces during this period. The goal is addition, not displacement.

Separate Feeding Stations

Food is one of the most common triggers for inter-pet conflict. Before your new pet arrives, establish where each animal will eat and ensure these spaces are not within sight of each other. Feeding time anxiety can undermine an otherwise smooth introduction, and it is far easier to set this up from day one than to unpick it later.

Baby Gates and Crates

Baby gates are one of the most useful tools in a multi-pet household introduction. They allow animals to see and smell each other through a barrier without direct contact — which is exactly what the early stages of introduction require. If you do not already have one, invest in a tall, solid-framed gate before your new pet arrives.

💡 NZ Tip: Bunnings, Mitre 10, and most major NZ pet stores stock pet gates at a range of price points. A basic model is perfectly adequate for introductions — you do not need anything elaborate.

Introducing a New Dog to Your Existing Dog

Step 1 — Days Before Arrival

Scent Introduction

Before your new dog comes home, bring something that carries their scent — a blanket, towel, or soft toy from the breeder, foster home, or shelter — and let your resident dog investigate it at their own pace. Place it somewhere neutral, not in their bed or favourite spot. This gives your existing dog advance notice that a new animal is coming, which reduces the shock of the first physical meeting.

Step 2 — Day of Arrival

First Meeting on Neutral Territory

The first physical meeting should never happen inside your home. The home is your resident dog's territory, and introducing a stranger directly into that space triggers defensive behaviour.

Instead, arrange to meet on neutral ground — a park, quiet street, or beach neither dog has a strong connection to. Have two adults present so each dog has their own handler. Keep both dogs on leads, but keep the leads loose — tension in a lead transmits directly to a dog and heightens anxiety.

Let them approach each other sideways or in a curved arc rather than head-on. Head-on approaches are challenging in canine body language; a curved approach is friendlier. Allow a brief sniff, then redirect attention with forward movement — walk in parallel for five to ten minutes before any direct interaction. Parallel walking signals cooperation rather than confrontation.

Step 3 — First Hours at Home

Controlled Entry and Separate Spaces

When you bring the new dog home, take your resident dog for an extra walk first if possible — a tired dog is a calmer dog. Have the new dog enter the home before your resident dog if you can manage it, so they are not walking into an occupied space.

For the first day or two, use baby gates or separate rooms to keep the dogs apart when unsupervised. Supervised together-time should be brief and positive — short sessions with plenty of calm praise and treats for non-reactive behaviour, followed by separation.

Step 4 — Weeks One and Two

Gradual Increase in Shared Time

Gradually extend the time the dogs spend together, always with supervision. Watch for relaxed body language on both sides — loose tails, soft eyes, play bows. Signs of tension to watch for include stiff posture, hard staring, raised hackles, and low growling. If you see these signs, calmly separate the dogs and reduce the duration of subsequent sessions before building back up.

Do not punish growling. A growl is communication — it is your dog saying “I am uncomfortable.” Punishing it suppresses the warning signal without addressing the underlying discomfort, and can lead to escalated conflict without warning.

Introducing a New Cat to Your Existing Cat

Cats are highly territorial and typically take longer than dogs to accept a new feline household member. Weeks-long introductions are normal; expecting overnight acceptance is not realistic.

The Separate Room Phase (Days 1–7)

Your new cat should spend the first several days entirely in their separate room. This serves two purposes: it gives the new cat time to decompress from the stress of moving, and it gives your resident cat time to process the new scent through the closed door without direct confrontation.

Feed both cats near the door from their respective sides — this creates a positive association (food = good) with the smell of the other cat.

Scent Swapping (Days 3–7)

Once both cats are eating confidently near the door, begin swapping bedding between them so each cat sleeps on the other's scent. You can also rub a clean cloth on one cat's cheeks (where their scent glands are) and let the other cat investigate it.

Visual Introduction (Week 2)

Once both cats seem calm at the closed door — not hissing, not refusing to eat — crack the door slightly or use a baby gate so they can see each other without direct contact. Keep these visual sessions short and positive. Never force proximity.

Supervised Shared Space (Week 2 Onwards)

When both cats appear genuinely relaxed during visual contact, allow brief supervised meetings in a neutral shared space. Ensure there are multiple escape routes and elevated resting spots — cats feel safer when they can retreat vertically. Never corner a cat during introductions.

⚠️ Hissing and Growling Is Normal: Some hissing during early cat-to-cat introductions is expected and does not mean the introduction has failed. Sustained, escalating aggression or either cat refusing to eat are the real warning signs. If those occur, go back to the previous stage and slow down.

Introducing a Dog and Cat to Each Other

Dog-cat introductions require careful management of energy levels and escape routes.

Stage What to Do How Long
Scent only Swap bedding through closed door Days 1–3
Visual, cat protected Dog on lead in other room; cat can observe from safe height Days 3–7
Shared space, dog on lead Dog on lead and focused on you; cat free to approach or leave Week 2
Supervised off-lead Dog training solid; cat has high escape routes available Week 3+
Unsupervised access Only when both are consistently calm around each other Weeks 4–8+

The critical rule for dog-cat introductions is that the cat must always have the option to leave. Install a cat flap in a door to a dog-free room, or create elevated safe zones the cat can access but the dog cannot. A cat that feels cornered will either panic or escalate to aggression.

Your dog's baseline prey drive matters enormously here. A dog with a strong instinct to chase will need more time, more lead-work, and more careful management than a naturally mellow dog. Be honest about your dog's temperament and adjust your timeline accordingly.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Rushing the timeline. The most common introduction mistake in New Zealand households. There is no “fast track” for animals adjusting to each other. Pushing too fast creates setbacks that take weeks to undo.
  • Leaving them alone too soon. Unsupervised time between pets who are still in the introduction phase can result in conflict that sets back the entire process. Separate when you cannot watch.
  • Neglecting the resident pet. Your existing pet is experiencing a significant disruption. Maintain their routines, give them dedicated one-on-one time, and ensure they do not feel displaced.
  • Punishing negative interactions. This increases anxiety around the other animal rather than reducing it. Redirect and separate instead.
  • Expecting immediate friendship. Tolerance is the goal first, friendship comes later — and sometimes not at all. Some animals co-exist peaceably without ever becoming close companions, and that is completely fine.

When to Seek Professional Help

Most pet introductions in New Zealand go smoothly with patience and a structured approach. Some do not. Signs that you should consult a professional behaviourist or vet include:

  • Either animal refusing to eat for more than two days
  • Escalating aggression despite slowing down the introduction timeline
  • Physical injuries from altercations
  • Severe, ongoing anxiety in either animal (hiding completely, not using the litter tray, unable to settle)
  • A complete breakdown of relationship after initial acceptance

The SPCA New Zealand, veterinary behaviourists, and certified applied animal behaviourists operate across the country and can provide professional guidance tailored to your specific situation.

How a Pet Sitter Can Help During the Transition

The introduction period is often the most demanding time in a new multi-pet household. You need to be present, calm, and attentive — which is difficult if you also have work, family, and everything else to manage.

An experienced pet sitter can provide invaluable support during this period. They can supervise the pets during the day when you are at work, manage separate feeding routines, and provide the calm, consistent presence that both animals need. An experienced sitter who understands animal behaviour can also provide an objective assessment of how the introduction is progressing — something that is difficult to judge when you are emotionally invested in both animals.

New Pet Introduction Checklist
  • Separate spaces set up before new pet arrives
  • Scent introduction done (bedding or cloth swap)
  • Baby gate or separation door in place
  • Separate feeding stations established
  • First dog-dog meeting on neutral territory
  • Resident pet's routine maintained throughout
  • No unsupervised together-time in first two weeks
  • Vet or SPCA contact handy if escalation occurs

The Long View: Building a Harmonious Multi-Pet Household

The introduction phase has an end date. Most multi-pet households in New Zealand settle into their new normal within six to twelve weeks, even if the early stages were bumpy. What you invest in a patient, structured introduction pays dividends in the form of a calmer, more harmonious household for the years ahead.

The animals who seem most incompatible at first are often the ones who eventually become inseparable. Give them time, space, and a calm framework, and you may be surprised at what develops.
