Persian Cat Care Guide NZ 2026

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  July 2, 2026  •  12 min read

Persian cats are one of the most recognisable and beloved breeds in New Zealand. Their luxurious coats, calm demeanour, and expressive flat faces make them irresistible companions. But that beauty comes with responsibility. This guide covers everything NZ Persian cat owners need to know about grooming, health, diet, indoor living, and finding a sitter who truly understands the breed.

Why Persian Cats Are Special in New Zealand

Persians rank among the top ten pedigree breeds registered with the New Zealand Cat Fancy (NZCF). Their gentle, laid-back temperament suits Kiwi lifestyles well, but their brachycephalic (flat-faced) anatomy and long coats mean they need significantly more care than the average moggie. Understanding these needs before you bring a Persian home, or if you already share your life with one, makes all the difference between a thriving cat and recurring vet visits.

Grooming: The Non-Negotiable Daily Commitment

Let us be direct: if you are not prepared to groom a Persian cat daily, this is not the breed for you. That magnificent coat does not maintain itself.

Daily Brushing

Persian coats can reach 15 centimetres in length. Without daily brushing, mats form quickly, especially under the arms, behind the ears, and around the hindquarters. Use a wide-toothed metal comb first to work through tangles, then follow with a slicker brush for smoothness. A pin brush is ideal for the final polish.

Ziggy Tip: Start grooming sessions when your Persian is a kitten. Most adult Persians learn to tolerate and even enjoy daily brushing if it has always been part of their routine. Keep grooming sessions to 10-15 minutes and reward with a treat afterwards.

Bathing Schedule

Most Persian owners bathe their cats every 4 to 6 weeks. Use a cat-specific shampoo with conditioning properties. In New Zealand, where humidity can be high in summer, you may find bathing more frequently helps prevent greasy buildup along the coat. Always blow-dry on a cool or warm (never hot) setting to avoid skin irritation.

Eye Care

Persian cats produce excess tears due to their flat facial structure. The tear ducts often cannot drain efficiently, leading to staining beneath the eyes. Wipe the eye area daily with a soft, damp cloth or veterinary-grade eye wipe. Left uncleaned, tear stains can cause bacterial infections and skin dermatitis.

Ear Cleaning

Check ears weekly for wax buildup or signs of infection. Persians are prone to ear mites and yeast infections, particularly in humid NZ conditions. Use a vet-recommended ear cleaner and cotton pad, never a cotton bud which can damage the ear canal.

Common Health Issues in Persian Cats

Understanding breed-specific health concerns allows you to be proactive rather than reactive. Here are the conditions every NZ Persian owner should monitor.

Condition Signs to Watch For Prevention
Brachycephalic Obstructive Airway Syndrome (BOAS) Noisy breathing, snoring, exercise intolerance Keep weight in check, avoid heat stress, use a harness not a collar
Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD) Increased thirst, weight loss, lethargy Genetic screening before breeding, annual ultrasound from 1 year
Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA) Bumping into objects, dilated pupils, night blindness Genetic testing, regular vet eye exams
Dental Disease Bad breath, difficulty eating, drooling Daily tooth brushing, annual dental checks, veterinary cleanings
Dermatophytosis (Ringworm) Hair loss, scaly patches, itching Regular coat inspection, clean environment, prompt vet treatment
Feline Hyperthyroidism Weight loss despite appetite, hyperactivity, vomiting Annual bloodwork from age 7, T4 monitoring
Important: Always purchase Persian kittens from NZCF or catZ registers breeders who perform PKD and PRA DNA testing on breeding cats. A responsible breeder will show you the certificates. If they cannot, walk away.

Nutrition and Diet for Persian Cats

Persians are not particularly active cats. Their calm temperament means they are prone to weight gain if overfed. A measured, high-quality diet is essential.

Choosing the Right Food

  • Protein-first: Look for foods where a named meat (chicken, turkey, fish) is the first ingredient, not a grain or by-product.
  • Omega fatty acids: Essential for coat health. Foods enriched with omega-3 and omega-6 support that signature Persian sheen.
  • Dental-specific kibble: Because of their brachycephalic jaw structure, Persians often swallow kibble whole. Dental-shaped kibble (larger, textured) forces chewing and reduces plaque buildup.
  • Wet food inclusion: At least 50% of the diet should be wet food. Persians are notoriously poor drinkers, and wet food helps prevent urinary tract issues and kidney strain.

Feeding Schedule

Feed adult Persians twice daily rather than free-feeding. Measure portions according to your vet\'s recommendation based on weight and activity level. Treats should not exceed 10% of daily caloric intake.

Ziggy Tip: Persian cats can be fussy eaters. If you need to change food, transition gradually over 7-10 days by mixing increasing amounts of the new food with the old. Sudden changes can cause stomach upset and refusal to eat.

Indoor Living: The Only Responsible Choice

In New Zealand, keeping Persian cats indoors is strongly recommended for multiple reasons:

  • Predator vulnerability: Persians are slow, docile, and lack the agility to escape dogs or territorial neighbourhood cats.
  • Coat damage: Bush, gorse, and burrs common in NZ will destroy a Persian coat in a single outdoor excursion.
  • Native wildlife: All cats pose a risk to native birds and lizards. Keeping Persians indoors protects both your cat and New Zealand\'s precious wildlife.
  • Theft risk: Pedigree cats are targeted in New Zealand. An outdoor Persian is an easy target.
  • Sun exposure: Light-coloured Persians are susceptible to squamous cell carcinoma on the ears and nose, particularly in NZ\'s high-UV environment.

Creating an Enriching Indoor Environment

An indoor Persian still needs stimulation. Here is how to keep them happy:

  • Vertical space: Cat trees, wall shelves, and window perches. Persians love observing the world from height, even if they are not agile climbers. Choose sturdy, low-impact options with wide platforms.
  • Interactive play: 15 minutes twice daily with wand toys or laser pointers. Persians may seem lazy, but they enjoy short, gentle play sessions.
  • Puzzle feeders: Slow their eating and provide mental stimulation.
  • Cat-safe plants: Cat grass, spider plants, and catnip provide sensory enrichment.
  • Window access: A secure cat enclosure (catio) attached to a window gives fresh air and bird-watching without the dangers of free roaming. See our catio guide for NZ cats for ideas.

Seasonal Care Considerations for NZ Persians

Summer (December-February)

  • Persians struggle with heat. Keep your home cool with fans or air conditioning.
  • Always have multiple water stations. Consider a cat water fountain to encourage drinking.
  • Trim the coat underneath (belly and hindquarters) to help with heat dissipation, but never shave a Persian completely as the coat provides UV protection.
  • Watch for signs of heat stress: panting, lethargy, and excessive grooming are red flags.

Winter (June-August)

  • Persians generally handle cold better than heat, but ensure warm sleeping spots away from draughts.
  • Indoor heating dries the air, which can cause dry skin and coat. Consider a humidifier.
  • Continue daily grooming. Winter coats can be even more prone to matting.

Finding the Right Pet Sitter for Your Persian

Leaving a Persian cat with a sitter requires more care than most breeds. Not just any pet sitter will do. Here is what to look for and prepare.

What Your Sitter Needs to Know

  • Daily grooming routine and which brush to use
  • Eye-wiping technique and frequency
  • Exact food portions and schedule (no free-feeding)
  • Signs of breathing difficulty (wheezing, open-mouth breathing)
  • Normal tear production so they can spot changes
  • Vet contact details and emergency after-hours clinic location
  • Where you keep the tear stain remover, ear cleaner, and flea treatment
  • Any behavioural quirks (food aggression, hiding patterns, favourite spots)

Questions to Ask a Potential Sitter

  • Have you cared for brachycephalic breeds before? (This is essential.)
  • Are you comfortable with daily grooming and eye cleaning?
  • Do you know the signs of respiratory distress in flat-faced cats?
  • Can you follow a strict feeding schedule rather than free-feeding?
  • Will you send daily photo and video updates?

Veterinary Care Checklist

Regular vet visits are critical for Persians. Here is a recommended schedule:

Age Vet Visit Frequency Key Screenings
Kitten (0-12 months) Every 3-4 months Vaccinations, deworming, PKD ultrasound, FIV/FeLV test
Adult (1-7 years) Annually Dental check, weight, blood panel, eye exam, PKD monitoring
Senior (7+ years) Every 6 months Blood panel including T4 (thyroid), kidney function, blood pressure, dental

Grooming Supplies Checklist for NZ Persian Owners

  • Wide-toothed metal comb
  • Slicker brush (soft to medium)
  • Pin brush for finishing
  • Dematting tool (for emergency tangles)
  • Cat-specific shampoo and conditioner
  • Veterinary eye wipes or saline solution
  • Ear cleaner (vet-recommended)
  • Pet-safe blow dryer (cool/warm setting)
  • Nail clippers (scissor type, not guillotine)
  • Dental toothbrush and cat-specific toothpaste

Choosing a Persian Kitten in New Zealand

If you are considering adding a Persian to your family, do it right:

  • Use NZCF or catZ registered breeders only. These breeders must follow code of ethics and health testing requirements.
  • Ask for PKD and PRA certificates for both parents. A responsible breeder will provide these without hesitation.
  • Visit the cattery. Check that kittens are raised indoors, are socialised, and that the environment is clean.
  • Expect to wait. Ethical breeders often have waitlists. A Persian kitten available immediately should raise questions.
  • Budget $1,500 to $3,000 for a well-bred Persian kitten from health-tested parents in New Zealand.
  • Consider adoption. Persian Rescue NZ and breed-specific rescue groups occasionally have Persians needing homes. These cats often come vet-checked and desexed.

Key Takeaways

  • Persian cats require daily grooming and daily eye cleaning to stay healthy and comfortable.
  • Brachycephalic anatomy means Persians are vulnerable to breathing difficulties and heat stress, especially in NZ summers.
  • Indoor living is the only safe option for Persians in New Zealand, both for their safety and for native wildlife.
  • Always buy from NZCF or catZ registered breeders who provide PKD and PRA health certificates.
  • When choosing a pet sitter, ensure they have experience with brachycephalic breeds and understand daily grooming needs.
  • Budget $2,750 to $3,850 annually for proper Persian cat care in New Zealand.
