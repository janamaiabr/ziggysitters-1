Pet Sitting While Hospitalised in NZ: Your Emergency Pet Care Guide

Published 22 June 2026 · 8 min read · ZiggySitters Team

Blog › Emergency Pet Sitting While Hospitalised

Nobody plans for a hospital stay. But if you share your life with a dog, cat, or other pet in New Zealand, an unexpected medical emergency can leave you with two worries: your own health and who will look after your animals. This guide walks through everything you need to know about arranging emergency pet sitting when you or a loved one is hospitalised in NZ, from creating a backup plan before you need it to finding a sitter at short notice.

Why Emergency Pet Care Planning Matters

Over 60% of New Zealand households include at least one pet. Yet most Kiwis do not have a written plan for what happens to their animals if they are suddenly unable to care for them. Unlike childcare, there is no universal safety net. If you are rushed to hospital, your pet depends entirely on whatever arrangements you made beforehand, or on the quick thinking of family and friends.

The best time to sort emergency pet care is before you need it. A prepared pet owner with a simple backup plan can reduce stress for everyone, including the animal, who will already be coping with your absence.

Time-critical: If you are reading this during an active emergency, skip to Urgent Steps below. Otherwise, read through and set up your plan now.

Creating Your Pet Emergency Backup Plan

A pet emergency backup plan is a short document that tells anyone who needs to step in exactly what to do. Keep it somewhere easy to find, such as on your fridge, in your phone, and with a trusted neighbour.

What to Include

  • Your full name, address, and phone number
  • Pet names, species, breeds, ages, and descriptions
  • Microchip numbers and registration details (NZ Companion Animal Register)
  • Vet clinic name, address, and after-hours phone number
  • Feeding schedule, food brand, and portion sizes
  • Medication names, doses, and administration times
  • Behavioural notes (anxiety triggers, fear of men, escape artist tendencies)
  • Primary emergency contact (friend or family member who has a key)
  • Secondary emergency contact (neighbour or pet sitter)
  • Insurance details if your pet is insured
  • Authorisation for emergency veterinary treatment up to a stated amount
  • Your vet pre-authorisation signature on file
ZiggySitters Tip: Take a photo of your backup plan and save it in your phone under a contact called ICE Pet Care (ICE stands for In Case of Emergency). First responders often check ICE contacts.

Urgent Steps: You Need a Pet Sitter Now

If you or someone you know has been hospitalised and pets need immediate care, here is what to do:

  1. Call your emergency contact first. They should have a key and know the animals.
  2. Post on ZiggySitters for same-day or next-day emergency pet sitting. Many sitters offer last-minute bookings specifically for urgent situations.
  3. Contact your vet if you cannot find a sitter quickly. Some clinics board animals short-term or can recommend emergency foster carers.
  4. Check with the SPCA in your area. While they are not a boarding service, some branches offer emergency temporary fostering for genuine crises.
  5. Ask hospital social workers. Some NZ hospitals, particularly in larger cities like Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch, have connections with local pet care services for patients without family support.

In-Home vs Out-of-Home Pet Sitting During a Hospital Stay

In-Home Pet Sitting

Having a sitter come to your home is often the best option when you are hospitalised. Your pet stays in familiar surroundings, which reduces the stress of your absence. The sitter can also collect mail, water plants, and maintain the appearance of an occupied home, which is a security benefit.

For dogs, a live-in sitter maintains feeding, walking, and bedtime routines. For cats, even two daily visits (morning feed and evening check) can keep them content at home, though a live-in arrangement is preferable for anxious cats or those on medication.

Out-of-Home Boarding

If your pet needs medical monitoring, or if your home situation makes in-home sitting impractical, boarding at a vet clinic or commercial facility is the alternative. Vet clinics are the right choice for pets recovering from their own health issues or those requiring daily medication. Commercial kennels and catteries suit healthy animals but require current vaccination records, which can be a hurdle in an emergency.

Prepare ahead: Keep your pet\'s vaccination records in an easy-to-find folder at home. In an emergency, whoever collects your pet will need these for any boarding facility.

Special Considerations for Different Pets

Dogs

Dogs are the most straightforward to arrange emergency care for, simply because boarding and sitting options are plentiful. The main challenge is ensuring the sitter can handle your dog\'s exercise needs and any behavioural quirks. A high-energy border collie needs a very different sitter than a senior pug on heart medication. Include detailed walking and exercise notes in your backup plan.

Cats

Cats are often more stressed by environmental change than dogs. If at all possible, keep your cat at home with a visiting sitter rather than moving them to a cattery. Cats can develop stress-related conditions like feline lower urinary tract disease (FLUTD) or stop eating entirely when moved to an unfamiliar environment, which can become life-threatening within days.

Small Animals, Birds, and Reptiles

Rabbits, guinea pigs, birds, and reptiles need specialised care and are harder to board. Many standard pet sitters are not experienced with exotics. Your best bet is a vet clinic that treats exotic animals, or a sitter on ZiggySitters who lists small animal care in their profile. Include very specific care notes: temperature requirements for reptiles, dietary needs for birds, and handling instructions for small mammals.

Financial Considerations and Support

Emergency pet sitting costs can add up quickly, especially for extended hospital stays. Here are some ways NZ pet owners manage:

  • Pet insurance: Some comprehensive policies cover boarding fees if you are hospitalised. Check your policy wording or contact your insurer.
  • ACC: If your hospitalisation results from an accident covered by ACC, you may be able to claim pet care costs as part of your home support arrangement, though this is discretionary and not guaranteed.
  • Community support: Local Facebook groups (search for your suburb plus "pet community" or "neighbours") often mobilise quickly in genuine emergencies. People are remarkably generous when it comes to animals in need.
  • Workplace benefits: Some employer assistance programmes (EAPs) include emergency pet care referrals or financial support.

Preparing Your Home for an Emergency Sitter

If a sitter is coming to your home on short notice, these preparations will make their job much easier:

  • Leave a key in a lockbox or with a trusted neighbour (share the code with your emergency contact)
  • Write a one-page care sheet and stick it to the fridge
  • Ensure there is enough food, treats, and litter for at least two weeks
  • Leave cleaning supplies in an obvious spot (paper towels, enzyme cleaner, bin bags)
  • Make sure your pet\'s carrier is accessible and in good condition
  • Label all medications clearly with the pet\'s name and dosage
  • Leave your vet\'s phone number on the fridge alongside your emergency contact details
  • If your pet has a safe hiding spot, tell the sitter where it is so they do not panic if the animal disappears

What to Tell the Hospital

Hospital staff in New Zealand are increasingly aware that patients worry about pets left at home. When you are admitted, tell the admitting nurse that you have pets at home who need care. While hospitals cannot arrange pet sitting themselves, social workers can sometimes help coordinate community resources, especially for elderly patients or those without family nearby.

If you are being discharged and your pet care arrangement has fallen through, let the discharge team know. They may be able to connect you with local services or extend your stay slightly while arrangements are confirmed. This is not standard practice, but it has happened in genuine emergencies.

After the Hospital Stay: Reuniting with Your Pet

Coming home from hospital is emotional for you and your pet. Some animals react with over-the-top excitement, others become clingy, and a few may seem initially aloof. All of these are normal. Here is how to make the reunion smoother:

  1. Let your pet come to you. Do not force affection, especially if you are still recovering physically.
  2. Maintain the sitter\'s routine for a few days before gradually shifting back to your normal schedule.
  3. Watch for stress signs: decreased appetite, hiding, excessive vocalisation, or changes in toilet habits can indicate your pet found the experience difficult.
  4. Thank your sitter and ask for an honest report on how your pet coped. This feedback helps you refine your backup plan for next time.

Longer-Term Planning: What If You Cannot Return Home?

For some hospital stays, recovery means weeks or months away from home. In these situations, consider:

  • Extended in-home sitting: Many professional sitters offer discounted weekly or monthly rates. ZiggySitters lists sitters who specialise in longer assignments.
  • Fostering: Local rescue organisations sometimes offer temporary foster care for pets whose owners are in extended recovery. This is more common for dogs than cats.
  • Family relocation of the pet: If a family member can take the pet into their own home, this is often the least stressful option, provided the environment is suitable.

Key Takeaways

  • Create a pet emergency backup plan before you need it. Keep it on your fridge, in your phone, and with your emergency contact.
  • Professional pet sitters can usually respond within 24 hours, sometimes same day. ZiggySitters makes it easy to find one quickly.
  • In-home sitting is less stressful for most pets than boarding, especially for cats and small animals.
  • Keep vaccination records, medication lists, and vet contact details accessible at all times.
  • Tell hospital staff you have pets at home. They may be able to help coordinate care through social work services.
  • Financial support may be available through pet insurance, ACC, or community networks.

Being prepared for the unexpected is one of the most responsible things you can do as a pet owner in New Zealand. Take twenty minutes today to write your backup plan, save it on your phone, and share it with someone you trust. Your future self, and your pet, will thank you.
