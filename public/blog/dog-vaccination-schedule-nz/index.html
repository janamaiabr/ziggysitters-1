Dog Vaccination Schedule in New Zealand: Everything You Need to Know

Getting your dog vaccinated in New Zealand isn\'t just a box to tick at the vet. It\'s the difference between a healthy, happy companion and a preventable tragedy. Parvovirus still kills puppies in NZ. Kennel cough sweeps through doggy daycares every winter. And leptospirosis, while less common, is a genuine threat in rural and semi-rural areas.

Here\'s the full picture on dog vaccinations in New Zealand — what your dog needs, when they need it, and what it actually costs.

Core Vaccines: The Must-Haves

Every dog in New Zealand should receive the core vaccines. These protect against diseases that are either widespread, highly contagious, or genuinely life-threatening.

The core vaccine in NZ is typically a combination shot known as DHP (Distemper, Hepatitis, Parvovirus) or sometimes DHPPi (adding Parainfluenza). Here\'s what each one does:

DiseaseWhat It DoesHow Serious in NZ
Canine DistemperAttacks respiratory, gastrointestinal, and nervous systems. Often fatal.Rare now thanks to vaccines, but outbreaks still occur in unvaccinated populations.
Infectious Canine HepatitisViral infection of the liver, kidneys, and eyes. Can kill within hours in severe cases.Uncommon but always present in wild dog populations.
Canine ParvovirusDestroys the intestinal lining. Severe vomiting, bloody diarrhoea, dehydration. Fatal without intensive treatment.Still common in NZ. Outbreaks happen regularly, especially in Northland, parts of Auckland, and the Bay of Plenty.
ParainfluenzaViral component of kennel cough complex. Causes coughing, nasal discharge.Very common, especially where dogs mix (daycare, boarding, parks).
Parvovirus is the big one. Treatment can cost $2,000–$8,000 at a NZ vet clinic, and even with treatment, survival rates are roughly 85-90%. The vaccine costs about $40–60. Do the maths.

Non-Core Vaccines: Depends on Your Dog\'s Lifestyle

Non-core vaccines aren\'t required for every dog, but they\'re essential for some. Your vet will recommend them based on where your dog goes and what they\'re exposed to.

VaccineWho Needs ItWhy
Kennel Cough (Bordetella)Dogs going to daycare, boarding, grooming, dog parks, or any group settingHighly contagious. Most boarding facilities and daycares in NZ require it.
LeptospirosisDogs in rural areas, farms, near rivers/lakes, or that huntSpread through urine of infected animals (rats, possums, livestock). Can cause kidney and liver failure. Also transmissible to humans.
Canine CoronavirusNot routinely recommended in NZDifferent from SARS-CoV-2. Usually mild and self-limiting. Most vets skip this one.
Going to a boarding kennel or daycare? Nearly every facility in NZ requires proof of kennel cough vaccination (within the last 12 months) plus current core vaccines. Book your vaccines at least 7–10 days before boarding so immunity is active.

Puppy Vaccination Schedule

Puppies need a series of vaccinations because maternal antibodies (passed from mum) can interfere with the vaccine\'s effectiveness. The multi-shot schedule ensures coverage as those maternal antibodies fade.

Standard Puppy Schedule in NZ

AgeVaccineWhat It Covers
6–8 weeksFirst DHP (or DHPPi)Initial protection against core diseases. Not fully protective on its own.
10–12 weeksSecond DHP + Kennel Cough (if needed)Booster for core. Kennel cough can start here if puppy will be socialising.
14–16 weeksFinal puppy DHP + Kennel Cough boosterCompletes the puppy series. Full protection kicks in roughly 7–14 days after this final shot.

Critical rule: Your puppy is NOT fully protected until 7–14 days after the final vaccination at 14–16 weeks. Before that, avoid high-risk areas — dog parks, popular walking tracks, pet stores, and any area where unvaccinated dogs may have been.

What About Socialisation?

This is the tricky part. Puppies have a critical socialisation window (roughly 3–14 weeks) where positive experiences shape their adult temperament. But they\'re also most vulnerable to disease during this period.

The NZ Veterinary Association recommends:

  • Socialise puppies with known, vaccinated, healthy dogs in safe environments (your home, friends\' homes)
  • Carry your puppy in public places rather than putting them on the ground
  • Avoid dog parks, beaches with heavy dog traffic, and pet stores until fully vaccinated
  • Puppy preschool classes that require vaccination certificates are generally safe after the first vaccination

The risk of a poorly socialised dog (anxiety, reactivity, aggression) is real. But so is parvovirus. Balance both — don\'t lock your puppy away, but be smart about where they go and whose dogs they meet.

Adult Dog Booster Schedule

Once your dog has completed the puppy series, they need ongoing boosters to maintain immunity. The schedule has changed in recent years as research showed that some vaccines provide longer-lasting protection than previously thought.

VaccineBooster FrequencyNotes
DHP (Core)Annual for the first year after puppy series, then every 3 yearsMost NZ vet clinics now follow the 3-year core vaccine protocol. Confirm with your vet.
Kennel CoughAnnualImmunity wanes after about 12 months. Required yearly for boarding/daycare.
LeptospirosisAnnual (if applicable)Not a core vaccine, but if your dog needs it, it requires annual boosters.

Many vet clinics in NZ offer puppy packages that bundle vaccinations, microchipping, deworming, and flea treatment for a reduced total price. It\'s worth asking about — you can save $50–$100 compared to booking everything separately.

Some SPCAs and community vet clinics also run low-cost vaccination days, particularly in areas with high parvovirus rates. If cost is a barrier, check with your local SPCA.

What If Your Dog Is Behind on Vaccines?

Life happens. You move, you forget, the vet\'s closed for three weeks over Christmas. If your dog has lapsed on their vaccinations:

  1. Less than 18 months since last core vaccine: A single booster is usually sufficient. Your vet may do a titre test first to check existing immunity.
  2. More than 18 months, or never vaccinated: Your dog will likely need the full two-dose course (similar to the puppy schedule but in two shots, 2–4 weeks apart).
  3. Titre testing: Some NZ vets offer blood tests that measure antibody levels. If titres are adequate, you may be able to skip unnecessary boosters. Not cheap ($100–$200) but useful for dogs with vaccine sensitivities.

Common Questions About Dog Vaccinations in NZ

Can my dog go outside after the first vaccination?

Limited, controlled exposure is fine — carrying your puppy, letting them sniff around your own fenced yard. But avoid public dog areas until 7–14 days after the final puppy vaccination at 14–16 weeks.

Do indoor dogs need vaccinations?

Yes. Core diseases like parvovirus can survive in the environment for months. You can bring it home on your shoes. Indoor dogs still need the core vaccines.

Are there risks with vaccination?

Mild side effects are common — lethargy, mild swelling at the injection site, slight fever for 24 hours. Serious reactions (allergic response) are rare but can happen. Your vet will advise you on what to watch for.

Does my older dog still need boosters?

Yes, though the frequency may decrease. Senior dogs still need protection, especially if they interact with other dogs or visit boarding facilities. Talk to your vet about a risk-based approach.

What about titre testing instead of boosters?

Titre testing is available in NZ and can confirm whether your dog still has adequate immunity. It\'s a valid option for owners who prefer to minimise unnecessary vaccinations. However, most boarding facilities still require proof of vaccination regardless of titre results, so check before you travel.

The Bottom Line

Vaccinating your dog is one of the cheapest, most effective things you can do for their health. The core vaccines protect against diseases that are still present in New Zealand and can be devastating. Non-core vaccines depend on your dog\'s lifestyle — if they go to daycare, boarding, or rural areas, they probably need kennel cough and possibly leptospirosis.

Keep the schedule, keep the records, and keep your vet in the loop. Your dog will thank you for it — probably by licking your face immediately after the needle, because that\'s what dogs do.
