Pet Obesity and Weight Management in New Zealand: A Complete Guide

Published on 14 July 2026 | By ZiggySitters

Pet obesity is one of the most significant health challenges facing New Zealand companion animals, and most owners do not even realise their pet has a problem. Studies consistently show that around 40 percent of dogs and over 50 percent of cats in developed countries are overweight or obese, and New Zealand mirrors this trend. The consequences are serious: diabetes, joint disease, heart problems, respiratory distress, and a shortened lifespan. The good news is that pet obesity is almost entirely preventable and, with commitment, reversible. This guide covers how to tell if your pet is overweight, what to do about it, and how to maintain a healthy weight long term in the New Zealand context.

Is Your Pet Overweight? How to Tell

The hardest part about pet obesity is recognising it. Most owners think their pet looks fine, or even cute, at a weight that is actually unhealthy. Veterinary professionals use a Body Condition Score (BCS) system to assess whether a pet is underweight, ideal, overweight, or obese. The 9-point scale is the most commonly used in New Zealand veterinary practice.

Body Condition Scoring for Dogs

  • 1-3 (Underweight): Ribs, spine, and hip bones are visibly prominent. No palpable fat. Loss of muscle mass may be visible.
  • 4-5 (Ideal): Ribs are easily felt with light pressure but not visibly protruding. Viewed from above, the dog has a visible waist behind the ribs. From the side, the abdomen tucks up noticeably.
  • 6-7 (Overweight): Ribs are difficult to feel under a layer of fat. The waist is barely visible or absent. A slight abdominal fat pad may be present.
  • 8-9 (Obese): Ribs cannot be felt. No visible waist. Prominent fat deposits over the spine, tail base, and chest. Abdominal distension is obvious.

Body Condition Scoring for Cats

Cats follow the same 9-point scale but with feline-specific landmarks. At ideal weight (4-5), you should be able to feel the ribs with minimal pressure, see a visible waist from above, and notice a minimal abdominal fat pad. Cats at 7-9 have a rounded appearance, a fat pad that swings when they walk, and difficulty grooming themselves, particularly around the hindquarters and tail base.

The Home Test

You do not need to be a vet to do a basic body condition check at home. Try this:

  • Run your hands along your pet\'s sides. Can you feel the ribs easily without pressing hard? If not, there is too much fat.
  • Stand above your pet and look down. Can you see a waist behind the ribs, or does the body form a straight line or oval? A waist is a healthy sign.
  • View your pet from the side. Does the abdomen tuck up toward the hind legs, or does it hang low? A tuck is healthy; a sag is a sign of excess weight.
  • Check for a fat pad over the tail base. In cats, this is one of the first places excess fat accumulates.

If you are unsure, ask your vet at the next check-up. They can give you a precise BCS and tell you exactly how much weight your pet needs to lose, if any.

Why New Zealand Pets Are Getting Heavier

Several factors unique to the New Zealand lifestyle contribute to pet weight gain:

Indoor Living and Limited Exercise

New Zealand has one of the highest rates of cat ownership in the world, and a growing proportion of cats live entirely indoors, particularly in urban areas. Indoor cats burn far fewer calories than cats that roam and hunt. Many indoor cats eat the same amount as their outdoor counterparts would, creating a significant calorie surplus. Dogs face similar issues: busy owners working long hours means shorter walks, fewer beach runs, and more time on the couch.

Treat Culture and Overfeeding

We love spoiling our pets, and New Zealand pet owners are no exception. Treats, dental chews, table scraps, and titbits from the barbecue all add up. A single small dog treat can contain 50 to 100 kilojoules, and giving three or four a day can add 10 percent or more to a small dog\'s daily calorie requirement. For cats, a tablespoon of wet food on top of their regular meals can push them into calorie surplus over time.

Neutering and Hormonal Changes

Desexed animals have lower metabolic rates and reduced energy requirements compared to intact animals. The metabolic slowdown can be as much as 20 to 30 percent after desexing. Many owners continue feeding the same amount after their pet is desexed, not realising that their pet\'s calorie needs have dropped significantly. Your vet should discuss post-neutering dietary adjustments, but if they did not, it is worth asking.

Breed Predisposition

Some breeds popular in New Zealand are genetically predisposed to weight gain. Labrador Retrievers, Beagles, Cavalier King Charles Spaniels, Pugs, and British Shorthair cats are all prone to obesity. If you have one of these breeds, you need to be extra vigilant about portion control and exercise from day one, not after the weight has already crept on.

Health Risks of Pet Obesity

Obesity is not a cosmetic issue. It is a disease that reduces quality of life and shortens lifespan. The health risks are well-documented and significant:

Joint Disease and Arthritis

Excess weight puts enormous mechanical stress on joints. In dogs, obesity accelerates the development of osteoarthritis, particularly in the hips, knees, and elbows. Overweight cats develop arthritis too, though they may hide their pain more effectively. Every kilogram of excess weight on a medium-sized dog is equivalent to a human carrying an extra 10 to 15 kilograms. The impact on joint health is dramatic and cumulative.

Diabetes

Obese cats are up to four times more likely to develop diabetes mellitus than cats at a healthy weight. Feline diabetes requires daily insulin injections and regular blood glucose monitoring, creating significant cost and lifestyle disruption. In dogs, the link between obesity and diabetes is also established, though less pronounced than in cats.

Respiratory Problems

Fat around the chest and abdomen restricts lung expansion, making breathing harder. Overweight brachycephalic breeds like Pugs, French Bulldogs, and British Bulldogs are at particular risk, as they already have compromised airways. Heat intolerance goes hand in hand with respiratory difficulty, and New Zealand\'s humid summers can be dangerous for obese, flat-faced breeds.

Heart Disease

Obesity increases blood volume and cardiac workload, contributing to hypertension and heart disease. While heart disease in pets has multiple causes, obesity is a significant and entirely preventable risk factor.

Cancer

Research has linked obesity to several types of cancer in dogs and cats, including mammary tumours, transitional cell carcinoma, and lymphoma. The inflammatory state produced by excess fat tissue creates an environment that promotes tumour development.

Reduced Lifespan

A landmark study following Labrador Retrievers over their lifetimes found that dogs maintained at a lean body weight lived on average 1.8 years longer than their overweight counterparts. That is nearly two extra years of life, simply from maintaining a healthy weight. For a breed with a typical lifespan of 10 to 12 years, that represents a 15 to 18 percent increase in lifespan.

Creating a Weight Management Plan

Step 1: See Your Vet

Before starting any weight loss programme, have your pet examined by a veterinarian. Weight gain can sometimes be a symptom of an underlying condition such as hypothyroidism, Cushing\'s disease, or fluid retention. Your vet will rule out medical causes, establish a current weight and body condition score, and help you set a realistic target weight and timeline.

Healthy weight loss for dogs is 1 to 2 percent of body weight per week. For cats, it should be even more gradual: 0.5 to 1 percent per week. Rapid weight loss in cats can cause hepatic lipidosis, a potentially fatal liver condition. Never put a cat on a crash diet.

Step 2: Calculate the Right Calories

Your vet can calculate your pet\'s Resting Energy Requirement (RER) and Maintenance Energy Requirement (MER) to determine how many kilojoules they need per day. As a rough guide, the formula for RER in kilojoules is:

RER (kJ/day) = 293 x (body weight in kg) raised to the power of 0.75

For weight loss, feed approximately 70 to 80 percent of the MER for your pet\'s target weight, not their current weight. This means you need to know the target weight first, which is why veterinary guidance is essential.

Step 3: Choose the Right Food

Weight management diets are not all equal. Look for:

  • High protein, low fat: Protein preserves lean muscle mass during weight loss while fat is reduced to cut calories. Look for diets with at least 25 percent protein on a dry matter basis for dogs and 35 percent for cats.
  • High fibre: Fibre helps pets feel full without adding calories. Beet pulp, cellulose, and psyllium are common fibre sources in weight management diets.
  • L-carnitine: This amino acid helps transport fatty acids into mitochondria for energy production, supporting fat metabolism during weight loss.
  • Prescription vs over-the-counter: If your pet needs to lose more than 15 percent of their body weight, a veterinary prescription weight loss diet is more effective and safer than a retail brand.

New Zealand brands offering weight management formulas include Hill\'s Prescription Diet Metabolic, Royal Canin Satiety, and Eukanuba Weight Control. Your vet can recommend the most appropriate option for your pet.

Step 4: Measure Everything

Estimating portion sizes is the single biggest reason weight loss plans fail. Use a kitchen scale to weigh food, not a measuring cup. A heaped cup can contain 20 to 30 percent more food than a level cup, and over weeks that discrepancy adds up to significant overfeeding. Record every meal, treat, and scrap. If it goes in your pet\'s mouth, it goes in the diary.

Step 5: Cut the Treats (Strategically)

Treats should make up no more than 10 percent of your pet\'s daily calorie intake. For a 10-kilogram dog on a weight loss plan, that might be as little as 100 kilojoules, roughly equivalent to one small commercial treat. Options that work better:

  • Carrot sticks, green beans, and cucumber slices are near-zero-calorie options that most dogs accept enthusiastically
  • Small pieces of cooked chicken breast (no seasoning) as a high-value training reward
  • Frozen kibble from their daily allocation, soaked in water and frozen in a Kong or similar toy
  • Cat grass or catnip for cats, which provide enrichment without calories

Exercise for Weight Loss

For Dogs

Exercise is essential but it must be appropriate for the dog\'s current condition. An obese dog cannot safely run 5 kilometres any more than an obese human could. Build up gradually:

  • Week 1-2: Two 10-minute walks per day on flat ground. Focus on consistency, not speed.
  • Week 3-4: Increase to two 15-minute walks. Add gentle inclines if your dog is comfortable.
  • Week 5-6: Two 20-minute walks. Introduce short intervals of slightly faster pace.
  • Week 7+: Gradually increase duration and intensity based on your dog\'s fitness. Aim for 30 to 45 minutes of sustained activity twice daily.

New Zealand offers excellent exercise options for dogs: beach walks, regional park trails, and off-leash areas in most cities. Swimming is particularly good for overweight dogs with joint problems, as it provides cardiovascular exercise without impact stress. Many public pools and veterinary physiotherapy clinics in New Zealand offer hydrotherapy for dogs.

For Cats

Cats are trickier to exercise than dogs, but it is absolutely possible. The key is short, frequent play sessions that tap into their predatory instincts:

  • Wand toys and feather teasers: Five to ten minutes of active chasing, twice daily. This mimics the hunt-stalk-catch pattern cats naturally follow.
  • Food puzzles and foraging toys: Make your cat work for their food. Puzzle feeders and treat balls slow down eating and add physical activity.
  • Vertical spaces: Cat towers, shelving, and window perches encourage climbing and jumping, which builds muscle and burns calories.
  • Laser pointers: Good for getting a lazy cat moving, but always end with a physical toy they can catch. The chase without the catch can cause frustration.

The New Zealand Advantage

New Zealand\'s climate and landscape offer unique advantages for pet exercise. Our extensive network of off-leash dog beaches, regional parks, and walking tracks makes it easy to keep dogs active year-round. The mild climate in most regions means outdoor exercise is possible most days, unlike colder countries where winter limits activity for months at a time.

How Pet Sitters Can Support Weight Management

If your pet is on a weight loss plan, consistency is critical. A single day of overfeeding can undo a week of careful calorie counting. This is where a knowledgeable pet sitter makes a real difference.

What to Tell Your Sitter

  • Your pet\'s exact daily food allowance, measured and pre-portioned if possible
  • Which treats are allowed and how many per day
  • Your pet\'s exercise requirements: duration, intensity, and any limitations
  • What to do if your pet refuses food (sometimes overweight pets go on hunger strike when their portions are cut)
  • Warning signs to watch for: excessive panting, limping, lethargy, or refusing to walk

What a Good Sitter Does

A weight-management-aware sitter will:

  • Stick to the feeding plan exactly, with no extras or table scraps
  • Ensure daily exercise happens, rain or shine
  • Monitor food intake and report any changes in appetite
  • Use play and enrichment instead of food as rewards
  • Weigh the pet if scales are available, and record the weight
  • Keep a daily log of food, exercise, and behaviour

Maintaining Weight Long Term

Reaching your pet\'s target weight is an achievement, but maintenance is where most people slip. Studies show that around 50 percent of dogs that lose weight regain it within 12 months if the maintenance phase is not managed carefully. Here is how to keep the weight off:

  • Weigh your pet monthly. A small gain caught early is easy to correct. A 5 percent weight creep that goes unnoticed for six months becomes a major problem.
  • Continue measuring food. Do not go back to estimating. The difference between maintenance and overfeeding can be as little as 10 percent of daily calories.
  • Keep exercising. The activity level that got your pet to their target weight is the activity level that will keep them there. Do not reduce walks or play sessions.
  • Adjust for life stage. As pets age, their metabolism slows. Senior dogs and cats typically need 10 to 20 percent fewer calories than younger adults. Talk to your vet about adjusting portions as your pet ages.
  • Be honest about treats. It is easy for treat calories to creep back in. Set a firm daily limit and stick to it.

When to Seek Veterinary Help

If your pet has been on a calorie-controlled diet and regular exercise programme for eight weeks with no measurable weight loss, something else may be going on. Medical conditions that can cause weight gain or prevent weight loss include:

  • Hypothyroidism (dogs)
  • Hyperadrenocorticism, also known as Cushing\'s disease (dogs)
  • Insulinoma (dogs and cats)
  • Acromegaly (cats)
  • Medication side effects, particularly long-term corticosteroid use

Blood work and a thorough physical examination can identify or rule out these conditions. Do not assume your pet is simply "lazy" or "greedy" if weight loss stalls despite your best efforts.

Quick Weight Management Checklist

  • Ask your vet for a body condition score at every visit
  • Weigh your pet at home monthly and record the result
  • Measure all food with a kitchen scale, not a cup
  • Limit treats to no more than 10 percent of daily calories
  • Choose low-calorie treat alternatives like vegetables
  • Exercise your dog twice daily, building gradually
  • Play with your cat for 10 to 15 minutes, twice daily
  • Use food puzzles and slow feeders to extend mealtime
  • Adjust portions after desexing and as your pet ages
  • Brief your pet sitter on your weight management plan
  • See your vet if weight loss stalls for more than eight weeks
