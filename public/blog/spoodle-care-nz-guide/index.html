Spoodle Care Guide for New Zealand Owners

Published 29 March 2026 • 8 min read • By ZiggySitters

The Spoodle — a cross between a Cocker Spaniel and a Poodle — has become one of New Zealand's most beloved family dogs. With their teddy-bear looks, low-shedding coats, and genuinely affectionate nature, it's easy to see why. But owning a Spoodle well requires understanding their specific needs around grooming, exercise, mental stimulation, and health. This guide covers everything NZ owners and pet sitters need to give a Spoodle the life they deserve.

Quick Answer: What Do Spoodles Need?

Spoodles thrive on daily exercise (45–60 minutes), regular professional grooming every 6–8 weeks, consistent positive-reinforcement training, and plenty of human companionship. They are not suited to long periods alone and can develop separation anxiety without proper conditioning. Get these four things right and your Spoodle will be a happy, healthy companion for 12–15 years.

Understanding the Spoodle Temperament

Spoodles inherit the best traits from both parent breeds. Cocker Spaniels bring warmth, gentleness, and a strong bond with family. Poodles contribute intelligence, trainability, and problem-solving ability. The result is a dog that is exceptionally easy to live with but demands genuine engagement.

Most Spoodles are:

  • People-oriented: They follow their owners around and want to be involved in everything
  • Good with children: Gentle and patient, though still need supervision with toddlers
  • Sociable with other dogs: Usually friendly at the dog park, especially if socialised early
  • Alert but not yappy: They will bark at the door but are not nuisance barkers by nature
  • Intelligent and biddable: They pick up commands quickly and genuinely want to please
NZ Note: Spoodles are listed under the informal name in New Zealand. Internationally, the same cross is often called a Cockapoo. Both terms refer to the same breed mix.

Coat Types and Grooming Requirements

One of the most important things to understand about Spoodles is that their coats vary significantly, even within the same litter. The coat type they inherit determines how much grooming work is involved.

Coat Type Description Grooming Frequency
Wavy Loose waves, low-moderate shedding Brush 3x/week, groom every 6–8 weeks
Curly Tight curls, minimal shedding Brush 4x/week, groom every 6 weeks
Straight Flat coat, more shedding Brush 2x/week, groom every 8 weeks

Regardless of coat type, all Spoodles need attention to the following areas:

  • Ears: Floppy, hairy ears trap moisture and are prone to infection. Clean weekly with a vet-approved ear cleaner and check for redness or odour.
  • Eyes: Wipe eye discharge daily with a damp cloth. Tear staining is common, especially in lighter-coloured dogs.
  • Paws: Trim the hair between paw pads to prevent matting and grass seed penetration — a real risk in NZ summers.
  • Teeth: Brush 2–3 times per week or use dental chews. Small crossbreeds can be prone to dental disease.
Grooming Warning: Skipping brushing for even a week or two can result in severe matting, particularly behind the ears, under the armpits, and around the collar area. Severely matted coats often require a full clip-down by a groomer and can cause skin irritation.

Exercise and Mental Stimulation

Spoodles have moderate-to-high exercise needs. They are athletic dogs that enjoy a variety of activities, and their Poodle heritage means they also need mental challenges to stay satisfied.

Daily Exercise Targets by Size

Spoodle Size Adult Weight Daily Exercise
Miniature Spoodle 5–10 kg 30–45 minutes
Standard Spoodle 10–18 kg 45–60 minutes

Good activities for Spoodles in NZ include:

  • Off-lead runs at dog parks (check your local council's rules — Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch all have off-lead areas)
  • Fetch and retrieve games — they love using their nose and mouth
  • Swimming at dog-friendly beaches, particularly in summer
  • Short hikes on dog-friendly NZ trails
  • Trick training sessions (10 minutes counts as mental exercise)
  • Puzzle feeders and sniff mats to slow meal times and engage the brain
Wet Weather Tip: NZ winters can mean days of rain in a row. Keep an indoor enrichment kit ready: frozen Kongs, snuffle mats, training sessions, and hide-and-seek games using kibble. A mentally tired Spoodle is a well-behaved Spoodle.

Feeding a Spoodle in New Zealand

Spoodles do well on high-quality commercial dog food formulated for small-to-medium dogs. The key is choosing a food with real meat as the first ingredient and no fillers like corn syrup or artificial preservatives.

Approximate Daily Feeding Guide

Dog Weight Daily Dry Food (approx.) Meals Per Day
5–8 kg 90–130g 2
8–12 kg 130–190g 2
12–18 kg 190–260g 2

Always check the feeding guide on your specific brand, as caloric density varies. Treats should not exceed 10% of daily caloric intake, especially if your Spoodle tends to gain weight easily.

Foods to avoid:

  • Grapes and raisins (toxic to all dogs)
  • Onions, garlic, and leeks
  • Macadamia nuts (common in NZ households)
  • Xylitol (found in some NZ peanut butter brands and sugar-free products)
  • Cooked bones (splinter risk)

Common Health Issues in NZ Spoodles

Like all crossbreeds, Spoodles can inherit health conditions from either parent breed. Being aware of these helps you catch problems early.

  • Ear infections: The most common issue. Floppy, hair-filled ear canals trap moisture, creating the perfect environment for bacteria and yeast. Regular cleaning is essential, especially after swimming or bathing.
  • Hip dysplasia: Inherited from Cocker Spaniels. Ask breeders for hip scores on parent dogs. Symptoms include stiffness, reluctance to jump, and a bunny-hop gait.
  • Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA): A genetic eye condition that can cause progressive blindness. DNA testing of parent dogs can rule this out.
  • Skin allergies: Environmental allergies are relatively common. Signs include itching, redness, and recurring skin infections. A vet can help identify triggers and recommend management strategies.
  • Luxating patella: Kneecap dislocation, more common in miniature Spoodles. Mild cases are managed conservatively; severe cases may require surgery.
Pet Insurance Tip: Given the potential for ear issues and orthopaedic problems, pet insurance is worth considering for Spoodles. Several NZ providers (Southern Cross, PD Insurance, Petsy) offer policies that cover hereditary conditions if taken out before symptoms appear.

Training Your Spoodle

Spoodles are among the easier breeds to train. Their Poodle intelligence means they pick up new concepts quickly, and their Cocker Spaniel eagerness means they genuinely want to make you happy. The combination makes them ideal for first-time dog owners.

Key training principles that work well with Spoodles:

  • Use positive reinforcement exclusively. Harsh corrections or punishment-based methods are counter-productive with this sensitive breed and can cause lasting anxiety.
  • Keep sessions short and varied. Five to ten minutes of focused training twice a day beats one long, boring session.
  • Prioritise recall early. Spoodles can become easily distracted by smells and other dogs. Rock-solid recall makes off-lead exercise safe and enjoyable.
  • Address separation anxiety proactively. Start alone-time training from day one, with short departures that gradually increase in length. Do not wait for problem behaviour to appear.

Spoodles and Separation Anxiety

This is arguably the most important section in this guide. Spoodles form very deep bonds with their owners and can struggle significantly when left alone. Separation anxiety can manifest as barking, destructive behaviour, house soiling, and extreme distress.

Prevention is far easier than treatment:

  • Practise short departures from puppyhood (even 5-minute trips outside without the dog)
  • Avoid dramatic goodbyes and hellos — keep departures and arrivals calm
  • Provide a safe crate or den space where your dog feels secure
  • Use puzzle feeders or frozen Kongs to occupy them during your absence
  • Consider a dog walker or midday visit if your dog is home alone for more than 4–5 hours

If your Spoodle already shows anxiety signs, consult your vet or a certified animal behaviourist. Medication combined with behaviour modification can make a significant difference.

Common Mistakes Spoodle Owners Make

  • Skipping grooming appointments: It feels like a cost-saving measure but leads to painful matting, potential skin problems, and expensive correction grooms.
  • Assuming they're hypoallergenic: Spoodles are lower-shedding, but no dog is truly hypoallergenic. If allergies are a concern, spend time with the breed before committing.
  • Underestimating their intelligence: A bored Spoodle will find their own entertainment — usually involving your shoes, cushions, or garden.
  • Leaving them alone too long: Spoodles are companion dogs. If your lifestyle involves 8–10 hours away from home without pet care arrangements, a different breed may suit you better.

What to Tell Your Spoodle's Pet Sitter

If you're booking a pet sitter for your Spoodle, share the following information upfront:

  • Exact feeding amounts, food brand, and meal schedule
  • Any known allergies or sensitivities
  • Exercise routine and favourite activities
  • How they cope with being alone and any anxiety triggers
  • Grooming needs during the stay (especially ear cleaning if it's a long booking)
  • Commands they know and training methods used
  • Vet contact details and any current medications

Key Takeaways

  • Spoodles are intelligent, affectionate dogs that thrive on companionship and consistent care
  • Grooming is non-negotiable — budget for professional grooming every 6–8 weeks
  • Ear care is the number one health priority — weekly cleaning prevents costly infections
  • Exercise needs are moderate but daily and non-negotiable for a happy dog
  • Separation anxiety is a real risk — address it proactively from puppyhood
  • With the right care, Spoodles make extraordinary family companions for 12–15 years

Frequently Asked Questions

How much exercise does a Spoodle need each day?

Spoodles need around 45 to 60 minutes of exercise daily. This can be split into two walks plus off-lead play in a secure yard or park. They also benefit from mental stimulation through puzzle toys and training sessions.

How often should a Spoodle be groomed?

Spoodles should be brushed at least three times a week to prevent matting, and professionally groomed every 6 to 8 weeks. Their wavy or curly coats keep growing and will mat quickly without regular attention.

Are Spoodles good with children and other pets?

Yes. Spoodles are known for their gentle, sociable temperament and usually get on well with children and other pets when properly socialised from puppyhood.

What health issues are common in Spoodles in NZ?

Common health issues include ear infections (due to their floppy, hairy ears), hip dysplasia inherited from Cocker Spaniels, progressive retinal atrophy, and skin allergies. Regular vet checks help catch these early.

How long do Spoodles live?

Spoodles typically live 12 to 15 years, which is healthy for a dog of their size. Good nutrition, regular exercise, and routine vet care all contribute to a long lifespan.
