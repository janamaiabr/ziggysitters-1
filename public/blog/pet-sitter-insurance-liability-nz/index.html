Pet Sitter Insurance & Liability in New Zealand: What Owners and Sitters Need to Know

16 May 2026 — ZiggySitters

You trust someone with your furry family member. But what happens when things go wrong? Whether your dog slips its lead and causes a traffic incident, your cat needs emergency vet care while you are away, or a sitter accidentally damages your property, understanding pet sitter insurance and liability in New Zealand is essential for both owners and sitters.

This guide breaks down exactly what you need to know, what protections exist, and how to make sure you are covered before handing over the keys (and the kibble).

Why Pet Sitter Insurance Matters

New Zealand does not have specific legislation governing pet sitting. There is no "Pet Sitters Act" and no mandatory licensing regime. That means the legal framework relies on general contract law, the Accident Compensation Corporation (ACC), and common law principles of negligence.

For pet owners, the risk is real: if your pet injures someone or damages property while in a sitter's care, you could still be liable as the owner. For sitters, the risk is different but equally significant: if a pet in your care causes damage or injury, or if the pet itself is injured due to your negligence, you could be personally responsible.

Insurance exists to transfer these risks from your bank account to an insurer's. Let us look at what that means in practice.

Types of Insurance Relevant to Pet Sitting

1. Pet Sitter Liability Insurance

This is the core policy for professional pet sitters. It typically covers:

  • Third-party injury — If a dog in your care bites someone or causes them to fall.
  • Third-party property damage — If a pet damages someone else's property (chewing furniture, scratching a car, digging up a garden).
  • Care, custody, and control (CCC) — Covers veterinary costs if a pet is injured or becomes ill while in your care, provided you were not grossly negligent.
  • Key cover — Reimburses the cost of replacing locks if client keys are lost or stolen.

Important: Standard public liability insurance does not automatically cover animals in your care. You need a policy specifically designed for pet sitting, or a specialist extension.

2. Home and Contents Insurance (For Pet Owners)

If you are a pet owner hiring a sitter, your home and contents policy may be relevant, but check the fine print:

  • Some policies exclude damage caused by pets, including damage caused while a sitter is present.
  • Some policies exclude liability when the home is occupied by a non-resident (i.e., a house-sitter) unless you have notified the insurer.
  • Theft by a sitter is typically not covered unless you can prove forced entry.

Action item: Before hiring a sitter, call your insurer and confirm your policy covers house-sitter scenarios.

3. Pet Insurance

Pet insurance in New Zealand can cover:

  • Accident-only — Covers veterinary costs for injuries (hit by car, dog bite, fall, etc.). The most affordable tier.
  • Accident and illness — Adds coverage for diseases, infections, and chronic conditions.
  • Comprehensive — Adds routine care, dental, and sometimes behavioural therapy.

Pet insurance is worth having regardless of whether you use a sitter. But if your pet needs emergency care while you are away, insurance means the sitter can authorise treatment without worrying about a massive bill.

Major NZ pet insurers include Petplan, Southern Cross Pet Insurance, and PD Insurance. Compare policies carefully — coverage, exclusions, and claim limits vary significantly.

4. Dog Owner Liability

Under the Dog Control Act 1996, dog owners in New Zealand have strict liability for damage caused by their dogs. This means:

  • If your dog bites someone, you are liable regardless of whether you were negligent.
  • If your dog rushes at or startles someone (causing injury), you are liable.
  • If your dog damages property, you are liable.

This applies even if the dog is with a sitter at the time. You own the dog, you carry the liability. This is why it is critical that your sitter has their own liability insurance and that you understand what your homeowner or pet insurance covers.

What to Ask a Pet Sitter Before Hiring

Whether you find a sitter through ZiggySitters, a friend, or an online platform, ask these questions:

  1. Do you have public liability insurance that specifically covers pet sitting? — Not just general public liability. Ask for the policy document and check that animals in care are included.
  2. What is the coverage limit? — NZ$1 million is standard. Anything less may not cover a serious incident.
  3. Does your policy cover vet bills for pets in your care? — This is the CCC extension. Without it, you may be relying on your own pet insurance or paying out of pocket.
  4. What happens if my pet injures someone? — Understand who is responsible and how claims would be handled. The answer depends on both your insurance and theirs.
  5. Do you have a written agreement or contract? — A pet sitting contract should outline responsibilities, emergency procedures, and liability allocation.
  6. What is your emergency vet procedure? — Which vet would they take your pet to? How would they contact you? Do they have authority to authorise emergency treatment?

What Pet Sitters Need to Protect Themselves

If you are a pet sitter in New Zealand, whether casual or professional, here is what you need:

Insurance

  • Pet sitter liability insurance — Non-negotiable for professional sitters. Even casual sitters doing favours for friends should consider it. A single dog bite claim can run into tens of thousands of dollars.
  • Professional indemnity — If you offer advice on pet care, training, or behaviour, this covers claims arising from that advice.
  • Key cover — Losing a client's keys is more common than you think. Replacing locks on a typical NZ home costs $300-800.

NZ Pet Sitter Insurance Providers

  • Pet Sitters International (PSI) — Offers policies that cover NZ-based sitters. Widely recognised in the industry.
  • PetCare — NZ-specific pet business insurance, including liability and CCC coverage.
  • Cover-More — General business insurance that can be extended for pet care professionals.
  • BDAA (Business Disability & Accident Association) — Covers pet professionals including sitters, groomers, and walkers.

Expect to pay NZ$200-500 per year for a basic pet sitting liability policy, depending on coverage limits and the number of pets you care for simultaneously.

Contracts

A written pet sitting agreement should include:

  • Pet details (name, breed, age, microchip number, vet details)
  • Emergency contact for the owner
  • Emergency vet authorisation (spending limit and designated vet)
  • Specific care instructions (feeding, medication, exercise)
  • House rules (which rooms are off-limits, alarm codes, visitor policy)
  • Liability and indemnification clause
  • Payment terms and cancellation policy
  • What happens if the sitter is unable to complete the booking (illness, emergency)

Common Scenarios and Who Pays

Scenario 1: Dog Bites Someone While on a Walk with Sitter

Legal liability: The dog owner, under the Dog Control Act 1996.

Insurance response: The owner's pet insurance (if it includes third-party liability) or home contents liability extension would typically respond. The sitter's liability insurance may also contribute if the sitter was negligent (e.g., walking a known reactive dog off-lead).

Scenario 2: Pet Gets Sick and Needs Vet Care While Owner Is Away

Legal liability: Depends on whether the sitter's actions caused the illness.

Insurance response: If the sitter has CCC coverage, that may pay. Otherwise, the owner's pet insurance should cover it. If neither has insurance, the owner pays out of pocket.

Practical tip: Always leave a signed vet authorisation letter and a credit card on file with your regular vet before going away.

Scenario 3: Sitter Accidentally Damages Owner's Property

Legal liability: The sitter, if negligent.

Insurance response: The sitter's liability insurance should cover accidental damage to the client's property. Some policies specifically include this; others exclude damage to property in your care, custody, or control. Check carefully.

Scenario 4: Pet Escapes and Is Found by the Council

Legal liability: The owner is responsible for registration and microchipping. The sitter may be responsible if the escape was due to their negligence (leaving a gate open).

Costs: Council impound fees in NZ typically range from NZ$50-200 plus daily boarding fees. Additional fines may apply if the dog is unregistered or not microchipped.

Scenario 5: Sitter Is Injured by the Pet

Legal liability: ACC covers personal injury in New Zealand, regardless of fault. The sitter does not need to sue the owner for injury costs — ACC will cover medical treatment and rehabilitation.

However: ACC does not cover pain and suffering, loss of enjoyment, or emotional harm. For those, the sitter would need to bring a common law claim, which is rare but possible in serious cases.

The ACC Factor: What Makes NZ Different

New Zealand's Accident Compensation Corporation (ACC) is a no-fault insurance scheme that covers personal injury. This fundamentally changes the liability landscape compared to countries like Australia or the UK:

  • If a person is injured by a pet, ACC covers their medical costs — regardless of who is at fault.
  • You cannot sue for personal injury in NZ — ACC is the sole remedy for injury claims.
  • But ACC does not cover property damage — if a dog chews up a couch or destroys a garden, that is a civil matter between the owner and the sitter.
  • ACC levies apply to dog owners — the ACC levy for dog-related injuries is currently included in your local council dog registration fee.

This means liability insurance in NZ is less about covering personal injury claims (ACC handles that) and more about covering property damage, veterinary costs, and legal defence costs.

Checklist: Before You Hire a Pet Sitter

  • Verify the sitter has pet sitting liability insurance with CCC coverage
  • Check that your home and contents insurance covers house-sitter scenarios
  • Confirm your pet insurance is current and covers emergency care
  • Leave a signed vet authorisation letter with a spending limit
  • Provide emergency contacts (your number, a local friend, your vet)
  • Ensure your pet's microchip and council registration are current
  • Complete a written pet sitting agreement covering all scenarios above
  • Do a meet and greet before the booking to assess compatibility
  • Store valuables and fragile items safely before the sitter arrives
  • Take photos of your home before and after the sitting period

Checklist: Before You Start Pet Sitting

  • Obtain pet sitting liability insurance with CCC and key cover
  • Draft a standard pet sitting agreement for all clients
  • Set up an emergency vet procedure (know which vets are 24/7 in your area)
  • Keep a first aid kit for pets (and know how to use it)
  • Complete a pet first aid course — Red Cross and various NZ providers offer these
  • Take photos of the home when you arrive and before you leave
  • Ask owners for veterinary authorisation and a spending limit for emergencies
  • Get emergency contact numbers for the owner and a local backup
  • Verify the pet's registration and microchip details
  • Understand the Dog Control Act 1996 obligations for dogs in your care

Key Takeaways

  1. Insurance is not optional — whether you are an owner or a sitter, the financial risk of an incident far exceeds the cost of a policy.
  2. ACC changes the game in NZ — personal injury is covered regardless of fault, but property damage and vet bills are your responsibility.
  3. The Dog Control Act creates strict liability — dog owners are responsible for their dogs' actions, even when a sitter is in charge.
  4. Get it in writing — a pet sitting agreement protects both parties and sets clear expectations.
  5. Check your existing policies — your home insurance may not cover house-sitter scenarios unless you notify the insurer.
