Siamese Cat Care Guide for New Zealand Owners

Published 18 June 2026 — ZiggySitters NZ

The Siamese cat is one of the most recognisable and beloved breeds in New Zealand. With their striking blue eyes, sleek coats, and famously vocal personalities, Siamese cats bring energy and companionship to any home. But their intelligence and social nature mean they need more attention than your average moggie. This guide covers everything Kiwi Siamese owners need to know, from daily care routines to finding a pet sitter who truly understands the breed.

Sieamese Cat Personality and Temperament

Siamese cats are often described as the extroverts of the feline world, and for good reason. They are:

  • Highly vocal — Siamese cats will talk to you constantly, from soft chirps to loud, demanding yowls. If you live in a New Zealand apartment, be aware that neighbours may hear your Siamese holding court.
  • Deeply social — They bond intensely with their humans and do not tolerate loneliness well. A Siamese left alone for long stretches can develop separation anxiety, destructive behaviours, and depression.
  • Intelligent and curious — These cats learn to open doors, drawers, and cupboards. Cat-proofing your home is essential. Puzzle feeders and interactive toys are a must.
  • People-oriented — A Siamese will follow you from room to room, supervising your every move. They want to be involved in everything you do.

If you work long hours or travel frequently, a companion cat or a dedicated pet sitter is not a luxury for a Siamese — it is a necessity.

Feeding Your Siamese Cat in New Zealand

Siamese cats are active and lean, with fast metabolisms that benefit from high-protein, low-carbohydrate diets. Here is what works well in NZ:

  • Wet food as the foundation — Brands available in NZ like Ziwi Peak, Feline Natural, and K9 Natural (made right here in New Zealand) offer excellent high-meat-content options. Aim for at least 70% meat content.
  • Raw feeding — Many NZ Siamese owners raw-feed using commercially prepared raw diets from companies like Raw Essentials or The Butchers Dog. If raw-feeding, ensure the diet is balanced with taurine, calcium, and essential fatty acids.
  • Dry food sparingly — Siamese cats are prone to dental issues, so crunching some dry kibble can help, but it should not be the primary food source. Look for grain-free, high-protein options.
  • Fresh water always — Siamese cats are notoriously bad drinkers. A cat water fountain (available from pet stores like Animates and Pet.co.nz) encourages hydration and helps prevent urinary tract issues.

Adult Siamese typically need 200–250 calories per day, split between two or three meals. Kittens need more frequent feeding — four small meals daily until six months of age.

Grooming Your Siamese

One of the joys of owning a Siamese is their low-maintenance coat. The short, fine fur requires minimal upkeep:

  • Brushing — Once a week with a soft bristle brush or rubber grooming mitt is plenty. This removes loose hair and distributes natural oils.
  • Bathing — Rarely needed. Siamese cats are fastidious self-groomers. Only bathe if your cat gets into something toxic or smelly.
  • Nail trimming — Every two to three weeks. Siamese cats are active climbers, so keeping nails trimmed protects your furniture and curtains.
  • Dental care — This is critical. Siamese cats are genetically predisposed to dental disease. Brush teeth several times a week with a cat-specific toothpaste (never human toothpaste). Annual dental checks at your NZ vet are essential.
  • Ear cleaning — Check weekly. Siamese cats can accumulate wax. Clean gently with a vet-recommended ear cleaner.

Common Health Issues in Siamese Cats

While generally healthy, Siamese cats have breed-specific health concerns that NZ owners should watch for:

  • Dental disease — Periodontal disease is extremely common. Regular brushing and professional dental cleanings at your vet are non-negotiable.
  • Respiratory issues — Siamese cats are prone to upper respiratory infections, especially in humid NZ climates. Keep vaccinations current and watch for sneezing, nasal discharge, or watery eyes.
  • Amyloidosis — A genetic condition affecting the liver and kidneys. Regular blood work at your vet can catch early signs. Ask your breeder about testing if buying a kitten.
  • Progressive retinal atrophy (PRA) — Reputable NZ breeders test for this. If adopting, ask for health screening records.
  • Asthma — Siamese cats have higher rates of feline asthma. Avoid scented litters, smoke, and strong chemical cleaners in your home.
  • Crossed eyes and kinked tails — These are mild breed traits, not health problems. Modern breeding has reduced their prevalence, but they do not affect quality of life.

NZ-specific tip: Register with a vet experienced with pedigree cats. The New Zealand Cat Fancy (NZCF) can recommend breed-knowledgeable veterinarians in your area.

Indoor Living and Enrichment

New Zealand’s wildlife and traffic make indoor living the safest choice for Siamese cats. But these intelligent cats need significant enrichment to stay happy:

  • Vertical space — Cat trees, wall shelves, and window perches. Siamese cats love height and will claim the highest spot in any room.
  • Interactive play — Wand toys, laser pointers, and fetch games. Siamese cats can learn to retrieve toys and will happily play for 20–30 minutes at a time.
  • Puzzle feeders — These satisfy their intelligence and slow down eating. Try the Trixie activity boards available from NZ pet retailers.
  • Training — Yes, you can train a Siamese. They learn sit, high-five, and even agility courses. Clicker training is highly effective and provides mental stimulation.
  • Catios and safe outdoor access — A secure cat enclosure (catio) lets your Siamese enjoy the NZ sunshine without risking wildlife or traffic. See our catio guide for NZ cats for design ideas.

Sieamese Cats and Other Pets

Siamese cats generally do well with other animals, especially other cats. In fact, many breeders recommend getting two Siamese kittens together because they are so social. Introductions should be gradual — see our guide to introducing new pets for a step-by-step approach.

With dogs, Siamese cats often become best friends, provided the dog is cat-friendly and the introduction is done properly. Their confident, outgoing nature means they are less likely to flee and more likely to investigate.

Finding a Pet Sitter for Your Siamese Cat

This is where Siamese care gets serious. A Siamese cat who is left alone or with someone who does not understand the breed can become anxious, vocal, and destructive. Here is what to look for in a pet sitter:

  • Experience with vocal breeds — A sitter who is surprised by a Siamese talking to them may not handle the breed well. Your sitter should expect and welcome the conversation.
  • Willingness to engage — Siamese cats need active play sessions, not just food and litter changes. Make sure your sitter is prepared to spend 30+ minutes per visit on interactive play.
  • Understanding of separation anxiety — Siamese cats can become deeply distressed when their person leaves. A sitter who recognises the signs — excessive vocalisation, refusing food, hiding — and knows how to comfort them is essential.
  • Overnight stays preferred — For trips longer than a day, an overnight pet sitter is strongly recommended. Siamese cats thrive on company and do poorly with only drop-in visits.
  • Familiarity with your cat’s routine — Siamese cats are creatures of habit. Disruptions to feeding, play, and sleep schedules can cause stress. Provide a detailed routine sheet for your sitter.

Seasonal Care Tips for NZ Siamese Cats

Summer (December–February)

  • Siamese cats have light coats but can still overheat. Keep your home cool and provide plenty of fresh water.
  • Avoid midday sun on light-coated Siamese — they can get sunburn, especially on their ears and nose.
  • Flea and tick prevention is essential in the NZ summer. Talk to your vet about safe preventative treatments.

Winter (June–August)

  • Siamese cats feel the cold due to their lean build and short coat. Provide heated cat beds or self-warming mats.
  • Keep them active indoors with extra play sessions to prevent winter weight gain.
  • Dry winter air can cause skin issues — consider a humidifier if your home is heated.

Choosing a Siamese Kitten in New Zealand

If you are adding a Siamese to your family, choose a reputable breeder registered with the New Zealand Cat Fancy. A good breeder will:

  • Provide health screening results for PRA and amyloidosis
  • Raise kittens in a home environment with socialisation
  • Offer a health guarantee
  • Be available for ongoing support and advice
  • Never let kittens go before 12 weeks of age

Expect to pay $1,200–$2,500 for a Siamese kitten from a registered NZ breeder. Avoid classified ads offering kittens at significantly lower prices — these often come from unregistered breeders with no health testing.

Rescue is also an option. organisations like the SPCA and breed-specific rescue groups occasionally have Siamese cats looking for homes. These cats are typically adults, which means their personality is already established — you know exactly what you are getting.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Siamese cats good with children?

Yes, Siamese cats are generally excellent with children. They are social, patient, and enjoy interactive play. However, young children should be taught to handle cats gently. Siamese cats will set boundaries with a swat or a vocal complaint if needed.

How long do Siamese cats live?

With good care, Siamese cats live 15–20 years. Some reach their early twenties. Regular vet check-ups, quality nutrition, and an enriched indoor environment all contribute to longevity.

Can Siamese cats be left alone during the workday?

A single Siamese cat alone for 8–10 hours daily will likely become anxious and destructive. Solutions include: a companion cat, a pet sitter for midday visits, or cat-proofing a safe room with enrichment toys. See our guide to leaving pets home alone for strategies.

Do Siamese cats get along with dogs?

Most Siamese cats get along very well with dogs, especially if introduced properly. Their confident, outgoing nature means they often become best friends with dog companions. See our pet introduction guide.

Conclusion

Sieamese cats are rewarding, entertaining, and deeply affectionate companions — but they are not a low-maintenance breed. They need company, stimulation, and an owner who appreciates a cat that demands to be part of every conversation. If you can provide those things, a Siamese will reward you with a bond unlike any other cat.
