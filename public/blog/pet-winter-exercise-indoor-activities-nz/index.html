Winter Exercise & Indoor Activities for NZ Pets: Beat the Cold

Published 23 July 2026 — ZiggySitters NZ

New Zealand winters are mild compared to much of the world, but the shorter days, cold mornings, and relentless rain in places like Wellington and Dunedin can make outdoor exercise miserable for pets and owners alike. This guide is packed with indoor exercise ideas, enrichment games, and practical strategies to keep your dog or cat active, stimulated, and happy right through the coldest months.

Why Winter Exercise Matters for Pets

Pets that do not get enough physical and mental stimulation develop problems quickly. Bored dogs become destructive dogs. Under-exercised cats gain weight and become lethargic. The winter months in NZ — June through August — are when routine falls apart most easily. Shorter daylight hours mean evening walks happen in the dark, and morning walks compete with frost on the ground.

The key insight: mental stimulation tires pets almost as effectively as physical exercise. A 15-minute sniff session or puzzle game can be equivalent to a 30-minute walk in terms of satisfaction and tiredness. Winter is the time to lean into enrichment.

Indoor Exercise Ideas for Dogs

Stair Runs

If you have stairs, you have a built-in dog treadmill. Throw a toy or treat to the top of the stairs, call your dog back down, and repeat. Five minutes of stair work is genuinely tiring for most medium and large dogs. For small dogs or those with joint issues, limit this to a few gentle repetitions and monitor for any signs of discomfort.

Hide and Seek

A classic that works brilliantly indoors. Tell your dog to stay, go hide in another room, and call them to find you. Start with easy hiding spots and increase difficulty. This game combines recall practice, scent work, and physical movement — three benefits in one.

Tug-of-War

Tug is one of the highest-energy indoor games you can play. Use a rope toy or a long fabric tug. Let your dog win sometimes — it builds confidence. Rules: the game stops if teeth touch skin, and you decide when to start and end. A 10-minute tug session can exhaust most dogs.

Indoor Obstacle Course

Use household items to create a simple agility course. Cushions to jump over, a broomstick balanced on books to limbo under, a blanket tunnel to crawl through. Guide your dog through with treats. Change the course each time to keep it interesting.

Flirt Pole

A flirt pole is essentially a giant cat wand for dogs — a long stick with a rope and lure on the end. You stand in one place and move the lure around while your dog chases it. It is extraordinarily effective for burning energy in a small space, especially for terriers and herding breeds. You can buy one or make one with a PVC pipe and some rope.

Indoor Enrichment Games for Dogs

Sniff Mats (Snuffle Mat)

A snuffle mat is a fabric mat with strips of fleece tied through a base. You hide treats in the strips and let your dog sniff them out. This is pure nose work — low physical intensity but extremely high mental stimulation. Ten minutes of sniffing can leave a dog as satisfied as a 30-minute walk.

Kong and Stuffable Toys

Stuff a Kong with wet food, yoghurt, or peanut butter (xylitol-free — check labels, as some NZ peanut butters contain it) and freeze it. A frozen Kong takes 20–45 minutes to work through, depending on the dog. Rotate between different fillings to maintain novelty.

Safety: Always check that peanut butter and other fillings do not contain xylitol (birch sugar), which is highly toxic to dogs. Common NZ brands like Pics and Fix & Fogg are generally safe, but always verify the label.

Puzzle Toys and Slow Feeders

Puzzle toys require your dog to manipulate sliders, lift flaps, or rotate compartments to access treats. Brands available in NZ include Nina Ottosson (Outward Hound), Kong, and West Paw. Start with level 1 (easy) and work up. Slow feeder bowls turn mealtime into a 10-minute enrichment activity.

Muffin Tin Puzzle

Place treats in a muffin tin, cover each hole with a tennis ball, and let your dog figure out how to remove the balls to get the treats. Simple, cheap, and surprisingly engaging.

Cardboard Box Destruction

Give your dog an empty cereal box, egg carton, or delivery box (remove any staples or tape) with a few treats inside. Let them shred it. Most dogs love this, and cleanup is easy. This satisfies a natural chewing and foraging instinct.

Name That Toy

Teach your dog the names of their toys. Start with two: place them in front of your dog, name one, and reward when they pick it up. Once they know two, add a third. Some dogs can learn 20+ toy names. This is serious mental work and excellent rainy-day training.

Indoor Exercise Ideas for Cats

Wand Toy Sessions

The single most effective indoor exercise for cats. Spend 10–15 minutes twice daily with a wand toy (feather, ribbon, or fabric lure). Move it like prey: dart, pause, hide, flutter. Let your cat catch it periodically — a cat that never catches the lure gets frustrated and gives up. End each session with a small treat to simulate a successful hunt.

Laser Pointer with a Catch

Laser pointers are great for getting cats moving, but the lack of a physical catch frustrates some cats. Solution: end each laser session by pointing at a treat or a physical toy your cat can grab. This gives them the satisfaction of a catch.

Vertical Climbing

Cats need vertical territory. If you do not have a cat tree, create climbing opportunities: clear a high shelf, place a blanket on top of a bookshelf, or invest in wall-mounted cat shelves. Climbing is exercise for cats, and vertical territory reduces stress by giving them safe observation points.

Food Puzzles for Cats

Cat-specific food puzzles (available from Petpost, Animates, and other NZ retailers) make your cat work for their food. Start easy — a toilet paper roll with holes and treats inside — and graduate to commercial puzzles. Cats in the wild spend 6–12 hours per day hunting; free-feeding from a bowl eliminates that natural work. Food puzzles restore it.

Fetch (Yes, Some Cats Love It)

Many cats — especially Bengals, Siamese, and Maine Coons — will chase and return small toys. Try crinkle balls, small mice, or rolled-up foil. Toss gently down a hallway and see if your cat brings it back. Even if they do not return it, the chasing is good exercise.

Box Forts and Paper Bag Tunnels

Cats and boxes are a cliché because it is true. Cut holes in a few cardboard boxes, line them up as a tunnel system, and sprinkle treats inside. Paper bags (never plastic) make great crinkly hiding spots. Rotate the layout every few days to keep it novel.

Winter Pet Safety Reminders

Antifreeze and Coolant

If you have a garage or driveway, be aware that antifreeze (ethylene glycol) is extremely toxic to pets and tastes sweet. Even a small lick can be fatal. Clean up any spills immediately and store containers securely.

Heating Hazards

Heat pumps and fires are essential in NZ winters, but they create risks. Cats will curl up near — or on — heat sources. Use fire guards, keep cats away from heat pump vents (they can dry out their airways), and ensure electric blankets and heating pads are pet-safe. Never leave a heating pad on unattended with a pet.

Paw Care After Wet Walks

Rain, mud, and puddles are facts of NZ winter walking. After every wet walk, dry your dog thoroughly and check between their toes for mud, small stones, or irritation. Prolonged wetness can cause bacterial skin infections (moist dermatitis, also called hot spots). A quick towel-down and paw check takes 30 seconds and prevents expensive vet visits.

Arthritis Flare-Ups

Cold weather makes arthritis worse in older pets. If your senior dog or cat is stiff in the mornings, reluctant to jump, or slower on walks, talk to your vet about joint supplements, anti-inflammatory medication, or a referral for hydrotherapy. DIY Enrichment Ideas (No Shopping Required)

You do not need to buy anything to keep your pet stimulated indoors. Here are free options that work brilliantly:

  • Toilet roll treat dispenser: Fold the ends of a toilet roll inward, put a few treats inside, fold the other end closed. Let your dog or cat rip it open.
  • Towel roll: Lay a towel flat, scatter treats across it, roll it up tightly. Your dog has to unroll it to get the treats. Increase difficulty by tying a knot in the rolled towel.
  • Muffin tin tennis balls: As described above — treats in a muffin tin, tennis balls on top. Cost: zero.
  • The shell game: Three plastic cups, one treat. Shuffle and let your dog guess. Simple, effective, and teaches focus.
  • Paper bag surprise: Put treats in a paper bag, fold it closed. Let your cat bat it around and tear into it.
  • Cardboard box maze: Cut holes in several boxes, tape them together into a tunnel system. Hide treats inside. Hours of fun for cats and small dogs.
  • Ice block treats: Freeze treats or kibble in an ice cream container. The resulting block takes dogs 30–60 minutes to work through. Best done outside or on a hard floor.

When to Call the Vet: Winter Warning Signs

Some winter behaviours are normal (sleeping more, being less active), but watch for these signs that something is wrong:

  • Shivering that does not stop — even indoors with warmth. This can indicate pain, illness, or hypothermia.
  • Limping or stiffness that lasts more than a day — possible arthritis flare or injury.
  • Coughing or sneezing — could be kennel cough or a respiratory infection.
  • Refusing food for more than one meal — pets eat less in winter but a complete loss of appetite is a red flag.
  • Excessive drinking or urination — can indicate kidney issues or diabetes, which are easier to manage when caught early.
  • Dry, flaky skin or excessive scratching — indoor heating dries out pet skin just like human skin.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much exercise does my dog need in winter?

Most dogs need the same amount of total activity year-round — roughly 30–60 minutes for low-energy breeds, 60–90 minutes for moderate, and 90+ minutes for high-energy working breeds. When outdoor time drops, make up the difference with indoor mental stimulation and active play.

My cat has gained weight over winter. What should I do?

Switch from free-feeding to measured meals, use food puzzles to slow eating, and increase active play sessions to 15 minutes twice daily. See our pet weight management guide for a full approach.

Is it safe to walk my dog in the rain?

Light to moderate rain is fine for most dogs. Towel them off afterwards, check paws for mud and debris, and dry them thoroughly. Avoid walking in storms with thunder and lightning, and skip walks in very heavy rain that reduces visibility for both you and your dog.

Should I get a coat for my dog?

Short-haired breeds (greyhounds, whippets, staffies, dachshunds, French bulldogs) genuinely benefit from a coat in temperatures under 10°C. Thick-coated breeds (huskies, malamutes, Newfoundlands) do not need one. If your dog shivers on walks, get a coat.

Can I use a treadmill for my dog?

Dog-specific treadmills exist, and some dogs can be trained to use human treadmills at low speed with supervision. However, treadmill training requires patience and should never be forced. Most dogs get enough exercise from indoor games and walks. If you are considering a treadmill, consult a qualified dog trainer first.

How do I stop my cat from destroying furniture when bored?

Bored cats scratch and chew. Provide multiple scratching posts (vertical and horizontal), rotate toys weekly, and ensure at least two 10–15 minute active play sessions per day. See our cat scratching behaviour guide for detailed solutions.
