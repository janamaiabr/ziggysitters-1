Breed Overview

The Cavalier King Charles Spaniel originated in Britain as a royal companion dog, and that heritage is written into every part of its personality. These are dogs that live to be near their people. They follow you from room to room, curl up on your lap, and greet every visitor like a long-lost friend. In New Zealand, the Cavalier consistently ranks among the most popular small-to-medium breeds, and it is easy to see why.

Cavaliers are adaptable. They do well in apartments and large family homes alike, provided they get enough companionship and moderate daily exercise. They are gentle enough for toddlers and calm enough for retirees, yet playful enough to delight active families. Their eagerness to please makes training straightforward, and their low prey drive means they generally coexist peacefully with cats and other dogs.

That said, Cavaliers are not low-maintenance dogs in every sense. Their coats require regular grooming, their health carries some breed-specific risks that every NZ owner should understand, and their attachment to people means they do not cope well with long periods alone. Understanding these needs before you commit is the foundation of responsible Cavalier ownership.

Exercise Needs in NZ

Cavaliers are moderately active dogs. They need around 45 to 60 minutes of exercise per day, split across two outings. Unlike high-energy breeds, they are content with a brisk morning walk and an afternoon play session rather than demanding marathon hikes. That said, they genuinely enjoy exploring, and New Zealand's wealth of parks, coastal paths, and off-leash areas gives them plenty of opportunity.

Ideal Activities

  • Walks in dog-friendly parks: Cavaliers love sniffing and exploring at a gentle pace. Let them lead on occasion to satisfy natural curiosity.
  • Off-leash play: Fenced areas are ideal. Cavaliers rarely stray far but can get distracted by interesting smells.
  • Fetch and garden games: Short play sessions in a secure yard suit Cavaliers perfectly.
  • Swimming: Many Cavaliers enjoy shallow water, making NZ beaches and river spots a treat in warmer months.
NZ Tip: During Auckland and Northland summers, exercise your Cavalier in the early morning or after 5pm. Their flat-faced (brachycephalic) tendencies mean they overheat faster than longer-snouted breeds, particularly in humidity.

Mental stimulation matters as much as physical exercise for Cavaliers. Puzzle feeders, sniff walks, and basic obedience sessions prevent boredom and reduce the separation anxiety this breed is prone to.

Grooming Your Cavalier

A Cavalier's silky, medium-length coat is one of the breed's most striking features, and it requires consistent maintenance to stay healthy and tangle-free. New Zealand's variable climate, from humid Auckland summers to wet Wellington winters, affects coat condition and the frequency of grooming needed.

Brushing Routine

Brush your Cavalier at least three times a week, daily if possible during shedding seasons (spring and autumn). Use a slicker brush to remove tangles, followed by a soft bristle brush to smooth the coat. Pay particular attention to:

  • Behind the ears and under the collar, where mats form fastest
  • Under the armpits and between the back legs
  • The feathering on the legs and tail

Bathing

Bathe every four to six weeks using a gentle dog shampoo suited for silky coats. Always dry thoroughly, particularly around the ears, to prevent moisture-related skin infections, which are common in NZ's humid regions.

Ears

Cavaliers have long, floppy ears that restrict airflow, creating warm, moist conditions perfect for ear infections. Check and gently clean ears weekly with a vet-approved ear cleaner. This is especially important after swimming or rainy walks.

Nails and Teeth

Trim nails every three to four weeks. Cavaliers are prone to dental disease, so daily tooth brushing is ideal. At minimum, use dental chews and schedule professional cleans with your vet.

Feeding & Nutrition

Cavaliers are enthusiastic eaters and will happily overindulge if given the chance. Obesity is a genuine concern with this breed, as excess weight places additional strain on their hearts, which are already a known health risk area.

Life Stage Daily Meals Key Nutrients
Puppy (under 12 months) 3-4 meals/day Protein, DHA for brain development, calcium
Adult (1-7 years) 2 meals/day Lean protein, omega-3s for coat health, controlled calories
Senior (7+ years) 2 meals/day Joint support (glucosamine), heart-supporting diets, lower sodium

Choose a premium dog food formulated for small-to-medium breeds. Avoid foods with excessive sodium, which can exacerbate heart conditions in older Cavaliers. New Zealand vets frequently recommend cardiac-supportive diets (lower sodium, supplemented with omega-3 fatty acids) for Cavaliers once they pass seven years old.

Treat discipline: Treats should make up no more than 10% of daily calories. Swap high-calorie treats for vegetables like carrot sticks or plain cooked chicken, which Cavaliers love and which support a healthy weight.

Health Watch-outs for NZ Owners

The Cavalier King Charles Spaniel is, unfortunately, one of the breeds with the most significant hereditary health concerns. Being aware of these before you bring one home, and selecting a reputable NZ breeder who health-tests their dogs, is essential.

Mitral Valve Disease (MVD)

The most serious health concern for the breed. Mitral valve disease causes a deterioration of the heart valves, leading to heart murmurs and eventually heart failure. The majority of Cavaliers develop some degree of MVD by age ten. Responsible NZ breeders participate in the Cavalier Heart Scheme, testing breeding dogs to delay the onset of the disease in offspring. Annual cardiac checks by your vet are strongly recommended from age one.

Syringomyelia (SM) and Chiari Malformation (CM)

A neurological condition where the skull is too small for the brain, causing fluid-filled cavities in the spinal cord. Signs include scratching at the neck and shoulders (often in the air), sensitivity around the head and neck, and in serious cases, weakness in the limbs. MRI screening is available, and responsible breeders test for this condition. If you notice phantom scratching in your Cavalier, consult your vet promptly.

Eye Conditions

Cavaliers can be prone to cataracts, dry eye (keratoconjunctivitis sicca), and retinal problems. Annual eye checks are advisable, particularly from middle age.

Hip Dysplasia

Less common in Cavaliers than in larger breeds, but worth monitoring. Keep your dog at a healthy weight and provide low-impact exercise to protect joint health.

Pet insurance in NZ: Given the breed's health profile, comprehensive pet insurance is strongly recommended for Cavalier owners. Cardiac treatment and neurological specialist consultations can cost thousands of dollars in New Zealand. Start insurance while your dog is young and healthy to avoid pre-existing condition exclusions.

NZ Climate Considerations

New Zealand's diverse climate presents specific challenges for Cavalier owners depending on where you live.

Hot and Humid Regions (Auckland, Northland, Bay of Plenty)

Cavaliers struggle in heat and humidity due to their slight brachycephalic (flat-faced) structure. During summer, ensure your Cavalier always has access to shade and fresh water. Avoid walks during the hottest part of the day. Air conditioning or fans make a significant difference to their comfort and safety.

Wet Climates (Wellington, West Coast)

Wellington's wind and regular rain means your Cavalier's long ears are frequently wet, raising the risk of ear infections. Dry thoroughly after every outing and check ears weekly.

Cooler Southern Regions (Canterbury, Otago, Southland)

Cavaliers feel the cold due to their moderate coat density. In winter, a dog jacket is worthwhile for morning walks in Christchurch or Dunedin. Ensure indoor areas are adequately heated for resting periods.

Training & Socialisation

Cavaliers are bright, eager-to-please dogs that respond beautifully to positive reinforcement. Harsh training methods are unnecessary and counterproductive with this sensitive breed. Keep sessions short (10 to 15 minutes), upbeat, and reward-based.

Key Training Priorities

  • Recall: Essential for off-leash safety. Cavaliers can be distracted by scent, so a reliable recall takes consistent practice from puppyhood.
  • Alone time training: Because Cavaliers are so people-focused, systematically teaching them to be calm alone prevents separation anxiety from developing. Start with very short departures from week one.
  • Basic obedience: Sit, stay, leave it, and loose-lead walking are all achievable and make daily life much more enjoyable for both dog and owner.

Socialise puppies broadly during the 8-to-16-week window: different people, other animals, urban sounds, cars, and varied environments. Well-socialised Cavaliers become the confident, friendly adults the breed is known for.

Finding the Right Pet Sitter for Your Cavalier

Cavaliers form deep attachments to their families, which means choosing the right pet sitter is especially important. A poor match can trigger separation anxiety and leave your dog miserable while you're away.

What to Look For in a Pet Sitter

  • Experience with small companion breeds: Cavaliers are not the same as terriers or working dogs. A sitter who understands gentle, affectionate breeds will handle them better.
  • Willingness to provide companionship: Cavaliers should not be left alone for more than four hours. A great sitter keeps them company, not just fed and watered.
  • Knowledge of health signs: Cardiac symptoms and SM signs to watch for. Ensure your sitter has your vet's details and an emergency contact number.
  • Calm home environment: Cavaliers are sensitive to stress. A chaotic household with multiple large, boisterous dogs is not ideal.

Questions to Ask Your Sitter

  • Have you cared for Cavaliers or similar companion breeds before?
  • How many hours per day will my dog have company?
  • Are you familiar with signs of heart or neurological distress?
  • Do you have a plan if my dog needs veterinary attention?
