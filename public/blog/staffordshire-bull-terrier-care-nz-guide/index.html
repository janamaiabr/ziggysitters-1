Staffordshire Bull Terrier Care Guide NZ 2026

By ZiggySitters NZ • Updated March 2026 • 10 min read

The Staffordshire Bull Terrier — affectionately known as the Staffy — is one of New Zealand’s most popular family dogs. Loyal, playful, and endlessly affectionate, Staffies thrive with the right care, exercise, and a sitter who understands their unique personality. This guide covers everything NZ Staffy owners need to know.

Understanding the Staffordshire Bull Terrier

The Staffordshire Bull Terrier is a medium-sized, muscular breed originally developed in England for bull-baiting before becoming a beloved companion dog. Today, Staffies are known throughout New Zealand for their fierce loyalty, boundless energy, and — perhaps surprisingly — their extraordinary gentleness with children. They are nicknamed the “nanny dog” in the UK for good reason.

Staffies typically weigh between 11 and 17 kg and stand 36–41 cm tall. Despite their solid build, they are remarkably agile. Their short, smooth coat comes in a wide range of colours including brindle, red, blue, fawn, white, and combinations of these with white.

Breed at a Glance
  • Size: Medium (11–17 kg)
  • Lifespan: 12–14 years
  • Energy Level: High
  • Grooming Needs: Low
  • Good with Kids: Excellent
  • Good with Other Dogs: Varies — early socialisation is key
  • Suitable for Apartments: Yes, with sufficient exercise

Exercise Requirements for NZ Staffies

Staffies are energetic dogs that need at least 60 minutes of exercise per day. Without adequate physical activity, they can become bored, which often leads to destructive behaviour — chewing furniture, digging, or excessive barking are classic signs of an under-exercised Staffy.

Best Exercise Activities for Staffies

  • Brisk walks and jogs: A 30-minute morning walk and a 30-minute evening session is a solid baseline. Staffies love to move at pace alongside their owners.
  • Fetch and ball games: Staffies have a natural retrieving instinct and can play fetch for extended periods. Off-leash parks are ideal for this.
  • Tug-of-war: A great indoor energy outlet, especially on wet Wellington or Dunedin days. Use a sturdy rope toy and teach a reliable “drop” command.
  • Swimming: Many Staffies enjoy water. NZ’s coastal beaches and rivers offer great opportunities — just supervise carefully as some are not natural swimmers.
  • Dog agility: Staffies excel at agility sports. Many NZ dog clubs run beginner agility courses open to all breeds.
NZ Summer Tip: Staffies have a short muzzle and can overheat quickly. During Auckland and Northland summers, avoid strenuous exercise between 10am and 4pm. Early morning or evening sessions are safest.

Training Your Staffy in New Zealand

Staffies are intelligent dogs but can be strong-willed. The good news: they are highly motivated by food and praise, which makes positive reinforcement training very effective. Consistent, patient training from puppyhood will give you a remarkably well-mannered dog.

Essential Training Priorities

  1. Basic obedience: Sit, stay, come, down, and leave it are non-negotiable. A well-trained Staffy is a pleasure to walk and board.
  2. Leash manners: Staffies are strong and can pull significantly. Invest in a front-clip harness and teach loose-leash walking early.
  3. Socialisation: Expose your Staffy puppy to other dogs, people, and environments between 8 and 16 weeks. Well-socialised Staffies are generally friendly with other dogs, though some individuals can be selective.
  4. Recall: A reliable recall is essential for off-leash play. Practice in low-distraction environments before progressing to dog parks.
  5. Impulse control: Staffies can be excitable greeters. Teaching a calm “sit” before greetings prevents jumping and sets good household manners.
Trainer Tip: Enrol in a puppy preschool or basic obedience class run by an accredited trainer. The New Zealand Institute of Animal Trainers (NZIAT) maintains a list of qualified trainers across NZ.

Grooming and Coat Care

One of the Staffy’s many appeals is their low grooming requirement. Their short, smooth coat needs only a weekly brush with a soft-bristle brush or grooming mitt to remove loose hair and keep it looking glossy.

Grooming Routine

  • Brushing: Once a week is enough. Staffies shed moderately year-round and slightly more in spring and autumn.
  • Bathing: Every 6–8 weeks, or when muddy. Use a gentle dog shampoo to preserve the natural oils in their coat.
  • Ears: Check and gently clean weekly. Staffies can be prone to ear infections, particularly after swimming.
  • Nails: Trim every 3–4 weeks. If you can hear clicking on hard floors, they’re overdue.
  • Teeth: Brush 3–4 times per week with dog-safe toothpaste. Dental chews and raw bones (appropriate size) also help maintain dental health.
  • Skin folds: Some Staffies have skin folds around the face. Check and gently wipe these weekly to prevent moisture build-up and irritation.

Feeding Your Staffy

Staffies are muscular dogs with a healthy appetite — and a well-known tendency to overeat if given the chance. Maintaining a healthy weight is important, as obesity puts stress on their joints and heart.

Life Stage Recommended Diet Meals Per Day
Puppy (8–12 months) High-quality puppy food, medium breed formula 3 times daily
Adult (1–7 years) Premium adult kibble or raw/mixed diet 2 times daily
Senior (7+ years) Senior formula, lower calorie if less active 2 times daily
Watch for Bloat: Like many deep-chested dogs, Staffies can be susceptible to bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus). Avoid vigorous exercise for at least 60 minutes before and after meals.

Health Watch-outs for NZ Staffies

Staffies are generally a hardy breed with fewer hereditary issues than many pedigree dogs, but there are some conditions NZ owners should monitor:

Common Health Concerns

  • Hip dysplasia: A hereditary condition affecting the hip joint. Purchase from breeders who health-test their breeding stock.
  • Skin allergies: Staffies can develop sensitivities to grass pollens, certain foods, or environmental allergens. Itching, redness, and recurring ear infections are common signs.
  • Hereditary cataracts: Staffordshire Bull Terriers have a known predisposition to hereditary cataracts. Reputable breeders should have their dogs eye-tested under the NZVA scheme.
  • L-2-hydroxyglutaric aciduria (L-2-HGA): A metabolic condition unique to Staffies that affects the nervous system. DNA testing is available and responsible breeders screen for it.
  • Mast cell tumours: Staffies have a higher-than-average incidence of mast cell tumours. Any new lump or bump should be checked by a vet promptly.
Vet Tip: Register with a vet soon after getting your Staffy. Annual health checks, vaccination updates, and parasite prevention (fleas, ticks, heartworm) are essential across all NZ regions.

Staffies and NZ Family Life

Staffies are famously good with children. Their patient, playful temperament makes them excellent family dogs, and their robust build means they can handle the rough-and-tumble of family play. However, as with any breed, interactions between dogs and young children should always be supervised.

Staffies thrive on human company and do not do well left alone for long periods. If your household involves regular long workdays, consider:

  • A dog walker or midday visit from a pet sitter
  • Doggy daycare a few days per week
  • Enrichment toys (puzzle feeders, Kongs, snuffle mats) to keep them occupied
  • A second pet for company (with careful introduction)

Staffies and Other Pets

While individual Staffies vary, the breed can be selective about other dogs — particularly of the same sex. Early and ongoing socialisation significantly improves dog-dog relationships. With cats, Staffies raised alongside them from puppyhood generally coexist well. Introductions should always be gradual and controlled.

Finding the Right Pet Sitter for Your Staffy

Not all pet sitters are experienced with Staffies, and finding someone who understands the breed makes a significant difference to your dog’s happiness and safety when you’re away.

What to Look for in a Staffy-Experienced Sitter

  • Experience with strong, energetic breeds: Your sitter should be confident handling a muscular, enthusiastic dog on the lead.
  • Understanding of Staffy personality: The best sitters know Staffies are not aggressive by nature — they’re people-oriented and people-loving.
  • Commitment to exercise routines: A good sitter will maintain your dog’s daily exercise schedule, not just pop in for a quick toilet break.
  • Secure property: Staffies are determined escape artists if motivated. Fencing should be solid and at least 1.5m high.
  • Positive reinforcement approach: Confirm your sitter uses reward-based methods consistent with your training at home.

Questions to Ask Your Potential Sitter

  1. Have you cared for Staffies or similar breeds before?
  2. What is your fencing situation, and how secure is it?
  3. How much exercise will my dog get each day?
  4. How do you handle a dog that pulls on the lead?
  5. Are you comfortable with Staffies interacting with other dogs in your care?
  6. What would you do in a veterinary emergency?

Staffy-Proofing Your Home

Before your sitter arrives — or before you bring a Staffy home — do a thorough safety check:

  • Secure all toxic plants (including garden varieties like daffodils, oleander, and wandering Jew)
  • Remove small items that can be chewed and swallowed
  • Lock away cleaning products, medications, and human food
  • Ensure fence gates have secure latches
  • Provide a cool, shaded area outside — Staffies can overheat in NZ summer
  • Keep fresh water accessible at all times, especially in warm weather

Summary: Is a Staffy Right for You?

The Staffordshire Bull Terrier is a wonderful dog for active New Zealand families who can commit to their exercise and social needs. They are affectionate, entertaining, and deeply loyal companions who repay good care with a lifetime of devotion.

The key to a happy Staffy is simple: plenty of exercise, consistent positive training, regular vet care, and lots of human interaction. When you do need to travel or head to work, a knowledgeable pet sitter who understands the breed makes all the difference.

Key Takeaways
  • Staffies need at least 60 minutes of exercise per day
  • Positive reinforcement training works best for this people-pleasing breed
  • Low grooming needs — weekly brush and monthly bath
  • Health screen for L-2-HGA and hereditary cataracts when buying from a breeder
  • Choose a sitter with genuine experience handling strong, energetic breeds
  • Maintain a healthy weight — Staffies love food and will overeat if allowed
