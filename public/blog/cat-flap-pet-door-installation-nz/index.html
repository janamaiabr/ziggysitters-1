Cat Flap and Pet Door Installation Guide for New Zealand Homes

Published 26 June 2026 · For Pet Owners

Giving your pet independent access to the outdoors sounds simple until you start shopping. There are microchip flaps, magnetic flaps, four-way lockable doors, wall tunnels, sliding panel inserts, and enough jargon to make your head spin. Add in New Zealand\'s mix of weatherboard villas, modern apartments, and rental restrictions, and choosing the right pet door becomes a genuine project. This guide walks you through every option, every installation type, and every cost, so you can pick the right solution for your pet, your home, and your budget.

Types of Cat Flaps and Pet Doors

Not all pet doors are created equal. The type you choose affects security, insulation, cost, and whether your neighbour\'s cat can waltz into your kitchen at 2am.

Basic manual cat flap

The simplest and cheapest option. A plastic flap that swings both ways. Any animal that can push it open can get through, including neighbourhood cats, stray cats, and occasionally possums or rats. Suitable for cats that live in areas with few roaming animals and owners who are not concerned about unwanted visitors. Prices start around 25 to 50 NZD.

Four-way lockable cat flap

A step up from the basic model. The four-way lock lets you set the flap to: fully open (in and out), entry only, exit only, or fully locked. This is useful if you want to keep your cat inside at night while still letting them go out during the day. Brands like Staywell and PetSafe offer reliable models from 50 to 90 NZD. The locking mechanism means you can control access without being home.

Magnetic cat flap

Uses a small magnet attached to your cat\'s collar to unlock the flap. Only a cat wearing the correct magnet can push through. This keeps out most unwanted animals, but it has limitations: if your cat loses their collar, they are locked out. Magnets can also attract nails and metal fragments over time. Cost is typically 60 to 120 NZD.

Microchip cat flap

The gold standard for cat access control. These flaps read your cat\'s existing microchip (the same one implanted by your vet) and only allow cats with approved chip numbers to enter. No collar required. Multiple cats can be programmed, and unwanted visitors are locked out. In New Zealand, where stray and feral cats are common in both urban and rural areas, a microchip flap is one of the best investments you can make. Popular brands include SureFlap (now PetSafe) and Cat Mate. Cost: 150 to 300 NZD for the unit.

NZ wildlife note: New Zealand has significant wildlife concerns with outdoor cats. A microchip cat flap ensures only your cat can enter, but you should also consider keeping your cat indoors at night to protect native birds and lizards. Many NZ councils encourage or require cat curfews.

Dog doors (small, medium, large, extra-large)

Dog doors are larger and more robust than cat flaps. They come in sizes from small (for toy breeds like Chihuahuas) through to extra-large (for German Shepherds and Great Danes). Most dog doors have a rigid flap rather than a flexible one, and many include a sliding lock panel for security. Prices range from 60 NZD for a small basic door to 300+ NZD for large insulated models.

Sliding door pet panel

A pet door that fits into the track of an existing sliding glass door. No cutting required. The panel sits in the door frame, with adjustable heights to fit most standard NZ sliding doors. These are ideal for renters and apartment dwellers who cannot modify the property. They can be removed when you move out. Cost: 120 to 280 NZD.

Wall tunnel pet doors

For homes where installing through a door is not practical (double-glazed windows, solid brick walls, or decorative doors), a wall tunnel pet door goes through an exterior wall. The tunnel lines the hole through the wall, with a flap on both the inside and outside. This is the most invasive installation but offers the most flexibility in placement. Cost: 80 to 200 NZD for the unit, plus significant installation costs.

How to Choose the Right Pet Door

Factor Questions to Ask
Pet size Measure your pet from shoulder to floor and across the chest. The flap opening should be at least 5cm wider and 5cm taller than your pet\'s measurements.
Number of pets If you have multiple pets of different sizes, buy for the largest. A microchip flap can store up to 32 chip numbers.
Unwanted visitors In most NZ suburbs, other cats will find an unsecured flap. If this is a concern, go microchip or magnetic.
Climate NZ winters get cold, especially in the South Island. Insulated flaps or double-flap models reduce heat loss.
Home type Renting? Go with a sliding door panel or window insert. Own your home? You have more flexibility for door and wall installations.
Budget Basic flaps start at 25 NZD. Microchip models are 150 to 300 NZD. Installation adds 100 to 600 NZD depending on surface type.

Installation Options by Surface

Wooden door installation

The simplest and most common installation in NZ. A wooden door can be cut with a jigsaw in under an hour. Most cat flap and pet door kits include a template. Trace the template, drill corner holes, cut with a jigsaw, and screw the flap into place. Total time: 30 to 60 minutes for a DIYer. Professional installation: 100 to 180 NZD including the hole cut and fitting.

1 Mark the position. Place the template at the right height for your pet. For cats, the bottom of the opening should be at belly height. For dogs, at chest height. Most doors need the flap roughly 10 to 15cm from the bottom of the door.
2 Drill corner holes. Use a drill bit slightly wider than your jigsaw blade. Drill one hole in each corner of the marked rectangle.
3 Cut the opening. Insert the jigsaw into one corner hole and cut along the template lines. Take it slow on the final cuts to avoid splintering.
4 Test the fit. Place the interior and exterior frames into the hole. They should sit flush. If the hole is too tight, sand the edges. If too loose, add a bead of silicone sealant.
5 Secure and seal. Screw both frames together through the door. Apply silicone sealant around the edges to prevent draughts and water ingress.

Glass door and window installation

Glass installation is more complex and usually requires a professional. Ordinary glass cannot be cut once it has been tempered or toughened, which is standard in modern NZ homes. You have two options: replace the glass pane with one that has a pre-cut pet door opening, or install a sliding door panel insert.

Important: Never attempt to cut toughened or laminated glass yourself. It will shatter. If your door has safety glass (required in all NZ homes built after 1970), you must order a new glass pane with the pet door cut-out from a glazier. Cost: 200 to 400 NZD including the new pane and installation.

Wall installation (brick, weatherboard, fibrolite)

Wall installations require cutting through exterior cladding, building paper, insulation, and interior lining. In NZ\'s older villas (weatherboard or brick), this is doable but messy. In homes with fibrolite (asbestos-containing sheeting common in pre-1980s NZ homes), do not cut it yourself. Asbestos removal requires a licensed professional. Wall tunnel installations cost 300 to 600 NZD professionally installed.

Sliding door panel installation

The easiest option for renters and the quickest for any home. A pet panel slides into the existing sliding door track. No cutting, no drilling, no modifications. Adjust the height screws to lock it in place, and the weather seal does the rest. To remove, simply unscrew and lift out. Cost: 120 to 280 NZD for the panel, zero installation cost if you do it yourself.

Microchip Cat Flaps Explained

If you live anywhere in New Zealand where other cats roam, and that is most of the country, a microchip cat flap is worth every dollar. Here is why:

  • No collar needed. The flap reads the microchip already implanted under your cat\'s skin. No collars to lose, no magnets to replace.
  • Selective entry. Only cats whose chip numbers you have programmed can enter. Your neighbour\'s cat, stray toms, and even small possums are locked out.
  • Multiple cats. Most microchip flaps store 25 to 32 chip numbers. If you have three cats, they can all come and go while the local stray stays outside.
  • Vet mode. Some models have a vet mode that locks the flap after your cat enters, useful for keeping them inside after vet visits or medication.
  • Curfew mode. Set the flap to lock at a specific time, helping you enforce cat curfews that many NZ councils recommend or require.
Best NZ brands: SureFlap (now PetSafe) is the most popular microchip cat flap in New Zealand. The PetSafe Microchip Cat Flap is available from Mitre 10, Bunnings, and most pet stores for 180 to 250 NZD. The Cat Mate Super Selective is a cheaper alternative at around 150 to 180 NZD.

Rental-Friendly Options

If you rent in New Zealand, modifying doors or walls usually requires landlord permission. The Residential Tenancies Act 1986 states that tenants must not make any alterations without the landlord\'s written consent. Here are your options:

Option 1: Sliding door pet panel

The best option for renters. No modifications needed. Fits into the existing sliding door track. Available in sizes for both cats and small to medium dogs. Remove it when you move out and the landlord will never know it was there. Cost: 120 to 280 NZD.

Option 2: Window insert cat flap

A cat flap mounted in a panel that sits in an open window, with adjustable sides to fit the window frame. Similar principle to the sliding door panel but for windows. Works best on sliding or casement windows. Cost: 80 to 180 NZD.

Option 3: Ask permission

Some landlords are pet-friendly and may agree to a cat flap installation, especially if you offer to restore the door when you leave. Get this in writing. A cat flap in a wooden door can be patched and painted. A hole in a glass panel is harder to reverse.

Tenancy tip: Under NZ tenancy law, any modification made without written landlord consent is a breach of the tenancy agreement. Even if the landlord seems relaxed, get it in writing. A text message counts. If you move out and there is a cat flap hole the landlord did not approve, they can deduct repair costs from your bond.

New Zealand-Specific Considerations

Weatherproofing

New Zealand\'s weather ranges from subtropical in Northland to alpine in Central Otago. A basic single-flap cat door that works fine in Auckland will let cold draughts into a Dunedin home. For South Island homes or anywhere prone to cold southerlies, look for:

  • Double-flap pet doors with an inner and outer flap for insulation
  • Brush-sealed edges that block wind and rain
  • Insulated tunnel extensions for wall installations
  • Magnetic or snap-close flaps that seal shut after your pet passes through

Cat containment laws

Some New Zealand councils and regional authorities have introduced cat containment requirements, particularly in areas near native wildlife habitats. If your area requires cats to be indoors at night or contained 24/7, a lockable or curfew-mode cat flap lets you comply while still giving your cat outdoor access during approved hours. Check your local council\'s website for current regulations.

Wildlife protection

New Zealand has unique wildlife that is vulnerable to cat predation. A microchip cat flap prevents other animals from entering, but it does not stop your cat from hunting outside. Consider:

  • Keeping your cat indoors at dawn and dusk when native birds are most active
  • Using a curfew-mode flap that locks at sunset
  • Attaching a bell or bib to your cat\'s collar to warn prey
  • Building a cat enclosure (catio) for safe outdoor access without hunting

Insulation and building code

New Zealand\'s Building Code (Clause H1) sets minimum insulation standards. A large, poorly sealed pet door can be a significant source of heat loss. When choosing a pet door, check the insulation rating and look for models with thermal breaks. This is especially important in newer, well-insulated homes where a single draughty pet door can undermine the entire thermal envelope.

Training Your Pet to Use the Flap

Some pets figure it out immediately. Others need convincing. Here is a training progression that works for most cats and dogs:

Step 1: Remove the flap entirely

If possible, take the flap off or tape it open so it creates an unobstructed doorway. Place treats on the other side and encourage your pet to walk through the open frame. Do this for two to three days until your pet is comfortable passing through the hole.

Step 2: Prop the flap partially open

Tape the flap open at a 45-degree angle so your pet can see through but needs to push past it slightly. Reward every successful pass. This teaches your pet that the flap moves and that is okay.

Step 3: Lower the flap gradually

Over several days, lower the flap incrementally until it hangs freely. Stand on one side with treats and encourage your pet to push through. For cats, a laser pointer or wand toy on the other side works brilliantly.

Step 4: Add the lock

Once your pet is confidently using the flap, introduce the locking mechanism. Show them that sometimes the flap does not open, and call them to the door when you unlock it. This prevents confusion and frustration.

Patient pets: Some cats, particularly older or anxious ones, may take two to four weeks to fully accept a cat flap. Do not rush the process. Forcing a scared cat through the flap will make them fear it. Let them learn at their own pace with positive reinforcement only.

Safety and Security

Security concerns

A pet door is, by definition, a hole in your home. While no cat or small dog door is large enough for a person to climb through, it is large enough for a hand to reach in and unlock a door. Mitigate this risk by:

  • Choosing a lockable pet door and locking it when you are away or asleep
  • Installing the pet door in a door that leads to a laundry or conservatory rather than directly into the main living area
  • Using a microchip cat flap that stays locked unless your pet approaches
  • Checking your insurance policy for pet door exclusions

Draught and weather

A poorly sealed pet door can let in rain, wind, and cold air. In New Zealand\'s changeable climate, this matters. Look for models with brush seals, double flaps, or magnetic closures that seal tightly. Apply silicone sealant around the frame during installation to eliminate gaps.

Pet safety outdoors

A cat flap gives your pet independent outdoor access, but it also means they can go outside unsupervised. Consider:

  • Setting a curfew (locking the flap at dusk) to reduce wildlife encounters and road risk
  • Ensuring your yard is fully fenced before giving a dog unsupervised outdoor access
  • Microchipping your pet and registering them on the NZ Companion Animal Register
  • Using a reflective collar if your cat goes out at dawn or dusk

Making It Work for Your Home

The right pet door depends on your pet, your home, and your budget. For most New Zealand cat owners, a microchip cat flap installed in a wooden door is the sweet spot of security, convenience, and cost. For renters, a sliding door panel is the way to go. For large dogs, an insulated four-way lockable door keeps warmth in and unwanted visitors out.

Whatever you choose, take the time to measure your pet properly, choose the right size, and train them patiently. A pet door that is too small is frustrating. One that is too big is draughty and insecure. One that your pet is afraid to use is just an expensive hole in your door.
