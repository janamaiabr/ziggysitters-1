Pet-Friendly Holiday Parks & Campsites in New Zealand (2026 Guide)

2026-07-20 · 12 min read · ZiggySitters Team

New Zealand is one of the best countries in the world for a holiday with your pet. From coastal holiday parks with dog-friendly cabins to inland campsites surrounded by walking trails, there are hundreds of options that welcome four-legged family members. The trick is knowing where to look, what to expect, and how to prepare.

This guide covers everything you need to plan a stress-free NZ holiday with your pet: the best pet-friendly holiday parks by region, campsite rules, booking tips, and practical advice that most guides skip.

Why Choose a Holiday Park Over Freedom Camping?

New Zealand has strict freedom camping rules, and most local councils prohibit dogs at freedom camping sites entirely. Holiday parks and commercial campsites are the reliable option because they have clear pet policies, proper facilities, and designated exercise areas. You avoid the risk of a $200 instant fine and the stress of turning up somewhere only to be turned away.

Holiday parks also offer real advantages: powered sites for your campervan, communal kitchens, proper showers, and often a dog wash area or off-lead exercise space. Many now have dedicated pet cabins so you can skip the tent entirely.

Understanding NZ Holiday Park Pet Rules

Every holiday park sets its own pet policy. Here is what you need to know before booking:

  • Advance declaration: Almost all parks require you to declare your pet at the time of booking. Showing up unannounced with a dog can result in being turned away.
  • Breed restrictions: Some parks restrict breeds classified as menacing under NZ law (American Pit Bull Terrier, Dogo Argentino, Brazilian Fila, Japanese Tosa, Perro de Presa Canario). A few parks go further and restrict large breeds generally.
  • On-lead rule: Nearly every holiday park requires dogs on lead at all times in common areas. Off-lead exercise happens in designated dog areas or outside the park boundary.
  • Number limits: Most parks cap pets at two per site or cabin.
  • Vaccination proof: Many parks ask for current vaccination records. Have a photo of your dog\'s vaccination certificate on your phone.
  • Supervision: Dogs must not be left unattended in cabins or on sites. If you want to visit an attraction that does not allow pets, arrange a pet sitter for the day.
  • Pet surcharge: Expect an additional fee of $5–$15 per pet per night on top of the standard site or cabin rate.

Best Pet-Friendly Holiday Parks by Region

Auckland & Northland

Waiwera Thermal Holiday Park (Waiwera, north of Auckland) — One of the few Auckland-region parks that welcomes dogs. Set in native bush with thermal pools on site. Dogs allowed on powered and unpowered sites with prior approval. Close to regional walking tracks.

Russell Holiday Park (Bay of Islands) — A relaxed coastal park in the heart of Russell. Dogs welcome on sites (not cabins) with prior notice. Walking distance to dog-friendly beaches along the Russell waterfront.

Taupo Bay Holiday Park (Far North) — A laid-back beachside campground that allows well-behaved dogs on sites. The adjacent beach is great for off-lead exercise in the early morning.

Waikato & Coromandel

Hot Water Beach TOP 10 Holiday Park (Coromandel) — Walking distance to the famous Hot Water Beach. Dogs allowed on sites and in select pet-friendly cabins with prior booking. A short drive to multiple dog-friendly beaches including Hahei.

Raglan Holiday Park (Raglan) — One of the most dog-friendly towns in NZ and the holiday park reflects that. Dogs welcome on sites year-round. The adjacent beach and estuary walk are off-lead friendly at certain times.

Waitomo Holiday Park (Waitomo Caves) — Set among farmland near the famous glow worm caves. Dogs allowed on sites with prior approval. While you explore the caves, your dog can relax at the park or with a local pet sitter.

Bay of Plenty & East Cape

Mount Maunganui Beachside Holiday Park (Tauranga) — Right at the base of Mauao with direct beach access. Dogs allowed on sites with strict on-lead rules. The beach walk around the Mount is a highlight. Book well ahead — this park fills up fast.

Ocean Beach Holiday Park (Whakatane) — A quieter alternative with beach access and large powered sites. Dogs welcome with prior arrangement. Great base for exploring White Island (book a pet sitter for boat trips).

Central North Island

Turangi Holiday Park (Turangi, near Lake Taupo) — An excellent base for fishing and walking. Dogs allowed on sites. The Tongariro River trail is a fantastic walk with dogs. Close to the Tongariro Alpine Crossing shuttle (dogs not permitted on the Crossing itself).

Oaklands Motor Lodge & Holiday Park (Rotorua) — One of the few Rotorua parks that accepts dogs. Walking distance to the Redwoods Treewalk area where dogs are welcome on the ground trails.

Wellington & Lower North Island

Wellington TOP 10 Holiday Park (Lower Hutt) — Dogs allowed on sites with advance notice. Close to Hutt River trails and dog parks. Easy access to Wellington via train (dogs travel free on Greater Wellington trains).

Levin Holiday Park (Levin) — A simple, well-run park that is explicitly dog-friendly on sites. Large grass areas and close to Horowhenua beaches where dogs can run off-lead in designated areas.

Nelson & Marlborough (Top of the South)

Nelson City TOP 10 Holiday Park (Nelson) — A well-located park close to the city centre that accepts dogs on sites. The adjacent Matai River path is a great daily walk. Close to the Centre of New Zealand walk.

Discovery Park (Marahau, Abel Tasman) — At the gateway to Abel Tasman National Park. Dogs not allowed in the National Park itself, but the park has pet-friendly accommodation and fenced areas. Great base with a pet sitter arranged for your kayak or walking trip.

Christchurch & Canterbury

Christchurch TOP 10 Holiday Park (Papanui) — A well-maintained urban park that welcomes dogs on powered sites with prior approval. Close to dog-friendly Hagley Park with massive off-lead areas.

Akaroa Holiday Park (Akaroa) — A picturesque harbour-side park. Dogs allowed on sites with prior arrangement. The Banks Peninsula walks are stunning and many are dog-friendly outside of kiwi breeding zones.

Queenstown & Central Otago

Queenstown TOP 10 Holiday Park (Queenstown) — Dogs allowed on sites with advance booking. Close to the Queenstown Gardens and lakefront walks. Many trails around Queenstown welcome leashed dogs.

Wanaka Holiday Park (Wanaka) — One of the most scenic holiday parks in NZ. Dogs welcome on sites. Mount Iron track and lakeside walks are dog-friendly. Book early in summer — Wanaka is extremely popular.

West Coast

Hokitika Holiday Park (Hokitika) — A relaxed West Coast park that welcomes dogs on sites. The beach is right there and the sunset walk with your dog is unbeatable. Close to the Hokitika Gorge.

Greymouth TOP 10 Holiday Park (Greymouth) — Dogs allowed on sites. A solid base for exploring the West Coast. Point Elizabeth walk is a nearby dog-friendly trail.

What to Pack for a Holiday Park Stay with Your Pet

Packing for your pet is just as important as packing for yourself. Here is a practical checklist:

  • Vaccination records — Digital copy on your phone and a printed backup
  • Council registration tag — Legally required for all NZ dogs in public
  • Lead and harness — Bring a spare lead; they get lost on holiday
  • Portable water bowl — Collapsible bowls save space and keep your dog hydrated on walks
  • Poop bags — Bring more than you think you need. Some parks supply them, many do not
  • Dog bed or blanket — Something familiar from home reduces anxiety in a new environment
  • Towel — Beach visits and wet West Coast weather mean muddy paws
  • Tie-out stake or cable — For your campsite if the park allows it (check first)
  • Food and medication — Bring enough for the trip plus two extra days. Rural vet access can be limited
  • Pet first aid kit — Bandages, antiseptic, tweezers for grass seeds, tick removal tool
  • Flea and tick prevention — Apply before your trip, especially for Northland and West Coast visits

Seasonal Considerations

Summer (December–February)

Peak season means peak demand. Book pet-friendly parks at least 2–3 months ahead. Hot weather brings sandflies, ticks, and hot pavement. Walk your dog early morning or late evening. Always carry water. Some beaches have dog restrictions during peak hours (typically 10am–6pm).

Autumn (March–May)

The sweet spot for NZ pet holidays. Fewer crowds, mild weather, and most parks still have full facilities. Easter is the one busy weekend to book ahead for. Autumn trails are spectacular and mostly dog-friendly.

Winter (June–August)

Many holiday parks reduce facilities or close altogether in winter. Check opening dates before booking. Northland, Bay of Plenty, and Nelson are the best winter destinations with milder temperatures. Pack warm bedding for your dog — nights drop below 5 degrees in most of the South Island.

Spring (September–November)

Lambing season means many farms and rural walking tracks restrict dog access from August through October. Check local council and DOC websites before planning walks. Spring weather is unpredictable — pack for all four seasons in one day.

Holiday Parks vs Pet Sitters: When to Choose What

Not every holiday works with a pet. If your itinerary includes national parks, boat tours, wine tastings, or multi-day hikes where dogs are not allowed, consider the combination approach:

  • Bring your pet when your days are flexible, you are staying in one place, and your activities are mostly outdoor
  • Use a pet sitter when you have full days of pet-free activities, you are moving between locations fast, or your accommodation is not pet-friendly
  • Combine both — Bring your pet for the road trip portion and arrange a local pet sitter for specific day trips. Many holiday parks can recommend local sitters

Tips for a Smooth Holiday Park Stay

  1. Book early and mention your pet. Pet-friendly sites and cabins are limited. Call the park directly if the online booking form does not have a pet option.
  2. Arrive before dark. Setting up a campsite with an excited dog is much easier in daylight. Your dog can also explore the boundaries before nightfall.
  3. Introduce your dog to neighbours. A quick hello goes a long way. Other campers are more tolerant when they know your dog is friendly and supervised.
  4. Respect quiet hours. Most parks enforce 10pm–7am quiet hours. If your dog is a barker, consider a cabin away from high-traffic areas or discuss anti-anxiety options with your vet before the trip.
  5. Always pick up. This is the number one reason parks ban pets. Carry bags everywhere. No exceptions.
  6. Never leave your dog unattended. This is a universal park rule and for good reason. Anxious dogs bark, destroy property, and create complaints.
  7. Use the exercise area. Let your dog burn energy in the designated off-lead area before settling in for the evening. A tired dog is a quiet dog.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Assuming all TOP 10 parks accept pets. They do not. Each park sets its own policy. Always check before booking.
  • Forgetting council dog registration. If you are travelling between districts, your home registration is still valid, but you need the tag on your dog at all times.
  • Letting your dog off-lead in the park. Even if your dog is perfectly behaved, this violates most park rules and creates issues with other guests and their dogs.
  • Ignoring kiwi zones. In Northland, Coromandel, and parts of the West Coast, dogs pose a serious threat to kiwi. Stick to formed tracks, keep dogs on short leads, and obey all DOC signage.
  • Not booking ahead in summer. Pet-friendly sites are the first to sell out. Book 2–3 months ahead for December–February travel.
