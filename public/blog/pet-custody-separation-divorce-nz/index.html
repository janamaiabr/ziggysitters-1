Pet Custody After Separation & Divorce in New Zealand

Published 30 June 2026 • 10 min read • By ZiggySitters

When a relationship ends, the question of who keeps the dog, cat, or other pet can become one of the most emotionally charged disputes. In New Zealand, the law treats pets as property — not as family members with rights. This means the legal framework for pet custody is fundamentally different from child custody, and most couples are surprised by how little protection the law offers their animals. This guide explains how pet ownership works under NZ law, what options you actually have, and how to create a practical care agreement that puts your pet first.

Quick Answer: How Does Pet Custody Work in NZ?

Under New Zealand law, pets are classified as personal property. There is no formal pet custody system like there is for children. If you and your ex-partner cannot agree, the court will treat your pet the same way it treats a television or a car — it decides who owns it and divides it under relationship property rules. The solution is to create a written pet care agreement that covers who keeps the animal, visitation, cost-sharing, and decision-making, and file it as part of your separation agreement.

How NZ Law Treats Pets

The legal reality is blunt: New Zealand legislation does not recognise pets as sentient beings with their own rights in property disputes. The key statutes are:

  • Property (Relationships) Act 1976 — classifies pets as relationship property when acquired during the relationship, or separate property if owned before the relationship began
  • Animal Welfare Act 1999 — creates obligations for anyone who owns or is responsible for an animal, but does not address custody or care arrangements between separating partners
  • Family Proceedings Act 1980 — allows for separation agreements that can include pet arrangements, but only as contractual terms, not as enforceable custody orders
Key Distinction: Child custody focuses on the best interests of the child. Pet disputes focus on who owns the property. The court will not consider which person the dog prefers or who provides better care unless both parties agree to include those factors in a contractual arrangement.

Legal ownership is the starting point for any dispute. Evidence of ownership includes:

Before vs During the Relationship

If you owned the pet before the relationship, it is generally your separate property. If you acquired the pet together during the relationship, it is relationship property and subject to equal sharing under the Property (Relationships) Act. This distinction matters enormously because it determines whether you have an automatic entitlement or need to negotiate.

ZiggySitters Tip: If you brought a pet into the relationship, make sure the microchip and registration are still in your name. Many couples re-register pets in one person's name without realising this creates a legal ownership presumption. If you want joint ownership, register both names.

Creating a Pet Care Agreement

A pet care agreement is the most practical tool available to separating couples in New Zealand. Unlike court orders, it is flexible, inexpensive, and can prioritise the animal's welfare. Here is what a thorough agreement should cover:

1. Primary Residence

Who will the pet live with most of the time? Consider whose home is more suitable — space, yard access, proximity to the usual vet, and who has more time for daily care. If you have multiple pets, address whether they stay together or separate.

2. Visitation and Time-Sharing

If both people want ongoing contact with the pet, define the schedule clearly:

  • Weekend visits (every second weekend, for example)
  • Holiday arrangements (Christmas, public holidays, summer breaks)
  • Drop-off and pick-up logistics — who transports the pet and where
  • How changes to the schedule are communicated and agreed

3. Financial Responsibilities

Be explicit about money. Vague agreements about “sharing costs” lead to conflict later. Address:

  • Routine veterinary care (annual check-ups, vaccinations, flea and worm treatment)
  • Emergency vet visits — who pays and who makes decisions
  • Pet insurance — who holds the policy and who pays premiums
  • Food, grooming, and day-to-day expenses
  • Boarding or pet sitting costs when neither person is available

4. Decision-Making Authority

Who decides if the pet needs surgery? Who chooses the vet? Who decides on euthanasia if the situation arises? These are the questions that cause the worst disputes. Your agreement should specify:

  • Routine decisions: primary caregiver decides
  • Major medical decisions: both parties must agree, with a tiebreaker mechanism (such as the treating vet making the recommendation)
  • End-of-life decisions: advance directive stating preferences

5. What Happens if Circumstances Change

Include provisions for:

  • One person moving overseas or to a different city
  • One person becoming unable to care for the pet (illness, financial hardship)
  • New partners or children entering the household
  • The pet developing special needs as it ages

When You Cannot Agree: Dispute Resolution

If negotiation fails, you have several options before going to court:

Mediation

Family mediation through organisations like the Family Dispute Resolution service or private mediators can help you reach agreement without court. Mediators can help frame the discussion around the pet's welfare even though the law does not require it. Costs range from free (community mediation) to several hundred dollars for private sessions.

Arbitration

An arbitrator can make a binding decision about the pet as property. This is faster and cheaper than court but still treats the animal as an asset. It is best used when both parties agree in advance to accept the arbitrator's decision.

Family Court

If the dispute is part of a wider relationship property settlement, the Family Court will deal with it as property division. The court will consider:

  • Legal ownership (whose name is on the documents)
  • Financial contribution to the pet's care
  • Whether the pet is relationship property or separate property

The court will not consider the emotional bond between the pet and either person. It will not order visitation or shared care arrangements. If you want those, you need a contractual agreement, not a court order.

Important: Going to court over a pet is expensive, stressful, and almost never results in shared care arrangements. The court treats your pet as a thing to be divided, not a being to be cared for. A written agreement is always the better path.

Realistic Pet Sharing Arrangements

Shared pet care between ex-partners is common in New Zealand but rarely looks like the idealised version people imagine. Here are realistic models that work:

Weekend Sharing

The most common arrangement. The primary caregiver has the pet Monday to Friday, and the other person has the pet every second weekend. This works best for dogs who adjust easily to different homes. Cats generally do not cope well with frequent moves.

Holiday and Extended Visits

Some couples prefer less frequent but longer contact. The pet spends school holidays or extended breaks with the other person. This reduces transition stress but means longer gaps between visits.

Single Home with Visitation

The pet stays in one home full-time, and the other person visits for walks, play time, or care sessions. This is the most stable option for cats and small animals who are stressed by travel.

One Person Takes Over Completely

Sometimes the cleanest solution is for one person to keep the pet with a right of first refusal — meaning if they can no longer care for the animal, they must offer it to the other person before rehoming. This is especially common when one person is moving overseas or into housing that does not allow pets.

ZiggySitters Tip: If you share a dog between two homes, keep duplicate sets of essentials — bed, bowls, leash, toys, and food — at both houses. This reduces transition anxiety and makes handovers smoother. For cats, strongly consider a single-home arrangement with visitation instead.

Pet Care During the Transition Period

The period between deciding to separate and finalising arrangements is often the messiest time for pet care. Practical steps:

  • Do not use the pet as leverage. Withholding access to the pet to punish your ex-partner harms the animal and creates legal problems.
  • Establish a temporary arrangement immediately. Even a short-term written agreement prevents misunderstandings.
  • Maintain the pet's routine. Keep feeding times, walking schedules, and vet appointments consistent.
  • Use a pet sitter for neutral care. If neither person can care for the pet temporarily, a professional pet sitter provides stability without taking sides.
  • Keep records. Document who has the pet, when, and what expenses were paid. This helps if the dispute escalates.

Special Considerations for Different Pets

Dogs

Dogs are the most commonly disputed pet in NZ separations. They bond strongly with their people and can develop separation anxiety when routines change. A dog that shuttles between homes needs consistent rules, feeding schedules, and commands at both locations. Consider whether the dog has bonded more with one person — not legally relevant, but practically important for the animal's welfare.

Cats

Cats are territorial and almost always better off staying in one home. Moving a cat between houses every week causes significant stress, which can manifest as hiding, inappropriate toileting, aggression, or illness. If you share a cat, let the cat stay in its primary home and the other person visits there.

Small Animals and Birds

Rabbits, guinea pigs, birds, and reptiles are even more sensitive to environmental change than cats. They should almost always remain in one stable home. Their care requirements are specific and complex — a person unfamiliar with their needs may unintentionally cause harm.

Multiple Pets

If you have bonded pairs or a multi-pet household, consider whether splitting the group causes harm. Dogs that have lived together for years may become distressed when separated. Cats that tolerate each other may become stressed when one disappears. Address the group, not just individual animals.

Protecting Your Pet Before Problems Arise

If you are in a relationship and own a pet together, or are thinking about getting one, take these steps now:

  1. Register the microchip in both names — this creates a presumption of joint ownership
  2. Keep records of who pays for what — vet bills, food, insurance, registration
  3. Discuss a plan openly — have a conversation about what would happen to the pet if you separated, before it becomes adversarial
  4. Consider a pre-nuptial or contracting-out agreement — under the Property (Relationships) Act, you can agree in advance how pets will be treated
  5. Keep adoption or purchase documentation — original receipts, SPCA adoption papers, breeder contracts

Pet Insurance and Financial Protection

Pet insurance becomes even more important during and after separation. Consider:

  • Who holds the policy? — the person whose name is on the policy receives payouts and makes claims
  • Policy transfer — if the pet moves to a new owner, the insurance policy must be transferred or a new one started; gaps in coverage can be costly
  • Cost-sharing in the agreement — specify whether premiums are shared 50/50 or paid by the primary caregiver
  • Excess payments — agree in advance who pays the excess on claims

Major pet insurers in New Zealand include Petplan, MediPet, Southern Cross Pet Insurance, and AA Pet Insurance. Costs range from roughly $30 to $80 per month depending on the animal, breed, and coverage level.

When Children Are Involved

If you have children together, the pet is often deeply connected to their wellbeing. Practical considerations:

  • Children form strong bonds with pets, and losing a pet during an already traumatic separation compounds their distress
  • The pet should ideally stay with the children wherever they are living
  • If the pet moves between homes, keep the child's routine with the pet consistent — same walk times, same feeding responsibilities
  • Never tell a child they have to choose between parents or the pet

Getting Legal Help

If you need formal legal advice about pet ownership disputes in New Zealand:

  • Community Law Centres — free legal advice for those who qualify
  • Family Court — can process separation agreements that include pet arrangements
  • Private family lawyers — can draft pet care agreements as part of broader relationship property settlements
  • Mediation services — FairWay Resolution and community mediators can help negotiate pet arrangements
ZiggySitters Tip: A pet care agreement does not require a lawyer to be valid, but having one review it ensures the terms are enforceable. At minimum, get independent legal advice before signing anything. A poorly drafted agreement can be worse than no agreement at all.

Key Takeaways

  • Pets are property under NZ law — there is no formal custody system for animals
  • A written pet care agreement is your best protection and should cover residence, visitation, costs, and decision-making
  • Microchip registration, vet records, and adoption papers determine legal ownership
  • Cats and small animals almost always do better staying in one home
  • Dogs can adapt to shared arrangements if both homes maintain consistent routines
  • Court should be a last resort — it treats your pet as property, not family
  • Protect your pet now by registering microchips jointly, keeping records, and having the conversation early
