Why Cats Scratch and How to Save Your Furniture

2026-05-29 · 9 min read · ZiggySitters Team

If you have come home to find your couch shredded, your curtains in tatters, or your wallpaper stripped in long vertical lines, you are far from alone. Scratching is one of the most common behavioural concerns reported by New Zealand cat owners, and it is also one of the most misunderstood. Cats do not scratch your furniture to spite you. They scratch because it is a fundamental, deeply ingrained natural behaviour that serves several essential physical and emotional purposes. Understanding why cats scratch is the first step toward finding solutions that work for both you and your feline companion.

Why Cats Scratch: It Is Not Destruction, It Is Communication

Scratching serves four distinct biological functions. First, it removes the outer dead layer of the claw, revealing the sharp new claw underneath. This is nail care, pure and simple, and it is as natural to a cat as brushing your teeth is to you. Second, scratching leaves visual marks — the gouges in the surface — and scent marks from specialised glands between the cat\'s paw pads. These scent marks contain feline facial pheromones that communicate territorial boundaries and emotional state to other cats. Third, scratching provides a full-body stretch, particularly of the back and shoulder muscles, which helps cats stay physically limber. Fourth, scratching is an emotional release. Cats scratch when they are excited, stressed, frustrated, or even happy. A cat who greets you at the door and immediately scratches the nearest surface is expressing excitement, not destructive intent.

In New Zealand, where approximately 1.2 million households include at least one cat, scratching complaints are among the top reasons cats are surrendered to shelters or relegated outdoors. The tragedy is that nearly all scratching problems can be resolved with the right approach — one that works with the cat\'s instincts rather than against them.

What Normal Scratching Looks Like

A healthy cat scratches multiple times per day, usually after waking from a nap, after eating, or during periods of excitement. Most cats have preferred surfaces and locations. Horizontal scratchers may prefer carpet or rugs, while vertical scratchers seek out tall surfaces like the arms of couches, doorframes, and tree trunks. Some cats prefer sisal rope, others prefer corrugated cardboard, and some will only scratch natural wood. Texture preference is highly individual and understanding your cat\'s preference is key to redirecting the behaviour.

Normal scratching frequency varies, but most cats scratch between 5 and 15 times per day. Each scratching session lasts roughly 5 to 30 seconds. If your cat is scratching obsessively for extended periods or causing self-injury, this may indicate an underlying issue such as fleas, allergies, or anxiety, and you should consult your veterinarian.

When Scratching Becomes a Problem

Scratching becomes a problem when the cat chooses surfaces that humans value — furniture, carpets, wallpaper, doorframes, or speaker grilles. Several factors can increase problem scratching. Boredom and lack of appropriate outlets top the list. A cat who does not have access to appealing scratching surfaces will create their own. Stress is another major driver. Changes in the household such as moving, new pets, renovations, or the absence of a family member can increase scratching frequency as the cat seeks comfort through scent marking. In multi-cat households, territorial tension can escalate scratching behaviour as cats compete for resources and space.

New Zealand\'s housing stock poses particular challenges. Many rentals and apartments have limited space for large cat trees, and landlords may prohibit modifications like wall-mounted scratching posts. Additionally, NZ\'s warm climate means many cats have access to both indoor and outdoor living, which can create inconsistent scratching patterns when outdoor surfaces like fences and tree trunks become unavailable during illness, injury, or indoor-only periods.

Choosing the Right Scratching Post

The single most effective intervention for problem scratching is providing an appealing alternative. But not all scratching posts are created equal, and buying the wrong one is money wasted. Here is what to look for.

Height Matters

Vertical scratchers need posts that are tall enough for a full body stretch. A good scratching post should be at least 60 to 90 centimetres tall for an average cat, and taller for large breeds like Maine Coons. The post must also be stable. If it wobbles or tips when the cat leans into it, the cat will reject it and return to your couch, which does not wobble.

Texture Is Everything

Observe what your cat currently scratches. Carpet? Try a carpet-covered post. Fabric? Sisal rope or fabric might appeal. Wood? Consider a natural log. Many NZ cats love corrugated cardboard scratchers, which are affordable and available at most pet stores. The key is matching the texture to your cat\'s demonstrated preference, not your interior design taste.

Placement Is Critical

Place scratching posts near the surfaces your cat is already scratching. Cats choose scratching locations strategically — they want prominent places where their scent marks will be noticed. Putting a scratching post in a spare bedroom corner defeats this purpose. Put it right next to the couch arm they have been destroying. If your cat scratches near doorways, place a post there. Once the cat is consistently using the post, you can gradually move it to a more convenient location, a few centimetres at a time.

Most cats need multiple scratching stations throughout the home. A good rule of thumb is one scratching surface per cat, per floor, plus one extra. So a two-cat household on a single level should have at least three scratching options.

Redirecting Scratching Behaviour

Providing alternatives is necessary but may not be sufficient on its own. You also need to make the inappropriate surfaces unappealling while making the appropriate surfaces irresistible.

Making Furniture Unappealing

Double-sided sticky tape applied to the scratched areas of furniture is remarkably effective because cats dislike the sticky sensation on their paws. Aluminium foil draped over surfaces works similarly. Commercial deterrent sprays containing citrus or herbal extracts can help, though they need to be reapplied regularly. Plastic furniture protectors that slip over couch arms provide a physical barrier and are available from many NZ pet supply stores. The goal is not to punish the cat but to make the surface feel unpleasant to scratch so they choose the post instead.

Making Posts Irresistible

Catnip rubbed into a new scratching post can attract initial interest, though not all cats respond to catnip. Silver vine, available from many NZ pet shops, is an alternative that appeals to cats who do not respond to catnip. You can also drape toys or feather wands over the post to encourage investigation. When you see your cat using the post, offer immediate praise and a treat. Positive reinforcement builds the habit faster than any deterrent.

What Not to Do

Never punish a cat for scratching. Yelling, squirting water, or physically reprimanding the cat does not stop the behaviour — it simply teaches the cat to scratch when you are not watching, or worse, to fear you. Declawing is illegal in New Zealand and many other countries for good reason: it is the amputation of the last bone of each toe and causes chronic pain, behavioural problems, and increased biting as the cat loses its primary defence. Nail caps, which are glued over the claws, are a temporary cosmetic solution but do not address the underlying need to scratch and can cause stress if applied incorrectly.

Trimming Your Cat\'s Claws

Regular claw trimming reduces the damage from scratching and is a good practice for all indoor cats. Most cats tolerate claw trimming if introduced gradually and paired with treats. Use small animal clippers available from NZ pet stores, and only trim the clear tip of the claw, never the pink quick which contains blood vessels and nerves. If you are unsure, your vet clinic or a professional pet sitter can demonstrate the technique. Most cats need their claws trimmed every 2 to 4 weeks.

When Scratching Signals Something Deeper

Sometimes problem scratching is not about territory or nail care at all. Excessive or sudden-onset scratching can indicate anxiety, particularly separation anxiety or stress from environmental changes. If your cat suddenly begins scratching aggressively after a change in routine — a new work schedule, a house move, a new pet, or your absence for travel — the scratching is likely an anxiety behaviour, not a preference. In these cases, addressing the underlying anxiety through environmental enrichment, pheromone diffusers, or professional behaviour advice is more effective than simply redirecting the scratching.

Scratching and Multi-Cat Households

In homes with multiple cats, scratching serves an important social function. Cats use scent marks to negotiate territory and avoid conflict. When one cat scratches, they are not just maintaining their claws — they are posting a notice that says "I live here." In multi-cat households, providing multiple scratching posts in different locations allows each cat to establish their own territory markers, which reduces tension and the likelihood of conflict. Place posts at room entrances and near resting areas, as these are the most socially significant locations for scent marking.

If one cat is blocking access to scratching posts, this is a territorial control issue, not a scratching issue. Adding more posts in different rooms and on different levels gives subordinate cats their own outlets. Vertical space, such as cat shelves and tall cat trees, also helps by providing multiple escape routes and reducing competition for ground-level resources.

Seasonal and Environmental Considerations in NZ

New Zealand\'s distinct seasons can influence scratching behaviour. During spring and summer, increased daylight and activity levels may lead to more frequent scratching as cats become more energetic and territorial. Indoor-only cats may scratch more during spring as breeding instincts, even in desexed cats, can increase territorial marking behaviour. If your cat has outdoor access, they may scratch less indoors during warmer months when they can mark outdoor territory. Conversely, during winter when cats spend more time indoors, you may see an increase in indoor scratching as they seek to maintain their territory within the home.

Earthquake-prone regions of New Zealand, particularly Wellington and the lower North Island, may also see increased scratching after seismic events. Cats are highly sensitive to vibrations and changes in their environment, and many will increase scent-marking behaviour after earthquakes as they re-establish their sense of territorial security.

A Final Word on Patience

Redirecting scratching behaviour takes time, consistency, and patience. Most cats take 2 to 6 weeks to fully transition from furniture to posts. During this transition period, protect your furniture with covers or deterrents and resist the urge to punish. Celebrate every time your cat uses the post, and remember that scratching is not a behaviour problem — it is a natural need that simply needs an appropriate outlet. With the right combination of appealing alternatives, strategic placement, and gentle redirection, your cat can have their scratching needs met while your furniture stays intact.
