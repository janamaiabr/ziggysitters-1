Pet-Friendly Public Transport NZ Guide: Travelling with Dogs & Cats on Buses, Trains & Ferries

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  May 15, 2026  •  12 min read

New Zealand is one of the more pet-friendly countries when it comes to public transport, but the rules vary significantly between cities, operators, and transport types. Whether you are commuting across Auckland with a small dog, catching the Interislander with your cat, or hopping on a Wellington bus, this guide covers every rule, restriction, and practical tip you need for stress-free travel with your pet.

Public transport is part of everyday life in New Zealand\'s main cities. For pet owners who do not drive, or who simply prefer not to leave their animal companion behind, understanding the transport rules is essential. The good news is that most NZ operators allow pets under certain conditions. The catch is that those conditions are not always obvious, and they differ between Auckland Transport, Wellington Metlink, Christchurch Metro, and regional services.

Auckland Transport (AT) Pet Policy

Auckland is New Zealand\'s largest public transport network, covering buses, trains, and ferries. Auckland Transport has a relatively straightforward pet policy, but there are important details to know before you board.

Buses

  • Small pets in enclosed carriers are permitted on all AT buses
  • The carrier must fit on your lap or under the seat — it cannot block aisles or take up a passenger seat
  • Dogs too large for a carrier are generally not allowed on buses
  • Driver discretion applies: if the bus is crowded, the driver may ask you to wait for the next service
  • No pets are allowed during peak hours (7:00–9:00 AM and 4:30–6:30 PM on weekdays)

Trains

  • Pets in enclosed carriers are welcome on all AT train services
  • Large dogs on a short leash are permitted at the conductor\'s discretion, but you must sit in designated areas
  • Muzzles are recommended but not mandatory for large dogs on trains
  • Avoid peak commuter services if possible — crowded carriages make pet travel stressful for everyone

Ferries

  • Fullers360 and Explore Group ferries (Gulf Harbour, Devonport, Waiheke) allow pets in carriers
  • Waiheke Island ferries are particularly pet-friendly, reflecting the island\'s relaxed lifestyle
  • Some ferry operators allow leashed dogs on outside decks — check with the specific operator before travelling
ZiggySitters Tip: If you are travelling during peak hours, consider booking a pet-friendly rideshare instead. The extra cost is worth avoiding a stressful, crowded journey for both you and your pet.

Wellington Metlink Pet Policy

Wellington\'s public transport network, managed by Metlink, has a reputation for being reasonably pet-friendly, though the hilly terrain and compact carriages present unique challenges.

Buses

  • Small pets in secure, enclosed carriers are allowed on Metlink buses
  • The carrier must not occupy a seat or block walkways
  • Peak-hour restrictions apply on high-demand routes
  • Drivers have the final say on whether a pet is allowed on board

Trains (KiwiRail & Metlink)

  • Pets in carriers are welcome on all Metlink train services (Kapiti, Hutt Valley, Johnsonville, and Wairarapa lines)
  • Leashed dogs may be permitted at staff discretion — always check before you travel
  • Designated pet areas are sometimes available on longer-distance services
  • The Wairarapa and Kapiti lines are popular with pet owners travelling to regional areas
Important: Wellington\'s cable car and funicular services do not allow pets. If you need to get from the waterfront to the botanic gardens with a pet, use the bus or walk the accessible paths instead.

Christchurch Metro Pet Policy

Christchurch\'s Metro bus network follows similar guidelines to Auckland and Wellington, but the city\'s flatter terrain and less congested services make pet travel somewhat easier.

  • Pets in enclosed carriers are welcome on all Metro bus services
  • Carriers must fit on your lap or under the seat
  • Large dogs not in carriers are generally not permitted on buses
  • Christchurch\'s relatively uncrowded services mean fewer peak-hour issues
  • Driver discretion still applies

Regional & Long-Distance Services

Interislander Ferry

The Interislander and Bluebridge ferries crossing Cook Strait between Wellington and Picton are well set up for pet travel:

  • Pets travel free of charge in carriers
  • Dogs on leashes can access designated outside deck areas
  • Pet cabins are available on some sailings (book in advance)
  • Cats must remain in carriers at all times
  • Ensure your pet has access to water for the 3.5-hour crossing

Great Journeys of New Zealand (KiwiRail Scenic)

KiwiRail\'s scenic train services have specific pet policies:

  • Northern Explorer (Auckland–Wellington): Pets in carriers only, must be booked in advance
  • Coastal Pacific (Christchurch–Picton): Pets in carriers with prior arrangement
  • TranzAlpine (Christchurch–Greymouth): Pets in carriers, limited spaces available
Book Ahead: Long-distance and scenic train services have limited pet spaces. Always call ahead to confirm availability and reserve a spot for your pet carrier.

Essential Pet Travel Gear

Having the right equipment makes public transport travel much smoother for both you and your pet:

For Dogs

  • Airline-approved carrier: Hard-sided or soft-sided, well-ventilated, with a secure latch. Measure your dog standing up — they should be able to turn around comfortably.
  • Short leash (1.2m or less): Essential for train travel where leashed dogs are permitted
  • Muzzle: Not always required, but having one avoids issues with cautious staff
  • Travel water bowl: Collapsible silicone bowls are lightweight and easy to pack
  • Poop bags and wipes: Accidents happen — be prepared to clean up immediately
  • Comfort item: A familiar blanket or toy helps reduce anxiety in unfamiliar environments

For Cats

  • Secure cat carrier: Hard-sided is best for public transport. Soft carriers can be crushed in crowded spaces.
  • Absorbent pad: Line the carrier with puppy pads in case of accidents
  • Cover or towel: Drape over the carrier to reduce visual stimulation and keep your cat calm
  • Pheromone spray: Spray Feliway inside the carrier 15 minutes before leaving to help calm your cat

Tips for Stress-Free Pet Travel

Before You Leave

  1. Practise at home first. Let your pet spend time in the carrier before travel day. Feed them meals inside it. Make it a positive space.
  2. Take a short trial trip. Ride one or two stops on a quiet service before attempting a full commute. This helps your pet adjust to the motion and noise.
  3. Exercise before travel. A tired pet is a calmer pet. Give your dog a good walk before boarding.
  4. Avoid feeding right before travel. A light meal 2–3 hours before is ideal. A full stomach plus bus motion equals motion sickness.
  5. Check the weather. On hot days, carriers can overheat quickly. Choose services with air conditioning and avoid midday travel.

During the Journey

  1. Sit near exits. If your pet becomes distressed, you can step off quickly at the next stop.
  2. Keep the carrier on your lap. Do not place it on the floor where it can be kicked or tripped over.
  3. Stay calm yourself. Pets pick up on their owner\'s anxiety. Speak softly and maintain a relaxed posture.
  4. Offer water at every stop. If the journey is longer than 30 minutes, give your pet a drink at each major stop.
  5. Respect other passengers. Not everyone loves animals. Keep your pet contained and quiet.
Peak Hours Warning: Most NZ transport operators discourage or prohibit pet travel during peak commuter hours (typically 7–9 AM and 4:30–6:30 PM weekdays). Even when not explicitly banned, crowded services are stressful and dangerous for pets. Plan your travel outside these times whenever possible.

What If Your Pet Has an Accident?

Accidents happen, even with well-trained pets. Here is how to handle the situation gracefully:

  1. Stay calm. Your pet is already stressed. Do not add to it by panicking or scolding.
  2. Contain the mess immediately. Use your poop bags, wipes, and paper towel to clean up.
  3. Apologise to nearby passengers. A brief apology goes a long way.
  4. Notify the driver or conductor. They may have cleaning supplies and will appreciate being informed.
  5. Move to a less crowded area if possible, or exit at the next stop if the mess cannot be fully contained.
  6. Carry disposable cleaning supplies every time you travel with a pet. Puppy pads, antibacterial wipes, and small rubbish bags are essential.

Travelling with Anxious Pets

Some pets find public transport overwhelming. The noise, vibration, crowds, and unfamiliar smells can trigger anxiety. If your pet is prone to stress:

  • Start with very short trips (one or two stops) and gradually increase distance
  • Use a covered carrier to reduce visual stimulation
  • Try calming aids: Feliway for cats, Adaptil for dogs, or Zylkene supplements (consult your vet first)
  • Travel during off-peak hours when services are quieter
  • Consider a pet sitter instead if your pet simply cannot cope — there is no shame in leaving them at home where they feel safe

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I take my dog on an Auckland bus without a carrier?

Generally no. Auckland Transport requires pets to be in enclosed carriers on buses. If your dog is too large for a carrier, you will need to use a pet-friendly taxi or rideshare service instead.

Are there extra fees for travelling with pets?

On most NZ public transport services, pets travel free as long as they meet the carrier requirements. Uber Pet charges a small surcharge. Some long-distance train services may charge a pet fee — check when booking.

What about assistance dogs?

Registered assistance and guide dogs have the legal right to travel on all forms of public transport in New Zealand, without a carrier, at any time. This is protected under the Human Rights Act 1993 and the Dog Control Act 1996.

Can I take my pet on domestic flights within NZ?

Domestic airlines (Air New Zealand, Jetstar) allow pets as checked baggage or cargo, but not in the cabin. Service animals are the exception. Book well in advance as pet spaces are limited.

My cat hates the carrier. What can I do?

Start carrier training well before travel day. Leave the carrier open at home with treats and familiar bedding inside. Gradually increase the time your cat spends inside with the door closed. Feliway pheromone spray can help reduce stress. For very anxious cats, ask your vet about short-acting anti-anxiety medication.

Conclusion

Travelling with pets on New Zealand public transport is entirely possible with the right preparation. The key points are simple: use an appropriate carrier, avoid peak hours, carry cleaning supplies, and respect other passengers. Every city has slightly different rules, so always check the specific operator\'s policy before you travel.
