Border Collie Care Guide NZ 2026

Published: 25 February 2026 • By ZiggySitters NZ • 9 min read

Border Collies are extraordinary dogs — widely regarded as the most intelligent breed in the world, with an energy level and work ethic that can leave even experienced dog owners breathless. In New Zealand, where the breed has deep roots in farming and mustering, Border Collies are beloved. But caring for one requires real commitment. This guide covers everything a NZ Border Collie owner needs to know — and what to look for in a pet sitter who truly understands this remarkable breed.

Border Collies in New Zealand: A Breed With Deep Roots

New Zealand has a long, proud history with Border Collies. Descended from sheepdogs brought over by Scottish and English settlers in the 19th century, Border Collies became indispensable on NZ farms for mustering sheep across vast, rugged terrain. The breed’s legendary intelligence and tireless work ethic made them perfect for the demands of high-country farming.

Today, while many Border Collies in NZ live as urban or suburban pets, the breed has not changed. A Border Collie born in Auckland has the same genetic drive as one working the Mackenzie Basin in Canterbury. This is both a beautiful thing and a serious responsibility for owners.

Understanding the Border Collie Mind

Before talking about food, grooming, or vet visits, it’s essential to understand what makes a Border Collie tick — because almost every aspect of their care flows from it.

They Are Wired to Work

Border Collies were bred not just to run, but to think. They need a job. In the absence of a real job (herding sheep, competing in agility, learning complex tricks), they will invent one — often one you won’t enjoy. Destructive behaviour, obsessive behaviours, barking, and anxiety in Border Collies are almost always the result of under-stimulation, not disobedience.

The Eye

Border Collies are famous for their intense herding stare, known as “the eye.” They may attempt to herd children, other pets, cyclists, or even cars. This is instinct, not aggression, but it needs to be redirected constructively through training and adequate exercise.

Sensitivity

Despite their driven nature, Border Collies are emotionally sensitive dogs. They pick up on household stress, raised voices, and inconsistency from their humans. They thrive with calm, clear, and consistent handling — and can suffer anxiety when routines are disrupted.

Exercise Requirements: The Non-Negotiable

If there is one thing that separates Border Collie ownership from other breeds, it is the exercise commitment. This is not a dog that will be satisfied with a 20-minute stroll around the block.

Age Exercise Guideline Notes
Puppy (under 12 months) 5 min per month of age, twice daily No high-impact jumping — protect developing joints
Young adult (1–3 years) 2–3 hours per day Mix of physical and mental stimulation essential
Adult (3–8 years) 1.5–2.5 hours per day Agility, fetch, swimming, hiking ideal
Senior (8+ years) 45–90 minutes per day Shorter, gentler sessions; watch for joint issues

New Zealand is, thankfully, an excellent country for meeting these needs. Wide open beaches (many dog-friendly during off-peak hours), forest trails, off-lead parks, and rural areas make it easier than in many countries. In cities like Wellington, Auckland, Hamilton, and Christchurch, knowing which parks allow off-lead exercise is essential.

Best NZ Activities for Border Collies: Dog agility clubs (NZ Dog Agility Federation has clubs nationwide), flyball, frisbee/disc dog, nose work and scent tracking, tramping on dog-friendly tracks, and swimming at safe beaches or rivers.

Mental Stimulation: Just as Important as Exercise

A physically tired Border Collie that has had no mental challenge is only half-satisfied. Mental stimulation is not optional — it is as critical as physical exercise.

Training

Border Collies excel at and genuinely love learning. Teaching new commands, tricks, and problem-solving games is one of the most effective ways to tire them out mentally. A 15-minute training session can be as exhausting as a 30-minute run. Commit to ongoing positive-reinforcement training — this breed never stops learning, and never stops wanting to.

Puzzle Feeders and Enrichment

Swap the food bowl for a puzzle feeder, lick mat, or Kong stuffed with their meal. Scatter feeding in the garden engages their nose and slows eating. These small changes add up to significant mental enrichment across the week.

Dog Sports

If you can commit to it, enrolling your Border Collie in an agility club is one of the best investments you can make. The NZ Dog Agility Federation has affiliated clubs in most major centres. It provides physical exercise, mental challenge, and the satisfaction of a job done well — exactly what the breed craves.

Nutrition and Feeding in NZ

Border Collies are active dogs with high metabolic demands, but they can also be prone to weight gain if activity levels drop (illness, recovery, age). Feeding should match their current activity level, not their potential.

Choosing a Food

Whether you feed a premium dry kibble, raw diet (BARF), or a combination, look for:

  • High-quality protein as the first ingredient (chicken, lamb, beef, fish)
  • No excessive fillers like corn or soy
  • Omega-3 fatty acids for coat health and joint support
  • Age-appropriate formulation (puppy, adult, senior)

New Zealand has excellent locally sourced dog food options. Green Tripe, K9 Natural (NZ-made), and Ziwi Peak (NZ-made) are all well-regarded. For raw feeding, many NZ butchers and pet stores supply affordable chicken frames, beef mince, and offal.

Joint Health Supplements

Border Collies can be prone to hip dysplasia and Collie Eye Anomaly (CEA). Ask your vet about joint supplements (glucosamine, fish oil) as a preventative measure, particularly for working or sport dogs.

Health Note: Border Collies are known to carry the MDR1 gene mutation, which makes them sensitive to certain drugs including some antiparasitic medications (e.g., ivermectin at certain doses). Always inform your vet that your dog is a Border Collie before any medication is prescribed.

Grooming Your Border Collie in NZ

Border Collies come in two coat types: rough (long, feathered) and smooth (shorter, dense). Both are double-coated and shed seasonally — meaning twice a year, you will find fur absolutely everywhere.

Brushing

  • Rough coat: Brush 3–4 times per week with a slicker brush and metal comb. Pay attention to behind the ears, armpits, and the feathering on the legs where matting forms.
  • Smooth coat: Weekly brushing is usually sufficient, with more frequent sessions during seasonal shedding.

Bathing

Border Collies don’t need frequent bathing — every 4–8 weeks is sufficient unless they’ve had a particularly muddy adventure. Use a dog-specific shampoo that won’t strip the natural oils from their double coat.

Nail Trimming and Ear Care

Active Border Collies often wear their nails down naturally on hard surfaces, but check monthly. Floppy-eared dogs are more prone to ear infections; check weekly and clean gently with a vet-approved ear solution.

Common Health Issues in NZ Border Collies

  • Collie Eye Anomaly (CEA): A genetic condition present in many Border Collies. DNA testing before breeding is recommended. Most affected dogs have mild symptoms and live normal lives.
  • Hip Dysplasia: Managed through appropriate exercise during puppyhood, healthy weight, and joint support.
  • Epilepsy: Idiopathic epilepsy occurs at higher rates in Border Collies than in many breeds. Typically managed with medication if seizures are frequent.
  • Obsessive Compulsive Behaviours: Chasing shadows, lights, or balls obsessively. Usually a sign of under-stimulation; consult a veterinary behaviourist if severe.

Finding the Right Pet Sitter for Your Border Collie

This is where breed knowledge becomes critical. A Border Collie left alone for eight hours with only a quick walk will be miserable — and potentially destructive. When looking for a pet sitter, you need someone who genuinely understands the breed.

Questions to Ask a Potential Sitter

  1. Have you cared for Border Collies or other working breeds before?
  2. How much exercise and mental stimulation can you provide daily?
  3. Are you comfortable with active, high-energy dogs?
  4. What would you do if my dog showed signs of anxiety or became destructive?
  5. Can you continue their training routine (basic commands, puzzle feeders, etc.)?

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Sitter suggests a Border Collie will be “fine in the backyard all day.” No, they won’t.
  • No experience with working or herding breeds.
  • Unable to commit to more than one walk per day.
  • No fenced outdoor space for boarding situations.

What a Great Border Collie Sitter Looks Like

The best sitters for Border Collies are often active people themselves — runners, hikers, agility enthusiasts, or those with farming backgrounds. They understand that a Border Collie’s behaviour is a direct reflection of how much quality stimulation they have received, and they take pride in returning a happy, tired dog to you.

Top Tip: Leave your sitter a “Border Collie boredom busters” kit: a stuffed Kong in the freezer, a puzzle feeder loaded with kibble, and a list of 5–10 commands your dog knows. Ask the sitter to do a 10-minute training session each day. Your dog will love them for it.

Preparing for a Pet Sitting Stay

To set your Border Collie (and their sitter) up for success, prepare a thorough care sheet covering:

  • Daily routine: wake time, meal times, walk times, sleep routine
  • Exercise requirements and preferred activities
  • Known triggers (e.g., cyclists, skateboards, cats)
  • Commands they know and how to give them
  • Any medications or health conditions (especially the MDR1 gene)
  • Vet contact details and after-hours vet location
  • Signs that they are stressed vs. content

The more context you give, the better the sitter can serve your dog. Border Collies thrive on consistency — the more closely a sitter can mirror your normal routine, the smoother the stay will be.

Final Thoughts

A well-cared-for Border Collie is one of the most rewarding dogs you will ever share your life with — loyal, brilliant, funny, and endlessly engaged with the world. In New Zealand, with our wide open spaces and outdoorsy culture, the conditions are ideal for this breed to thrive.

But they are not a low-maintenance dog, and they are not a good fit for every household or every pet sitter. Take the time to find carers who genuinely understand the breed — your Border Collie will repay that investment tenfold with their trust, their joy, and their extraordinary company.
