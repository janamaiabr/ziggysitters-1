Pet Dehydration in Summer: Prevention & Treatment Guide NZ

By Juliano Ramos | Updated 16 April 2026 | 10 min read

New Zealand summers can be brutally hot, with temperatures regularly exceeding 25 degrees Celsius in many parts of the country. While we humans can reach for a cold drink or crank up the air conditioning, our pets rely on us to keep them safe and hydrated. Dehydration in pets is a serious condition that can quickly become life-threatening if not addressed promptly.

Whether you have an energetic dog that loves summer runs, a curious cat that spends time outdoors, or a combination of both, understanding how to prevent and recognise dehydration is essential for every pet owner in New Zealand.

Why Pets Are at Risk of Dehydration

Unlike humans, dogs and cats do not sweat in the same way humans do. Dogs have a limited number of sweat glands, primarily in their paw pads, and cats sweat even less. Instead, these animals rely on panting to cool down - a process that loses significant amounts of water through respiration.

When the weather is hot, or when animals are exercised, they lose water much faster than they can replace it. This makes them particularly vulnerable to dehydration, especially during New Zealand's scorching summer months from December through February.

Several factors can increase your pet's risk of dehydration:

  • Age: Puppies, kittens, and senior pets are more vulnerable
  • Breed: Brachycephalic breeds (flat-faced dogs like Bulldogs and Pugs) pant less efficiently
  • Size: Small pets have a higher surface-area-to-body-mass ratio, losing water faster
  • Coat: Thick or dark-coated animals can overheat more easily
  • Health conditions: Pets with kidney disease, diabetes, or other conditions are at higher risk
  • Activity level: Highly active pets lose water more quickly

Signs of Dehydration in Pets

Recognising the early signs of dehydration can save your pet's life. Here is what to look for:

Early Signs

  • Excessive panting: Heavier or faster panting than normal
  • Drooling: More saliva than usual, especially thick or sticky saliva
  • Lethargy: Less energy than usual, seeming tired or weak
  • Reduced appetite: Not interested in food or treats
  • Dry gums: Gums that feel tacky or dry instead of moist
  • Loss of skin elasticity: Skin that does not snap back when gently pinched

Severe Signs (Emergency!)

  • Sunken eyes: Eyes that appear to be recessed or dull
  • Rapid heart rate: Heart beating noticeably faster than normal
  • Vomiting or diarrhoea: Especially if contains blood
  • Collapse: Unable to stand or walk
  • Disorientation: Seeming confused or uncoordinated
  • Refusal to drink: Even when water is offered

The Skin Elasticity Test: Gently pinch a small amount of skin on the back of your pet's neck. In a well-hydrated pet, the skin should snap back immediately. If the skin stays tented or returns very slowly, your pet is likely dehydrated. This is a simple test you can do at home to check your pet's hydration status.

How to Prevent Dehydration in Summer

Prevention is always better than cure. Here are essential strategies for keeping your pets hydrated during New Zealand summers:

Always Provide Fresh Water

Ensure your pet has access to clean, fresh water at all times. Change water daily, or more frequently in very hot weather. Consider having multiple water stations around your home and garden. For outdoor pets, provide water in shaded areas.

Use Pet Water Fountains

Many pets prefer running water to still water. Pet water fountains encourage drinking and can significantly increase water intake. These are especially useful for cats, who are often attracted to moving water.

Add Ice to Water Bowls

On particularly hot days, add ice cubes to your pet's water bowl. This not only helps keep the water cool but provides a fun element that can encourage pets to drink.

Provide Wet Food

Consider adding wet food to your pet's diet during summer. Wet food contains significant amounts of water and can help with hydration. You can also add water to dry food, creating a moist meal that provides additional fluids.

Create Cool Spaces

Ensure your pet has access to cool, shaded areas throughout your home. Use fans or air conditioning strategically. Cooling mats can also provide relief. For outdoor pets, provide a shaded area with good airflow.

Time Your Walks Wisely

Avoid walking your dog during the hottest parts of the day - typically between 11am and 4pm in summer. Walk your dog early in the morning or late in the evening when temperatures are cooler. Consider walking on grass rather than hot concrete or asphalt, which can burn paw pads and increase body temperature.

Limit Exercise

Reduce the intensity and duration of exercise during hot weather. Watch for signs of overheating, and allow your pet plenty of rest and recovery time.

Know Your Pet's Limits

Every pet is different. Learn to recognise your individual pet's signs of heat stress, and respect their limits. If your pet seems tired or reluctant to continue, give them a break.

Treating Dehydration at Home

If you suspect your pet is mildly dehydrated, there are steps you can take at home:

Step 1: Move to a Cool Area

Immediately move your pet to a cool, shaded area or an air-conditioned space. Remove them from direct sunlight and any hot surfaces.

Step 2: Offer Small Amounts of Water

Do not force your pet to drink large amounts of water at once, as this can cause vomiting, which worsens dehydration. Instead, offer small amounts frequently - a few laps at a time.

Step 3: Apply Cool (Not Cold) Water

Apply cool (not ice cold) water to your pet's paw pads, ears, and belly. These areas have less fur and can help bring body temperature down gradually. Using ice or very cold water can cause blood vessels to constrict and actually trap heat.

Step 4: Monitor Closely

Watch your pet closely for improvement. If they are not showing signs of improvement within 15-20 minutes, or if symptoms worsen, seek veterinary care immediately.

When to See a Veterinarian

Some cases of dehydration require professional veterinary treatment. Seek immediate veterinary care if:

  • Your pet shows severe symptoms as listed above
  • Your pet is unable to keep water down
  • There is blood in vomit or diarrhoea
  • Your pet collapses or cannot stand
  • There is no improvement with home treatment within 20 minutes
  • Your pet is a puppy, kitten, or senior animal
  • Your pet has pre-existing health conditions

Veterinarians can provide intravenous fluids, which are the most effective way to rehydrate a pet quickly. They can also assess for and treat any complications of heat exposure.

Special Considerations for Different Pets

Dogs

Dogs are particularly susceptible to dehydration during exercise. Always carry water when walking your dog in summer. Consider using a collapsible water bowl. Be especially careful with brachycephalic breeds like Bulldogs, Pugs, and French Bulldogs, as their compromised airways make them less able to cool themselves effectively.

Cats

Cats can also become dehydrated, especially those that eat only dry food. Provide multiple water sources, and consider a water fountain. Indoor cats may also benefit from ice cubes in their water or added to wet food.

Small Animals

Rabbits, guinea pigs, and other small pets are also at risk. Ensure they have constant access to fresh water in bottles with sipper tubes. Provide fresh vegetables with high water content, such as cucumber and lettuce (avoid iceberg lettuce - it has no nutritional value).
