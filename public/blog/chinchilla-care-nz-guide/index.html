Chinchilla Care Guide NZ: Housing, Diet, Health & Pet Sitting

Published 30 June 2026 • 12 min read • By ZiggySitters

Chinchillas are extraordinary pets — soft, active, and surprisingly long-lived. But they are also one of the most misunderstood small animals in New Zealand. Their dense fur, strict temperature requirements, and 15 to 20 year lifespan make them a serious commitment, not a casual purchase. Whether you are a new chinchilla owner or a pet sitter preparing for your first chinchilla client, this guide covers what you actually need to know to keep these animals healthy and happy in New Zealand conditions.

Quick Answer: What Does a Chinchilla Need?

A chinchilla needs a tall, multi-level cage (minimum 1m wide x 1m deep x 1.5m tall for a pair), temperatures consistently below 25 degrees Celsius, a diet of grass hay and limited pellets, daily dust baths, and a same-species companion. They are nocturnal, long-lived (15 to 20 years), and must never get wet. NZ summers are a genuine risk — air conditioning is not optional in most parts of the country.

Are Chinchillas Legal in New Zealand?

Yes. Chinchillas are classified as unrestricted organisms under the Biosecurity Act 1993, which means you can legally own, breed, and sell them in New Zealand without permits. They are available through breeders and occasionally through Trade Me or rescue organisations. However, import restrictions mean new genetic lines cannot be brought into the country, so the NZ chinchilla population is limited and inbreeding is a genuine concern. Always buy from a reputable breeder who tracks lineage.

Lifespan and Commitment

This is the single most important thing to understand about chinchillas: they live 15 to 20 years. That is not a typo. A chinchilla purchased for a ten-year-old child will still be alive when that child is in their late twenties. This makes them one of the longest-lived small pets, comparable to some parrots. Before getting a chinchilla, consider whether you can commit to two decades of care, including pet sitting arrangements for holidays and contingency plans if your life circumstances change.

Long-Term Commitment: Chinchillas commonly live 15 to 20 years. They are not a starter pet or a short-term commitment. If you are not prepared for a pet that may outlive your current living situation, a chinchilla is not the right choice.

Housing: The Cage and Environment

Chinchillas are active jumpers and climbers. They need vertical space, not just floor space. A cage that looks large enough based on floor area alone is usually inadequate.

Cage Materials and Setup

  • Shelves: Kiln-dried pine boards are the best shelf material. Avoid plastic (chewed and ingested), MDF (toxic glues), and untreated cedar or pine (contains harmful oils). Fleece covers over pine shelves add comfort and are washable.
  • Hide houses: At least one wooden hide per chinchilla. They need a dark, enclosed space to sleep during the day. Multi-entrance hides reduce territorial disputes.
  • Water bottle: Glass bottles mounted outside the cage with a metal guard. Chinchillas chew plastic bottles, and glass is easier to keep clean.
  • Food bowl: Heavy ceramic or mounted stainless steel. Lightweight bowls get tipped over.
  • Dust bath container: A large, flat container that the chinchilla can roll in freely. Do not leave it in the cage permanently — it causes skin dryness.
  • Wheel: If you add a wheel, it must be at least 38 cm in diameter with a solid running surface. Smaller wheels cause spinal damage.

Dangerous Cage Materials

Never use in a chinchilla cage:
  • Plastic shelving, tubes, or hides — chinchillas chew and swallow plastic, causing intestinal blockages
  • Cedar or non-kiln-dried pine bedding — aromatic oils cause respiratory and liver damage
  • Exercise balls — they overheat and restrict natural movement
  • Hamster-style small wheels — spinal damage risk
  • Wire flooring with gaps over 1.5 cm — causes bumblefoot and foot injuries

Temperature: The NZ Summer Problem

Chinchillas evolved in the Andes mountains at high altitude. Their dense fur — up to 75 hairs per follicle, more than any other mammal — is designed to retain heat in cold, dry conditions. This makes New Zealand summers a serious and potentially fatal risk.

Temperature RangeRisk Level
Below 10°CCold stress — provide extra bedding and shelter

25–28°CDanger zone — active cooling required immediately
Above 28°CEmergency — high risk of fatal heat stroke

Cooling Strategies for NZ Summers

  • Air conditioning: The only reliable solution for rooms above 25°C. A portable unit is the minimum; a fixed system is better.
  • Granite or marble slabs: Placed in the cage, these stay cool to the touch and give chinchillas a surface to lie on. Freeze them for extra cooling effect.
  • Frozen water bottles: Wrapped in fleece and placed in the cage, these provide localised cooling. Replace every few hours.
  • Positioning: Keep the cage away from windows, direct sun, and heat-producing appliances. North-facing rooms are cooler in NZ summers.
  • Fans: Fans alone do not cool chinchillas effectively because they do not sweat. Use fans with air conditioning or ice-based cooling, not as a standalone solution.
Heat Stroke is Fatal: Chinchillas cannot sweat and cannot cool themselves effectively. A chinchilla left in a room above 28°C can die within hours. This is not an exaggeration — NZ summer temperatures regularly exceed this threshold. If you are travelling and leaving your chinchilla with a sitter, make sure they understand the temperature requirement and have air conditioning available.

Diet and Nutrition

Chinchilla digestion is specialised and fragile. Their gut flora is adapted to a high-fibre, low-fat, low-sugar diet. Deviations cause serious health problems quickly.

The Core Diet

  • Grass hay (unlimited): Timothy hay is the gold standard. Meadow hay is acceptable. Alfalfa (lucerne) is too high in calcium and protein for adult chinchillas — reserve it for growing kits under six months or nursing females.
  • Pellets (1 to 2 tablespoons per day): Plain, high-fibre chinchilla pellets. Avoid mixes with seeds, dried fruit, or coloured pieces — chinchillas pick out the treats and leave the nutrition. Good NZ-available brands include Burgess Excel and Oxbow Essentials.
  • Fresh water (always available): Filtered or boiled tap water. Change daily and clean the bottle with a bottle brush to prevent bacterial growth.

Treats (Strictly Limited)

Treats should make up no more than 5 percent of the daily diet. Safe options in very small quantities:

  • Dried rose hips (1 to 2 per day)
  • Dried hibiscus flowers (1 to 2 petals)
  • Small piece of apple wood stick (also wears teeth)
  • Half a teaspoon of rolled oats (not instant)
  • One small piece of dried chamomile
Never Feed: Fresh fruit (too much sugar and water), vegetables (cause bloating), seeds and nuts (too much fat), chocolate, dairy, bread, cereal, or any human food. Chinchilla digestive systems are not designed to process these and even small amounts can cause gut stasis or fatal bloat.

Hay Quality in New Zealand

Not all hay sold in NZ pet shops is suitable for chinchillas. Look for:

  • Green colour — brown or yellow hay is old and has lost nutritional value
  • Fresh smell — musty or dusty hay can cause respiratory problems
  • Long strands — short, chopped hay does not wear teeth effectively
  • Stored in dry conditions — damp hay grows mould, which is toxic

Buying directly from NZ hay suppliers or equestrian stores often gives better quality and value than small pet shop bags. A bale of timothy hay stored correctly lasts months and costs a fraction of the per-bag price.

Dust Baths

Dust bathing is not optional for chinchillas — it is how they clean their fur. Their dense coat cannot be washed with water, and without regular dust baths, the fur becomes matted, oily, and prone to fungal infections.

How to Dust Bathe

  • Provide a dust bath 2 to 3 times per week for 15 to 20 minutes at a time
  • Use specifically formulated chinchilla dust — not sand, not play sand, not bath powder
  • Fill a container about 5 cm deep with dust — the chinchilla needs to be able to roll fully
  • Remove the bath after the session — constant access causes dry skin and eye irritation
  • Replace dust every 2 to 3 weeks or when it appears clumpy or soiled

Good chinchilla dust is available from most NZ pet shops and online retailers. Volcanic ash-based dust is the closest to what chinchillas use in the wild. Avoid anything labelled as “bathing sand” — it is too coarse and can damage the fur.

Health and Common Conditions

Chinchillas hide illness well, as prey animals do. By the time you notice something is wrong, the condition may be advanced. Know the warning signs:

Red Flags — See a Vet Immediately

  • Not eating for more than 12 hours — gut stasis can be fatal within 24 to 48 hours
  • Drooling or wet chin — dental disease or malocclusion
  • Lethargy or hunching — pain response
  • Difficulty breathing or wheezing — respiratory infection
  • Bloating or swollen abdomen — gas accumulation, potentially fatal
  • Eye or nose discharge — infection
  • Fur slipping in patches without regrowth — fungal infection or stress

Dental Health

Chinchilla teeth grow continuously throughout their life. Without enough hay to wear them down, teeth overgrow, develop sharp edges, and cause painful mouth ulcers. This is the most common health problem in captive chinchillas. Signs of dental disease include drooling, dropping food, weight loss, favouring soft foods, and pawing at the mouth. Treatment requires veterinary dental work under anaesthesia, which is expensive and may need to be repeated.

Finding an Exotic Vet in NZ

Not all New Zealand veterinarians are experienced with chinchillas. Before you need one urgently, find a vet who treats exotic animals. Good options include specialist exotic clinics in Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch, and some larger veterinary groups with exotic teams. Ask specifically about their chinchilla experience — a vet who sees mostly cats and dogs may not recognise the early signs of chinchilla-specific conditions like gut stasis or dental disease.

Companionship and Social Needs

Chinchillas are social animals. In the wild, they live in colonies of 14 to 100 individuals. A lone chinchilla is a depressed chinchilla.

Pairing Options

  • Same-sex pair (two females): The easiest and most stable pairing. Two females generally accept each other quickly.
  • Same-sex pair (two males): Works well if introduced young or in neutral territory. Adult males may fight if females are nearby.
  • Neutered male with female: Very common and generally harmonious. The male must be neutered to prevent breeding.
  • Single chinchilla: Only acceptable if you can spend significant time interacting daily. Even then, it is a compromise — chinchillas communicate in ways humans cannot replicate.

Introducing a New Chinchilla

Introductions must be done slowly over weeks, not days:

  1. Side-by-side cages: Let them see and smell each other through the bars for at least a week
  2. Neutral territory playtime: Short supervised sessions in a space neither chinchilla has claimed
  3. Swap bedding: Exchange hide houses and fleece items so they get used to each other's scent
  4. Monitor closely: Some chasing and dominance mounting is normal. Fur slipping is a warning sign. Biting or drawing blood means separate immediately
  5. Full cohabitation: Only after multiple successful neutral-territory sessions with no aggression

Exercise and Enrichment

Chinchillas need daily out-of-cage time in a safe, chinchilla-proofed area. This is not optional — lack of exercise leads to obesity, boredom, and behavioural problems.

Chinchilla-Proofing a Room

  • Electrical cords: Cover or remove all cords. Chinchillas will chew through them, which is fatal.
  • Gaps and holes: Chinchillas can squeeze through surprisingly small spaces. Block gaps behind furniture, under doors, and around pipes.
  • Furniture: They will chew wooden furniture legs, baseboards, and anything reachable. Protect or remove items you value.
  • Other pets: Dogs, cats, and ferrets are predators. Never allow unsupervised access to the same space.
  • Toilets and bathrooms: Chinchillas can drown in toilets and the damp environment is dangerous. Keep bathroom doors closed.
  • Windows: Ensure all windows are closed or have secure screens. A chinchilla can jump surprisingly high and far.

Safe Enrichment Items

  • Apple wood sticks and pumice stones for chewing
  • Wooden ledges and platforms to jump between
  • Large PVC pipes (15 cm diameter) for running through
  • Hanging wooden toys designed for chinchillas
  • Cardboard boxes for shredding (plain brown, no ink or tape)
  • Willow balls and tunnels

Pet Sitting for Chinchillas

Finding a pet sitter who understands chinchillas is significantly harder than finding one for a dog or cat. Most pet sitters have never cared for a chinchilla, and the consequences of errors — wrong food, too-hot room, getting the animal wet — can be severe.

What Your Sitter Needs to Know

Before booking a sitter, provide a detailed care sheet covering:

  • Feeding schedule: Exact amounts of hay, pellets, and any treats. Emphasise that fresh fruit, vegetables, and most human food are dangerous.
  • Dust bath schedule: Which days, how long, and where to put the bath container. Make it clear the bath must be removed after use.
  • Temperature requirements: The maximum room temperature and what to do if it gets too hot. Show the sitter where the air conditioning controls are and what temperature to set.
  • Emergency contacts: Your exotic vet's phone number, address, and after-hours contact. Write down the signs that require urgent vet care.
  • Handling instructions: Chinchillas do not like being grabbed. Demonstrate how to scoop them up from underneath, supporting the hindquarters. Never grab by the tail — the fur will strip off (fur slip) and the tail can break.
  • Behavioural notes: What is normal for your chinchilla — activity times, favourite spots, vocalisations — so the sitter can recognise when something is off.

In-Home vs Away Sitting

Chinchillas should almost always be cared for in their own home. Moving a chinchilla to an unfamiliar environment causes significant stress, and the sitter's home may not be temperature-controlled appropriately. In-home sitting also means the chinchilla stays in their established territory, which reduces anxiety and the risk of behavioural problems.

ZiggySitters Tip: When booking a sitter for your chinchilla, specifically ask whether they have experience with exotic animals. A good sitter will be honest about their experience level. If they have not cared for a chinchilla before, provide a detailed care sheet and do a paid trial visit before you leave so you can supervise their handling and feeding technique.

Handling and Taming

Chinchillas are not naturally cuddly. They are prey animals that prefer four feet on a solid surface. With patience, many chinchillas learn to climb on their owner voluntarily and accept gentle handling, but they rarely enjoy being held like a cat or dog.

Safe Handling

  • Approach from the side or front — never from above (predator approach)
  • Scoop from underneath with one hand under the chest and one under the hindquarters
  • Hold close to your body for security — a falling chinchilla can suffer serious injuries
  • Let the chinchilla come to you during playtime rather than chasing it
  • Never grab the tail — fur slip is a defence mechanism and the tail bones are fragile
  • Never squeeze — their ribcage is delicate

Building Trust

Sit near the cage and let the chinchilla approach you. Offer treats like a dried rose hip from your open palm. Speak quietly. Over days and weeks, most chinchillas learn to trust their owner and will climb onto your hand or shoulder voluntarily. Rushing the process always backfires — a chinchilla that has been grabbed or cornered becomes harder to tame, not easier.

Common Mistakes New Chinchilla Owners Make

  1. Buying a cage that is too small. Pet shops sell tiny chinchilla cages that are genuinely cruel. Minimum is 1m x 0.8m x 1.5m for a pair.
  2. Using water to bathe a chinchilla. Their fur traps moisture and cannot dry. This causes fungal infections and hypothermia. Dust baths only.
  3. Keeping them in a hot room. NZ summers kill chinchillas. If you do not have air conditioning, reconsider whether a chinchilla is the right pet for your home.
  4. Feeding treats liberally. Even safe treats cause problems in excess. A chinchilla's gut is delicate — one wrong food item can trigger gut stasis.
  5. Housing a chinchilla alone. They are colony animals. A single chinchilla is a depressed chinchilla.
  6. Using plastic in the cage. Chinchillas chew everything. Ingested plastic causes intestinal blockages that require surgery or are fatal.
  7. Not having an exotic vet lined up before you need one. Emergency exotic vet visits are expensive and stressful. Find one before you bring your chinchilla home.

Key Takeaways

  • Chinchillas live 15 to 20 years — this is a long-term commitment, not a casual pet
  • Temperatures above 25°C are dangerous; above 28°C can be fatal. Air conditioning is essential in most NZ homes
  • Diet is strict: unlimited grass hay, limited pellets, and minimal treats. No fruit, veg, or human food
  • Dust baths 2 to 3 times per week, never water — their fur cannot get wet
  • Pairs or small groups are essential — chinchillas are colony animals
  • Cages must be tall, multi-level, and free of plastic
  • Find an exotic-experienced vet before you need one urgently
  • Always use in-home pet sitters experienced with exotic animals — moving chinchillas causes severe stress
