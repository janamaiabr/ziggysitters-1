How to Start a Pet Sitting Business in New Zealand

2026-05-29 · 10 min read · ZiggySitters Team

New Zealand has one of the highest pet ownership rates in the world. With over 4.6 million companion animals across the country and 64 percent of households including at least one pet, the demand for reliable, professional pet care is enormous. Whether you are looking for a flexible side hustle, a career change, or a way to turn your love of animals into meaningful income, starting a pet sitting business in NZ is more accessible than you might think. This guide covers everything you need to know to launch, grow, and sustain a successful pet sitting operation.

Understanding the NZ Pet Sitting Market

Pet sitting in New Zealand encompasses a range of services: drop-in visits where you check on pets at their home, overnight house-sitting where you stay in the client\'s home, dog walking combined with pet care, and specialised care for animals with medical needs. The market has grown significantly as more Kiwis travel domestically and internationally, and as remote work patterns have increased pet ownership among younger professionals.

Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch represent the largest markets, but there is strong demand in regional centres like Tauranga, Hamilton, Queenstown, and Napier. Holiday destinations such as the Coromandel, Bay of Islands, and Central Otago see seasonal spikes in demand, particularly during summer and school holidays. Pet owners in rural and semi-rural areas often struggle to find reliable sitters, making these markets particularly underserved and potentially lucrative.

The typical NZ pet owner is willing to pay between 25 and 60 dollars per night for house-sitting services, 15 to 35 dollars per drop-in visit, and 20 to 45 dollars per dog walk. Rates vary by region, with Auckland and Wellington commanding the highest prices, and by service complexity, with medical administration, multi-pet households, and exotic animal care commanding premiums.

Legal and Regulatory Requirements

New Zealand does not require a specific licence to operate as a pet sitter, which makes entry relatively straightforward. However, there are important legal and regulatory considerations that separate a professional operation from a casual arrangement.

Business Registration

If you plan to earn more than occasional pocket money, you should register as a business. Sole trader registration through the Companies Office is the simplest structure and costs approximately 60 NZD. You will need an IRD number for tax purposes, and you must declare pet sitting income in your annual tax return. If your turnover exceeds 60,000 NZD per year, you must register for GST. Most new pet sitters will not hit this threshold initially, but rapid growth can sneak up on you, so track your income carefully from day one.

Insurance

Professional indemnity insurance and public liability insurance are essential. Professional indemnity covers you if a pet in your care is injured or becomes ill and the owner claims negligence. Public liability covers damage to client property while you are house-sitting. Several NZ insurance providers offer pet sitter-specific policies, typically costing between 300 and 600 NZD per year. While not legally required, operating without insurance is a significant financial risk. A single claim for veterinary bills or property damage could easily exceed your annual earnings.

Animal Welfare Act 2015

All pet sitters in New Zealand must comply with the Animal Welfare Act 2015, which establishes duty of care requirements for anyone responsible for an animal. This includes providing adequate food, water, shelter, and veterinary care. Understanding your obligations under this legislation is not optional — breaches can result in prosecution, fines, and imprisonment in severe cases. Familiarise yourself with the relevant codes of welfare for the species you plan to care for, available through the Ministry for Primary Industries website.

Skills and Qualifications That Set You Apart

No formal qualification is required to start pet sitting, but having relevant credentials dramatically increases client trust and allows you to charge higher rates. Consider investing in the following.

  • Pet First Aid Certification: Courses through St John or the Red Cross cover basic first aid for animals, including CPR, wound management, and emergency response. These courses typically cost 100 to 200 NZD and take one to two days.
  • NZ Certificate in Animal Care: Offered by several polytechnics and private training establishments, this Level 3 qualification covers animal health, behaviour, and handling. While not necessary to start, it signals professionalism and depth of knowledge.
  • Species-Specific Experience: If you have experience with particular breeds, medical conditions, or exotic animals, highlight this in your profile. Owners of greyhounds, parrots, reptiles, and diabetic cats are desperate for sitters who understand their animals\' specific needs.
  • Driving Licence: Essential for reaching clients across your service area and for emergency veterinary trips.

Setting Your Pricing

Pricing is one of the most challenging decisions for new pet sitters. Set rates too high and you will struggle to win your first clients. Set them too low and you will burn out for inadequate compensation. Research what other sitters in your area charge through platforms like ZiggySitters, then position yourself based on your experience and service level.

A recommended approach for new sitters is to start at the lower end of the local market range for the first 10 to 15 bookings while you build reviews and reputation, then gradually increase rates as demand grows. Always be transparent about what your rate includes. Does overnight house-sitting include dog walking? Are drop-in visits 30 minutes or 60 minutes? Does the rate cover one pet or multiple pets? Clarity prevents disputes and builds trust.

Consider offering package deals for regular clients, such as a discount for bookings of five or more nights, or a reduced rate for daily dog walking contracts. Many NZ pet sitters also offer add-on services like plant watering, mail collection, and bin night management for house-sitting clients, which add value without significantly increasing workload.

Building Your Profile and Reputation

Your profile is your storefront. On ZiggySitters, your profile is the first thing potential clients see, and its quality directly impacts your booking rate. Here is what makes a profile stand out.

Photos

Use clear, well-lit photos that show you with animals. A professional headshot alongside candid photos of you walking dogs, cuddling cats, or caring for pets creates an immediate connection. Avoid blurry photos, group shots where you are hard to identify, or images without animals. Clients want to see that you genuinely enjoy the company of pets.

Biography

Write a bio that is warm, specific, and honest. Mention the animals you have owned or cared for, any relevant qualifications or experience, your approach to pet care, and what you love about working with animals. Avoid generic statements like "I love all animals." Instead, say something like "I grew up with rescue greyhounds and have been providing specialised greyhound sitting for three years, including administering medication and managing their unique dietary needs."

Reviews

Early reviews are gold. Ask every satisfied client to leave a review. Respond to all reviews, positive and negative, with professionalism and gratitude. A thoughtful response to a less-than-perfect review often impresses potential clients more than a string of five-star ratings with no engagement. After your first 10 bookings, you should have enough reviews to establish credibility and attract clients organically.

Marketing Your Pet Sitting Business

Beyond your ZiggySitters profile, several marketing channels are effective for NZ pet sitters.

Local Community Groups

Neighbourly and local Facebook community groups are powerful for finding clients in your area. Post genuinely helpful pet care advice and mention your services casually rather than hard-selling. Residents trust recommendations from engaged community members over anonymous advertisers.

Veterinary Clinics

Introduce yourself to local vet clinics and leave business cards. Vets are frequently asked for pet sitter recommendations, and being top of mind when that question arises is invaluable. Some clinics maintain a list of recommended sitters — ask to be included.

Word of Mouth

Deliver exceptional service and your clients will recommend you to their friends, family, and colleagues. Pet owners trust personal recommendations above all other sources. Each happy client is a marketing asset. Consider offering a referral discount — 10 percent off a future booking for both the referrer and the new client — to incentivise recommendations.

Social Media

An Instagram or Facebook page showcasing your pet sitting adventures builds awareness and trust. Share photos (with owner permission), pet care tips, and behind-the-scenes glimpses of your day. NZ pet owners are active on social media, and a well-curated feed can drive significant organic traffic to your ZiggySitters profile.

The Meet and Greet

Always offer a free meet-and-greet before your first booking. This 20 to 30 minute meeting at the client\'s home serves multiple purposes. You meet the pets and learn their routines, personalities, and any quirks. You assess the home layout, locate supplies, and identify potential hazards. You discuss expectations around feeding, exercise, medication, and communication. Most importantly, you establish trust with both the pet and the owner.

During the meet-and-greet, take detailed notes. Ask about feeding schedules, portion sizes, treat policies, exercise requirements, medication administration, veterinary contact details, emergency procedures, and any behavioural quirks like anxiety during storms or reactivity towards other animals. Document everything in a pet care sheet that you can refer to during the sit. This level of thoroughness is what separates professional sitters from casual ones.

Managing Your Business Operations

Running a pet sitting business involves more than just caring for animals. Efficient operations are what allow you to scale from a side hustle to a sustainable business.

Scheduling and Calendar Management

Use a digital calendar to track all bookings, client details, and availability. Google Calendar is free and integrates well with most booking platforms. Block out personal time, holidays, and buffer periods between sits to allow for travel and rest. Overbooking is the fastest way to burn out and deliver substandard service.

Communication

Establish clear communication expectations with clients. Many sitters send daily photo updates and brief reports during the sit. Decide whether you prefer text messages, WhatsApp, or email, and communicate this preference upfront. Prompt, reliable communication is one of the top reasons clients rebook with a sitter.

Record Keeping

Maintain accurate records of all income and expenses for tax purposes. Track mileage if you drive between clients, as this may be deductible. Keep receipts for pet first aid supplies, insurance premiums, marketing costs, and any other business expenses. A simple spreadsheet or accounting app like Xero, which is built in New Zealand, will suffice for most sole traders.

Handling Emergencies

Emergencies are rare but inevitable in pet sitting. Having a clear protocol protects both you and the animal in your care. Before every sit, ensure you have the client\'s emergency contact number, their preferred veterinary clinic, and written authorisation to seek veterinary treatment up to a specified amount. Carry a pet first aid kit in your car, and know the location of the nearest 24-hour emergency vet to each client\'s home.

If an emergency occurs, contact the client immediately, take the pet to the agreed veterinary clinic, and document everything with photos and notes. Professional indemnity insurance will cover you if the owner disputes the cost or claims negligence, but only if you acted reasonably and in the animal\'s best interest.

Scaling Your Business

Once you have a steady stream of clients and positive reviews, you may reach a point where demand exceeds your availability. This is a good problem to have, and there are several ways to scale.

You can increase your rates gradually as your reputation grows. You can expand your service area, though factor in travel time and fuel costs. You can add complementary services such as dog walking, pet taxi, or small animal boarding. You can hire trusted sitters to work under your brand, though this adds management responsibility and requires additional insurance coverage. Or you can specialise in high-demand niches such as medical pet care, puppy and kitten sitting, or exotic animal care, which command premium rates.
