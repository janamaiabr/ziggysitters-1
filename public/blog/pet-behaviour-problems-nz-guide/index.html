Common Pet Behaviour Problems in New Zealand: Causes, Solutions & When to Seek Help

Every pet owner in New Zealand has been there. Your usually placid dog barks at every passing shadow. Your fastidiously clean cat suddenly refuses to use the litter tray. Your rabbit chews through another charging cable. Pet behaviour problems are not just common; they are practically universal. The challenge is separating normal animal expressions from genuine issues that need intervention, and knowing when a problem you can manage at home has become something that requires professional help.

This guide covers the most frequent behaviour problems reported by NZ pet owners, explains what drives them, and offers practical steps for managing or resolving them. Separation Anxiety in Dogs

Separation anxiety is arguably the most widely discussed behaviour problem in New Zealand dogs, and for good reason. The New Zealand lifestyle, where many households have someone home for much of the day, can inadvertently create dogs that have never learned to be comfortable alone. When a work pattern changes, a relationship ends, or a holiday requires kennels or pet sitting, the dog suddenly confronts a situation they have never practised: being by themselves.

The signs are unmistakable. A dog with separation anxiety may howl or bark continuously within minutes of your departure. They may destroy furniture, chew through doors, scratch walls, or eliminate indoors despite being fully house-trained. They may pace, pant excessively, drool, or refuse food. These behaviours are not spite or boredom; they are panic responses from an animal that genuinely believes being alone is dangerous.

The solution involves gradual desensitisation to alone time. Start with very short absences, literally 30 seconds to a minute, and gradually increase duration over weeks. Pick up your keys, put on your shoes, and then sit back down. Repeat this dozens of times until the departure cues no longer trigger anxiety. Build up to leaving the house for two minutes, then five, then ten. The process requires patience and consistency, but it is the most effective long-term approach.

Environmental management helps while you work on the underlying anxiety. Leave a recently worn piece of clothing in your dog is sleeping area. Use food puzzles or stuffed Kongs to create positive associations with departure. Consider a pet sitter for extended absences rather than leaving an anxious dog alone for hours, because every severe panic episode reinforces the anxiety cycle.

In severe cases, consult your vet about medication. Anti-anxiety medication combined with behavioural modification is far more effective than either approach alone. Your vet can refer you to a qualified veterinary behaviourist if needed; the New Zealand Veterinary Association maintains a directory of practitioners with advanced behaviour qualifications.

Cat Litter Box Problems

When a cat stops using the litter box, it is almost never random. Cats are fastidiously clean by nature, and urinating or defecating outside the box is a signal that something is wrong. The problem is figuring out what.

Medical causes must be ruled out first. Urinary tract infections, bladder stones, kidney disease, arthritis that makes climbing into the box painful, and constipation can all cause litter box avoidance. A vet visit is the non-negotiable first step. In New Zealand, a standard consultation for this kind of investigation typically costs between $50 and $80 at most clinics, and many conditions are easily treatable once identified.

If the cat is medically clear, investigate environmental causes. Is the litter box clean enough? Most cats prefer a box that is scooped at least once daily and fully changed weekly. Is the litter type acceptable? Many NZ cats dislike strongly scented litters or the texture of crystal litters. Have you provided enough boxes? The rule is one box per cat plus one extra, placed in different locations. Is the box in a quiet, low-traffic area? Cats need privacy and escape routes. A box wedged into a corner with only one exit can feel like a trap, especially in multi-cat households.

Stress is the other major driver. Moving house, a new pet, a new baby, building work, or even a change in your schedule can trigger litter box avoidance. In these cases, addressing the underlying stressor is more productive than punishing the behaviour, which will only increase the cat is anxiety. Feliway diffusers, which release synthetic feline facial pheromones, are widely available in NZ pet stores and can help reduce stress-related marking in some cats.

Excessive Barking

New Zealand is a nation of dog owners, but it is also a nation of neighbours, and council noise regulations apply. Most territorial authorities in NZ define excessive barking as a noise nuisance under the Dog Control Act 1996. If your dog barks persistently, your council can issue an abatement notice, and repeated complaints can lead to fines or even removal of the dog. This is not hypothetical; it happens regularly across Auckland, Wellington, Christchurch, and smaller centres.

Excessive barking falls into several categories, each requiring a different approach. Territorial or alarm barking, where the dog alerts to people approaching the property, is managed by limiting visual access to the street and rewarding quiet behaviour. Demand barking, where the dog has learned that barking produces attention or food, is resolved by never reinforcing the barking and instead rewarding calm, quiet behaviour. Separation anxiety barking, as discussed above, requires desensitisation to alone time.

Boredom barking is the category most directly within your control. Dogs that receive insufficient physical exercise, mental stimulation, or social interaction will vocalise to release pent-up energy. For working breeds common in New Zealand, including border collies, heading dogs, and huntaways, a 20-minute walk is barely a warm-up. These dogs need sustained exercise, problem-solving tasks, and social engagement to stay quiet and content.

Anti-bark collars, including citronella spray collars, are available in NZ but should never be the first response. They address the symptom without resolving the cause, and they can worsen anxiety-based barking by adding punishment to an already distressed animal. Use them only under professional guidance, and never on a dog with separation anxiety.

Aggression in Dogs

Aggression is the behaviour problem that scares owners most, and for good reason. A dog that lunges, snaps, or bites is a danger to other animals, to people, and to the legal standing of its owner. Under the Dog Control Act, New Zealand councils can classify dogs as menacing or dangerous, which imposes strict requirements including muzzling in public, neutering, and in severe cases, destruction orders.

Aggression is not a personality trait; it is a behaviour with a cause. Fear aggression, the most common type, occurs when a dog perceives a threat and escalates to violence because escape or appeasement has not worked. Resource guarding aggression occurs when a dog defends food, toys, or resting places. Leash reactivity, extremely common in urban NZ, occurs when a dog feels trapped by the leash and unable to create distance from a perceived threat. Each of these requires a different management and modification approach.

The single most important piece of advice for any dog showing aggressive behaviour is to consult a qualified professional. In New Zealand, look for a canine behaviour consultant registered with the New Zealand Companion Animal Council or a veterinary behaviourist through the New Zealand Veterinary Association. Avoid anyone who promises quick fixes through dominance-based methods; the evidence is clear that punishment-based approaches increase aggression and damage the human-dog relationship.

While working with a professional, manage the environment to prevent practice of the aggressive behaviour. If your dog reacts to other dogs on walks, walk at quiet times and in locations with good sight lines so you can create distance before your dog reaches threshold. Use a front-clip harness or head halter for additional control. If your dog guards resources, feed in a separate area and avoid taking high-value items away by force.

Destructive Behaviour in Puppies and Adult Dogs

Chewing, digging, and shredding are normal dog behaviours. The problem arises when they are directed at your furniture, your shoes, your fence, or your garden rather than appropriate outlets. Puppies chew to explore their world and relieve the discomfort of teething. Adult dogs chew and dig out of boredom, anxiety, or insufficient exercise.

Management starts with providing appropriate alternatives. A range of chew toys in different textures and durability levels gives your dog choices. For NZ dogs that spend time outdoors, a designated digging pit in a corner of the yard, filled with loose soil and occasionally baited with hidden treats, can redirect digging away from garden beds and under fences.

Supervision and redirection are more effective than punishment after the fact. If you catch your dog chewing something inappropriate, interrupt calmly and redirect to an appropriate item. Yelling or physical punishment after the damage is done teaches your dog only that you are unpredictable, not that chewing is wrong.

For dogs with separation anxiety that manifest through destruction, refer to the separation anxiety section above. The destruction is a symptom of panic, not a discipline problem, and resolving the anxiety will resolve the destruction.

Cat Aggression Toward People

Cats that bite or scratch their owners are usually acting from fear, pain, overstimulation, or redirected aggression. Understanding the trigger is essential for modifying the behaviour.

Play aggression is common in kittens and young cats, particularly those that were not adequately socialised with littermates who would have taught them bite inhibition. The solution is to redirect play onto appropriate toys, never hands or feet, and to end play sessions immediately if the cat becomes overstimulated. Wand toys, laser pointers, and kicker toys allow your cat to express predatory behaviour without targeting human skin.

Overstimulation aggression occurs when petting crosses the threshold from pleasant to irritating. Cats give clear warning signs: tail twitching, flattened ears, dilated pupils, and skin rippling along the back. Learn to read these signals and stop petting before the cat reaches their limit. The threshold varies between cats; some tolerate five minutes of petting, others 30 seconds.

Redirected aggression happens when a cat is aroused by one stimulus, such as seeing another cat through the window, and then attacks the nearest available target, which is often the owner who happened to be walking past. This type of aggression can be frightening because it appears completely unprovoked. The management approach is to identify the trigger and block access, for example by closing curtains if outdoor cats are the stimulus, and to give the cat time to calm down before attempting interaction.

Pain-induced aggression should always trigger a vet visit. Cats are masters at hiding discomfort, and aggression can be the first visible sign that something hurts. Dental disease, arthritis, and internal conditions can all make a previously gentle cat defensive when touched in sensitive areas.

Resource Guarding

Resource guarding, where a dog growls, snaps, or bites when approached while eating, chewing, or resting near a valued item, is one of the most misunderstood behaviour problems. The instinctive human response is often to forcibly remove the item to show the dog who is boss. This is precisely the wrong approach and significantly increases the risk of a bite.

The effective approach is to change the dog is emotional response to your approach near valued items. Start by walking past your dog is food bowl at a distance where they show no reaction and tossing a high-value treat. Gradually decrease the distance over many repetitions. The goal is for your dog to learn that your approach predicts something good, not something being taken away. This process, called counter-conditioning, takes time but produces lasting results without increasing aggression.

If your dog guards resting spaces or furniture, manage access by keeping them off furniture until the guarding behaviour has been modified. Provide a comfortable dog bed on the floor and reward your dog for choosing it. For severe resource guarding, especially if children are in the home, seek professional guidance from a qualified behaviour consultant immediately.

When to Seek Professional Help

Not every behaviour problem requires a professional. Puppies chewing furniture is normal. A cat occasionally scratching the couch needs more scratching posts, not a behaviourist. But certain situations call for expert intervention, and knowing the difference can prevent small problems from becoming entrenched.

Seek professional help when:

  • Aggression is involved. Any bite that breaks skin, any growl or snap directed at a person, and any fight between dogs that results in injury warrant professional assessment. Do not wait for a second incident.
  • Anxiety is severe or worsening. If your dog is destroying the house every time you leave, if your cat has stopped eating, or if your pet is showing persistent signs of fear such as trembling, hiding, or refusal to engage, the underlying anxiety needs professional treatment.
  • The problem is escalating. Behaviours that increase in frequency or intensity over time are not going to resolve on their own. Early intervention is faster, cheaper, and more effective than waiting until the behaviour is deeply entrenched.
  • You have tried management without improvement. If you have been consistent with environmental changes, exercise adjustments, and training for four to six weeks with no progress, the problem likely has a component that requires expert diagnosis.
  • Multiple problems coexist. A dog with separation anxiety and leash reactivity, or a cat with litter box avoidance and inter-cat aggression, is dealing with compound stressors that interact in complex ways. Professional guidance can untangle the priorities and develop an integrated treatment plan.

In New Zealand, your first point of contact should be your regular vet. They can rule out medical causes and refer you to a qualified behaviourist. The New Zealand Veterinary Association and the New Zealand Companion Animal Council both maintain directories of accredited professionals. Avoid self-styled dog trainers who rely on dominance theory or promise guaranteed fixes; genuine behaviour modification is evidence-based, individualised, and realistic about timelines.

How a Good Pet Sitter Helps with Behaviour Management

The connection between pet sitting and behaviour management is closer than many owners realise. A pet sitter who understands behaviour can maintain your training progress while you are away, avoid reinforcing unwanted behaviours, and recognise early signs of emerging problems.

When you book a pet sitter, provide a detailed behaviour summary alongside your standard care instructions. Note any triggers your pet has, how they respond to strangers, whether they are comfortable with handling, and any specific management protocols you are following. A sitter who knows to avoid petting your cat past the two-minute mark, or to give your reactive dog extra space on walks, can prevent incidents that would set back your training.

For pets with separation anxiety, a sitter who stays in your home provides continuity that a drop-in visit cannot match. The presence of a calm, consistent human can make the difference between a pet that copes during your absence and one that spirals into distress. Discuss your pet is specific anxiety management plan with your sitter before the booking, and make sure they understand the desensitisation protocols you have in place.

Pet behaviour problems are not a sign of failure. They are a normal part of living with animals that perceive the world differently from us, communicate differently, and express their needs in ways that sometimes clash with human expectations. The key is to approach problems with curiosity rather than frustration, to seek professional help when the stakes are high, and to recognise that most behaviour issues are solvable with patience, consistency, and the right information.
