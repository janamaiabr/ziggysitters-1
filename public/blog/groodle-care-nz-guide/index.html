Groodle Care Guide NZ 2026

By ZiggySitters NZ · Updated March 2026 · 8 min read

Groodles — the beloved cross between a Golden Retriever or Labrador and a Poodle — have become one of New Zealand’s most popular family dogs. Friendly, intelligent, and often low-shedding, they suit NZ lifestyles beautifully. But their unique coat, high energy, and social needs mean they require an owner (and pet sitter) who truly understands the breed.

What Exactly Is a Groodle?

The name “Groodle” is used in New Zealand and Australia to describe a Golden Retriever x Poodle cross (also called a Goldendoodle in the US). Some Kiwi breeders and owners also use the term loosely for Labrador x Poodle crosses (technically Labradoodles), so the lines can blur. Both share a similar temperament profile: playful, affectionate, eager to please, and highly social.

Groodles come in three size ranges depending on the Poodle parent used:

SizeAdult WeightTypical NZ Use
Miniature Groodle7–15 kgApartments, smaller sections
Medium Groodle15–25 kgMost family homes
Standard Groodle25–40 kgLifestyle blocks, active families

First-generation (F1) Groodles are 50% Poodle, while later generations (F1b, F2) have more Poodle influence, typically resulting in curlier, lower-shedding coats — a major reason for their popularity among allergy-prone households.

Groodle Temperament: What to Expect

Groodles inherit the best of both worlds. From the Golden or Labrador side they get warmth, patience, and a love of people. From the Poodle they get sharp intelligence and trainability. The result is a dog that:

  • Bonds intensely with family members and struggles with long periods alone
  • Thrives on learning new commands and tricks — mental stimulation is non-negotiable
  • Tends to get along well with children, other dogs, and cats when socialised early
  • Can develop separation anxiety if left alone too often or too long
  • Is generally gentle and forgiving, making them excellent first-time family dogs
Tip for NZ owners: Groodles are social dogs — they do best when someone is home for most of the day, or when they have the company of a pet sitter or doggy daycare during work hours. Plan for this before you bring one home.

Exercise Needs in New Zealand’s Climate

Groodles need 45–90 minutes of physical exercise per day depending on their size and age. NZ’s varied terrain makes this easy — they love beach runs, bush tracks, and park play. But there are a few climate considerations:

Summer Heat (December – March)

Northern NZ summers can push past 30°C. Groodles with thick, curly coats are vulnerable to overheating. Walk during early morning or evening hours, avoid hot footpaths (the back-of-hand test: if the ground is too hot for your hand, it’s too hot for paws), and always carry water. Beach swims are a favourite — Groodles are typically excellent swimmers.

Winter in the South Island

Groodles handle South Island winters well thanks to their dense coats, but cold, wet conditions can lead to skin issues if the coat stays damp. After beach or river swims, dry thoroughly with a towel and consider a light blowdry session to prevent matting and skin irritation.

Daily Mental Exercise

Physical exercise alone is not enough. A bored Groodle will chew furniture, dig up gardens, or bark incessantly. Daily mental enrichment should include:

  • Training sessions (10–15 minutes — they love it)
  • Puzzle feeders or sniff mats at mealtimes
  • Scent work in the backyard
  • Interactive play with family members

Grooming: The Biggest Commitment for Groodle Owners

This is where Groodle ownership requires the most effort. Their coats — which range from wavy to tightly curled — grow continuously and mat quickly if not brushed regularly.

Brushing Routine

Brush your Groodle at least 3–4 times per week; daily is ideal for curlier coats. Use a slicker brush followed by a metal comb, paying special attention to mat hot spots: behind the ears, under the collar, armpits, groin, and around the tail base. Matting is painful and, if severe, requires a full clip-down at the groomer.

Professional Grooming

Budget for professional grooming every 6–10 weeks. A full Groodle groom in NZ typically costs $80–$150 depending on size, coat condition, and region. Common clip styles include the teddy bear cut, puppy cut, and kennel clip. Introduce your Groodle to the groomer as a puppy if possible — dogs that learn early tolerate grooming far better as adults.

Important: Never shave a Groodle to the skin in an attempt to “cool them down” in summer. Their coat actually provides insulation against heat. A professional tidy and regular brushing are far more effective than shaving.

Ears and Eyes

Floppy ears trap moisture and reduce airflow — Groodles are prone to ear infections. Check weekly, clean gently with a vet-approved ear cleaner, and keep the ear canal hair trimmed. Tear staining around the eyes is common in lighter-coloured Groodles; gentle daily wiping with a damp cloth keeps it manageable.

Feeding Your Groodle in NZ

A high-quality diet is important throughout your Groodle’s life. As a general guide:

Life StageRecommended Approach
Puppy (up to 12 months)Large-breed puppy kibble 3x/day; transition to 2x/day around 6 months
Adult (1–7 years)Quality adult dog food sized to energy level; avoid over-feeding (they love food)
Senior (7+ years)Senior formula with joint support; monitor weight carefully

Raw feeding (BARF diet) is popular in NZ and suits many Groodles well, but requires careful balancing. If you choose raw, work with a vet nutritionist or follow an established NZ-based BARF guide. Whatever you feed, keep treats to under 10% of daily calories — Groodles will eat well beyond their needs if given the chance.

NZ tip: Many Groodles are sensitive to grain-heavy kibble. If you notice loose stools, excessive gas, or itchy skin, try a grain-free or limited-ingredient formula and consult your vet.

Common Health Issues in Groodles

As a hybrid breed, Groodles benefit from hybrid vigour — they tend to be healthier than either parent breed. However, they can inherit health concerns from both Poodles and Retrievers:

  • Hip and elbow dysplasia — common in Retrievers; keep your Groodle at a healthy weight and avoid hard exercise on puppies under 12 months
  • Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA) — ask breeders for DNA eye clearances in both parents
  • Ear infections — as noted above, routine ear care is essential
  • Skin allergies — environmental and food allergies are fairly common; symptoms include itching, red paws, and hot spots
  • Bloat (GDV) — more common in standard Groodles; feed two smaller meals rather than one large one

Groodle Puppies: The First 12 Months

The early weeks and months set the foundation for your Groodle’s entire life. Prioritise:

  • Socialisation (3–16 weeks): Expose your puppy to different people, sounds, surfaces, animals, and environments. This window is critical and cannot be fully replicated later.
  • Puppy school: Most NZ vets and dog clubs run puppy classes — highly recommended for Groodles, who thrive with structured social learning.
  • Crate training: Teaches independence, aids toilet training, and gives your Groodle a safe space when you cannot supervise directly.
  • Low-impact exercise: Avoid repetitive jumping, long runs, or stairs until growth plates close (around 12–15 months for standards). Stick to free play and short leash walks.

Finding a Pet Sitter Who Understands Groodles

When you need to travel or work long hours, a Groodle’s social and grooming needs mean the right pet sitter makes a real difference. Not every sitter has experience with high-energy, curly-coated dogs — here’s what to look for:

  • Experience with similar breeds: Sitters who have cared for Poodles, Labradors, or other doodle breeds will understand the coat and temperament requirements.
  • Willingness to do daily brushing: Even a short trip of 3–4 days can result in significant matting without brushing. Confirm your sitter is comfortable doing this.
  • Active lifestyle: Your Groodle needs proper exercise, not just backyard access. A sitter who commits to 60+ minutes of daily walking is a must.
  • No lone confinement for long periods: Groodles should not be left alone for more than 4–5 hours at a stretch. A sitter doing overnight or in-home stays is often far better than a drop-in service.
Quick Reference: Groodle Care in NZ
  • Exercise: 60–90 min/day (physical + mental stimulation)
  • Brushing: 3–4x per week minimum; daily for curly coats
  • Professional grooming: every 6–10 weeks ($80–$150)
  • Ear checks: weekly, clean monthly or as needed
  • Vet visits: annually (plus puppy vaccinations on schedule)
  • Alone time: maximum 4–5 hours; highly social breed
  • Best for: active families, work-from-home owners, families with children

Final Thoughts

Groodles are genuinely wonderful companions — intelligent, loving, and endlessly entertaining. But they are emphatically not a low-maintenance breed. Their coats need consistent attention, their minds need daily stimulation, and their hearts need plenty of human company. Owners who go in with realistic expectations tend to become completely devoted Groodle advocates for life.

Whether you’re a first-time Groodle owner, a seasoned doodle fan, or a pet sitter preparing to look after one, understanding their specific needs makes all the difference. Happy Groodle, happy home.
