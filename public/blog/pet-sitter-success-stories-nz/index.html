Story 1: Sarah and Milo the Rescue Greyhound

The Challenge

Sarah, a Wellington-based graphic designer, adopted Milo from a Greyhound adoption programme in late 2025. Like many retired racing greyhounds, Milo was a gentle soul indoors but had never been left alone in a home environment. His first week with Sarah involved constant shadowing, whining when she stepped into the next room, and a torn cushion within 20 minutes of her first grocery run.

When a family wedding in Christchurch meant Sarah needed to be away for four days, the stakes felt enormous. She had tried a commercial boarding kennel briefly, but Milo refused to eat and lost nearly a kilogram in three days—a frightening amount for a lean breed.

The Solution

Sarah found a ZiggySitters sitter named Emma, who had specific experience with greyhounds and anxious rescue dogs. Before the sit, they arranged a two-hour meet-and-greet at Sarah\'s apartment. Emma brought high-value treats (roast chicken, which Sarah had pre-approved) and spent the session sitting on the floor, letting Milo approach at his own pace.

Key strategies Emma used during the four-day sit:

  • Maintained Sarah\'s routine exactly. Same walk times, same feeding schedule, same spot on the couch for evening relaxation. Consistency was everything for a dog still learning that home was safe.
  • Daily photo and video updates. Sarah received three updates per day—morning walk, midday check-in, and evening cuddle time. This was not just reassurance; it helped Sarah confirm Milo was eating and comfortable.
  • Gradual alone-time practice. On day two, Emma stepped out for 15 minutes to build Milo\'s confidence that people return. By day four, he settled for a 45-minute absence without vocalising.
  • Enrichment instead of punishment. When Milo scratched the hallway door, Emma redirected him to a stuffed Kong and placed a blanket over the scratched area to reduce visual triggers.

The Result

Milo gained back 200 grams during the sit. He greeted Sarah at the door with a full-body wag—tail low and swishing, which is greyhound for pure joy. The experience also gave Sarah a trusted sitter she now books regularly, including for half-day sits when she has long work meetings.

Lesson: For rescue dogs with separation anxiety, a sitter who will maintain routine and provide gradual alone-time practice is worth their weight in gold. Meet-and-greets are non-negotiable.

Story 2: The Chand Family and Two Anxious Cats in Auckland

The Challenge

Priya and Raj Chand live in a Remuera apartment with two indoor cats: Socks, a four-year-old domestic shorthair with a history of urinary stress, and Pepper, a two-year-old tabby who hides under the bed for hours when anyone new enters the apartment. Their previous experience with a house sitter had been a disaster—both cats stopped eating, Socks developed a urinary blockage requiring emergency vet care, and the sitter was overwhelmed.

The Chands had a two-week family trip to Rarotonga booked and were genuinely considering cancelling it.

The Solution

Through ZiggySitters, they connected with Aroha, a vet nurse who specialised in cat behaviour and medical needs. Aroha\'s approach was methodical:

  • Three pre-sit visits. Aroha visited once a week for three weeks before the trip. Visit one: she sat quietly in the living room, letting the cats observe from safe distances. Visit two: she offered treats and engaged Pepper in wand play. Visit three: she fed the cats their evening meal while the Chands were briefly out of the room.
  • Medical protocol for Socks. Aroha reviewed Socks\' urinary health history with Priya, noted the specific stress-food the vet had recommended, and set up a water fountain in every room to encourage drinking—a key preventive measure for urinary issues in cats.
  • Safe-space setup. Pepper\'s hiding spots were made comfortable with heated pads. Aroha did not try to extract Pepper from hiding; instead, she placed food and water near the hiding spots and sat quietly nearby, reading a book, until Pepper chose to emerge on her own terms.
  • Feliway diffusers and cat music. Aroha brought Feliway plug-ins and played species-appropriate music designed to calm felines—both evidence-based strategies the Chands had not considered.

The Result

Socks maintained normal eating and drinking throughout the two weeks. No urinary symptoms. Pepper emerged from hiding on day three and was playing with Aroha by day five. The Chands came home to two relaxed cats and a detailed log of eating, toileting, and behaviour patterns.

Lesson: For multi-cat households with medical and behavioural concerns, invest in a sitter with relevant qualifications. Three pre-visits cost a fraction of one emergency vet bill.

Story 3: Marcus and the Farm Dogs in Hawke\'s Bay

The Challenge

Marcus runs a lifestyle block near Havelock North with two working dogs—Turbo, a heading dog, and Bess, a huntaway. He and his partner planned a ten-day trip to visit family in Dunedin, but finding someone who could manage high-energy farm dogs on a rural property was proving difficult. Boarding kennels were an option, but both dogs were stressed in kennel environments and Marcus worried about them losing conditioning.

The Solution

Marcus found Jake, a sitter on ZiggySitters who grew up on a sheep station in the Waikato and understood working dogs. Jake stayed on-site, which was essential for a lifestyle block with livestock and a daily routine.

  • Stock work and exercise. Jake gave both dogs a solid run each morning—Turbo on the quad bike checking fences, Bess working the small mob of sheep. This was not just exercise; it was mental stimulation these intelligent dogs genuinely needed.
  • Property maintenance. Jake handled feeding out, water trough checks, and a minor fencing repair. For rural sits, a sitter who can manage the property is a game-changer.
  • Emergency response. On day six, one of the ewes got caught in brambles. Jake freed her, checked for injuries, and notified Marcus. Having someone capable and present meant the situation was handled immediately.

The Result

Both dogs maintained weight and fitness. Marcus returned to a well-run property and happy, tired dogs. He now books Jake for every trip, and Jake has become a trusted extension of the farm team.

Lesson: Rural and lifestyle block sits need sitters with practical skills. Look for experience with working breeds, livestock, and property management. An on-site sitter protects both your pets and your property.

Story 4: Emma\'s Senior Cat in Dunedin

The Challenge

Emma\'s 17-year-old cat, Whiskers, had hyperthyroidism and early kidney disease. Twice-daily medication, a special renal diet, and regular subcutaneous fluids made leaving her in anyone else\'s care feel risky. Emma had not taken a holiday in two years because finding a sitter who could handle feline medical needs was so hard.

The Solution

Through ZiggySitters, Emma connected with Lisa, a retired veterinary technician living in Dunedin. Lisa\'s experience meant she could:

  • Administer medication confidently. Pill pockets for the thyroid meds, fluid administration using the same technique Emma used—Lisa even sent Emma a video showing her technique so she could confirm it matched.
  • Monitor for deterioration. Lisa knew the early warning signs of kidney decompensation and hyperthyroid crashes. She checked water intake, litter box output, and weight daily.
  • Vet communication plan. Emma pre-authorised Lisa to take Whiskers to the Dunedin vet clinic if needed, with Lisa having the clinic\'s number and Emma\'s credit card on file. No delay in an emergency.

The Result

Whiskers maintained her weight, ate her renal diet consistently, and received all medications on schedule. Emma had the first proper break in two years and came back refreshed to a stable, content cat.

Lesson: Senior pets with medical needs deserve sitters with clinical confidence. Retired vet nurses and technicians are an underutilised goldmine for complex care. Always create a vet authorisation document before you travel.

What These Stories Have in Common

Across all four cases, several patterns stand out:

  1. Meet-and-greets made the difference. Every successful sit involved at least one pre-sit meeting. For anxious or medical-needs pets, multiple visits were transformative.
  2. Detailed care instructions. The owners who had the best outcomes provided written, specific instructions—not just "feed twice a day" but exact portions, times, locations, and what to do if the pet refused food.
  3. Communication that matched expectations. Sitters who sent consistent, structured updates (photos at feed times, a summary message each evening) reduced owner anxiety dramatically.
  4. The right sitter for the right pet. Generic sitters work for easy-going pets. Anxious, senior, or working animals need sitters with relevant experience and temperament.
  5. Vet authorisation. Every sitter mentioned they had either a pre-signed vet authorisation or clear instructions for emergency care. This should be standard, not optional.

How to Set Up Your Own Success Story

Based on what worked for these NZ pet owners, here is a practical checklist:

Before You Book

  • Read sitter reviews carefully. Look for mentions of pets similar to yours in age, breed, or temperament.
  • Check for relevant qualifications—vet nursing, animal behaviour, first aid, or breed-specific experience.
  • Book early. The best sitters fill up quickly, especially around school holidays and summer.

Before the Sit

  • Arrange at least one meet-and-greet. For anxious pets, do two or three.
  • Write a detailed care sheet: feeding schedule, medication times, walk routes, vet contacts, emergency contacts, and quirks your pet has.
  • Prepare a vet authorisation letter allowing the sitter to seek emergency treatment.
  • Stock up on food, medication, and any supplies your sitter might need.

During the Sit

  • Agree on update frequency upfront—morning, midday, and evening is a good default.
  • Trust your sitter. Micromanaging every photo creates stress for everyone.
  • If something feels off, communicate promptly. Most issues are resolved quickly with a quick message.

After the Sit

  • Leave an honest review. It helps other pet owners find the right sitter.
  • Save your sitter\'s profile for future bookings. Continuity is one of the best things you can offer an anxious pet.
  • Provide feedback directly too. Sitters appreciate knowing what went well and what they could improve.

Finding the Right Sitter for Your Pet

Every pet is different, and the right sitter for a laid-back labrador is not necessarily the right sitter for a anxious rescue greyhound. The key is matching experience and temperament to your pet\'s specific needs.
