How to Prepare Your Pet for Vet Visits in New Zealand

June 5, 2026 · 10 min read · ZiggySitters Team

For many New Zealand pets, a trip to the vet means one thing: stress. The carrier, the car ride, the unfamiliar smells, the poking and prodding — it can all add up to a miserable experience. But vet visits do not have to be traumatic. With the right preparation, you can turn a stressful ordeal into something your pet tolerates, or even accepts calmly.

This guide covers everything Kiwi pet owners need to know about preparing dogs, cats, and small animals for vet visits — from carrier training to choosing the right clinic, plus how a pet sitter can help when you cannot make the appointment yourself.

Why Vet Visit Preparation Matters

Stress does not just make vet visits unpleasant — it can affect your pet’s health outcomes. Anxious animals have elevated heart rates and blood pressure, making it harder for vets to get accurate readings. Severely stressed cats may need to be sedated for routine exams, adding cost and risk. Dogs that lash out from fear may be muzzled, which only reinforces their terror.

The good news: most vet visit anxiety is learned, which means it can also be unlearned. Positive preparation done consistently over weeks or months makes an enormous difference.

Start with the Carrier

The carrier is usually the first trigger. If the only time your cat sees the carrier is when something unpleasant is about to happen, they will learn to fear it instantly.

Carrier Training for Cats

  • Leave the carrier out permanently in a common area with the door open
  • Line it with a soft, familiar blanket or towel
  • Place treats, catnip, or meals inside so your cat associates it with good things
  • Once your cat enters voluntarily, briefly close the door for a few seconds, then open and reward
  • Gradually increase the time the door is closed before releasing

Carrier Training for Small Dogs

  • Use a secure, well-ventilated crate or carrier sized appropriately
  • Feed meals inside the crate and give high-value chew toys there
  • Practice short car trips to fun destinations (the beach, a park), not just the vet
ZiggySitters Tip: If your pet has severe carrier anxiety, ask your vet about a calming supplement like Zylkene or a pheromone spray (Feliway for cats, Adaptil for dogs). These can take the edge off without sedation and are widely available at NZ vet clinics and pet stores.

Car Ride Desensitisation

Many pets associate car rides exclusively with the vet. Break that association by taking your pet on car rides to pleasant places. Even a five-minute drive followed by a walk at a local reserve helps your pet learn that cars do not always lead to needles and cold exam tables.

For cats, start with the car engine off. Sit in the parked car with the carrier, give treats, then get out. Next session, turn the engine on briefly. Build up to short drives around the block. This gradual approach can take a few weeks but pays off for years.

Handling Practice at Home

Vets need to examine ears, eyes, teeth, bellies, and paws. If your pet has never been touched in these areas, the experience will feel invasive. Practice at home:

  • Ears: Gently lift ear flaps and look inside. Reward calm behaviour.
  • Paws: Hold each paw for a few seconds. Progress to pressing the toe pads gently (mimicking nail trimming).
  • Mouth: Lift lips to check teeth. For dogs, practice opening the jaw gently.
  • Belly: Rub your pet’s belly regularly so they are comfortable being examined there.
  • Temperature: Practice holding the base of the tail gently (thermometer proximity).

Do these exercises for just one to two minutes per day. Pair every touch with a high-value treat — small pieces of cooked chicken or freeze-dried liver work well for most pets.

Choosing the Right Vet in New Zealand

The vet you choose shapes your pet’s experience. NZ has excellent veterinary care, but clinics vary widely in their approach to fearful patients.

What to Look For

  • Fear-free certification: Some NZ clinics have adopted fear-free protocols — lower lighting, separate dog and cat waiting areas, non-slip exam tables, and gentle handling techniques
  • Cat-friendly clinic: Look for practices accredited under the International Society of Feline Medicine’s Cat Friendly Clinic programme
  • Separate waiting areas: Essential if your cat is dog-reactive or your dog is cat-reactive
  • House calls: Some NZ vets offer mobile services, which eliminates the carrier and car entirely
  • Emergency hours: Check whether your clinic has after-hours cover or partners with an emergency hospital
Did you know? The New Zealand Veterinary Association (NZVA) recommends annual health checks for all companion animals, and twice-yearly checks for senior pets (7+ years for dogs, 10+ for cats). Prevention is always cheaper than treatment.

What to Bring to a Vet Visit

Being prepared makes the visit smoother for everyone. Here is a practical checklist:

  • Vaccination records and medical history
  • List of current medications and supplements
  • Notes on any symptoms or behavioural changes you have noticed
  • Stool sample (if your vet has requested one — fresh, in a sealed bag)
  • Favourite high-value treats for rewarding calm behaviour
  • A towel or blanket with familiar scent to place on the exam table
  • Carrier liner or puppy pad in case of accidents
  • Leash and harness (for dogs) — avoid retractable leads at the vet
  • Payment method (ask about payment plans for larger procedures)
  • Questions written down — it is easy to forget under pressure

During the Visit

Your behaviour matters as much as your pet’s preparation. Animals read our body language closely. If you are anxious, your pet will feel it.

What Works

  • Stay calm and breathe slowly — your pet mirrors your energy
  • Use a quiet, reassuring voice without excessive coddling
  • Bring treats and reward calm moments immediately
  • Let your vet take the lead on restraint — do not grab your pet
  • Ask for a fear-free approach if your pet is visibly distressed

What Does Not Work

  • Tightening the leash or gripping your pet — increases anxiety
  • Saying “it’s okay” over and over — pets learn this means something bad is happening
  • Punishing fear-based behaviours (growling, hiding, struggling)
  • Skipping visits because your pet hates the vet — avoidance makes it worse

After the Visit

Give your pet time to decompress. Some animals are sleepy or grumpy for hours after a vet visit due to stress hormones. Let them rest in a quiet spot. Cats especially may want to hide — provide a safe space and leave them alone.

If your pet had injections or a blood draw, monitor the area for swelling or tenderness over the next 24 hours. Mild lethargy is normal; vomiting, facial swelling, or difficulty breathing requires an immediate call to your vet.

How Pet Sitters Can Help with Vet Visits

Sometimes you simply cannot take time off work for a vet appointment. That is where a trusted pet sitter becomes invaluable.

What a Pet Sitter Can Do

  • Vet runs: Transport your pet to and from scheduled appointments
  • Pre-visit preparation: Continue carrier training and handling exercises you have started
  • Post-visit care: Administer medications, monitor recovery, and provide a calm environment
  • Emergency response: If your pet needs urgent care while you are away, a sitter can get them to the vet fast
ZiggySitters Tip: Always leave your vet clinic on file with your pet sitter, along with written authorisation for emergency treatment up to a dollar amount you are comfortable with. Most NZ vet clinics will treat an animal brought in by an authorised caregiver if you have called ahead or left details on file.

Special Considerations for NZ Pet Owners

Costs and Insurance

Vet care in New Zealand is not subsidised, so costs can add up quickly. A standard consultation typically ranges from $50 to $90, with procedures costing significantly more. Pet insurance is increasingly popular in NZ and can cover unexpected vet bills. Check our NZ pet insurance guide for detailed comparisons.

Rural Vet Access

If you live rurally, your nearest clinic may be 30 to 60 minutes away. Build a relationship with your local vet before you need them urgently. Many rural NZ vets offer phone consultations for initial triage.

Native Wildlife Risks

NZ pets face unique hazards — grass seeds in ears, tick bites, and encounters with native wildlife. Regular vet checks catch these early. If your dog has been sniffing around bush areas, a post-walk check for seeds in ears, eyes, and between toes can prevent painful vet visits later.

Quick Preparation Timeline

Two Weeks Before

  • Start daily carrier training if your pet is carrier-averse
  • Practice handling ears, paws, and mouth at home
  • Take a short car ride to a fun destination

The Night Before

  • Gather vaccination records and write down your questions
  • Prepare the carrier with a familiar-smelling blanket
  • Withhold food if your vet has asked (some blood tests require fasting)

On the Day

  • Allow extra time — rushing creates stress for both of you
  • Pack treats, a towel, and puppy pads
  • Stay calm and let your pet set the pace in the waiting room

Common Vet Visit Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Skipping preventive visits. Annual check-ups catch problems early when they are cheapest and easiest to treat.
  2. Not preparing the carrier. Shoving a cat into a carrier they have not seen in six months guarantees a stressed patient.
  3. Withholding information. Tell your vet everything — even if your pet ate something embarrassing. Full information leads to better diagnoses.
  4. Waiting too long. NZ vets consistently report that owners wait too long before bringing sick pets in. When in doubt, call — most clinics offer free phone advice on whether a visit is needed.
  5. Projecting your own anxiety. Your pet reads your stress. Stay calm and matter-of-fact.

Key Takeaways

  • Preparation starts weeks before the visit, not on the day
  • Carrier and car desensitisation are the two highest-impact exercises
  • Handling practice at home makes the exam feel routine, not invasive
  • Choose a fear-free or cat-friendly vet clinic when possible
  • A pet sitter can handle vet runs and aftercare when you are unavailable
  • Annual check-ups are essential — skipping them costs more in the long run

Vet visits are a non-negotiable part of responsible pet ownership in New Zealand. With consistent preparation and the right support, they do not have to be a battle. Your pet deserves care that feels safe — and you deserve peace of mind knowing they are getting it.
