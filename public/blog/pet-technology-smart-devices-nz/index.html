GPS Trackers for Dogs and Cats

GPS pet trackers attach to your animal\'s collar and use satellite positioning to pinpoint their location in real time. For NZ dog owners with escape-artist pets, or cat owners who want to know where their feline roams at night, these devices can be genuinely useful. The key consideration for New Zealand is connectivity: most GPS trackers rely on cellular networks to transmit location data, so you need a device that operates on NZ\'s 4G bands (primarily 700 MHz Band 28 and 1800 MHz Band 3). Devices designed for the US or European markets may not have the right cellular modem, which means they will not connect to any NZ network at all.

Several GPS trackers are available in New Zealand or ship here reliably. The Tractive GPS tracker is one of the most popular options globally and works on NZ networks. It offers real-time tracking, virtual fence alerts, and activity monitoring through a subscription-based app. The subscription cost is a key factor to consider, because most GPS trackers require an ongoing monthly or yearly fee on top of the hardware purchase. Tractive charges roughly $8 to $12 per month depending on the plan length. The Fi Series 3 collar is another option, though availability in NZ can be inconsistent and it operates on a different subscription model. For cat owners, the Tabcat and Tile trackers use Bluetooth rather than GPS, which means a much shorter range but no subscription fee and a smaller form factor that is more comfortable on a cat collar.

When choosing a GPS tracker for your pet in NZ, check three things first: that the device supports NZ cellular bands, that the subscription pricing works for your budget, and that the battery life suits your needs. Cats that roam all day need a tracker with at least 3 to 5 days of battery life between charges, while dogs that are mostly at home but occasionally escape can get away with shorter battery windows. Also consider the weight and bulk of the device. A 30-gram tracker is fine on a 25 kg Labrador, but uncomfortable on a 4 kg cat.

Pet Cameras: Keep Watch from Anywhere

Pet cameras let you check on your dog or cat remotely via a smartphone app. The basic models stream video and two-way audio, while premium versions add treat dispensing, laser toys for cats, and barking alerts that notify you when your dog is vocalising. For NZ pet owners who work in the office or travel domestically, a pet camera can provide reassurance that your animal is settled and comfortable, and can help you catch early signs of separation anxiety or illness before they escalate.

The Furbo Dog Camera is one of the most well-known pet cameras available in NZ. It offers 1080p video, night vision, two-way audio, treat tossing, and a barking alert feature that sends a push notification when your dog barks. The subscription-free basic features cover live streaming, two-way audio, and treat dispensing, while the optional Doggie Diary and bark alert features require a Furbo Care subscription. Other options include the Petcube Bites 2, which combines a wide-angle camera with a laser toy for cats and treat dispensing for dogs, and the TP-Link Tapo range, which offers budget-friendly security cameras that double as pet monitors without the treat-tossing extras.

Placement matters more than you might think. Position the camera at your pet\'s eye level, not ceiling height, so you can see their facial expressions and body language clearly. If you have a cat that spends most of the day on a particular shelf or windowsill, angle the camera toward that spot. Avoid placing cameras in direct sunlight or near heat sources, as NZ summer temperatures can cause overheating and degraded video quality. For multi-pet households, consider a camera with a wide-angle or pan-and-tilt lens so you can track animals moving between rooms.

Smart Pet Feeders

Smart pet feeders dispense measured portions of dry food on a schedule you set through a companion app. They are useful for maintaining consistent feeding times when your schedule is unpredictable, and for preventing overfeeding in multi-pet households where one animal tends to steal food from another. Some models also include a camera so you can watch your pet eat, and a recording feature that lets you play a voice message at mealtime, which can help anxious pets feel reassured that their human is still "present" even when away.

Key considerations for NZ buyers include the type of food the feeder accommodates. Most smart feeders work with dry kibble only, and the size of the kibble matters. If your pet eats large kibble or dental sticks, check the feeder\'s dispensing mechanism to ensure it will not jam. For wet food, only a few premium models support refrigerated compartments, and these tend to be expensive. The Petkit Fresh Element and WOpet smart feeders are both available in NZ and offer reliable dry food dispensing with app control and scheduling. The Petlibro Granary is another solid option with a larger capacity, suitable for households with two or more cats.

Power outages are a genuine concern in parts of New Zealand, particularly during winter storms and cyclone season. If you rely on a smart feeder as your primary feeding mechanism, choose a model with battery backup or keep a manual gravity feeder as a failsafe. Also check that the feeder connects via Wi-Fi on the 2.4 GHz band, which is standard in NZ, rather than requiring 5 GHz, which some older routers do not support. Finally, cleaning matters. Any feeder with a food hopper and dispensing auger can develop mould or bacterial growth in NZ\'s humid conditions, particularly in the North Island. Choose a model where the food hopper and dispensing tray are removable and dishwasher safe.

Microchip Cat Doors

If you have an indoor-outdoor cat, a microchip cat door is one of the most impactful pieces of pet technology you can invest in. These doors read your cat\'s existing microchip and only unlock for recognised animals, keeping neighbourhood strays, possums, and rats out of your home while giving your own cat free access. In New Zealand, where pest species are a serious ecological concern and many local councils have cat curfews or containment bylaws, a microchip cat door helps you comply with regulations while keeping your cat\'s quality of life intact.

The SureFlap Microchip Cat Door is the market leader in NZ and reliably reads most standard microchips implanted by NZ vets. It runs on batteries, typically lasting 6 to 12 months on a set of four AA batteries, and requires no Wi-Fi connection, which makes it resilient to power and internet outages. The SureFlap DualScan model adds a curfew mode that locks the door at set times, useful for council-mandated cat curfews or for keeping your cat indoors overnight when native birds like kiwi and morepork are most active. Installation is straightforward in wooden doors, but requires more effort in glass or wall mounting, where you may need a glazier or a professional installer.

One important note: the PetSafe brand also offers microchip cat doors in NZ, and while they function similarly, their compatibility with NZ-implanted microchips has been reported as inconsistent in online reviews. If you choose PetSafe, verify that it reads your cat\'s specific chip before installing. The SureFlap range has stronger local track record for chip compatibility across the 10-digit and 15-digit ISO formats used by NZ veterinary clinics.

Pet Activity Monitors

Activity monitors clip onto your pet\'s collar and track movement, rest, and play throughout the day. They are the pet equivalent of a fitness tracker, and they serve two main purposes: providing data on your pet\'s daily activity levels, and flagging changes in behaviour that might indicate health problems. If your dog suddenly starts sleeping 40 percent more than usual, or your cat\'s nighttime activity drops significantly, an activity monitor can alert you before visible symptoms appear.

The Tractive GPS tracker mentioned earlier includes activity monitoring as part of its subscription, making it a good two-in-one option if you want both location tracking and fitness data. The Fi collar offers similar combined functionality. For a dedicated activity monitor without GPS, the FitBark and Whistle (now part of Gnaw) ranges track activity and rest patterns, though availability in NZ can be sporadic and you may need to order through an international retailer with NZ shipping.

Activity data is most useful when you have a baseline. Wear the monitor for two to four weeks to establish your pet\'s normal patterns before you start interpreting changes. This baseline period accounts for day-to-day variation in your pet\'s behaviour and makes it easier to spot genuine trends versus one-off anomalies. Activity monitors are particularly valuable for senior pets, where gradual declines in movement can be hard to notice day by day but show up clearly in weekly and monthly trend graphs.

Automatic Litter Boxes

Self-cleaning litter boxes detect when a cat has used them and automatically sift or rake waste into a sealed compartment. For busy NZ cat owners, they reduce the daily scooping chore and help manage odour, particularly in apartments or homes where the litter box is in a communal area. The Litter-Robot is the most widely known brand globally and is available in NZ through select retailers and direct shipping. The Petkit Pura and CatGenie are alternatives that use different cleaning mechanisms.

Automatic litter boxes are not a set-and-forget solution. They require regular maintenance including emptying the waste drawer, replacing the waste drawer liner, and cleaning the sensors and rotating mechanism. The ongoing cost of liners, carbon filters, and in some cases proprietary litter adds up. If you have multiple cats, you may need a larger capacity model or multiple units. And some cats simply refuse to use them, particularly older cats or cats that are already anxious about their litter box situation. Introduce any automatic litter box gradually, keeping a traditional box available alongside it until your cat consistently uses the new one.

For NZ\'s climate, consider humidity. Automatic litter boxes with electronic components can struggle in humid environments, particularly in Auckland and other North Island cities. Condensation inside the unit can trigger false sensors and cause the cleaning cycle to run unnecessarily. Place the box in a well-ventilated area and consider a model with a moisture-resistant design if your home tends to be damp.

What to Consider Before Buying Pet Tech in NZ

  • Cellular compatibility: GPS trackers and some cameras need NZ 4G bands. Check specifications for Band 28 (700 MHz) and Band 3 (1800 MHz) support before buying.
  • Wi-Fi standard: Most smart pet devices require 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi, which is universal in NZ. Devices that only support 5 GHz may not connect to older routers or extenders.
  • Power supply: NZ uses 230V/50Hz with Type I outlets. Devices designed for the US (110V/60Hz) need a compatible power adapter, not just a plug converter. Check that the included power supply accepts 100-240V input.
  • Shipping and warranty: Some pet tech brands ship to NZ but do not offer local warranty support. Factor in potential return shipping costs and repair timelines when comparing prices from international versus NZ retailers.
  • Subscription costs: GPS trackers, cloud storage for cameras, and app features often require monthly subscriptions. A $150 tracker can cost $100+ per year in ongoing fees. Always calculate total cost of ownership over 2 to 3 years.
  • Privacy: Pet cameras stream video from inside your home. Review the privacy policy of any camera brand before purchasing. Look for end-to-end encryption and local storage options if you are concerned about cloud video storage.
  • Humidity and weather: NZ\'s humid subtropical climate in the north and temperate conditions in the south affect electronic devices differently. Outdoor trackers and cameras need weather-resistant ratings appropriate for your region.

Pet Tech for Specific NZ Situations

Rural and Lifestyle Block Pets

If you live on a lifestyle block or rural property, your pet tech needs differ from urban owners. GPS trackers become essential for working dogs and farm cats that cover large territories. Choose a tracker with extended battery life, because you may not be able to charge it daily. The Tractive GPS with its unlimited range mode and 2 to 5 day battery works well for rural NZ, though cellular coverage gaps in remote areas can create tracking blind spots. Pet cameras with local storage (SD card) rather than cloud-only storage are preferable if your rural property has limited internet bandwidth.

Apartment and City Living

In Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch apartments, smart feeders and pet cameras solve specific urban problems. Apartment dogs that spend the day alone benefit from a camera with barking alerts, letting you know if your dog is disturbing neighbours. A smart feeder ensures consistent meal times even when your commute runs long. For apartment cats, a microchip cat door on the balcony (if your body corporate allows it) gives safe outdoor access without the risk of falling or escaping. Indoor-only cats benefit from interactive camera toys like the Petcube laser, which provides mental stimulation during long days alone.

Travel and Holiday Care

If you travel frequently, a combination of pet camera, smart feeder, and a ZiggySitters pet sitter is the gold standard. The camera gives you visual confirmation that your sitter is visiting and your pet is eating. The smart feeder provides backup feeding consistency. And the sitter provides the human interaction, medication administration, and problem-solving that no device can offer. Share camera and feeder app access with your sitter before you leave, and set up notification alerts so both you and your sitter are informed if feeding is missed or your pet has not moved for an unusual period.
