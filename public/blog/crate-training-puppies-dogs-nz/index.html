Crate Training Puppies and Dogs in New Zealand: Step-by-Step Guide

June 5, 2026 · 12 min read · ZiggySitters Team

Crate training is one of the most valuable skills you can teach your dog. Done correctly, a crate becomes a safe den — a place your dog chooses to retreat to for rest and security. Done poorly, it becomes a source of anxiety. The difference comes down to method, patience, and consistency.

This guide walks New Zealand dog owners through crate training from start to finish: choosing the right crate, building positive associations, progressing to overnight stays, and troubleshooting the most common problems. Whether you have a brand-new puppy or a rescue dog with an unknown history, these steps will work.

Why Crate Train?

Crate training is not about confinement — it is about giving your dog a bedroom of their own. Dogs are den animals by nature. A properly introduced crate provides:

  • A safe space your dog can retreat to when overwhelmed (visitors, children, other pets)
  • House training assistance — dogs instinctively avoid soiling their sleeping area
  • Travel safety — essential for car trips, vet visits, and ferry crossings
  • Emergency management — if your dog needs to be confined for injury recovery or during a natural disaster
  • Better pet sitting experiences — a crate-trained dog settles faster with a sitter
Is crate training cruel? No — not when done correctly. The crate door should always be open unless you are actively training or there is a safety reason to close it. A crate should never be used as punishment. The goal is a space your dog loves, not a cell they fear.

Choosing the Right Crate

Size

Your dog should be able to stand up, turn around, and lie down comfortably. If the crate is too large, your dog may use one end as a toilet and the other as a bedroom — defeating the house-training purpose. For puppies, buy a crate sized for their adult weight and use a divider to shrink the space while they grow.

Where to Buy Crates in NZ

Crates are widely available at Petco, Animates, Kermit Vet, and online through Mighty Mart and TradeMe. Expect to pay $60–$200 depending on size and quality. A good crate lasts years — this is not the place to cut corners.

Step-by-Step Crate Training Process

Step 1: Introduce the Crate (Days 1–3)

Goal: Your dog voluntarily enters the crate.

  • Set the crate up in a common area (living room or kitchen) with the door fixed open
  • Place a comfortable bed or blanket inside
  • Scatter treats around the outside, then just inside the entrance, then at the back
  • When your dog enters, mark with “yes!” or a clicker and give a treat
  • Feed meals inside the crate — this creates the strongest positive association
  • Never close the door at this stage

Step 2: Build Duration (Days 4–7)

Goal: Your dog stays in the crate for 5–10 minutes with the door closed.

  • Ask your dog to enter the crate with a cue like “crate” or “bed”
  • Close the door for 5 seconds, then open and treat
  • Gradually increase to 30 seconds, then 1 minute, then 5 minutes
  • Stay in the room at first, then practice stepping out briefly
  • Give a long-lasting chew or stuffed Kong to keep them occupied
  • If your dog whines or barks, wait for a quiet moment before opening — never reward noise

Step 3: Extend Alone Time (Week 2)

Goal: Your dog stays in the crate for 20–30 minutes while you leave the room.

  • Practice leaving the room for short periods, then returning calmly
  • Do not make a fuss when you return — keep arrivals low-key
  • Vary your routine so your dog does not learn that crating always means you are leaving
  • Leave a radio on softly for background noise

Step 4: Overnight Crating (Week 2–3)

Goal: Your dog sleeps in the crate overnight.

  • Move the crate to your bedroom (or just outside the door) so your dog can hear and smell you
  • Tire your dog out with exercise before bed
  • Take your dog outside for a final toilet break right before crating
  • Cover the crate with a breathable blanket to create a den atmosphere
  • If your dog cries, take them outside for a toilet break, then return to the crate with minimal fuss
  • Over time, you can gradually move the crate to its permanent location

Step 5: Daytime Alone Crating (Week 3–4)

Goal: Your dog can be crated for 2–4 hours while you are away.

  • Start with short absences (30 minutes) and build up
  • Always provide a safe chew item or Kong
  • Do not crate for more than 4 hours at a stretch during the day
  • Puppies under 6 months: max 1–2 hours at a time (they need more frequent toilet breaks)

Age-Specific Guidance

Puppies (8–16 weeks)

Puppies have tiny bladders and short attention spans. The formula for maximum crate time is: age in months plus one, in hours. So a 2-month-old puppy can manage about 3 hours maximum. Expect nighttime wake-ups for toilet breaks. Be extremely patient — this is brand new for them.

Adolescents (4–12 months)

This is where many owners hit a wall. Adolescents have energy to burn and may resist crating. Increase physical and mental exercise before crating. A tired teenager is a compliant teenager. Puzzle feeders, training sessions, and a good walk make crate time easier.

Adult Rescue Dogs

You may not know a rescue dog’s crate history. Some take to it immediately; others may have been confined in traumatic conditions. Go slowly — back to Step 1. Never force a rescue dog into a crate. If they show severe panic (panting, drooling, attempts to escape), consult a qualified behaviourist. The NZ Companion Animal Register and the NZVA can help you find one.

Common Crate Training Mistakes

  1. Using the crate as punishment. The crate must always be a positive space. If you send your dog there for being naughty, they will learn to fear it.
  2. Crating for too long. Dogs should not be crated for more than 4 hours during the day. Puppies need breaks every 1–2 hours. If you work full-time, arrange a midday dog walk or pet sitter visit.
  3. Responding to whining. If you open the crate every time your dog whines, you teach them that noise equals freedom. Wait for quiet before opening.
  4. Skipping steps. Going from “entering the crate” to “leaving the house for two hours” will cause setbacks. Follow the progression.
  5. Inconsistent rules. If the crate is sometimes a fun place and sometimes a punishment, your dog will never trust it. Consistency is everything.
  6. Poor crate location. Isolating the crate in a garage or laundry makes it feel like banishment. Place it where the family spends time.

Crate Training and House Training Together

Crate training and house training go hand in hand. The principle is simple: dogs do not like to soil where they sleep. A correctly sized crate (just big enough to stand, turn, and lie down) makes this instinct work for you.

The routine is straightforward:

  1. Take your dog outside first thing in the morning
  2. Feed breakfast in the crate
  3. Wait 15–30 minutes, then take outside again
  4. Play time outside the crate
  5. Back in the crate with a chew while you work
  6. Midday toilet break (this is where a pet sitter helps)
  7. Afternoon play and exercise
  8. Dinner in the crate
  9. Evening toilet break
  10. Final toilet break before bed

If your dog has an accident in the crate, do not punish them. Clean it thoroughly with an enzymatic cleaner (available at any NZ pet store) to remove all scent traces, then reassess your schedule — the accident means the interval between breaks was too long.

NZ-Specific Considerations

Climate

New Zealand summers can be intense, especially in regions like Hawke’s Bay, Canterbury, and Central Otago where temperatures regularly exceed 30°C. Never leave a dog in a crate in a hot room or direct sunlight. In winter, a draft-free position with a warm blanket is essential. The crate should never feel like an oven or an icebox.

Rural Dogs

Farm dogs and lifestyle block dogs may not traditionally be crate trained, but the benefits apply equally. A crate-trained farm dog travels better to the vet, settles faster in unfamiliar environments (boarding kennels, holiday accommodation), and is easier to manage during lambing or calving when they need to be confined safely.

Body Corporates and Rentals

Many NZ rental properties and body corporate rules restrict pets. A crate-trained dog is easier to manage in rental properties because they are less likely to damage the home and can be safely confined when tradespeople visit. Always include crate training as part of your pet reference for landlords.

How Pet Sitters Maintain Crate Training

Consistency is the foundation of crate training. When you travel, your dog’s routine should not fall apart. A good pet sitter will:

  • Follow your exact crating schedule (times, cues, rewards)
  • Continue feeding meals in the crate
  • Maintain the same verbal cues (“crate”, “bed”, “kennel”)
  • Provide the same type of chew item or Kong
  • Never use the crate as punishment
  • Respect overnight crating if that is your routine

Key Takeaways

  • Crate training is about creating a safe den, not a prison — the crate door should be open most of the time
  • Go at your dog’s pace — rushing creates fear, not trust
  • Feed all meals in the crate to build the strongest positive association
  • Never use the crate as punishment
  • A pet sitter can maintain your crate training routine while you travel
  • Adjust for NZ climate — never crate in extreme heat or cold without ventilation and bedding

Crate training takes patience, but the payoff lasts a lifetime. A dog that loves their crate is a dog that travels well, settles quickly with a sitter, recovers safely from illness or injury, and feels secure in their own space. Start today, go slowly, and your dog will thank you — maybe not with words, but with the way they trot happily into their crate at bedtime.
