Maine Coon Cat Care Guide NZ

Published 28 March 2026 • 13 min read • ZiggySitters Team

The Maine Coon is one of the most beloved cat breeds in the world, and New Zealand owners are discovering what Americans have known for centuries: these gentle giants are extraordinary companions. With their dog-like personalities, magnificent coats, and affectionate temperaments, Maine Coons bring something truly special to NZ homes. This comprehensive guide covers everything you need to know about caring for a Maine Coon in New Zealand — from grooming and feeding to health checks, enrichment, and finding the right cat sitter when you travel.

Maine Coon Characteristics: What NZ Owners Should Know

Maine Coons are the largest domestic cat breed. Males typically weigh 6–9 kg and females 4–6 kg, with some males exceeding 10 kg. Despite their size, they are gentle, playful, and famously patient — which makes them an excellent choice for families with children and other pets. They grow slowly and do not reach full physical maturity until 3–5 years of age, so do not be surprised when your one-year-old still looks like a kitten relative to what is coming.

Their coats are semi-long, water-resistant, and feature a distinctive ruff around the neck, tufted ears, and bushy tails. Coat colours span over 75 recognised variations in NZ — from classic brown tabby to silver, tortoiseshell, and solid black. Underneath that impressive exterior is a cat with a chirping, trilling voice (rarely a traditional meow), exceptional intelligence, and an enduring love of human company.

Maine Coons are often described as “dog-like” because they tend to follow owners from room to room, respond to their names, enjoy supervised outdoor time on a harness, and greet visitors at the door. This loyalty makes them enormously rewarding companions — but also means they feel the absence of their owners acutely, which is an important consideration when planning travel.

Grooming Your Maine Coon in New Zealand

Grooming is one of the most important aspects of Maine Coon care. Their semi-long coats tangle and mat if not regularly maintained, and the NZ climate — with its humid summers and cool winters — can intensify seasonal shedding.

How Often to Brush

  • Minimum: 2–3 times per week, every week of the year
  • Ideal: Daily brushing, especially during autumn and spring shedding seasons
  • Tools: A wide-tooth metal comb for the main coat, a slicker brush for the undercoat, and a mat splitter for any tangles behind the ears or in the armpits

Focus your brushing sessions on the belly and chest ruff, behind the ears, and the inner thigh area — these spots mat fastest. Never try to pull out a mat; instead, work from the ends inward with a detangling spray, or use a mat splitter if necessary. Serious mats may need professional grooming or a veterinary clip under sedation if they reach the skin.

Bathing

Maine Coons generally tolerate bathing better than most cats, and many actually enjoy water. A bath every 6–8 weeks helps manage shedding and keeps the coat healthy. Use a cat-specific shampoo and conditioner, rinse thoroughly, and use a hairdryer on a low, warm setting — getting a Maine Coon fully dry is important to prevent skin irritation, especially in NZ's cooler months.

Additional Grooming Needs

  • Nails: Trim every 2–3 weeks to prevent snagging in carpet or furniture
  • Ears: Check weekly for wax build-up or dark debris that may indicate ear mites
  • Teeth: Brush 2–3 times per week with cat-safe toothpaste to prevent periodontal disease — a common issue in large cats
  • Eyes: Maine Coons can accumulate discharge at the inner corners — wipe gently with a damp cloth as needed
Grooming habit tip: Start grooming routines when your Maine Coon is a kitten. Early, positive experiences with brushing and bathing make adult grooming sessions far easier — for both you and your cat.

Feeding a Maine Coon in NZ

Maine Coons have large frames and remain active well into old age. Their diet needs to support significant muscle mass and long-term joint health. Cheap, high-carbohydrate cat foods are not appropriate for this breed.

Key Feeding Principles

  • High protein: Look for a named meat source (chicken, salmon, beef) as the first ingredient. Aim for 40%+ protein content in dry food and a meat-forward wet food.
  • Low carbohydrates: Cats are obligate carnivores. Excess carbohydrates from grain-heavy formulas can contribute to obesity and diabetes, both risks in a large, indoor cat.
  • Wet food component: Include wet food daily or at least several times per week. Maine Coons have a relatively low thirst drive and often do not drink enough water, making them susceptible to urinary tract issues.
  • Measured portions: Despite their size, Maine Coons can become overweight if free-fed. Feed measured portions twice daily and adjust based on your cat's body condition score — you should be able to feel their ribs without pressing hard.
  • Fresh water: Provide a clean water bowl at all times. Many Maine Coons prefer drinking from a running source — a pet water fountain encourages higher water intake and supports kidney health.

Feeding Stages

Life StageRecommended Approach
Kitten (up to 18 months)Kitten-specific high-protein food, 3–4 small meals daily
Junior (18 months – 3 years)Transition to adult formula, 2–3 meals daily
Adult (3–10 years)Premium adult formula, 2 measured meals daily
Senior (10+ years)Senior formula with joint support (glucosamine), softer wet food
NZ tip: Several quality NZ and Australian brands stock Maine Coon-appropriate food in local pet stores and online. Look for grain-free or limited-ingredient options and avoid foods listing “meat meal” or “cereal” in the first few ingredients.

Maine Coon Health in New Zealand

Maine Coons are generally robust cats, but like all pedigree breeds, they carry some genetic predispositions that NZ owners should be aware of.

Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)

HCM is the most common feline heart disease, and Maine Coons have a known genetic variant (MyBPC3) that increases risk. Reputable NZ breeders screen their breeding cats using DNA testing and echocardiography. Ask your breeder for test documentation before purchasing, and have your cat screened by a veterinary cardiologist from 2 years of age, then every 1–2 years thereafter. Early detection allows management strategies that significantly extend quality of life.

Hip Dysplasia

Maine Coons are one of the few cat breeds prone to hip dysplasia, a malformation of the hip joint that can cause pain and reduced mobility. Signs include reluctance to jump, stiffness after rest, or a changed gait. Management includes weight control, joint supplements (glucosamine and omega-3 fatty acids), and in severe cases, veterinary intervention. Maintaining a healthy weight is the single most effective prevention strategy.

Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA)

SMA is a genetic condition affecting the spinal cord motor neurons, causing muscle weakness in the hind limbs. Affected kittens show a distinctive swaying walk from around 3–4 months. DNA testing can identify carriers; ask your breeder if their lines are SMA-clear.

Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD)

While less common than in Persians, PKD has been reported in some Maine Coon lines. DNA testing is available and responsible NZ breeders screen for this condition.

NZ Vaccination and Parasite Prevention

Core vaccinations in New Zealand cover:

  • Feline panleukopenia (FPV)
  • Feline herpesvirus (FHV-1)
  • Feline calicivirus (FCV)

Annual boosters, monthly flea and worm prevention, and regular vet check-ups are standard practice. Maine Coons' dense coats can hide flea infestations until populations are significant — year-round prevention is recommended, particularly in humid NZ summers.

Enrichment and Exercise

Maine Coons are highly intelligent and remain playful well into adulthood. A bored Maine Coon will find their own entertainment — usually involving your furniture, curtains, or anything left unattended on the kitchen bench. Providing appropriate outlets for their mental and physical energy is essential.

Exercise Needs

  • Interactive play: At least two dedicated play sessions daily, 10–15 minutes each. Wand toys, feather teasers, and interactive laser pointers work well.
  • Puzzle feeders: Replace one or more meals with a puzzle feeder to engage their hunting instincts. Maine Coons excel at food puzzles and learn them quickly, so rotate complexity levels.
  • Cat trees and climbing: Maine Coons are powerful climbers. A sturdy, tall cat tree positioned near a window provides climbing exercise and territory surveying in one.
  • Harness training: Maine Coons are one of the easiest breeds to leash train. Supervised outdoor time on a harness provides environmental stimulation and exercise beyond what an indoor environment can offer.

Indoor vs Outdoor

In New Zealand, indoor or supervised outdoor access is recommended for Maine Coons. NZ's unique wildlife — particularly birds — is vulnerable to cat predation, and unsupervised outdoor access puts both native species and your cat at risk from traffic, toxins, and other animals. A secure outdoor enclosure (catio) or regular harness walks are excellent compromises that meet a Maine Coon's need for outdoor stimulation without the risks.

Important: Maine Coons can become destructive and develop stress behaviours if under-stimulated. Mental enrichment is not optional — it is core care for this breed.

Maine Coons and Other Pets

One of the Maine Coon's most celebrated traits is their remarkable tolerance and sociability. Most Maine Coons integrate well with dogs — particularly if introduced carefully as kittens — and generally do well with other cats. Their calm, confident nature means they rarely initiate conflict, and their size means they can stand their ground without feeling threatened.

Introductions should be gradual: start with scent swapping before any face-to-face meetings, use baby gates to allow visual contact without physical access, and allow each animal to retreat to a safe space. Most Maine Coons settle with other pets within 2–4 weeks given a patient, structured introduction process.

Leaving Your Maine Coon: Finding the Right Cat Sitter in NZ

Maine Coons form deep bonds with their people. Unlike some independent cat breeds that tolerate solitude well, Maine Coons feel the absence of their families. When you travel, simply leaving out extra food is not adequate care for this breed.

What to Look for in a Cat Sitter for a Maine Coon

  • Twice-daily visits at minimum — morning and evening visits for feeding, cleaning, and social time
  • Interactive play experience — your sitter should spend 10+ minutes actively playing with your cat each visit, not just filling the bowl and leaving
  • Grooming capability — at minimum, a daily brush during multi-day absences to prevent matting
  • Photo and video updates — Maine Coons are expressive, and regular updates reassure you that your cat is eating, active, and comfortable
  • Comfort with large cats — not all sitters have experience with a cat that weighs as much as a small dog; find someone who is confident handling a big, active feline
  • Emergency protocol — your sitter should know your regular vet's details and the nearest after-hours emergency clinic
Pro tip: Book at least one trial visit before your first trip away. Let your Maine Coon meet the sitter while you are home — you will be able to see immediately whether your cat is comfortable, and your sitter can learn your cat's specific routine, preferences, and quirks firsthand.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Maine Coon cats good for New Zealand homes?

Yes. Maine Coons adapt exceptionally well to NZ homes and lifestyles. Their adaptable, sociable nature suits families, couples, and single owners. Their thick coats handle NZ winters comfortably, and they enjoy both indoor and supervised outdoor time in safely enclosed spaces.

How often should you groom a Maine Coon?

Maine Coons should be brushed at least 2–3 times per week, and ideally every day during autumn and spring shedding seasons. Pay special attention to the chest ruff, belly, and behind the ears where mats form most easily.

Can Maine Coons be left alone when I travel?

Maine Coons are social cats that form strong bonds with their families. They do not cope well when left alone for more than 24 hours. When travelling, arrange an in-home cat sitter who can visit at least twice daily for feeding, grooming, and interactive play.

How big do Maine Coon cats get in NZ?

Maine Coons are the largest domestic cat breed. Males typically reach 6–9 kg and females 4–6 kg. They grow slowly and do not reach full size until around 3–5 years of age.

What do Maine Coon cats eat in NZ?

A high-protein diet is essential for Maine Coons given their large frame and active nature. A combination of quality wet food and premium dry kibble works well. Avoid high-carbohydrate foods. Fresh water must always be available as Maine Coons are prone to kidney issues.
