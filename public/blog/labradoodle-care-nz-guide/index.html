Labradoodle Care Guide NZ: Everything New Zealand Owners Need to Know

Breed Guide Dog Care NZ Published 25 March 2026 · 12 min read

At a glance: Labradoodles are one of New Zealand’s most popular hybrid breeds — intelligent, family-friendly, and often low-shedding. But they have specific care needs that every Kiwi owner should understand. This guide covers everything from grooming and exercise to health, diet, and finding a trusted pet sitter when you travel.

What Is a Labradoodle?

A Labradoodle is a cross between a Labrador Retriever and a Poodle. Originally developed in Australia in the late 1980s as a hypoallergenic guide dog, the breed quickly won over families worldwide — including across New Zealand. Their low-to-non-shedding coats and sociable temperament made them an instant hit.

In NZ today, you’ll find Labradoodles in three main size categories:

  • Standard: 23–30+ kg, suited to houses with large yards
  • Medium: 13–20 kg, the most common size in NZ households
  • Miniature: 7–13 kg, great for apartments or smaller sections

First-generation (F1) Labradoodles tend to vary more in coat and temperament. Multigenerational Australian Labradoodles have more consistent traits. When sourcing a puppy in NZ, always ask whether the breeder is focused on health testing and temperament, not just appearance.

Labradoodle Temperament: What to Expect

Labradoodles are energetic, affectionate, and highly social dogs. They tend to:

  • Bond deeply with their whole family, including children
  • Get on well with other dogs and cats when properly socialised
  • Be eager to please, making training relatively straightforward
  • Crave human company — they don’t enjoy long periods alone
  • Have a playful, sometimes boisterous energy well into adulthood

The Poodle side brings intelligence and a tendency to pick up commands quickly. The Labrador side brings warmth, friendliness, and an occasional love of food (and mischief). Together, you get a dog that’s both clever and affectionate — but that does need consistent mental stimulation and daily exercise.

⚠ NZ Tip: Labradoodles can develop separation anxiety if left alone regularly. If you work long hours, plan for a dog walker or pet sitter during the day to keep your Labradoodle happy and out of trouble.

Exercise Requirements in NZ

Labradoodles are active dogs that need at least 60 minutes of exercise per day. New Zealand is a brilliant place to own one — from long beach walks along the Coromandel or Marlborough Sounds to mountain trails in Queenstown or Fiordland.

Good exercise options for Labradoodles in NZ include:

  • Off-lead beach walks (always check local bylaws — many NZ beaches have seasonal dog restrictions)
  • Fetch games in a fenced yard or dog park
  • Swimming — most Labradoodles love the water, just like their Labrador ancestors
  • Hiking on pet-friendly tracks (check DoC guidelines)
  • Agility or flyball classes, which are widely available across Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch

Puppies under 12 months should not be over-exercised. A rough guide is five minutes of structured exercise per month of age, twice daily. Let them run and play freely, but avoid long walks until their joints are fully developed.

Grooming: The Big Commitment

This is where many Labradoodle owners are caught off guard. The coat that makes them low-shedding also requires significant maintenance. Without regular grooming, the fleece or wool coat mats quickly — sometimes to the skin — which can be painful and lead to skin issues.

Grooming Tips for NZ Owners

  • Start grooming routines early — puppies who are handled regularly accept grooming as adults
  • Use a slicker brush and a steel comb to work through the coat from skin to tip
  • Pay extra attention to ears: the fluffy ear canals trap moisture, making Labradoodles prone to ear infections
  • Professional grooming every 6–8 weeks is strongly recommended. Most NZ grooming salons are familiar with the breed
  • After beach swims in salt water, rinse your Labradoodle’s coat to prevent salt build-up and matting
💡 Pro tip: Budget around $80–$140 per professional groom in NZ (varies by size and coat condition). A standard Labradoodle groom in Auckland or Wellington averages $100–$120.

Diet and Nutrition in NZ

Labradoodles do well on a high-quality, protein-rich diet. Whether you choose premium dry kibble, raw food (BARF), or a combination, the key is meeting their nutritional needs without overfeeding — both Labradors and Poodles can be prone to weight gain.

Popular NZ-available brands suitable for Labradoodles include Royal Canin, Hills Science Diet, Advance, and Ziwi Peak (a premium NZ-made option). If feeding raw, consult your vet to ensure the diet is balanced for your dog’s life stage.

Always ensure fresh water is available. NZ tap water is generally safe for dogs. Avoid feeding grapes, raisins, onions, chocolate, macadamia nuts, and xylitol (found in some sugar-free products) — all toxic to dogs.

Health Issues Common in NZ Labradoodles

Labradoodles are generally healthy, but being a hybrid doesn’t make them immune to inherited conditions. Responsible NZ breeders health-test their breeding dogs. Ask for evidence of:

  • Hip and elbow dysplasia scoring — both Labradors and Poodles are susceptible
  • Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA) — a hereditary eye condition. DNA testing can rule this out
  • Exercise-Induced Collapse (EIC) — a Labrador-linked condition to screen for
  • Von Willebrand’s Disease (vWD) — a blood clotting disorder found in some lines
  • Addison’s Disease — more common in Poodle crosses; can be managed with medication

Regular vet checks, dental care, parasite prevention (fleas, ticks, and heartworm are relevant in NZ), and annual vaccinations are essential. Labradoodles typically live 12–15 years.

Ear Health

Ear infections are one of the most common health issues in Labradoodles. Their floppy, fluffy ears restrict airflow and trap moisture. In NZ’s humid regions (Auckland, Northland, the West Coast), this is especially relevant.

  • Check ears weekly for redness, odour, or discharge
  • Clean gently with a vet-recommended ear cleaner
  • Dry ears thoroughly after swimming or bathing
  • Your groomer can pluck hair from the ear canal if needed (not always recommended — ask your vet)

Training Your Labradoodle in NZ

Good news: Labradoodles are among the easiest dogs to train. Their intelligence and eagerness to please means they pick up commands quickly when you use positive reinforcement — treats, praise, and play work best.

Key training milestones to focus on:

  1. Puppy school (8–16 weeks): Enrol as soon as vaccinations allow. Most NZ cities have excellent puppy classes at vet clinics and training clubs.
  2. Recall training: Essential for off-lead beach and park time in NZ. Start early and practice daily.
  3. Loose-lead walking: Labradoodles are strong and enthusiastic walkers. Teach this early or daily walks become a battle.
  4. Crate training: A safe space helps with separation anxiety and makes travel easier.
  5. Advanced obedience or trick training: Keep their minds busy. A bored Labradoodle can be destructive.
🧠 Mental stimulation matters: Combine physical exercise with puzzle feeders, scent games, and training sessions. A Labradoodle that is mentally tired is a calm, happy dog.

Leaving Your Labradoodle: Finding the Right Pet Sitter in NZ

Labradoodles are not suited to kennels as their sole care solution. Their attachment to people means they thrive with in-home care — either a sitter coming to your home or a home boarding situation with an experienced carer.

When choosing a pet sitter for your Labradoodle in NZ, look for someone who:

  • Has experience with larger, energetic breeds
  • Can provide at least 60 minutes of exercise daily
  • Understands the social needs of the breed — they should not be left alone for long stretches
  • Is comfortable with the grooming needs (not full grooming, but brushing on longer sits)
  • Will maintain your dog’s feeding and training routines

Labradoodles and NZ Lifestyle: A Perfect Match

New Zealand’s outdoor culture, extensive trail networks, stunning beaches, and friendly dog community make it one of the best countries in the world to own a Labradoodle. Whether you’re heading to the Tongariro Alpine Crossing with your four-legged mate or simply walking the local reserve after work, your Labradoodle will be happiest when it’s right by your side.

With the right care — consistent grooming, daily exercise, quality nutrition, positive training, and thoughtful care during your absences — a Labradoodle will be one of the most rewarding dogs you’ll ever own.
