Pet Sitting for Birds & Small Animals in New Zealand

Published by ZiggySitters • May 2026 • 13 min read

Dogs and cats get all the attention when it comes to pet sitting. But what about the budgie who needs fresh seed and a chat? The rabbit who thrives on routine? The bearded dragon with specific temperature requirements? Small animals and birds have unique needs that not every sitter is prepared for. This guide covers everything Kiwi owners of non-traditional pets need to know about finding, preparing, and working with a pet sitter.

Why Small Animals Need Sitters Too

There is a common misconception that small animals are "easy" to look after and don't need a proper pet sitter. A neighbour can just pop in and scatter some food, right? Not quite. Small animals are often more sensitive to routine changes than dogs or cats, and many have specific environmental, dietary, and social needs that require genuine knowledge to manage correctly.

In New Zealand, popular small pets include budgerigars, cockatiels, canaries, lovebirds, rabbits, guinea pigs, hamsters, rats, mice, bearded dragons, blue-tongued lizards, turtles, and axolotls. Each of these species has distinct care requirements, and a sitter who doesn't understand them can unintentionally cause stress, illness, or worse.

Why Generic Pet Sitting Doesn't Cut It
  • Birds can die from Teflon fumes, scented candles, or aerosol sprays used in the home
  • Rabbits can go into gut stasis within 12 hours of not eating — a sitter must recognise the signs
  • Reptiles need precise temperature gradients; even a few degrees off can cause serious health issues
  • Guinea pigs need vitamin C daily or they develop scurvy
  • Many small animals hide illness instinctively; only an experienced sitter will notice subtle changes

Species-Specific Sitting Requirements

🐦 Birds (Budgerigars, Cockatiels, Canaries, Lovebirds, Parrots)

Birds are arguably the most misunderstood pets when it comes to sitting. They are highly intelligent, social, and sensitive to environmental changes. A sitter who treats a bird like a fish — feed and forget — is going to have problems.

Daily requirements:

  • Fresh food and water — replace seed and water daily; remove leftover fresh food after 2–4 hours to prevent bacterial growth
  • Social interaction — most companion birds need at least 30 minutes of direct interaction per day; some parrots need hours
  • Cage cleaning — spot-clean droppings daily; change cage liner every 1–2 days
  • Environmental check — ensure no drafts, no new hazards (open windows, ceiling fans, open toilet lids)
  • Cover cage at night — most birds need 10–12 hours of darkness for proper rest

What to tell your sitter:

  • What your bird can and cannot eat (avocado, chocolate, onion, and coffee are toxic)
  • Whether the bird is flighted or clipped — this affects safe handling
  • Whether the bird comes out of the cage — if so, how to manage this safely
  • Any behavioural quirks — does your cockatiel scream at 5pm? Does your budgie bite when scared?
  • Emergency signs: fluffed up and lethargic, sitting on cage floor, not eating, difficulty breathing
Critical Warning: Birds have extremely sensitive respiratory systems. Teflon (non-stick cookware) fumes, scented candles, essential oil diffusers, aerosol sprays, and self-cleaning ovens can kill a bird within minutes. Inform your sitter: NO non-stick cooking, NO candles, NO sprays, NO essential oil diffusers while you are away.

🐰 Rabbits

Rabbits are the third most popular pet in New Zealand after dogs and cats, and they require more specialised care than most people realise. They are also one of the small animals most likely to need an experienced sitter because of their delicate digestive systems.

Daily requirements:

  • Unlimited hay — Timothy or oaten hay should make up 80% of their diet; it keeps their gut moving and teeth worn down
  • Fresh greens — 1–2 cups of leafy greens daily (spinach, kale, bok choy, herbs)
  • Pellets — small amount (1/4 cup per 2kg body weight); not the main food source
  • Exercise — at least 3–4 hours of free-roaming time per day; rabbits shouldn't live in a hutch 24/7
  • Litter tray cleaning — spot-clean daily, full change every 2–3 days
  • Health check — observe eating, drinking, and toileting; gut stasis can be fatal within 24 hours

What to tell your sitter:

  • Gut stasis signs: not eating, not producing droppings, hunched posture, teeth grinding
  • Whether the rabbit is free-roaming, in a pen, or in a hutch — and the daily routine for each
  • Other pets in the house and how the rabbit interacts with them
  • Rabbits are prey animals and may not show pain; subtle behaviour changes are important
  • If the rabbit is bonded with another rabbit, they should NEVER be separated
Tip for Rabbit Owners: Leave your vet's details and explain that gut stasis is an emergency. A rabbit that has not eaten for 12 hours needs to see a vet immediately — it is not something that can "wait until morning." Make this very clear to your sitter.

🐹 Guinea Pigs

Guinea pigs are social, vocal, and surprisingly complex. They are herd animals that should always live in pairs or groups, and they have specific dietary needs that differ from rabbits in important ways.

Daily requirements:

  • Fresh vegetables — 1 cup of mixed veg per pig daily; bell peppers and leafy greens are staples
  • Vitamin C — guinea pigs cannot synthesise their own vitamin C and need 30–50mg daily; deficiency causes scurvy
  • Unlimited hay — Timothy or oaten hay (NOT alfalfa for adults)
  • Fresh water — in both a bottle and a bowl, as some piggies prefer one or the other
  • Cage cleaning — spot-clean daily; full clean every 3–4 days
  • Floor time — daily opportunity to run and popcorn (jump with joy)

What to tell your sitter:

  • Which vegetables are safe and which are not (no iceberg lettuce, no onions, no potato)
  • Guinea pigs are vocal — wheeking is normal, but shrieking is a sign of pain
  • Never pick up a guinea pig by the scruff; support the hindquarters
  • Never house guinea pigs with rabbits — rabbits can injure them and carry different bacteria
  • If one pig in a pair seems off, separate them only if fighting; otherwise, keep bonded pairs together

🦎 Reptiles (Bearded Dragons, Blue-Tongued Lizards, Geckos)

Reptiles are increasingly popular in New Zealand, but they require the most precise environmental management of any common pet. A sitter who doesn't understand temperature gradients, UVB requirements, and feeding schedules can easily make a mistake that harms the animal.

Daily requirements (bearded dragon example):

  • Temperature check — basking spot 38–42°C, cool end 24–28°C; measure with a digital thermometer, never guess
  • UVB lighting — must be on for 10–12 hours daily; replace bulbs every 6–12 months (UVB output degrades before the bulb dies)
  • Feeding — juveniles eat insects 2–3 times daily; adults eat insects 2–3 times per week with daily greens
  • Fresh water — change daily; some dragons prefer to drink from a shallow dish, others from misting
  • Spot cleaning — remove droppings daily
  • Handling — 10–15 minutes of gentle handling daily helps maintain tameness

What to tell your sitter:

  • The exact temperature settings for both the basking spot and the cool end
  • How to check temperatures with the thermometer you provide
  • What to feed, how much, and when — be specific (e.g., "5 medium crickets dusted with calcium, 9am and 5pm")
  • Which supplements to use and how often (calcium daily, multivitamin weekly)
  • What NOT to do: never use heat rocks, never pull stuck shed, never force-feed
  • Signs of illness: lethargy, not eating, swollen eyes, stuck shed, discoloured belly
Critical Warning: Reptiles are cold-blooded and cannot regulate their own body temperature. If a heat lamp blows or a thermostat fails while you are away, the reptile can become critically ill within hours. Leave a spare heat lamp and thermostat, and ensure your sitter knows how to replace them.

🐹 Hamsters, Mice, and Rats

Small rodents are often considered "easy" pets, but they have specific needs that a sitter should understand. Hamsters are solitary and nocturnal; rats are social and intelligent; mice are quick and easily stressed.

Key sitting differences:

  • Hamsters: Nocturnal — don't wake them during the day. Never put two hamsters together (they fight to the death). Syrian hamsters need a minimum 80cm × 50cm cage; dwarf hamsters need at least 60cm × 40cm
  • Rats: Highly social — must live in pairs or groups. Need at least 1 hour of free-roaming time daily. Intelligent and need enrichment (puzzle toys, foraging, training). Can be litter-trained
  • Mice: Females can live in groups; males must be housed alone (they fight). Fast and agile — escape artists. Need secure handling and quiet environments

🐢 Turtles and Axolotls

Aquatic and semi-aquatic pets need water quality management that many sitters aren't familiar with.

  • Long-necked turtles: Need large tanks (minimum 1m for one adult), basking area, UVB light, water temperature 24–26°C. Feed every 2–3 days. Water quality testing is essential
  • Axolotls: Must be kept at 14–20°C — above 22°C is dangerous. No gravel substrate (they swallow it). Feed earthworms or axolotl pellets every 2–3 days. Do not use lights; they prefer darkness

Finding the Right Sitter for Your Small Animal

What to Look For

  • Species experience — Ask specifically about their experience with your type of pet, not just "small animals" generally. A sitter who has kept rabbits may know nothing about reptile care
  • Vet knowledge — Can they recognise the signs of gut stasis in rabbits? Respiratory distress in birds? Impaction in lizards?
  • References — Ask for references from other owners of the same species
  • Confidence — A good sitter will ask you questions about your pet's specific needs rather than pretending to know everything
  • Communication style — You want a sitter who will send you updates and tell you if something seems off, not someone who disappears for days

The Meet and Greet

Always do a meet and greet before your first booking. For small animals, this is especially important because:

  1. You can demonstrate your pet's feeding and care routine in person
  2. The sitter can see how you handle your pet (picking up, restraint, health checks)
  3. You can observe whether the sitter seems comfortable and confident with your animal
  4. Your pet can get used to the sitter's scent and presence
  5. You can walk the sitter through your home setup (cage, enclosure, free-roaming areas, hazards)
Tip: For birds and reptiles, a second visit before you leave can be valuable. Let the sitter do a full care routine while you are home so you can correct anything on the spot. This is much better than correcting via text message from another country.

The Pet Sitting Instruction Sheet

Create a written instruction sheet and leave it next to your pet's enclosure. Don't rely on verbal instructions alone — your sitter will forget details, especially if they're caring for multiple animals. Here is what to include:

Essential Information

  • Feeding schedule: Exact times, amounts, and food types. Be specific: "2 tablespoons of pellets + 1 cup mixed greens (spinach, bok choy, capsicum)" not "feed as usual"
  • Water: How often to change, what type (filtered, treated, room temperature), and any additives
  • Environment: Temperature settings, lighting schedules, humidity levels if applicable
  • Handling: Whether and how to handle your pet, for how long, and any restrictions
  • Exercise: How much, when, and in what area (supervised free-roaming, playpen, etc.)
  • Cleaning: Daily spot-cleaning tasks and weekly deep-cleaning schedule
  • Medications: Name, dose, frequency, and how to administer (in food, by hand, etc.)

Emergency Information

  • Your vet: Name, address, phone number, and hours
  • After-hours vet: Name, address, phone number (different from your regular vet)
  • Emergency signs specific to your pet: "If my rabbit hasn't eaten in 8 hours, call the vet immediately"
  • Your contact details: Phone, email, and a backup contact (friend or family member)
  • Insurance details: If your pet is insured, include the policy number and provider

Household Rules

  • What the sitter can and cannot use in your home (kitchen, TV, Wi-Fi, etc.)
  • Products that are dangerous for your pet (non-stick pans, candles, sprays, essential oils)
  • Other animals the sitter may encounter (neighbours' cats, family dogs)
  • Whether windows and doors must remain closed (especially for birds and small animals)

Common Mistakes Owners Make

  1. Assuming any sitter can handle any pet — A great dog sitter may know nothing about birds or reptiles. Always check species-specific experience
  2. Not leaving enough food — Always leave 2–3 extra days of food beyond your planned return date, in case of delays
  3. Vague instructions — "Feed as normal" is not enough. Write exact amounts, brands, and schedules
  4. Forgetting to mention hazards — Teflon pans for birds, heat lamps for reptiles, open toilet lids for small animals — these can be fatal
  5. Not doing a trial run — Have the sitter do at least one visit while you're home before you leave
  6. Hiding medical issues — If your rabbit has a history of gut stasis, your sitter needs to know so they can watch for early signs
  7. Not providing emergency funds — Leave a way for the sitter to pay for a vet visit if needed (pet insurance details, or cash in an envelope marked "vet emergency")

Boarding vs Home Sitting for Small Animals

When Home Sitting Is Better

  • Birds — almost always better at home; transport is stressful, and new environments can trigger feather plucking or panic
  • Reptiles — moving enclosures disrupts temperature cycles and causes stress
  • Fish and axolotls — tank transport is risky and water chemistry can crash
  • Rabbits — home environment is safer for gut health; stress from travel or new environments can trigger gut stasis

When Boarding Might Work

  • Guinea pigs and hamsters — portable enough that a short car ride to a sitter's home is manageable
  • If your home has hazards the sitter cannot manage — aggressive dogs, unsafe enclosures, renovation work
  • For very short trips — a 1–2 day stay at a specialist small animal boarder can be fine for hardy animals
Tip: If you choose boarding, look for a boarder who specialises in your species. A "small animal boarder" who primarily boards rabbits may not know how to care for a bearded dragon, and vice versa. Ask for photos of their setup and references from owners of the same species.

Preparing Your Home for the Sitter

Before you leave, make your sitter's job as easy as possible:

  1. Clean everything before you go — fresh cage liner, clean water bottles, full food containers
  2. Pre-portion food — separate daily portions into labelled bags or containers
  3. Leave extras — 2–3 extra days of food, hay, bedding, and any supplies the sitter might need
  4. Label everything — supplements, medications, cleaning products
  5. Show the sitter where everything is — spare bulbs, thermostat, cleaning supplies, carrier for vet visits
  6. Set up a camera — a cheap Wi-Fi camera pointed at the enclosure gives you peace of mind without hovering over the sitter
  7. Remove hazards — put away Teflon pans, scented candles, essential oils, fly spray, and any toxic plants
  8. Secure the environment — close windows, block gaps under doors, ensure enclosures are fully latched

NZ-Specific Considerations

  • Heat: New Zealand summers can push indoor temperatures above 30°C, which is dangerous for many small animals. Leave clear instructions for cooling (fans, frozen water bottles, relocating enclosures)
  • Power outages: NZ storms can cause power cuts. For reptiles and fish, provide a battery backup or clear instructions for manual temperature management
  • Vet access: Not all NZ vets treat exotics. Research and provide the details of the nearest exotics-capable vet. In rural areas, this may be a significant drive
  • Biosecurity: If you live near a DOC reserve or conservation area, keep pets contained and inform your sitter of any local predator risks (stoats, ferrets, feral cats)
  • Earthquake preparedness: Ensure enclosures are secured against shaking; leave instructions for what to do in an earthquake
