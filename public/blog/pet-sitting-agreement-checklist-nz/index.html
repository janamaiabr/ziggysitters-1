Pet Sitting Agreement Checklist: What Every NZ Pet Owner Needs

2026-05-21 · 7 min read · ZiggySitters Team

A pet sitting agreement NZ owners can actually use does not need to be a scary legal document. It needs to be clear, practical, and written down before the sitter gets the keys. The goal is simple: everyone knows what care is expected, what happens in an emergency, how payment works, and who to call if something changes.

Whether you are booking a sitter for one night, a two-week overseas trip, or regular home visits while you work shifts, a written checklist protects your pet, your home, and the sitter. It also prevents the classic awkward messages: “Where is the cat food?”, “Is this pill once or twice daily?”, and “Was I allowed to use the spare room?”

Why you need a written pet sitting agreement

Most pet sitting problems are not caused by bad intentions. They are caused by assumptions. The owner assumes the sitter will water plants. The sitter assumes the dog is fine off-lead. The owner assumes payment includes petrol. The sitter assumes emergency vet treatment is pre-approved. A written agreement turns assumptions into instructions.

In New Zealand, pet sitting can overlap with consumer expectations, service quality, privacy, property access, and animal welfare responsibilities. A simple agreement will not replace professional legal advice for complex situations, but it creates a useful record of what both sides agreed before the booking began.

1. Names, dates, location, and contact details

Start with the boring basics. Include the owner name, sitter name, booking dates, service address, arrival and departure times, and all contact numbers. Add a backup contact who can make decisions if the owner is on a flight, out of coverage, or asleep in another time zone.

  • Owner mobile and email.
  • Sitter mobile and email.
  • Emergency contact name, phone, and relationship.
  • Vet clinic, after-hours vet, and preferred transport option.
  • Neighbour or property manager contact if relevant.

2. Emergency vet care authorisation

This is the clause you do not want to improvise. State clearly when the sitter may seek veterinary care, which vet to use, and whether there is a spending limit before extra approval is required. Many owners choose a practical threshold, such as authorising urgent stabilisation and asking the vet to contact the owner for non-urgent treatment decisions.

Include your pet insurance provider, policy number if available, and whether the sitter can share your details with the clinic. If your pet has a known condition, write down what is normal, what is concerning, and what counts as an emergency.

3. Feeding schedule and food rules

Feeding notes should be precise. “Feed twice a day” is not enough for a sitter who has never met your pet. Include amounts, times, location of food, approved treats, forbidden foods, and what to do if the pet refuses a meal.

  • Breakfast and dinner times.
  • Exact food brand, flavour, and portion size.
  • Treat limits and training rewards.
  • Food allergies or sensitive stomach warnings.
  • Whether pets must be separated during meals.

4. Medication and health instructions

Medication instructions need zero ambiguity. List the medication name, dose, time, method, storage, and what to do if a dose is missed or vomited. If your pet resists tablets, explain the trick that works: cheese, pill pocket, crushed in wet food, or a specific handling routine.

For senior pets, puppies, kittens, or animals recovering from surgery, add mobility restrictions, wound checks, cone instructions, toileting notes, and warning signs. Do not rely on a quick verbal handover at the door.

5. House rules and access

A house sitting agreement NZ owners use should cover both pet care and property access. Write down where the sitter may sleep, whether guests are allowed, whether alcohol is okay, heating or air conditioning expectations, rubbish collection days, Wi-Fi details, alarm codes, and any areas that are off-limits.

Key handover deserves its own line item. Agree how keys will be collected and returned, what happens if the sitter is locked out, and whether spare keys are held by a neighbour. If you use smart locks, provide temporary codes rather than sharing your permanent family code.

6. Walks, transport, and public outings

For dogs, define walk frequency, duration, route, lead rules, and whether dog parks are allowed. If your dog has recall issues, reactivity, prey drive, or fear of scooters, say so directly. It is not embarrassing; it is safety information.

If the sitter may drive your pet, include permission for transport and any crate, harness, or car restraint requirements. For cats, rabbits, birds, and other pets, state whether outdoor access is allowed at all. “Indoor only” should mean indoor only.

7. Liability, insurance, and realistic expectations

Discuss what happens if property is damaged, a pet becomes unwell, or a sitter cannot complete the booking due to illness. Check whether the platform, sitter, or owner has insurance that applies. Keep a record of valuables, fragile items, and any pre-existing pet health issues.

The New Zealand Consumer Guarantees Act may be relevant where pet sitting services are supplied in trade, because services are generally expected to be carried out with reasonable care and skill. That said, every arrangement is different, and private informal arrangements can be murkier. If the stakes are high, get proper advice.

8. Payment terms and cancellations

Write down the total price, deposit, payment date, included services, extra charges, and cancellation policy. Clarify whether additional walks, extra pets, late returns, public holidays, petrol, or last-minute medication tasks cost extra.

Cancellation terms should be fair and visible before booking. Owners need confidence that care is locked in; sitters need protection from losing income after holding dates. A written policy avoids uncomfortable arguments when travel plans change.

A good pet sitting contract checklist is not about distrust. It is about kindness to your future self, your sitter, and your pet. The clearer the agreement, the calmer the booking.
