Cat Urinary Tract Health NZ Guide: Prevention, Symptoms & Care

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  May 20, 2026  •  12 min read

Urinary tract issues are one of the most common reasons cats end up at the vet in New Zealand. Feline Lower Urinary Tract Disease (FLUTD) affects up to 3% of cats each year, and urinary blockages in male cats can become fatal within 24-48 hours. This guide covers everything Kiwi cat owners need to know about recognising, preventing, and managing urinary tract problems.

What Is FLUTD?

Feline Lower Urinary Tract Disease is not a single condition but an umbrella term covering several problems that affect the bladder and urethra. These include:

  • Urethral plugs — a mixture of crystals, mucus, and inflammatory cells that block the urethra
  • Urinary stones (uroliths) — mineral deposits that form in the bladder
  • Feline Idiopathic Cystitis (FIC) — inflammation with no identifiable cause, the most common diagnosis in NZ cats under 10
  • Urinary tract infections (UTIs) — bacterial infections, more common in older cats
  • Bladder cancer — rare but possible, especially in senior cats
ZG Tip: In New Zealand, FIC accounts for roughly 55-65% of FLUTD cases in cats under 10. Stress is the primary trigger, which is why environmental management matters as much as diet.

Warning Signs: When Your Cat Has a Problem

Cats are masters at hiding discomfort. By the time you notice something is wrong, the problem may have been developing for days or weeks. Watch for these signs:

Early Symptoms

  • Visiting the litter box more frequently than usual
  • Straining or squatting for extended periods without producing urine
  • Crying or vocalising while in the litter box
  • Peeing outside the litter box (on beds, carpets, laundry baskets)
  • Excessive licking of the genital area
  • Blood in the urine (may appear pink, red, or brownish)

Emergency Symptoms (Seek Immediate Vet Care)

⚠ Emergency: If your cat, especially a male, is straining and producing no urine at all, this is a life-threatening blockage. A blocked cat can die within 24-48 hours from toxin buildup and bladder rupture. Do not wait. Go to an emergency vet immediately.
  • Complete inability to urinate (no wet spots in litter box)
  • A distended, hard abdomen (the bladder is full and painful)
  • Vomiting, lethargy, or collapse
  • Refusing food and water
  • Hiding more than usual or aggressive when touched near the belly

Why Male Cats Are at Higher Risk

Male cats have a much narrower urethra than females, making them far more susceptible to complete blockages. A urethral plug or stone that would pass through a female cat can completely obstruct a male. If you have a male cat, you should be especially vigilant about urinary health.

Breeds with higher risk include:

  • Persians and Himalayans — genetically predisposed to calcium oxalate stones
  • Burmese — prone to struvite crystals
  • British Shorthairs — higher rates of FIC
  • Any overweight indoor male cat — the highest risk demographic in NZ vet clinics

Prevention: The Six Pillars of Cat Urinary Health

1. Hydration Is Everything

Most NZ cats eat dry kibble and live indoors, a combination that naturally deprives them of water. Cats evolved as desert animals with a low thirst drive, getting most of their moisture from prey. A dry-food-only diet means chronically concentrated urine, which is the perfect environment for crystal formation.

  • Feed wet food as at least 50-70% of the diet
  • Add warm water to dry food to create a broth
  • Place multiple water stations around the house, away from food and litter
  • Use a cat water fountain — many cats prefer flowing water and will drink significantly more
  • Consider flavouring water with a small amount of tuna juice or chicken broth (no onion/garlic)

2. The Right Diet

Not all urinary diets are created equal, and the right choice depends on what type of crystals or stones your cat forms:

Crystal Type pH Needed Dietary Approach
Struvite (most common in NZ) Acidic (pH 6.0-6.5) Prescription urinary diets (Hill\'s c/d, Royal Canin Urinary S/O)
Calcium oxalate Neutral to slightly alkaline Low-protein, low-oxalate diets; avoid excess vitamin C
Urate/cystine (rare) Alkaline Vet-specific dietary management
Important: Never start a urinary diet without vet guidance. If your cat has oxalate crystals and you acidify their urine further, you make the problem worse. Always get a urinalysis first.

3. Stress Management

Stress is the leading cause of FIC in New Zealand cats. Common triggers include:

  • Multi-cat household tension
  • Changes in routine (new work schedule, visitors, renovations)
  • Moving house
  • New pets or people in the home
  • Outdoor cats visible through windows
  • Lack of vertical territory or hiding spots

Practical stress reducers for NZ homes:

  • Provide one litter box per cat plus one extra (rule of n+1)
  • Place litter boxes in quiet, low-traffic areas
  • Use Feliway diffusers — available from all major NZ vet clinics and Petco
  • Create vertical spaces: cat trees, shelving, window perches
  • Keep a consistent feeding schedule
  • If you are away, a professional cat sitter who visits daily maintains routine and reduces the stress of your absence

4. Litter Box Best Practices

Poor litter box management is both a cause and an indicator of urinary problems:

  • Scoop at least twice daily — cats hate dirty boxes and will hold their urine
  • Use unscented, clumping litter (fragrances irritate many cats)
  • Replace litter completely every 2-3 weeks
  • Wash the box with mild soap, not harsh chemicals
  • Boxes should be 1.5 times the length of your cat (many sold in NZ are too small)
  • Avoid covered boxes if possible — they trap odours and make cats feel cornered

5. Weight Management

Overweight cats are significantly more prone to urinary issues. Extra body fat increases inflammation throughout the body, reduces activity levels, and leads to more concentrated urine. If your cat needs to lose weight:

  • Aim for gradual loss: 1-2% of body weight per week
  • Use puzzle feeders to slow eating and add activity
  • Switch to weight management wet food (higher water content helps double-duty)
  • Play interactive games daily: feather wands, laser pointers, treat balls

6. Regular Vet Check-Ups

Annual vet visits (or 6-monthly for cats over 8) should include a urinalysis if your cat has any history of urinary issues. Many NZ vet clinics offer wellness plans that include routine urine testing. Early detection of crystals or infection can prevent an emergency.

Diagnosis: What to Expect at the Vet

When you take your cat to a New Zealand vet for urinary symptoms, expect the following:

  1. Physical exam — the vet will palpate the abdomen to check bladder size and pain
  2. Urinalysis — to check pH, crystals, blood, bacteria, and concentration
  3. Urine culture — if infection is suspected, to identify the bacteria and choose the right antibiotic
  4. Ultrasound or X-ray — to check for stones, bladder wall thickening, or other abnormalities
  5. Blood work — especially if a blockage is suspected, to check kidney values and electrolytes
NZ Vet Costs: A basic urinary work-up (exam + urinalysis) typically costs $120-$250. Emergency unblocking of a male cat can cost $800-$3,000+ depending on severity and whether surgery (perineal urethrostomy) is needed. Pet insurance makes a real difference here.

Treatment Options

For FIC (Stress-Related Cystitis)

  • Environmental modification (the most effective long-term treatment)
  • Pain medication (NSAIDs or gabapentin, vet-prescribed)
  • Increased water intake
  • Prescription urinary food during flare-ups
  • Feliway and calming supplements (Zylkene, available at NZ vets)
  • Most cases resolve in 5-7 days with proper management

For Urinary Blockages (Emergency)

  • Emergency unblocking under sedation (catheterisation)
  • IV fluids to flush the system and restore hydration
  • Hospitalisation for 24-72 hours to monitor urine output
  • Pain management and anti-spasmodic medication
  • In recurrent cases: perineal urethrostomy (surgery to widen the urethra)

For Crystals and Stones

  • Prescription dissolution diet (3-6 weeks for struvite stones)
  • Surgical removal for calcium oxalate stones (they cannot be dissolved)
  • Long-term prescription diet to prevent recurrence
  • Regular urinalysis monitoring

When You Are Away: Why a Cat Sitter Matters

Cats with a history of urinary issues need close monitoring, especially when their routine changes. A professional cat sitter can be the difference between catching a flare-up early and an expensive emergency.

A good sitter will:

  • Monitor litter box output daily — noticing changes in frequency, volume, or colour
  • Ensure fresh water is always available and the fountain is clean
  • Stick to feeding schedules and medication routines
  • Recognise early signs of distress: hiding, vocalising, excessive licking
  • Contact you and your vet immediately if something seems off

Long-Term Management Checklist

If your cat has had a urinary issue, here is your ongoing management checklist:

  • ☑ Feed predominantly wet food or a vet-recommended urinary diet
  • ☑ Multiple clean water sources, including a fountain
  • ☑ Scoop litter box 2+ times daily and track output
  • ☑ Feliway diffuser in the main living area
  • ☑ Maintain a healthy weight through portion control and play
  • ☑ 6-monthly vet check-ups with urinalysis
  • ☑ Minimise stress: consistent routine, hiding spots, vertical territory
  • ☑ Have an emergency vet number saved in your phone
  • ☑ Brief your pet sitter on urinary history before going away
  • ☑ Keep prescription food stocked (do not run out and substitute)
