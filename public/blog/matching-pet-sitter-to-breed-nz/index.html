Matching a Pet Sitter to Your Breed: How to Find the Right Fit in New Zealand

2026-05-28 · 12 min read · ZiggySitters Team

Finding a pet sitter is easy. Finding the right pet sitter for your specific pet is harder. A sitter who is perfect for a chilled-out senior cat might be entirely wrong for a border collie that needs two hours of off-lead running before breakfast. A sitter experienced with confident, social dogs may be out of their depth with a rescue greyhound that freezes on walks. The breed, temperament, and individual personality of your pet should be the starting point for choosing a sitter, not an afterthought. This guide walks you through how to match a sitter to your pet\'s breed and personality, with specific advice for the most common breeds and temperaments in New Zealand.

Why Breed and Temperament Matter More Than You Think

Pet sitting is not a one-size-fits-all service. The skills, energy, and experience required to care for a high-drive working dog are fundamentally different from what is needed for a senior cat with arthritis. Yet many pet owners hire the first sitter with good reviews without considering whether that sitter\'s strengths align with their pet\'s needs. This mismatch is one of the most common reasons pet sits go wrong.

Breed characteristics give you a useful starting framework. A border collie was bred to work livestock for hours. A cavalier King Charles spaniel was bred as a companion dog. A greyhound was bred for short bursts of intense speed. A Bengal cat was bred for intelligence and activity. These instincts don\'t disappear because the animal lives in a Wellington apartment. They express themselves in behaviour, energy levels, training needs, and health vulnerabilities that a sitter must understand and manage.

But breed is only half the picture. Individual temperament varies enormously within breeds. A border collie can be calm and cuddly. A greyhound can be anxious and reactive. A ragdoll can be surprisingly aloof. The best approach is to consider both breed tendencies and your individual pet\'s personality when evaluating a sitter.

High-Energy Working Breeds

Border Collie, Australian Shepherd, Huntaway, Heading Dog

These breeds are the athletes of the dog world, and they are common across New Zealand, particularly in rural areas and lifestyle blocks. They need significant daily exercise, mental stimulation, and a handler who understands that "walked" does not mean "tired." A 30-minute leash walk around the block is a warm-up, not a workout.

What to look for in a sitter:

  • Experience with high-drive, intelligent breeds that need both physical and mental exercise
  • Willingness and fitness to provide 60 to 120 minutes of active exercise daily, including off-lead time in safe areas
  • Knowledge of enrichment activities, puzzle toys, and training games that provide mental stimulation
  • Understanding of herding breed behaviours, including nipping, circling, fixating on movement, and reactivity to bikes, children, or other animals
  • Ability to manage separation anxiety, which is very common in working breeds that bond intensely with their owners

Red flags: A sitter who says "I love long walks" but means a stroll to the café. A sitter without access to safe off-lead areas. A sitter who has only cared for low-energy breeds.

Huntaway and Heading Dog (NZ Farm Dogs)

These are New Zealand\'s own working breeds, and they deserve special mention. Huntaways are bred to use their voice to move stock, which means they bark. A lot. A sitter unfamiliar with the breed may find this challenging or try to suppress a behaviour that is fundamentally part of the dog\'s nature. Heading dogs are intense, focused, and need a job to do. Without one, they will invent their own, which usually involves herding other pets, children, or traffic.

What to look for in a sitter:

  • Understanding that huntaways vocalise and this is normal breed behaviour, not a problem to be fixed
  • Experience with farm dogs or high-drive breeds, even if not specifically huntaways
  • Comfort with dogs that need a purpose: fetch, agility, or training games during the sit
  • Rural experience is a bonus, particularly if the sit involves farm properties

Brachycephalic (Flat-Faced) Breeds

French Bulldog, Pug, British Bulldog, Cavalier King Charles Spaniel

Flat-faced breeds are hugely popular in New Zealand, especially in Auckland and Wellington apartments. They are charming, companionable, and relatively low-energy. They are also medically fragile in ways that a sitter must understand. Brachycephalic obstructive airway syndrome means these dogs struggle to breathe normally. They overheat rapidly, cannot regulate temperature through panting effectively, and are at risk of respiratory crisis in warm or humid conditions.

What to look for in a sitter:

  • Experience specifically with brachycephalic breeds and their breathing difficulties
  • Understanding that these dogs cannot be exercised in heat, need air-conditioned environments in summer, and may need to rest frequently on walks
  • Knowledge of bloat risk, eye prolapse risk, and skin fold infections common in these breeds
  • Patience with dogs that snore, snort, and may need assistance clearing their airways
  • Willingness to follow strict feeding routines and avoid exercise within an hour of meals

Red flags: A sitter who suggests long beach walks in summer. A sitter who does not ask about your dog\'s breathing history. A sitter who is unfamiliar with the term "brachycephalic."

Large and Giant Breeds

German Shepherd, Labrador, Golden Retriever, Great Dane

Large and giant breeds present different challenges. German shepherds are loyal and protective but can be reactive with strangers, which makes the sitter introduction process critical. Labradors and goldens are social butterflies but will eat anything, including items that can cause blockages. Great Danes are gentle giants but their sheer size creates practical challenges, from getting them into a car to managing them on a lead if they decide to pull.

What to look for in a sitter:

  • Physical capability to manage a strong, large dog on a lead, including unexpected lunges at distractions
  • Experience with the specific breed\'s common health issues: hip dysplasia in shepherds, obesity in labs, bloat in deep-chested breeds
  • Understanding that large dogs need structured exercise, not just free play, to protect their joints
  • For German shepherds: experience with guardian breeds and an understanding that initial wariness of strangers is normal, not aggression
  • For giant breeds: comfort with a dog that may lean its entire body weight against you as a sign of affection

Sighthounds and Rescue Greyhounds

Greyhound, Whippet, Italian Greyhound

New Zealand has a growing community of adopted racing greyhounds, and they are wonderful pets. They are also deeply misunderstood by people who have not lived with sighthounds. A greyhound that freezes on a walk is not being stubborn; it is overwhelmed. A greyhound that sleeps 18 hours a day is not depressed; it is being a greyhound. A sitter who misinterprets these normal breed behaviours can create significant stress for the dog.

What to look for in a sitter:

  • Experience with sighthounds, ideally greyhounds specifically
  • Understanding of "freezing" behaviour and how to handle it patiently without forcing the dog
  • Knowledge that greyhounds must never be off-lead in unfenced areas due to their prey drive and speed
  • Awareness of greyhound-specific needs: thin skin that tears easily, sensitivity to temperature, and the need for soft bedding
  • Comfort with a dog that may not want to walk far and prefers to sleep on the couch
  • Understanding that muzzles are normal for some greyhounds during play and are not a sign of aggression

Cats: Not All Felines Are Created Equal

Confident, Social Breeds: Bengal, Siamese, Ragdoll, Maine Coon

These breeds are the extroverts of the cat world. Bengals need interactive play sessions and vertical climbing space. Siamese are vocal and demand attention, and they can become destructive if bored. Ragdolls are famously laid-back and follow their humans from room to room. Maine Coons are large, playful, and surprisingly dog-like in their social needs.

What to look for in a sitter:

  • Willingness to engage in active play for 20 to 30 minutes per visit, not just a quick pat and feed
  • Experience with vocal breeds that will demand attention and may cry if left alone
  • Understanding that these cats need mental stimulation: puzzle feeders, rotating toys, and interaction
  • For Bengals specifically: comfort with a cat that climbs high, opens doors, and may be more dog than cat in its energy levels

Anxious or Shy Cats: Rescue Cats, Persian, British Shorthair

Some cats are naturally reserved. Others have been through trauma that makes them wary of new people. A sitter who expects every cat to come running for cuddles will be frustrated, and worse, will push the cat into further withdrawal. These cats need a sitter with patience, a light touch, and realistic expectations.

What to look for in a sitter:

  • Experience with shy, fearful, or undersocialised cats
  • Patience to sit quietly and let the cat approach on their own terms
  • Understanding that hiding is normal behaviour for stressed cats and should not be interrupted
  • Knowledge of feline body language: when a cat is relaxed vs. when it is tolerating interaction vs. when it is genuinely distressed
  • Willingness to do "quiet visits" where the sitter simply reads a book in the same room, building trust gradually
  • Experience administering medication to reluctant cats if needed

Puppies and Senior Pets

Puppies (Under 12 Months)

Puppies are in a category of their own. They need frequent feeding, regular toilet breaks, socialisation, supervision, and a sitter who understands developmental stages. A sitter who is great with adult dogs may be overwhelmed by a puppy that needs to go outside every 45 minutes, bites everything, and has not yet learned basic commands.

What to look for in a sitter:

  • Experience with puppies specifically, including knowledge of vaccination schedules and socialisation windows
  • Availability for multiple visits per day or overnight stays, as puppies should not be left alone for more than two to three hours
  • Patience for biting, chewing, accidents, and the general chaos of puppyhood
  • Willingness to follow your training approach consistently, rather than switching methods mid-sit

Senior Pets (7+ Years for Dogs, 10+ for Cats)

Older animals need a sitter who recognises that slowing down is normal but pain is not. A sitter who pushes a 12-year-old lab to walk as far as a 3-year-old is not doing the dog any favours. Equally, a sitter who dismisses subtle signs of discomfort as "just getting old" may miss early symptoms of conditions that need veterinary attention.

What to look for in a sitter:

  • Experience with senior pets and age-related conditions: arthritis, cognitive decline, incontinence, vision or hearing loss
  • Patience for slower walks, longer rest periods, and possible accidents in the house
  • Ability to administer medications reliably, including pills, eye drops, injections, and topical treatments
  • Knowledge of when to seek veterinary attention versus when something is within the range of normal for an older animal
  • Gentleness and understanding for a pet that may be confused, disoriented, or less responsive than it once was

The Meet-and-Greet: Breed-Specific Questions to Ask

When you meet a potential sitter, tailor your questions to your pet\'s breed and personality. Here are breed-specific questions worth asking:

  • High-energy breeds: "How would you manage a day with a dog that needs 90 minutes of active exercise and still has energy at bedtime?"
  • Brachycephalic breeds: "What would you do if my Frenchie started struggling to breathe on a warm day?"
  • Greyhounds: "Have you cared for sighthounds before? How would you handle a dog that freezes on a walk?"
  • Anxious cats: "My cat hides when strangers arrive. How would you approach the first visit?"
  • Puppies: "How do you handle biting and toilet training consistently during a sit?"
  • Senior pets: "How would you tell the difference between normal aging and something that needs a vet visit?"

A sitter who gives specific, informed answers to these questions is one who understands your breed. A sitter who gives generic answers like "I love all animals" or "I\'m sure I can handle it" may not have the breed-specific knowledge your pet needs.

What to Include on Your Care Sheet

Regardless of breed, your care sheet should include details that help a sitter understand your specific pet. Go beyond feeding times and vet contacts. Include:

  • Breed-specific behaviours that are normal for your pet but might alarm a sitter who doesn\'t know the breed (e.g., "She herds the cat; this is normal border collie behaviour, not aggression")
  • Exercise requirements with specifics (e.g., "Needs 45 minutes of off-lead running plus 15 minutes of training games; a walk on lead alone is not sufficient")
  • Health quirks specific to the breed (e.g., "He may reverse-sneeze; this is normal for French bulldogs and not an emergency")
  • Triggers and fears (e.g., "He is reactive to skateboards and other dogs on lead; cross the street if you see either")
  • Comfort preferences (e.g., "She will not eat from a bowl; food must be scattered or placed in a puzzle toy")

Final Thoughts

The right sitter for your pet is not necessarily the sitter with the most five-star reviews or the lowest price. It is the sitter whose experience, temperament, and daily routine match what your specific pet needs. A border collie needs a runner, not a cuddler. A greyhound needs a patient observer, not a drill sergeant. A senior cat needs a quiet companion, not an entertainer. A brachycephalic dog needs someone who knows what an emergency looks like, not someone who will figure it out when it happens.

Take the time to match your sitter to your pet\'s breed and personality. The meet-and-greet is your opportunity to ask the breed-specific questions that separate a great sitter from a merely adequate one. Your pet cannot tell you when something is wrong, so the sitter\'s ability to read breed-specific behaviour is not a nice-to-have, it is essential.
