Pet Sitting for Senior Pets: Special Care Guide for NZ

Published 10 June 2026 • 13 min read • ZiggySitters NZ

Your older companion has given you years of loyalty and love. When you need to leave them in someone else\'s care, the stakes are higher than with a young, healthy pet. Senior animals require specialised attention — from medication schedules and mobility support to monitoring subtle health changes that could signal an emergency. This guide helps you prepare your pet sitter for the unique needs of your ageing dog or cat.

When Is a Pet "Senior"?

The age at which a pet is considered senior varies by breed and size:

Cats10-12 years15+ years

In New Zealand, with our generally active outdoor lifestyle, many dogs stay physically capable well into their senior years. However, ageing is not just about the number on the chart. A 9-year-old Kelpie working on a South Island farm may have the joints of a much older dog, while a pampered indoor cat in Auckland might still be spry at 14. What matters most is your individual pet\'s health status, not their age alone.

Why Senior Pet Sitting Is Different

A standard pet sit might involve feeding, a walk, and some playtime. Senior pet sitting requires a fundamentally different approach:

  • Medication management: Older pets are often on multiple medications with specific timing requirements. Missing a dose of heart medication or pain relief can have serious consequences.
  • Mobility limitations: Arthritis, hip dysplasia, and spinal issues are common in senior NZ dogs. Your sitter needs to know which surfaces are slippery, where ramps or steps are needed, and how to support your pet safely.
  • Cognitive changes: Canine Cognitive Dysfunction (CCD) and feline cognitive decline can cause confusion, night-time restlessness, and changes in behaviour that a sitter might misinterpret as naughtiness.
  • Incontinence: Many senior pets have accidents. A sitter who does not understand this may become frustrated or punish the pet — which only worsens anxiety.
  • Appetite changes: Senior pets eat less, eat slower, and may need food warmed or specially prepared. A sitter who expects an empty bowl may overfeed or panic unnecessarily.

Preparing Your Pet Sitter: The Essential Briefing

1. Medical Information Sheet

Create a one-page document with:

  • Vet clinic name, address, and phone number (include after-hours number)
  • Your regular vet\'s name
  • All current medications: name, dosage, frequency, and administration method (pill, liquid, injection)
  • Known allergies and adverse reactions
  • Existing conditions: arthritis, kidney disease, diabetes, heart condition, cancer, etc.
  • Last blood work date and any abnormal results
  • Insurance details (if applicable) — NZ pet insurance provider and policy number
Critical: If your pet is on insulin for diabetes, make sure the sitter knows the exact timing relationship between insulin administration and meals. Skipping or delaying a meal after insulin can cause dangerous hypoglycaemia.

2. Medication Administration Guide

Do not assume your sitter knows how to give medication. Provide:

  • Pill pockets or wrapping technique: Show the sitter exactly how you hide pills. Many senior pets are wise to the old "pill in cheese" trick and will eat around it.
  • Liquid medication: Demonstrate how to use the syringe or dropper. Show the correct position (side of mouth, not throat) and how much to give.
  • Eye or ear drops: Demonstrate how you restrain your pet gently and apply the drops without stressing them.
  • Injections: If your pet requires subcutaneous injections (common for diabetic cats), only a sitter trained in this procedure should administer them. Consider boarding at your vet clinic if you cannot find a qualified sitter.
Pro tip: Pre-sort medications into a daily pill organiser labelled with times. This removes guesswork and reduces the chance of double-dosing. Include a photo of each pill next to its name so the sitter can verify they are giving the right medication.

3. Mobility and Comfort Guide

Document your pet\'s physical limitations clearly:

  • Which joints are affected and which side is worse
  • Whether they need help getting up from lying down
  • Which surfaces cause slipping (tiles, polished floorboards)
  • Whether they can manage stairs and how many
  • Whether they need a ramp for the couch or bed
  • Walking pace and duration — most senior dogs need shorter, more frequent walks rather than one long one
  • Whether they need a sling or support harness for walks

In New Zealand\'s varied climate, consider the impact of weather on mobility:

  • Cold, damp winter mornings (common in the South Island and Wellington) make arthritis stiffer. Morning walks may need to be shorter and later in the day.
  • Hot summer days (especially in Central Otago and Hawke\'s Bay) require early morning or evening walks to avoid heat stress, which senior pets are more susceptible to.
  • Rain and mud can make outdoor surfaces slippery. A sitter should know to towel off paws and keep walking routes on flat, grippy surfaces.

4. Feeding Instructions

Senior pet feeding is more complex than filling a bowl:

  • Exact food brand, formula, and whether it is a senior-specific diet
  • Portion size per meal (weigh it out in advance and leave labelled bags)
  • Whether food needs to be warmed (some senior pets with diminished smell appetite respond better to warm food)
  • Any toppers or supplements added (joint powder, probiotics, fish oil)
  • Water bowl locations — senior pets, especially those with kidney disease, need easy access to fresh water in multiple locations
  • What to do if the pet refuses food (when to worry, when it is normal for them to skip a meal)
Normal vs. Concerning for Senior Appetite:
Normal: Eating slightly less than usual, being slow to start, preferring food warmed
Concerning: Skipping more than one meal, refusing favourite treats, drooling excessively, vomiting after eating
Emergency: Not eating for 24+ hours (cats), not drinking for 12+ hours, visible pain when eating

Recognising Cognitive Decline in Senior Pets

Cognitive decline is one of the most under-recognised issues in senior pets. Many owners attribute changes to "just getting old" when the underlying cause is neurological. If your pet shows any of these signs, tell your sitter so they can respond with patience rather than frustration:

In Dogs (Canine Cognitive Dysfunction)

  • Disorientation — getting stuck behind furniture, staring at walls, appearing lost in familiar spaces
  • Changes in social interaction — becoming clingy with some people and withdrawn from others
  • Sleep-wake cycle disruption — pacing at night, sleeping during the day, night-time vocalisation
  • House soiling — forgetting housetraining, having accidents in front of the door
  • Anxiety or restlessness — panting, pacing, unable to settle

In Cats (Feline Cognitive Decline)

  • Vocalisation — excessive meowing, especially at night
  • Changes in grooming — matted fur, over-grooming, or stopping grooming entirely
  • Spatial confusion — getting trapped in rooms, failing to find the litter box
  • Changes in appetite — increased or decreased eating
  • Personality shifts — a previously affectionate cat becoming withdrawn, or a reserved cat becoming demanding
For sitters: If a senior pet seems confused or anxious, do not discipline them. Speak calmly, move slowly, and guide them gently to where they need to be. Confine them to a smaller, safe area at night to reduce wandering and anxiety.

The Senior Pet Emergency Plan

Senior pets deteriorate faster than young animals when something goes wrong. Your sitter needs an emergency plan that goes beyond the standard "call the vet" instruction:

  • What constitutes an emergency for THIS pet: For a diabetic pet, a skipped meal is an emergency. For a pet with heart disease, sudden panting and coughing is an emergency. For a pet with kidney disease, not urinating for 12 hours is an emergency.
  • Vet authorisation: Leave a signed letter authorising the sitter to seek emergency treatment and agree to pay up to a specified amount. Many NZ vet clinics will not treat an animal without the owner\'s consent unless it is a matter of immediate life or death.
  • Transport plan: Which car? Where are the keys? Does the pet need a specific carrier or sling? Can they jump into the boot, or do they need a ramp?
  • After-hours vet: Not all NZ towns have 24-hour clinics. Know the closest one and leave the address and phone number. In Auckland, the Animal Emergency Centre is open 24/7. In Wellington, the After Hours Veterinary Clinic in Ngauranga provides emergency care. In Christchurch, the Veterinary Emergency Clinic is available after hours.
  • When to call you vs. when to go straight to the vet: Define this clearly. "Call me if the cat vomits once. Go straight to the vet if the cat cannot urinate for 8 hours."

Home Modifications for Senior Pets

Before the sitter arrives, ensure your home is set up for your senior pet\'s comfort:

  • Non-slip mats on slippery floors in high-traffic areas (kitchen, hallway, near doors)
  • Ramps or pet steps to access beds, couches, or vehicles
  • Orthopaedic bedding in a warm, draught-free location — older joints ache on cold, hard surfaces
  • Elevated food and water bowls for dogs with neck or spinal issues
  • Night lights in hallways and near the pet\'s sleeping area — older eyes do not see as well in the dark
  • Pee pads or washable mats near doors for pets with incontinence
  • Litter box with low sides for older cats who cannot climb over high walls
  • Multiple water stations around the house — kidney-disease cats and senior dogs with mobility issues should never have to walk far for water

Choosing the Right Sitter for a Senior Pet

Not every pet sitter is equipped to handle senior animals. When searching on ZiggySitters, look for sitters who:

  • Have experience with senior or special needs pets
  • Are comfortable administering medication
  • Understand that older pets need patience, not discipline
  • Can recognise the early signs of common senior emergencies
  • Are willing to follow a detailed care plan without improvising
Meet and greet is non-negotiable for senior pets. Before booking, introduce the sitter to your pet in your home. Watch how they interact. Does the sitter move calmly? Do they get down to the pet\'s level? Does your pet seem comfortable with them? For anxious senior pets, two short visits before the actual sit are better than one.

NZ-Specific Considerations for Senior Pet Sitting

  • Earthquake preparedness: If you live in a seismically active area (Wellington, Christchurch, Napier), ensure the sitter knows where your pet\'s carrier, lead, and emergency kit are stored. Senior pets are more vulnerable during natural disasters.
  • Heat and cold extremes: Central Otago can drop below -5°C in winter, while Auckland can reach 30°C in summer. Senior pets cannot regulate temperature as effectively. The sitter should know your heating and cooling routines.
  • Ticks and fleas: New Zealand has tick species that can cause paralysis in dogs. Ensure parasite prevention is current and the sitter knows how to check for ticks after walks in bush areas.
  • Rural vet access: If you live in a rural area, the nearest vet may be 30-60 minutes away. Make sure the sitter has clear directions and knows the fastest route.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I board my senior pet at a vet clinic instead of using a pet sitter?

It depends on your pet\'s health. If your pet requires medical monitoring (insulin injections, IV fluids, seizure watch), a veterinary boarding facility is safer. For stable senior pets who are comfortable at home, in-home pet sitting is less stressful. Kennels and catteries expose older pets to noise, unfamiliar routines, and disease risks that home sitting avoids.

How long can I leave a senior pet with a sitter?

Most senior pets do best with sitters for trips of up to two weeks. Longer trips may cause stress-related health decline, particularly in cats. For extended absences, consider rotating two sitters or having a trusted friend visit periodically to maintain routine.

My senior pet has accidents. Should I tell the sitter upfront?

Absolutely. Full disclosure prevents frustration and ensures the sitter responds with appropriate care. Provide puppy pads, washable mats, and cleaning supplies. Assure the sitter that incontinence is a medical issue, not a behavioural one, and that you will not blame them for accidents.

Can a pet sitter give injections?

Some experienced sitters can administer subcutaneous injections (commonly needed for diabetic cats). Verify this during your search and demonstrate the technique at the meet-and-greet. If no qualified sitter is available, arrange for a veterinary nurse to visit for injections, or board at a vet clinic.
