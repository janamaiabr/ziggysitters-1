Dog Harness Fitting Guide NZ: Choosing, Sizing & Fitting the Right Harness

Published 18 July 2026 · Pet Equipment & Safety

If you have ever watched a dog strain against a collar with eyes bulging, gagging on the lead, or slipping out of a harness mid-walk and bolting toward a flock of sheep on a NZ hillside, you know that the wrong harness is more than an inconvenience. It is a safety issue. The right harness, fitted correctly, transforms walks from a battle into a partnership. This guide covers how to measure your dog, which harness type suits your situation, how to fit it properly, and the most common mistakes that make harnesses uncomfortable or dangerous.

Why a Harness Beats a Collar for Walking

Collars are fine for carrying ID tags and looking good. They are not fine for walking a dog that pulls, lunges, or gets overstimulated. Here is why:

  • Neck pressure: A pulling dog on a collar puts pressure on the trachea, oesophagus, and blood vessels. This causes coughing, gagging, and over time can lead to tracheal collapse, especially in small breeds.
  • Intraocular pressure: Studies show that pressure on a dog\'s neck increases pressure inside the eye. For breeds prone to glaucoma, this is a genuine risk.
  • Thyroid damage: The thyroid gland sits right where a collar presses. Chronic pulling can contribute to hypothyroidism.
  • Escape risk: Many dogs back out of collars when startled. A properly fitted harness with a martingale or double-clip attachment is far more secure.
NZ law reminder: Most NZ councils require dogs in public to wear a collar with their registration disc. Use both: a harness for walking and a collar for ID. Do not attach the lead to the collar.

That said, not all harnesses are equal. A poorly fitted harness can cause just as many problems as a collar: chafing under the armpits, restricting shoulder movement, or pressing on the throat. The key is choosing the right type and fitting it properly.

Harness Types Explained

Back-Clip Harness (Standard)

The leash ring sits on the dog\'s back, between the shoulder blades. This is the most common style and works well for dogs that do not pull much. It is comfortable, easy to put on, and does not interfere with the dog\'s natural gait. The downside: it does nothing to discourage pulling. Some dogs actually pull harder in a back-clip harness because the pressure is distributed across the chest, making it feel less uncomfortable than a collar.

Best for: Calm walkers, small dogs, seniors, and dogs recovering from injury.

Front-Clip Harness (No-Pull)

The leash ring sits on the dog\'s chest. When the dog pulls, the harness turns them back toward you, making pulling self-correcting. This is the single most effective tool for reducing pulling without punishment. It takes most dogs a few walks to adjust to the feeling, and some find it initially confusing, but with consistent use and positive reinforcement, it dramatically reduces leash pulling.

Best for: Pullers, reactive dogs, large breeds, and anyone who wants more control.

Dual-Clip Harness

Has both front and back attachment points. Use the front clip for training walks and the back clip for relaxed walks or running. Some dual-clip harnesses come with a double-ended lead that attaches to both clips simultaneously, giving you steering control from the front and stability from the back.

Best for: Dogs in training, reactive dogs, and owners who want versatility.

Step-In Harness

The dog steps into the harness with their front legs, and it buckles on the back. Easy to put on for cooperative dogs, but can be tricky with wriggly or anxious dogs. Step-in harnesses tend to sit further back on the body, which can restrict shoulder movement if not sized carefully.

Best for: Small dogs, calm dogs, and dogs that dislike having things slipped over their head.

Escape-Proof Harness

Features a martingale loop or belly band that tightens if the dog tries to back out. Essential for narrow-bodied breeds like Greyhounds, Whippets, and Italian Greyhounds, but also useful for any Houdini-dog that has slipped a harness before. The tightening action should be gentle and never choke.

Best for: Escape artists, sighthounds, anxious dogs that back out of gear.

Car Harness (Travel)

Designed to attach to a seatbelt anchor point. These are built from heavier webbing and tested for crash safety. A car harness is not the same as a walking harness. If you want both, look for a dual-rated option, but most walking harnesses are not rated for crash safety.

Best for: Car travel. Use alongside, not instead of, a walking harness.

How to Measure Your Dog

You need three measurements. Use a soft, flexible tape measure. Measure with your dog standing, not sitting or lying down.

MeasurementHow to MeasureWhat It Determines
Girth (chest)Wrap the tape around the widest part of the chest, just behind the front legs. This is the most critical measurement.Main size classification
Neck circumferenceMeasure where a collar would sit naturally, at the base of the neck, not high on the throat.Neck strap fit
Back lengthFrom the base of the neck (where shoulders meet) to the base of the tail.Whether the harness sits in the right position
Pro tip: If your dog is between sizes, go up, not down. A slightly loose harness is adjustable; a too-tight one causes chafing and discomfort. For puppies, buy a harness that fits now, not one they will grow into. An oversized harness is a slipping hazard. Re-measure puppies every 4-6 weeks.

Breed-specific measuring tips:

  • Deep-chested breeds (Greyhounds, Whippets, Dobermans): The girth measurement is disproportionately large compared to the neck. Look for sighthound-specific harnesses.
  • Brachycephalic breeds (French Bulldogs, Pugs, British Bulldogs): The neck measurement is wide relative to the body. A harness that fits the neck may be too loose on the body. Look for adjustable harnesses with multiple straps.
  • Barrel-chested breeds (Staffies, Pit Bulls, Rottweilers): The opposite problem. The girth is large but the neck is thick. Ensure the neck strap does not press on the throat.
  • Long-backed breeds (Dachshunds, Corgis, Basset Hounds): Avoid harnesses that restrict the back or put pressure on the spine. Look for Y-shaped front pieces that sit between the front legs.

Fitting Your Harness Correctly

The Two-Finger Rule

Once the harness is on and all straps are adjusted, you should be able to slide two fingers (flat, side by side) between the strap and your dog\'s body at any point. This is the gold standard for harness fit.

  • Cannot fit two fingers: Too tight. The harness will chafe, restrict breathing, and make your dog hate wearing it.
  • Can fit three or more fingers: Too loose. The harness will shift, rub, and could be slipped out of.

Checkpoints After Fitting

  1. Chest strap: Sits on the breastbone (sternum), not on the soft tissue of the throat. The Y-shaped front piece should sit between the front legs, not across the shoulder joint.
  2. Armpit clearance: The straps that run behind the front legs should not cut into the armpits. There should be visible clearance. Red fur or bare patches under the armpits mean the harness is too tight or the wrong shape.
  3. Back strap: Should sit flat across the back without twisting. It should not slide from side to side when the dog walks.
  4. Neck opening: Should not press on the throat. You should be able to slide a hand between the harness and the front of the throat.
  5. Range of motion: Watch your dog walk, trot, and sit. The harness should not restrict shoulder extension or prevent the dog from sitting comfortably.

The Walk Test

After fitting, take your dog for a 5-minute walk. Watch for these signs:

  • Pawing at the harness: Probably uncomfortable. Check for rubbing or too-tight straps.
  • Refusing to walk: May be confused by the front-clip sensation, or the harness may be restricting movement.
  • Changed gait: Shorter strides or a stiff walk means the harness is interfering with shoulder movement.
  • Chafing after a walk: Check for red marks under the armpits, on the chest, or along the back.
Good fit indicators: The harness stays in place without shifting, your dog moves naturally, you can fit two fingers under every strap, and there are no red marks after a 20-minute walk.

Common Fitting Mistakes

1. The Harness Is Too Loose

This is the most common mistake. Owners buy a harness, put it on, and never adjust it. A loose harness shifts during walks, causing chafing. It also lets dogs back out of it. If your dog can slip a leg out while walking, or if the harness rotates around the body, it is too loose. Tighten every strap and re-check the two-finger rule.

2. The Front Piece Presses on the Throat

Some harnesses have a horizontal strap across the front of the neck. This is fine for calm dogs that do not pull. For dogs that pull, this strap presses directly on the trachea when there is tension on the lead. Choose a Y-shaped or V-shaped front piece that sits on the breastbone instead.

3. Straps Under the Armpits

The straps that run behind the front legs should sit in the natural gap behind the armpit, not up inside it. Armpit chafing is painful, takes days to heal, and makes dogs resist putting the harness on. If you see thin straps sitting right in the crease of the armpit, either adjust the harness or choose one with wider, padded straps.

4. Using the Wrong Harness Type for the Situation

A back-clip harness on a strong puller gives the dog more leverage, not less. A front-clip harness on a dog that lunges sideways can twist them awkwardly. A step-in harness on a narrow-chested dog that backs out of gear is an escape waiting to happen. Match the harness type to your dog\'s walking style and behaviour.

5. Never Re-checking the Fit

Dogs change shape. Puppies grow. Adult dogs gain or lose weight. Coat thickness changes with the seasons. A harness that fit perfectly in summer may be too tight over a thick winter coat. Check the fit monthly and after any weight change.

What to Tell Your Pet Sitter About Your Dog\'s Harness

If you are leaving your dog with a sitter, give them clear instructions about the harness. This is not obvious to someone who has never used it before.

  1. Which harness to use: If you have multiple harnesses (car, walking, training), tell the sitter which one to use for walks. Put the walking harness where the sitter can find it, and write the dog\'s name on it with a marker.
  2. How to put it on: Some harnesses are confusing. Demonstrate the step-in, over-the-head, or slip-on method. Take a photo and leave it on the fridge.
  3. Where to clip the lead: Front clip, back clip, or both. This matters because attaching to the wrong clip on a no-pull harness defeats the purpose and can be unsafe.
  4. Fit check: Show the sitter the two-finger rule and where each strap should sit. Harnesses shift and need re-checking.
  5. Known issues: If your dog backs out of harnesses, chews straps, or has a sore spot from chafing, tell the sitter. This is safety-critical information.
  6. Emergency collar: Even if your dog wears a harness for walks, make sure the sitter has a collar with ID and registration tags as required by NZ law.
For sitters: When you arrive for a meet-and-greet, ask the owner to demonstrate how the harness goes on and off, where the lead attaches, and how to check the fit. If anything feels loose or the dog seems uncomfortable, adjust before the walk. Never force a harness onto a dog that is resisting. Call the owner first.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I leave a harness on my dog all day?

No. Harnesses are for walking and supervised activities. Remove the harness when your dog is resting at home. Long-term wear causes chafing, matting, and skin irritation. It also gives dogs something to chew on, which can destroy the harness and create a choking hazard.

My dog hates the harness. What do I do?

Desensitise gradually. Put the harness on the floor and let your dog sniff it. Give treats for approaching it. Hold it near the dog and treat. Put it on for 30 seconds, then treat and remove. Gradually increase the time. Most dogs accept a harness within a week of positive associations if you go at their pace.

Are no-pull harnesses cruel?

No. A properly fitted front-clip harness is not punitive. It works by redirecting the dog\'s momentum, not by causing pain. The dog learns that pulling does not get them where they want to go, and walking beside you does. It is a training tool, not a punishment. Avoid any harness that tightens around the neck or ribs as a correction.

Do I still need a collar if my dog wears a harness?

Yes. NZ council regulations require dogs in public to wear a collar with registration tags. Use the collar for ID and the harness for walking. Do not attach the lead to the collar.

How do I know when to replace a harness?

Replace a harness if you see frayed stitching, cracked plastic buckles, rusted metal hardware, stretched straps that no longer adjust tight enough, or if your dog has outgrown it. A failed buckle or strap during a walk near traffic is a scenario you want to avoid.

The right harness, properly fitted, makes walks safer and more enjoyable for both you and your dog. Take the time to measure correctly, choose the right type, and check the fit regularly. Your dog\'s comfort and safety depend on it.
