Cat Grass and Safe Indoor Plants for NZ Pets: Complete Guide 2026

20 April 2026 11 min read By ZiggySitters Team

Indoor cats and dogs in New Zealand often lack access to the fresh greenery their wild counterparts seek out instinctively. Cat grass — specifically cultivated grass that's safe for pets — provides valuable nutritional and behavioural benefits. This guide covers everything NZ pet owners need to know about cat grass, safe indoor plants, and which common houseplants to avoid if you have curious pets.

Why Cats and Dogs Seek Out Grass

You might have noticed your cat chomping on houseplants or your dog eating grass during walks. This behaviour isn't random — it's instinctive. In the wild, cats and dogs would consume grass as part of their diet, gaining fibre for digestion, triggering vomiting to clear hairballs or stomach irritants, and obtaining micronutrients like folic acid from fresh plant material.

For indoor pets, this natural instinct goes unsatisfied unless we provide safe alternatives. Cats without access to grass often turn to toxic houseplants out of necessity, which explains why so many emergency vet visits involve plant ingestion. Providing cat grass satisfies the instinct safely and prevents destructive behaviour toward your other houseplants.

Dogs eating grass occasionally is normal — most veterinarians consider it a digestive aid or behavioural quirk rather than a problem unless it becomes excessive or is accompanied by other symptoms. Dog-safe grass is less critical than for cats but still provides fibre benefits.

What Is Cat Grass?

Cat grass isn't one specific plant — it's a term for cultivated grass blends that are safe for feline consumption. The most common cat grass species are:

  • Wheatgrass (Triticum aestivum) — most popular and widely available, fast-growing, rich in vitamins A, C, and E
  • Oat grass (Avena sativa) — gentle on stomachs, high in fibre, good for cats with sensitive digestion
  • Barley grass (Hordeum vulgare) — packed with nutrients including beta-carotene, folic acid, and antioxidants
  • Ryegrass (Lolium perenne) — durable, fast-growing, but less commonly used in commercial cat grass mixes

These are not the same as lawn grass — garden lawns may have been treated with pesticides, fertilizers, or contain parasites from bird droppings. Cat grass is cultivated in sterile conditions specifically for pet consumption.

Benefits of Cat Grass for NZ Pets

Digestive Health

Cat grass provides insoluble fibre that aids digestion and helps cats vomit when they have hairballs or stomach discomfort. The grass acts as a natural laxative for constipation, and the act of chewing stimulates saliva production and swallowing, which can reduce acid reflux. For cats with chronic digestive issues, regular access to cat grass often reduces symptoms noticeably.

Nutritional补充

Fresh grass contains vitamins A, C, E, and K, plus folic acid — an essential B vitamin that supports healthy blood cell development. Cats on meat-only diets (which raw or commercial diets often are) may lack folic acid from plant sources. The chlorophyll in fresh grass also provides mild detoxifying properties.

Mental Stimulation and Enrichment

For indoor cats, cat grass provides environmental enrichment that satisfies natural hunting and foraging instincts. Watching cats bat at and chew grass provides entertainment, and the act of consuming plant material engages their senses more fully than kibble from a bowl. Cats with access to cat grass often show reduced stress behaviours and less interest in chewing other houseplants.

Safe Alternative to Toxic Plants

Without cat grass, curious cats will investigate other plants — many of which are toxic. Providing cat grass gives them their own designated green to focus on, dramatically reducing incidents of destructive or dangerous chewing behaviour on other plants.

Growing Cat Grass at Home: Step-by-Step

Growing cat grass at home takes about 7-10 days from seed to harvest and requires minimal equipment:

What You Need

  • Wheatgrass, oat grass, or barley grass seeds — organic untreated seeds preferred
  • Shallow tray or pot with drainage holes — a seedling tray, small pot, or any container 5-7cm deep works
  • Potting mix — standard organic potting soil
  • Spray bottle for watering

Growing Steps

  1. Soak seeds — place seeds in a bowl of water and soak for 8-12 hours before planting. Use roughly 2-3 tablespoons of seeds per standard-sized tray.
  2. Prepare tray — fill your tray with 3-4cm of potting mix, firm it gently, and water until evenly moist but not waterlogged.
  3. Plant seeds — spread soaked seeds in a single layer across the soil surface. Press them gently into the soil but don't bury them deeply — seeds should be just barely covered.
  4. Cover and dark — cover the tray with another tray or plastic wrap to create darkness, which encourages germination. Leave for 2-3 days.
  5. Uncover and light — once sprouts appear (2-5mm tall), remove cover and place in a sunny spot. Cat grass grows best with 4-6 hours of direct sunlight or equivalent artificial light.
  6. Water daily — keep soil consistently moist by misting with spray bottle once or twice daily. Don't allow soil to dry out.
  7. Ready in 7-10 days — grass is ready when it reaches 10-15cm tall, typically 7-10 days after planting depending on conditions.

After harvesting, you can plant a new batch immediately. Many NZ cat owners keep two trays on rotation — one growing while one is being consumed — for continuous supply.

Safe Indoor Plants for Cats and Dogs in NZ

Beyond cat grass, many common houseplants are actually safe for pets. Here\'s a curated list of pet-friendly plants readily available in NZ:

Cat and Dog Safe Plants

  • Spider Plant (Chlorophytum comosum) — virtually indestructible, thrives in NZ indoor conditions, completely safe for cats and dogs. Cats often enjoy playing with the long leaves and plantlets.
  • Boston Fern (Nephrolepis exaltata) — lush, humidity-loving fern that\'s safe for all pets. Requires regular misting in NZ\'s often-dry indoor environments.
  • African Violet (Saintpaulia) — small flowering plants perfect for low-light areas, completely non-toxic to cats and dogs.
  • Bamboo Palm (Chamaedorea seifrizii) — elegant, air-purifying palm safe for pets. Tolerates low light and irregular watering.
  • Areca Palm (Dypsis lutescens) — another pet-safe palm that adds tropical vibes to NZ homes without toxicity concerns.
  • Calathea varieties — stunning patterned leaves, pet-safe, though they require more humidity and indirect light.
  • Peperomia — compact, diverse foliage, completely safe for cats and dogs. Perfect for small spaces.
  • Prayer Plant (Maranta leuconeura) — safe and fascinating as leaves move in response to light throughout the day.
  • Cast Iron Plant (Aspidistra elatior) — virtually indestructible, tolerates neglect and low light, completely pet-safe.
  • Staghorn Fern (Platycerium) — mounted or potted, fascinating shape, safe for all pets.
  • Ponytail Palm (Beaucarnea recurvata) — not actually a palm but a succulent-like plant, safe for pets, requires minimal watering.
  • Haworthia (Haworthia species) — small, spiky succulents that are pet-safe and NZ-hardy. Avoid Aloes though — those are toxic.

Herbs Safe for NZ Pets

Many culinary herbs are also pet-safe and can be grown on kitchen windowsills:

  • Catnip (Nepeta cataria) — cats love it (obviously), and it\'s completely safe. Dogs don\'t experience the same effect but it\'s harmless to them too.
  • Catmint (Nepeta mussinii) — similar to catnip but more ornamental. Safe for both cats and dogs.
  • Parsley — safe for cats and dogs in moderation. Often used fresh as breath freshener for dogs.
  • Thyme — completely safe, sometimes used in small amounts as digestive aid for dogs.
  • Rosemary — safe for pets, strong scent may deter them from chewing anyway.
  • Basil — pet-safe herb that\'s easy to grow indoors year-round in NZ.

Plant Labels Can Be Misleading

Many plants marketed as "pet-friendly" in NZ nurseries refer to non-toxicity to pets, not palatability. Cats may still chew plants that aren\'t toxic but cause digestive upset. Cat grass remains the safest plant for cats who engage in plant-chewing behaviour.

Plants Toxic to Cats and Dogs: NZ Danger List

Many common NZ houseplants are toxic to pets. Being aware of these protects your animals:

Highly Toxic — Avoid Completely

  • Peace Lily (Spathiphyllum) — contains calcium oxalate crystals causing intense oral irritation, drooling, difficulty swallowing
  • Dieffenbachia (Dumb cane) — calcium oxalate crystals, causes painful swelling of mouth and tongue
  • Philodendron species — calcium oxalate crystals; ingestion causes oral pain and swelling
  • Pothos (Epipremnum aureum) — contains insoluble calcium oxalates, causes oral irritation and vomiting
  • Snake Plant (Sansevieria/Dracaena trifasciata) — causes nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea in cats and dogs
  • Aloe Vera — toxic to cats and dogs; causes vomiting, diarrhoea, lethargy, tremors
  • English Ivy (Hedera helix) — causes drooling, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhoea
  • Oleander (Nerium oleander) — extremely toxic, affects heart rhythm, can be fatal even in small amounts
  • Lily species (Lilium and Hemerocallis) — KNOWN KILLER for cats; even pollen ingestion causes acute kidney failure. Remove lilies entirely from cat households.
  • Poinsettia (Euphorbia pulcherrima) — mild toxicity causes drooling and vomiting but generally not serious

What to Do If Your Pet Eats a Toxic Plant

If you catch your pet eating a potentially toxic plant:

  1. Remove plant material from mouth if possible without getting bitten or scratched
  2. Identify the plant — take a photo or save a sample for identification
  3. Call your vet — even if symptoms aren\'t obvious yet, call ahead for advice
  4. NZ 24/7 Vet Emergency: Several NZ clinics have after-hours services. Keep your nearest emergency vet number saved.
  5. Symptoms requiring immediate vet attention: Difficulty breathing, collapse, seizures, severe vomiting, blood in stool, unresponsive behaviour

For cats specifically, if lilies were in the house, treat it as an emergency regardless of whether you saw the cat eat any part of the plant. Even grooming pollen off fur can cause lethal kidney damage.

Creating a Pet-Safe Indoor Plant Zone

For NZ pet owners who love plants, strategic placement creates harmony:

  • Elevate safe plants — hanging planters and high shelves keep safe plants accessible while toxic ones go in rooms pets don\'t access
  • Deters for toxic plants: Citrus peels, vinegar solutions, or commercial bitter sprays deter curious pets from chewing
  • Use physical barriers — decorative cages or terrariums protect curious pets from accessing soil and plants
  • Know your pet\'s jumping ability — cats can reach surprisingly high places; if in doubt, keep plants in pet-free zones

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does cat grass last?

Cat grass typically remains viable for 2-4 weeks once fully grown, depending on light conditions and how often pets consume it. Commercial pots from NZ pet shops often last 2-3 weeks before grass yellows. DIY-grown cat grass lasts slightly longer with proper care. When grass turns yellow or brown, it\'s lost nutritional value and should be replaced with a fresh crop.

Is wheatgrass the same as cat grass?

Wheatgrass is the most common type of cat grass and what most NZ suppliers sell as "cat grass." Other safe varieties like oat grass and barley grass are sometimes marketed specifically as cat grass or as alternatives. All provide similar benefits. Wheatgrass is popular because it\'s fast-growing (7-10 days from seed) and cats seem to prefer its sweet taste over other grass types.

Can dogs eat cat grass?

Yes, dog-safe grass exists and dogs can safely consume cat grass without issues. Unlike cats who actively seek out plant material, dogs more casually nibble grass. Cat grass is safe for dogs, though some dogs experience mild vomiting after eating grass (which is a normal digestive adjustment, not toxicity). If your dog eats large quantities of cat grass, monitor for signs of digestive upset.

Why does my cat eat plants but not cat grass?

Some cats take time to discover cat grass, especially if it\'s in an unfamiliar location or presented differently than other plants. Place cat grass near your cat\'s favourite resting spots. Some cats prefer oat grass over wheatgrass. If your cat ignores fresh cat grass, try spritzing it with a tiny bit of tuna water or moving it to different locations. Most cats discover and use cat grass regularly once they realise it\'s available.

Should I fertilise my cat grass?

If growing at home, use organic fertiliser at half-strength or choose slow-release granules. After fertilising, wait 2-3 waterings before letting your pet consume the grass to avoid chemical ingestion. Commercial cat grass from NZ pet shops is typically pre-fertilised appropriately. Signs of over-fertilisation include brown tips on grass blades — if this occurs, flush soil with water and hold off fertilising until next crop.
