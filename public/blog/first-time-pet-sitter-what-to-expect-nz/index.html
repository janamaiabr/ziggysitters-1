First-Time Pet Sitter: What to Expect When Booking in NZ

Published 25 June 2026 · For Pet Owners

Leaving your pet with someone new feels like leaving your child with a stranger. It is normal to feel nervous. But pet sitting in New Zealand is straightforward when you know the process, what to ask, and how to prepare. This guide walks you through every step, from your first search to a successful sit, so you can go away with confidence that your pet is in good hands.

How Pet Sitting Works: The Full Process

Understanding the process removes most of the anxiety. Here is what happens, step by step:

1 Browse sitters in your area

On ZiggySitters, search by location, dates, and pet type. Each profile shows experience, reviews, verification status, and rates. You can filter for sitters experienced with dogs, cats, birds, rabbits, or other animals.

2 Send a message or booking request

Reach out to sitters who look like a good fit. Introduce yourself, your pet, and your dates. Ask any initial questions. This is a conversation, not a commitment.

3 Arrange a meet-and-greet

Most sitters offer a free or low-cost meet-and-greet before the booking. This is where your pet meets the sitter in your home. It is the most important step.

4 Confirm the booking

Once you and your pet are comfortable, confirm the booking through the platform. Payment is held securely and released to the sitter after the sit begins. You are protected if the sitter cancels.

5 Prepare your home and leave a care sheet

Before you leave, prepare your home, stock up on supplies, and write a detailed care sheet covering routine, food, medication, contacts, and house rules.

6 Stay connected during the sit

Most sitters send daily photo and text updates. You can also request video calls. ZiggySitters messaging keeps all communication in one place for your records.

How to Choose the Right Sitter

Not every sitter is right for every pet. Here is what to look for:

Experience with your type of pet

A sitter who has only walked Labradors may not be the best match for your anxious rescue cat. Look for sitters who explicitly mention experience with your pet\'s species, breed, and temperament. On ZiggySitters, profiles show which animals the sitter has experience with.

Reviews from other pet owners

Reviews are the most reliable signal. Look for reviews that mention pets similar to yours. A five-star review from a relaxed golden retriever owner is less relevant if you have a reactive terrier. Read for specifics, not just star ratings.

Verification and background checks

ZiggySitters verifies identity through government-issued ID. Look for the verified badge on profiles. Some sitters also hold pet first-aid certificates, which is valuable for senior pets or pets with medical conditions.

Communication style

Pay attention to how a sitter communicates before you book. Do they respond promptly? Do they ask good questions about your pet? Do they seem engaged and interested? A sitter who asks detailed questions about your pet\'s routine is usually a sitter who cares about doing the job well.

Questions to Ask Before You Book

Do not be shy about asking questions. A good sitter welcomes them. Here are the questions that matter most:

Experience and qualifications

  • How long have you been pet sitting?
  • Have you cared for pets like mine before? (Breed, age, temperament, medical needs)
  • Do you have a pet first-aid certificate?
  • Have you dealt with emergencies before? What happened?

Logistics

  • What is your daily routine when house-sitting?
  • How many pets do you sit for at the same time?
  • Will you be away from the house for extended periods?
  • Do you have your own transport in case of an emergency?
  • How will you send me updates? Photos, videos, texts?

Emergency handling

  • What would you do if my pet got sick or injured?
  • Which vet would you take them to?
  • Do you have a car to get to the vet quickly?
  • Would you contact me before taking my pet to the vet, or go immediately?

References and reviews

  • Can I read reviews from other pet owners?
  • Do you have repeat clients?
Pro tip: A sitter who asks you lots of questions is usually a good sign. It means they are invested in understanding your pet\'s needs, not just collecting a booking fee.

The Meet-and-Greet: The Most Important Step

The meet-and-greet is a short visit, usually 20 to 30 minutes, where the sitter comes to your home to meet your pet before the booking. Think of it as an interview for both sides.

What to do

  1. Introduce slowly: Let your pet approach the sitter at their own pace. Do not force interaction. Some pets take 10 minutes; some take the entire visit.
  2. Show the sitter around: Walk them through the feeding area, where the food is stored, where the litter box is, where the lead and harness hang, and which rooms your pet can access.
  3. Demonstrate the routine: Show how you feed, walk, and settle your pet. If there are specific commands or rituals, teach them now.
  4. Discuss medical needs: If your pet takes medication, demonstrate how you administer it. Show where the first-aid kit is.
  5. Ask your questions: This is your chance to assess the sitter in person. Watch how they interact with your pet and whether your pet seems comfortable.

What to watch for

  • Does the sitter approach your pet calmly and respectfully?
  • Do they listen to your instructions and ask follow-up questions?
  • Does your pet seem relaxed around the sitter after the initial few minutes?
  • Is the sitter patient? A good sitter will not rush the introduction.
Red flags at the meet-and-greet: The sitter is rough or impatient with your pet. They dismiss your concerns or instructions. They are distracted, checking their phone constantly. They are vague about their schedule or availability. They have no questions about your pet. Trust your instincts — and your pet\'s.

Preparing Your Home and Pet

Preparation is what separates a smooth sit from a stressful one. Do this before you leave:

Write a care sheet

Leave a written care sheet on the kitchen bench or another visible spot. Include:

  • Feeding routine: Times, portions, where food is stored, any dietary restrictions
  • Medication: Name, dose, frequency, how to administer, where it is stored
  • Walk routine: Times, duration, route preferences, lead and harness location
  • Behaviour notes: Fear of thunderstorms, separation anxiety signs, escape attempts, food aggression, reactions to visitors
  • Vet details: Your regular vet and the nearest after-hours emergency vet, including addresses and phone numbers
  • Emergency contacts: Your phone number, a local backup contact (friend, neighbour, or family member), and your travel itinerary
  • House rules: Which rooms are off-limits, furniture rules, alarm instructions, rubbish day, mail collection

Stock up on supplies

Leave more food, treats, waste bags, and litter than you think you need. If your trip runs long or your sitter cannot find the exact brand, having extras prevents stress. Label everything clearly.

Secure your home

  • Lock away anything dangerous: cleaning products, medications, chocolate, small items your pet could swallow
  • Close doors to rooms you do not want your pet in
  • Ensure fences and gates are secure; check for gaps under fences
  • Leave a spare key in an agreed spot or provide clear lock instructions
  • Show the sitter where the water main, electrical box, and first-aid kit are

Prepare your pet

  • If your pet is anxious with new people, leave a worn t-shirt with your scent on their bed
  • Leave their favourite toys and comfort items accessible
  • Make sure vaccinations and flea and tick prevention are up to date
  • If your pet is on medication, pre-sort doses into a pill organiser

During the Sit: What to Expect

Communication

Most professional sitters send daily updates including at least one photo. Some send multiple messages per day. Agree on your preferred communication frequency at the meet-and-greet. If you want more updates, say so upfront. If you prefer minimal contact so you can enjoy your trip, that is fine too.

What if something goes wrong?

Good sitters handle minor issues without panicking you. A vet visit for a minor concern can usually wait until they have informed you. For genuine emergencies, expect an immediate call. Discuss at the meet-and-greet how you want emergencies handled: should the sitter take your pet to the vet immediately, or call you first?

Most sitters say: "If I think it is an emergency, I go straight to the vet and call you from the car. If it is minor, I call you first." This is a reasonable default. Discuss it upfront so there is no confusion.

Common First-Time Concerns Answered

Will my pet be okay with a stranger?

Most pets adjust within the first few hours, especially if the sitter follows their routine. Cats may hide initially but usually come out once they realise the sitter is not a threat. Dogs are often thrilled to have a new person giving them attention. The meet-and-greet makes a huge difference because your pet has already met the sitter.

What if my pet does not eat?

It is common for pets to eat less on the first day of a sit. Leave extra-tasty food (wet food, boiled chicken, or your vet\'s recommended high-value toppers) that the sitter can mix in. Most pets return to normal eating within 24 to 48 hours. If your pet goes more than 48 hours without eating, ask the sitter to contact your vet.

What if the sitter cancels at the last minute?

This is every pet owner\'s fear. On ZiggySitters, sitters who cancel last minute lose their booking fee and receive a negative mark on their profile. To protect yourself, have a backup: a friend, neighbour, or a second sitter you have messaged but not booked. Book early, especially for peak periods like Christmas and school holidays.

Should I leave my pet at home or send them to the sitter?

For most pets, staying in their own home is less stressful. They have their bed, their smells, their routine. But some social dogs thrive in a boarding environment with other dogs and active company. Consider your pet\'s temperament: anxious pets do better at home; outgoing pets may enjoy the adventure of a new place.

Is it normal to feel guilty?

Yes. Every pet owner feels a pang of guilt when they leave. But remember: your pet will likely spend most of the day sleeping, playing, and receiving one-on-one attention from someone who chose this job because they love animals. You are not abandoning them. You are making sure they are cared for while you recharge.

First-Time Booking Checklist

Use this checklist before you travel:

Before the meet-and-greet

  • Search for sitters in your area on ZiggySitters
  • Read reviews from owners with similar pets
  • Message two or three sitters to compare availability and approach
  • Schedule a meet-and-greet with your preferred sitter

At the meet-and-greet

  • Watch how the sitter interacts with your pet
  • Show them the feeding, walking, and sleeping routine
  • Discuss medical needs, behaviour quirks, and emergency plans
  • Ask your questions (experience, logistics, references)
  • Confirm dates, rates, and communication preferences

Before you leave

  • Write and print a care sheet (feeding, medication, contacts, house rules)
  • Stock extra food, treats, waste bags, and litter
  • Leave vet contact details (regular and after-hours emergency)
  • Leave a spare key and show the sitter where it is hidden outside
  • Secure dangerous items (cleaning products, medications, toxic food)
  • Leave a worn item with your scent for anxious pets
  • Confirm your travel itinerary and how to reach you
  • Make sure your pet\'s microchip details and council registration are up to date

During the sit

  • Respond to updates promptly so the sitter feels supported
  • Trust the sitter to handle minor decisions without calling you
  • Save the emergency vet number in your phone for quick reference

After the sit

  • Leave an honest review on ZiggySitters
  • Tip your sitter if they exceeded expectations (not required, but appreciated)
  • Book the same sitter for your next trip to maintain consistency for your pet
