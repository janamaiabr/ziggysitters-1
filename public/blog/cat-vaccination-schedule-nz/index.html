Cat Vaccination Schedule NZ: Complete Guide for Kittens and Adults

What vaccinations your cat needs in New Zealand, when to get them, and how to stay on top of boosters throughout your cat's life.

📅 April 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 🐾 ZiggySitters NZ

Vaccination is one of the most important things you can do to protect your cat's long-term health. Yet many cat owners in New Zealand are unsure exactly which vaccines their cat needs, how often boosters are required, or what the difference is between core and non-core vaccines.

This guide walks through the standard NZ cat vaccination schedule, explains what each vaccine protects against, covers adult booster timing, and outlines what to share with your vet and pet sitter to keep your cat safe.

📋 Disclaimer This guide is for general educational purposes only. Always follow the specific advice of your NZ-registered veterinarian, who can tailor a vaccination plan to your cat's age, lifestyle, and health status.

🐱 Core vs Non-Core Vaccines for Cats in NZ

Cat vaccines in New Zealand are divided into two categories: core vaccines (recommended for all cats regardless of lifestyle) and non-core vaccines (recommended based on risk factors like outdoor access or exposure to other cats).

Core Vaccine

Feline Panleukopenia (FPL)

Also called feline parvovirus. Highly contagious, often fatal. Part of the F3 combination vaccine.

Core Vaccine

Feline Herpesvirus (FHV-1)

Causes cat flu. Upper respiratory infection, eye discharge, fever. Very common in NZ. Part of F3.

Core Vaccine

Feline Calicivirus (FCV)

Another cause of cat flu — mouth ulcers, respiratory signs. Part of F3. Multiple strains exist.

Non-Core Vaccine

Feline Leukaemia Virus (FeLV)

Recommended for cats with outdoor access or contact with unknown cats. Suppresses immune system and causes cancer.

Non-Core Vaccine

Chlamydiosis (Chlamydophila felis)

Causes conjunctivitis and respiratory signs. Recommended for multi-cat households and breeding catteries.

Non-Core Vaccine

Bordetella bronchiseptica

Bacterial respiratory infection. Recommended for cats in high-density environments like shelters.

The F3 vaccine (sometimes called the 'triple') covers Panleukopenia, Herpesvirus, and Calicivirus in a single injection. It is the baseline for all cats in NZ. The F4 adds Chlamydophila felis, and F5 adds FeLV on top of that. Your vet will advise which combination suits your cat.

📅 Kitten Vaccination Schedule NZ

Kittens are born with some immunity from their mother's milk, but this maternal antibody protection wanes between 6 and 16 weeks of age — leaving a window of vulnerability. The kitten vaccine series is timed to bridge this gap.

Age Vaccine Notes
6–8 weeks F3 (first dose) Early start if kitten is at risk or in multi-cat environment. Some vets begin at 8 weeks.
10–12 weeks F3 (second dose) Plus FeLV if outdoor lifestyle planned. Vet health check recommended at this visit.
14–16 weeks F3 (third dose) Third dose ensures coverage past maternal antibody interference. Some protocols stop at 2 doses — confirm with your vet.
⚠ Keep kittens indoors until 2 weeks after final kitten vaccination Protection is not fully established until approximately two weeks after the last kitten dose. Avoid contact with unknown cats and high-risk environments until this window has passed.

📈 Adult Cat Booster Schedule

After the kitten series, your cat will need ongoing boosters. NZ vets generally follow World Small Animal Veterinary Association (WSAVA) guidelines, which differentiate between how often different vaccines need repeating.

Vaccine Booster at 12 months Ongoing frequency
F3 (FPL + FHV + FCV) Yes — one dose at 12 months post-kitten series Every 3 years for FPL in low-risk indoor cats. FHV and FCV may be recommended annually depending on exposure risk.
FeLV Yes — if initiated Annual or every 2–3 years depending on risk assessment and product used.
Chlamydophila felis Annual Annual if cat remains at risk.

Individual vets may recommend slightly different schedules based on the specific products available in NZ, your cat's health history, and risk factors. An annual wellness check is a good opportunity to review your cat's vaccination status even if a booster isn't due that year.

📩 What to Bring to Your Cat's Vaccination Appointment

Vet Appointment Checklist

  • Vaccination certificate or record from previous vet (if transferring)
  • A secure, well-ventilated cat carrier (leave it open at home beforehand to reduce stress)
  • Any concerns about your cat's current health, behaviour, or appetite
  • Information about your cat's lifestyle: indoor only, outdoor access, contact with other cats
  • Notes on any previous adverse reactions to vaccines

🚨 Recognising Vaccine Reactions in Cats

Most cats tolerate vaccinations well with only mild, short-lived side effects. It is normal for your cat to be quieter than usual, slightly off their food, or have mild tenderness at the injection site for 24 to 48 hours after vaccination.

Contact your vet promptly if your cat shows:

  • Facial swelling, hives, or redness
  • Persistent vomiting or diarrhoea
  • Collapse, difficulty breathing, or extreme lethargy
  • A firm lump at the injection site that persists beyond four weeks (rare — possible feline injection-site sarcoma)

Serious reactions are rare but require immediate veterinary attention. Do not wait to see if symptoms resolve on their own if your cat appears distressed.

🏠 Indoor vs Outdoor Cats: Does It Change the Vaccine Plan?

Yes, significantly. An indoor-only cat with no contact with other cats has a different risk profile to a cat that roams freely or lives in a multi-cat household. Core F3 vaccines are recommended for all cats regardless. However:

  • Outdoor cats are typically recommended FeLV vaccination due to contact risk with stray or unknown cats
  • Multi-cat households may benefit from Chlamydophila felis vaccination
  • Indoor-only cats can often follow a triennial (every 3 year) core schedule for FPL after the 12-month booster
  • Cats in boarding or cattery facilities may be required to have up-to-date vaccinations as a condition of entry

📞 Vaccinations and Pet Sitting: What You Need to Know

If you use a pet sitter, cattery, or boarding facility for your cat, vaccination records are often required. A responsible pet sitter will ask about your cat's health and vaccination status during the initial consultation. This protects both your cat and any other animals in the sitter's care.

When booking a pet sitter, have ready:

  • Your cat's vaccination certificate showing dates and vaccines given
  • Your vet's contact details
  • Details of any known health conditions or medications
  • Information on whether your cat is indoor-only or has outdoor access while you're away

🕵 FAQs About Cat Vaccinations in NZ

How much do cat vaccinations cost in NZ?

Costs vary by clinic and location, but as a general guide: kitten vaccination courses typically run $120–$250 for the full series. Annual or triennial boosters are usually $80–$150 including the consultation. Costs are higher in Auckland and Wellington than in smaller centres.

My cat is overdue for a booster. Do we restart the full series?

Not usually. If the gap is less than three years, a single booster is typically sufficient to restore protection for most core vaccines. If it has been longer, your vet may recommend restarting the two-dose primary course. Always confirm with your vet.

Can an indoor cat skip vaccinations?

It's not recommended. Indoor cats can still be exposed to disease via new cats brought into the home, escape incidents, or viral particles carried in on clothing. The core F3 is low risk and provides important baseline protection for all cats.

Does NZ require cats to be vaccinated by law?

There is no nationwide legal requirement to vaccinate cats in NZ (unlike dog registration). However, many catteries, boarding facilities, and pet sitters require proof of current vaccination before accepting a cat into their care.

What is the FIV vaccine and is it available in NZ?

Feline Immunodeficiency Virus (FIV) causes feline AIDS. An FIV vaccine was previously available in NZ but has been discontinued. Desexing and keeping cats indoors remain the primary prevention strategies for FIV in New Zealand.
