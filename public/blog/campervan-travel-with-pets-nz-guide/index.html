Campervan Travel with Pets in NZ: The Complete Guide (2026)

2026-07-07 · 13 min read · ZiggySitters Team

New Zealand was practically designed for campervan holidays — winding coastlines, remote DOC campsites, and a freedom that you just don\'t get from hotel rooms. Bringing your dog or cat along turns a good road trip into an unforgettable one. But travelling by campervan with pets in Aotearoa comes with specific rules, practical challenges, and a fair amount of preparation that most first-timers underestimate. This guide covers everything: choosing the right vehicle, keeping your pet safe on the road, understanding where you can and can\'t stay, and making the whole experience work for both of you.

Is Campervan Travel Right for Your Pet?

Not every pet is a natural road tripper. Dogs that already enjoy car rides and adapt well to new environments tend to thrive in a campervan. Cats are more complicated — they\'re territorial by nature and many find constant movement stressful. That said, some cats take to van life surprisingly well, especially if you create a stable "home zone" inside the vehicle that stays consistent from stop to stop.

Consider your pet\'s age, health, and temperament honestly before committing. Senior dogs with arthritis may struggle with the physical demands of jumping in and out of a van repeatedly. Anxious pets that hate car travel won\'t magically enjoy a campervan just because it\'s bigger. Puppies and kittens under 12 weeks are generally too young for extended travel — they need stable environments for their vaccination schedule and socialisation.

If your pet has existing health conditions requiring regular medication, campervan travel is still possible but requires more planning. You\'ll need to locate vets along your route, carry adequate medication supplies, and maintain any dietary restrictions even when remote. The key is honest assessment — the best trip is one where your pet is as comfortable as you are.

Choosing the Right Campervan for Pet Travel

The vehicle you choose makes an enormous difference to your pet\'s comfort and safety. Most rental companies in New Zealand do not explicitly advertise pet-friendly vehicles, but many allow pets for an additional cleaning deposit. Always confirm the pet policy before booking — some companies refuse pets entirely, while others charge fees ranging from $100 to $300 for deep cleaning.

For dogs, a campervan with a separate sleeping area works best. This gives your dog a defined space that feels like "theirs" rather than sharing your bed every night. Vehicles with a dinette that converts to a bed are ideal — the dinette area can become your dog\'s zone during the day, complete with their bed and toys. For cats, you need a vehicle where you can set up a stable, enclosed litter tray area that doesn\'t slide around on corners. A motorhome with a fixed bathroom area often works well, as you can place the litter tray on the non-slip floor.

Ventilation is critical. New Zealand summers can push campervan interiors above 40 degrees Celsius when parked in direct sun. Look for vehicles with roof hatches, side windows that open, and ideally a ventilation fan. Air conditioning that runs on mains power at holiday parks is a bonus but shouldn\'t be your only cooling strategy since you\'ll often be at DOC sites without power. Slide-out or pop-top roofs provide additional headroom and airflow, making the space far more comfortable for both you and your pet during hot weather.

Storage space matters more than you think. A medium-sized dog needs food, bedding, toys, leads, bowls, waste bags, towels, and possibly medication. Cats need litter, a litter tray, food, scratching surfaces, and a carrier. All of this eats into your living space quickly. Choose a van with adequate under-seat or exterior storage compartments so the pet gear doesn\'t dominate your living area.

Vehicle Setup: Making the Van Pet-Safe

Before you drive anywhere, pet-proof the campervan the same way you would a house. Secure all loose items that could become projectiles during sudden stops — this includes kitchen utensils, toiletries, and anything on counters. Install non-slip mats on floors and steps to prevent your pet from slipping, especially when the van is moving.

For dogs travelling in the cab area, a dog seatbelt harness is non-negotiable. New Zealand law requires that animals be transported safely, and a loose dog in the front of a campervan is dangerous for everyone. Most pet stores sell harnesses that clip into standard seatbelt buckles. For dogs travelling in the rear living area, a travel crate secured to the floor provides the safest option. Some campervan owners install custom barriers that create a dedicated dog zone behind the front seats.

Cats should always travel in a secure carrier anchored to the van structure. A loose cat in a moving vehicle is dangerous for the driver and terrifying for the cat. Line the carrier with absorbent pads in case of accidents, and place a familiar-smelling blanket inside. Many cats settle better when the carrier is covered with a light cloth that still allows airflow.

Set up your pet\'s "station" in the same spot every time you park. Consistency helps anxious animals feel grounded. For dogs, this means their bed, water bowl, and a couple of familiar toys in the same corner. For cats, place the litter tray in the same spot, preferably on a non-slip mat near a door for easy cleaning access. This consistency is the single most important thing you can do to reduce travel stress for your pet.

New Zealand Rules: Where You Can and Can\'t Take Pets

This is where many pet owners get caught out. New Zealand has strict biosecurity and conservation rules that significantly restrict where dogs and cats can go. The Department of Conservation (DOC) manages most of the stunning backcountry campsites that make NZ campervanning special, and the majority of DOC land prohibits dogs entirely. This includes all national parks, most scenic reserves, and many conservation areas.

DOC does permit dogs on some designated tracks and in specific areas, but the rules vary by region and are often seasonal. Kiwi breeding season (August to February) triggers additional restrictions in many North Island forests, where dogs pose a direct threat to ground-nesting native birds. Always check the DOC website for the specific area you plan to visit before assuming your dog can come along. The fines for having a dog in a prohibited DOC area start at $800 and can reach $5,000.

Regional council parks have their own rules. Many Auckland regional parks allow leashed dogs on designated tracks, while Wellington regional parks tend to be more restrictive. Christchurch\'s Port Hills and some Banks Peninsula reserves permit dogs on leash. The key is researching each specific area rather than assuming — rules change frequently and signage is not always clear at trailheads.

Beaches are generally more dog-friendly, but local council bylaws apply. Most NZ beaches allow leashed dogs year-round, with off-lead areas in specific zones. However, during shorebird nesting season (September to March), many popular beaches restrict or prohibit dogs entirely to protect dotterels, variable oystercatchers, and other ground-nesting species. Check local council websites for seasonal restrictions before heading to the coast.

Pet-Friendly Holiday Parks and Campgrounds

New Zealand has a network of holiday parks (previously called holiday parks or TOP 10 parks) that are far more pet-friendly than DOC sites. Most TOP 10 Holiday Parks welcome well-behaved dogs for a small additional fee, typically $5 to $10 per night. These parks offer powered sites, proper facilities, and often dog exercise areas. Some even have dedicated dog wash stations — surprisingly handy after a day at the beach.

The Camping New Zealand website and app show pet-friendly campsites across both islands, though always call ahead to confirm current pet policies. Popular pet-friendly holiday park chains include the TOP 10 group, Kiwi Holiday Parks, and many independently owned parks. During peak summer season (December to February), pet-friendly sites book out early, so reserve well in advance.

Freedom camping with pets is theoretically possible at designated freedom camping sites, but most district councils restrict dogs at these locations, and many freedom sites lack the facilities (bins, water) that make pet waste disposal practical. If you plan to freedom camp, research each specific site\'s pet policy and come prepared with waste disposal bags and a plan for exercising your dog away from other campers.

On the Road: Daily Routine and Safety

Establish a daily routine as quickly as possible. Pets thrive on predictability, and a consistent schedule of meals, walks, and rest times helps them adjust to life on the road. Feed at the same times you would at home. Walk your dog before driving each morning — this burns energy and reduces restlessness in the van. Plan mid-day exercise stops at dog-friendly parks or beaches, and finish the day with a longer walk at your overnight stop.

Never leave your pet alone in a campervan during warm weather. Even with windows cracked, interior temperatures can rise dangerously fast in a NZ summer. If you need to visit attractions where dogs are not allowed, plan your day so one person stays with the pet, or use a local pet sitter through ZiggySitters for a few hours. This is increasingly common in tourist towns like Queenstown, Rotorua, and Taupo, where day-trip activities often exclude pets.

Hydration is critical. Carry at least 5 litres of fresh water specifically for your pet at all times, separate from your drinking supply. Offer water at every stop — dogs particularly lose moisture through panting during warm-weather walks. A collapsible silicone bowl takes up almost no space and makes water breaks easy. For cats, a pet water fountain that runs on USB power helps encourage drinking, as many cats prefer running water and may reduce intake in unfamiliar settings.

Motion sickness affects some pets, particularly young dogs and most cats. If your pet shows drooling, lip-smacking, or vomiting in the vehicle, speak to your vet about anti-nausea medication before the trip. Cerenia (maropitant) is commonly prescribed for dogs and can make an enormous difference. For milder cases, ginger treats, feeding a light meal a few hours before driving, and keeping the van well-ventilated can help. Gradually increasing drive times over a few weeks before a big trip helps most pets adjust.

The Best Pet-Friendly Campervan Routes in NZ

Northland and the Bay of Islands is hard to beat for a pet-friendly campervan trip. The subtropical climate means comfortable travelling year-round, and the region has numerous dog-friendly beaches. Stay at the TOP 10 Holiday Park in Paihia or Kerikeri, both of which welcome dogs and offer easy access to dog-walkable coastal paths. The Waitangi grounds don\'t allow dogs, but the Haruru Falls track does — a lovely 5km return walk through wetland forest.

Coromandel Peninsula offers stunning coastal driving with multiple pet-friendly stops. Whangamatā and Whitianga holiday parks both accept dogs, and many Coromandel beaches allow leashed dogs outside nesting season. Hot Water Beach is technically accessible with dogs outside peak hours, though check seasonal restrictions. The 309 Road provides a scenic alternative route that dogs can enjoy from the van.

Napier and Hawke\'s Bay combine excellent holiday parks with dog-friendly vineyards — yes, several Hawke\'s Bay wineries welcome well-behaved dogs in their outdoor tasting areas. The region\'s wide, flat cycling trails also double as excellent dog-walking paths. Napier\'s Marine Parade and Ahuriri beach area are popular with local dog owners and provide good exercise options.

Central Otago and the South Island lakes require more planning because of the strict DOC restrictions around national parks, but the region offers superb dog-friendly options if you know where to look. Lake Dunstan holiday park near Cromwell welcomes dogs and provides easy water access. The Alexandra to Clyde river trail is a beautiful dog-friendly walk. Many South Island holiday parks in Queenstown, Wanaka, and Tekapo accept dogs, giving you comfortable bases while exploring the region.

Cat-Specific Campervan Considerations

Travelling with a cat in a campervan is less common but entirely doable with the right preparation. The biggest challenge is the litter tray — it needs a stable, dedicated location that doesn\'t slide around. Most motorhomes with a shower area work well, as the tray can sit on the textured floor with a non-slip mat underneath. Choose a low-sided tray for easier access and keep it away from your own living space as much as the van layout allows.

Cats should be harness-trained before any campervan trip. Even if your cat stays inside the van most of the time, you need a harness and lead setup for emergencies, vet visits, or times when the door must stay open for extended periods. Start harness training at home weeks before departure — most cats resist at first but eventually accept it with patient, reward-based training. Never attach a lead to a collar alone; cats can slip collars easily under stress.

Microchipping is essential for any cat travelling in New Zealand. If your cat escapes at a campsite — and this happens more often than you\'d expect — a microchip dramatically increases the chances of reunion. Ensure your contact details on the NZ Companion Animal Register are current before departure. Consider a GPS tracker collar as an additional safety measure; several NZ-compatible options are available from pet stores.

What to Pack: Pet Campervan Essentials Checklist

Beyond the obvious food and bedding, these items make campervan life with pets smoother:

  • Non-spill water bowl with a non-slip base (several brands make purpose-built travel bowls)
  • Collapsible silicone bowls for food and water — pack flat when not in use
  • Absorbent towels — at least three, because one will always be wet or dirty
  • Pet first-aid kit including bandages, antiseptic wipes, tick removal tool, and any regular medications
  • Poop bags — pack more than you think you need, at least 100 for a two-week trip
  • Portable dog wash or at least dog shampoo for beach and mud cleanup
  • Tie-out stake and lightweight lead for campsites — check each park\'s rules before using
  • USB-powered pet water fountain for cats
  • Familiar bedding that smells like home — do not wash it right before departure
  • Vaccination records and microchip details — keep these accessible, not buried in luggage
  • Tick and flea prevention — essential for NZ bush and beach areas, even in winter
  • Carpet cleaner or enzyme spray for inevitable indoor accidents

Health and Veterinary Preparedness

Before any extended campervan trip, schedule a vet check-up. Confirm your pet\'s vaccinations are current, discuss flea and tick prevention suitable for the regions you\'ll visit, and ask about motion sickness medication if needed. Get a printed copy of vaccination records — some holiday parks and boarding facilities require proof, and digital copies aren\'t always accessible in remote areas.

Research vet clinics along your route before departure. Rural areas of New Zealand have limited veterinary services, and after-hours emergency care may require significant travel. The NZ Veterinary Association website has a clinic finder, and most towns of 5,000+ residents have at least one vet practice. For remote South Island routes, note the locations of 24-hour emergency clinics in Queenstown, Dunedin, and Christchurch.

Stock a basic pet first-aid kit that includes gauze, non-stick bandages, adhesive tape, antiseptic solution, digital thermometer, tweezers for grass seed removal, and any medications your pet takes regularly. Add emergency contact numbers for vet clinics along your route. In remote areas without mobile coverage, this kit and your own basic first-aid knowledge may be all you have until you can reach help.

When Campervan Life Isn\'t Working for Your Pet

Sometimes the best decision is recognising that campervan travel isn\'t working for your pet. Signs of chronic stress include loss of appetite lasting more than a day, persistent hiding or refusal to leave the crate, excessive vocalisation that doesn\'t improve after the first few days, and stress-related health issues like diarrhoea or urinary problems. If you see these signs, it\'s kinder to adjust your plans than to push through.

Options include shortening the trip, booking into a pet-friendly holiday park for several nights instead of moving daily, arranging a local pet sitter for a few days while you explore restricted areas, or in extreme cases, cutting the trip short and arranging home care. Having a backup plan before departure removes the pressure of making difficult decisions on the road. Many ZiggySitters offer last-minute bookings, and most tourist towns have at least one sitter available for day-care or overnight care.

Campervanning with your pet can be one of the best experiences you share together. New Zealand\'s landscapes, pet-friendly holiday parks, and the growing network of local pet sitters make it more achievable than ever. The key is preparation, honest assessment of your pet\'s needs, and the flexibility to change plans when necessary. Start with shorter trips to test how your pet adjusts, and build up to longer adventures once you know the routine works for both of you.
