Hamster & Gerbil Care in New Zealand: The Complete Guide (2026)

2026-07-20 · 13 min read · ZiggySitters Team

Hamsters and gerbils are fantastic small pets — compact, entertaining, and surprisingly full of personality. But they are not low-maintenance just because they are small. Proper housing, nutrition, enrichment, and healthcare all matter, and New Zealand has specific considerations around what you can keep, where you can buy supplies, and how to find care when you travel.

This guide covers everything a NZ pet owner needs to know about keeping hamsters and gerbils happy and healthy, from choosing the right species to finding a sitter who actually knows how to handle small animals.

Choosing Your Small Pet: Hamster vs Gerbil

Both make excellent pets, but they suit different lifestyles and personalities. Here is an honest comparison:

Hamsters

  • Solitary: Most species (especially Syrian hamsters) must live alone. Housing two together leads to fighting and serious injury.
  • Nocturnal: Hamsters are most active in the evening and at night. If you want an interactive pet during the day, a hamster will disappoint you.
  • Handling: Can be tamed with patience, but some hamsters never fully enjoy being held. Syrian hamsters tend to be calmer than dwarf species.
  • Lifespan: 2–3 years on average.
  • Best for: People who are home in the evenings, want a single pet, and enjoy watching natural behaviours like burrowing and wheel-running.

Gerbils

  • Social: Gerbils need at least one companion. They live in pairs or small groups and become depressed alone. Always get same-sex pairs from the same litter.
  • Diurnal: Active during the day with rest periods. Much better suited to families who want interaction during waking hours.
  • Handling: Generally more curious and less nippy than hamsters once settled. They are fast, though, so handling requires confidence.
  • Lifespan: 3–4 years on average.
  • Best for: Families with children, anyone who wants daytime interaction, and people who enjoy watching social animal behaviour.

NZ Regulations: What You Need to Know

New Zealand has strict biosecurity laws that affect what small animals you can keep:

  • Hamsters are NOT allowed in New Zealand. This surprises many people. The Ministry for Primary Industries (MPI) prohibits the importation and keeping of hamsters as they are classified as an unwanted organism. If you see hamsters advertised for sale in NZ, report it to MPI. This is a serious biosecurity concern.
  • Gerbils (Mongolian gerbils) are permitted and can be kept as pets in NZ. They are classified as an allowed species.
  • Other small pets like guinea pigs, rats, mice, and chinchillas are all permitted and well-established in NZ.

Important: If you have recently arrived from overseas and are looking for a hamster, you will not find one legally in NZ. Consider a gerbil, guinea pig, or rat instead — all make wonderful pets and are readily available.

The rest of this guide focuses on gerbil care (since hamsters cannot be kept in NZ) with notes on other small pets where relevant.

Housing: Getting the Enclosure Right

The biggest mistake new small pet owners make is buying a cage that is too small. Pet shops in NZ still sell tiny enclosures that barely meet minimum standards. Here is what your gerbil actually needs:

Enclosure Size

  • Minimum for a pair of gerbils: 100cm L × 50cm W floor space (the German standard, which is widely regarded as the minimum for welfare)
  • Ideal: 120cm+ L × 50cm+ W with deep substrate for burrowing
  • Height: At least 30cm to allow for substrate depth plus climbing space

Enclosure Types

  • Glass tank (terrarium): The best option. Provides deep substrate for burrowing, good ventilation with a mesh lid, and no bar-chewing. A 120cm glass tank from a NZ pet shop or secondhand is ideal.
  • DIY bin cage: A large plastic storage bin with mesh panels cut into the lid and sides. Budget-friendly and effective. Popular in NZ DIY pet communities.
  • Wire cage: Not recommended for gerbils. They dig and burrow, and wire floors cause foot injuries. Wire cages also do not hold substrate deep enough for natural burrowing behaviour.

Substrate & Bedding

Gerbils need deep substrate — at least 20–30cm — to express their natural burrowing instinct. In NZ:

  • Paper-based bedding (Carefresh or similar) — Safe, absorbent, available at most NZ pet shops
  • Aspen shavings — A safe wood-based option available in NZ
  • Hay — Timothy hay for nesting and chewing (adds enrichment)
  • Avoid: Pine and cedar shavings (toxic), sawdust (respiratory risk), and newspaper print (toxic inks)

Where to Buy Supplies in NZ

  • Petstock, Animates, and Pet.co.nz — Major chains with a decent selection of small pet supplies
  • Trade Me — Best source for secondhand tanks and large enclosures at good prices
  • Small animal breeders — Many sell quality hay and bedding in bulk
  • The Warehouse — Basic supplies for emergencies, but limited range for proper gerbil care

Feeding Your Gerbil

A balanced diet is essential for longevity and health. Gerbils are omnivores and need variety:

Daily Diet

  • Seed mix base: A quality gerbil or hamster seed mix (available at Animates, Petstock) as the foundation. Look for mixes with millet, canary seed, and safflower — avoid mixes heavy in sunflower seeds (too fatty).
  • Pellets: A small amount of high-quality lab block or pellet ensures complete nutrition. Mazuri and Oxbow are available through NZ pet shops and online.
  • Fresh vegetables: Small pieces of broccoli, carrot, cucumber, or spinach 2–3 times per week. Introduce new vegetables gradually to avoid diarrhoea.
  • Protein: A mealworm, boiled egg piece, or small amount of plain cooked chicken once or twice a week.
  • Treats: Sunflower seeds and pumpkin seeds as occasional treats only (high fat). Small pieces of plain popcorn or dried rose petals.

Water

Use a glass water bottle attached to the enclosure wall. Change water daily. Ceramic bowls tip over and get substrate in them. A 100ml bottle is sufficient for a pair.

Foods to Avoid

  • Chocolate, onions, garlic, citrus, raw potatoes, avocado, rhubarb, and anything salty, sugary, or processed. These are toxic or harmful to gerbils.

Enrichment: A Bored Gerbil Is an Unhappy Gerbil

Gerbils are intelligent and active. Without enrichment, they develop stereotypic behaviours (repetitive bar-gnawing, circling) and can become aggressive. Here is how to keep them stimulated:

Essential Enrichment

  • Wheel: A solid-surface wheel at least 28cm diameter (gerbils have long tails). Never use wire or mesh wheels — they cause foot and tail injuries. Silent Runner wheels are available on Pet.co.nz.
  • Chewing: Gerbils teeth never stop growing. Provide untreated wood blocks, apple branches, willow sticks, and cardboard tubes constantly. Toilet roll tubes are free and perfect.
  • Burrowing: Deep substrate is not optional — it is core enrichment. Gerbils that cannot dig become stressed and destructive.
  • Tunnels: Cork tunnels, ceramic pipes, and wooden bridges create a naturalistic environment. Trade Me is a good source for cheap terracotta pipes.
  • Scatter feeding: Instead of a food bowl, scatter seed mix through the substrate. This encourages natural foraging behaviour.
  • Sand bath: A shallow dish with chinchilla sand (not dust) for rolling. This keeps their coat clean and is a joy to watch.

Rotation

Swap toys and rearrange the enclosure every 1–2 weeks. Gerbils are curious and benefit from novelty. You do not need to buy new items constantly — rearranging existing items and adding fresh cardboard is often enough.

Health: Common Issues in NZ Gerbils

Gerbils are generally hardy, but watch for these conditions:

  • Respiratory infections: Sneezing, wheezing, or discharge from eyes or nose. Often caused by inappropriate bedding (pine, cedar) or poor ventilation. See a vet immediately.
  • Tail degloving: Never pick up a gerbil by the tail. The skin slides off, exposing bone. Always scoop from underneath. This injury requires urgent vet care.
  • Overgrown teeth: If a gerbil is not chewing enough, teeth can overgrow and cause eating difficulty. Provide constant chew materials.
  • Tyzzers disease: A bacterial infection that causes diarrhoea, lethargy, and sudden death. Stress is a major trigger. Good hygiene and low-stress environments prevent it.
  • Tumours: Gerbils are prone to skin and internal tumours, especially over age 2. Any lump should be checked by a vet.
  • Mites: Excessive scratching, hair loss, or scaly skin. Treated with anti-parasitic medication from an exotic pet vet.

Finding an Exotic Pet Vet in NZ

Not all NZ vets are experienced with small mammals. Look for clinics that specifically list exotic or small animal care. Key options include:

  • Auckland: The Nest Vet Hospital (Grey Lynn), Unitech Vets, Animal Emergency Centre
  • Wellington: The Veterinary Emergency Group, Wellington Bird & Exotic Clinic
  • Christchurch: Biggerstaff Veterinary Surgery, Companion Animal Vets
  • Other areas: Ask your local vet if they treat small mammals, or call the nearest exotic specialist for a referral

Taming and Handling

Whether you adopt from a breeder or a rescue, your gerbil will need a settling-in period. Here is how to build trust:

  1. Days 1–3: Leave them alone. Let them settle into their new home without handling. Talk softly near the enclosure so they get used to your voice.
  2. Days 4–7: Hand feeding. Offer sunflower seeds or mealworms from your palm. Let them come to you. Do not chase or grab.
  3. Week 2: Scooping. Place both hands flat in the enclosure and let them walk onto you. Scoop gently from underneath. Never grab from above — that triggers a prey response.
  4. Week 3+: Gradual increase. Short handling sessions on a sofa or bed (in a closed room). A gerbil playpen or small enclosed space prevents escapes.

Gerbils are fast and can jump surprisingly far. Always handle them close to the floor or over a soft surface. A fall from even a small height can cause serious injury.

Finding a Pet Sitter for Small Animals

Small pets are often overlooked in the pet sitting world. Many sitters specialise in dogs and cats but have no experience with gerbils, guinea pigs, or rats. Here is what to look for:

What a Good Small Pet Sitter Needs to Know

  • How to handle gerbils without grabbing the tail
  • How to check water bottles are actually dispensing (bottles can clog)
  • How to assess whether the substrate needs topping up
  • How to spot signs of illness: hunching, puffed fur, lethargy, diarrhoea, excessive scratching
  • How to feed the correct portions and not over-treat
  • How to safely clean the enclosure without stressing the animals

Preparing Your Pet Sitter

Before you travel, leave a care sheet with your sitter that includes:

  • Feeding schedule and portion sizes
  • Water bottle location and how to check it is working
  • Vet contact details (including after-hours emergency)
  • Normal behaviour notes so the sitter can spot changes
  • What NOT to do (no grabbing by the tail, no wire wheels, no citrus treats)
  • Emergency contact for you while you are away

Keeping Gerbils Cool in NZ Summers

New Zealand summers can be brutal, and gerbils are desert animals — but they can still overheat. Here is how to manage heat:

  • Keep the enclosure below 26°C. Use a digital thermometer on the enclosure wall.
  • Move the tank away from direct sunlight and windows.
  • Place a ceramic tile or chilled stone in the enclosure for them to lie on.
  • Freeze a water bottle, wrap it in a towel, and place it next to (not inside) the enclosure for gentle cooling.
  • Ensure good ventilation in the room. A fan pointed away from the enclosure (not directly at it) helps circulate air.
  • Signs of heat stress: lethargy, stretched out flat, rapid breathing, refusal to eat. Move to a cool room immediately and contact a vet if symptoms persist.

Adopting vs Buying

In New Zealand, you have two main options for getting a gerbil:

  • Breeders: The NZ Gerbil Club and small animal breeders on Trade Me are your best source for healthy, well-socialised gerbils. Good breeders will let you visit, answer questions, and provide health history. Expect to pay $20–$40 per gerbil.
  • Rescue: SPCA and small animal rescues occasionally have gerbils needing homes. Adoption fees are similar, and you are giving an animal a second chance. Rescue animals may need more patience during taming.

Avoid: Pet shop gerbils from shops that do not sex their animals properly. Ending up with an accidental litter is stressful for everyone. Always confirm the sex of your gerbils with an experienced handler.

Final Thoughts

Gerbils are rewarding, entertaining, and surprisingly affectionate small pets. They deserve proper housing, quality nutrition, and knowledgeable care — not the tiny cages and poor diets that pet shop advice often pushes.

If you are in New Zealand and considering a small pet, gerbils are an excellent choice. They are legal, social, active during the day, and relatively straightforward to care for once you have the right setup. The key is investing in proper housing from the start, finding a vet who knows small mammals, and arranging reliable pet sitting for when you travel.
