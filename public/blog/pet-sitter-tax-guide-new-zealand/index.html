Pet Sitter Tax Guide New Zealand: Income, Deductions & IRD Obligations

So you have started pet sitting and the money is coming in. Great. Now comes the part most pet sitters would rather ignore: tax. Whether you are pet sitting casually on weekends or running it as a full-time business, the IRD expects you to declare your income and pay tax on it. The good news is that understanding your obligations is not as complicated as it sounds, and there are legitimate deductions that can reduce your tax bill. This guide covers everything you need to know about pet sitting tax in New Zealand.

Do I Need to Pay Tax on Pet Sitting Income?

Yes. Any income you earn from pet sitting is taxable in New Zealand, regardless of whether you consider it a hobby, a side gig, or a business. The key question is not whether you have to pay tax (you do), but how you declare it.

Here is how the IRD sees it:

  • Casual or irregular income: If you pet sit occasionally and earn a small amount, you still need to declare it. It gets added to your personal income and taxed at your marginal rate.
  • Regular income: If you pet sit consistently, whether part-time or full-time, you are effectively self-employed and need to register as such with the IRD.
  • Income through a platform: If you earn through ZiggySitters or another platform, you are responsible for declaring that income. The platform does not deduct tax on your behalf.

There is no threshold below which pet sitting income is tax-free. Even $100 earned from watching a neighbour\'s cat is technically taxable income. In practice, the IRD is unlikely to pursue very small amounts, but the legal obligation exists.

Registering as Self-Employed with the IRD

If you are earning regular income from pet sitting, you should register as self-employed. This is a straightforward process:

Step 1: Get an IRD Number

If you do not already have one, apply through the IRD website. Most New Zealand residents already have an IRD number from employment, but if you are new to the country, you will need to apply.

Step 2: Register as a Sole Trader

Most pet sitters operate as sole traders. This is the simplest business structure in NZ. You do not need to incorporate a company or register a business name unless you want to. To register:

  • Go to the IRD website and select "Register as a self-employed person"
  • Provide your IRD number, contact details, and a description of your business activity (e.g., "Pet sitting and dog walking services")
  • Choose your balance date. Most sole traders use 31 March, which aligns with the NZ tax year

Step 3: Start Filing Individual Income Tax Returns

Once registered as self-employed, you will need to file an Individual Income Tax Return (IR3) each year instead of the standard IR5 that employees file. The IR3 includes a business income section where you declare your pet sitting earnings and expenses.

Step 4: Consider Provisional Tax

If your residual income tax (the tax you owe after deductions) was more than $5,000 in the previous year, you become a provisional taxpayer. This means you pay tax in instalments during the year rather than one lump sum at the end. For most casual or part-time pet sitters, this threshold will not apply in the first year or two.

What Income Do I Need to Declare?

All income from pet sitting activities is taxable. This includes:

  • Pet sitting fees — overnight stays, day visits, drop-in checks
  • Dog walking fees — individual or group walks
  • House sitting fees — if you charge separately for this
  • Additional services — pet transport, grooming assistance, administering medication
  • Tips and gratuities — yes, even tips are taxable income
  • Cancellation fees — if you have a policy and a client cancels but still pays
  • Platform earnings — all payments received through ZiggySitters or any other platform

Keep records of every payment. A simple spreadsheet with date, client name, service, and amount is sufficient to start. If you are using accounting software like Xero, MYOB, or Wave, even better.

Legitimate Deductions for Pet Sitters

Deductions reduce your taxable income, which means you pay less tax. You can claim expenses that are directly related to earning your pet sitting income. Here are the most common deductions for NZ pet sitters:

Vehicle and Travel Expenses

If you drive to clients\' homes for pet sitting or dog walking, you can claim vehicle expenses. You have two options:

  • Kilometre rate method: Multiply the business kilometres by the IRD mileage rate (83 cents per km for the first 14,000km for the 2025-26 tax year). This is the simplest method and works well for most pet sitters.
  • Actual cost method: Claim a percentage of your actual vehicle costs (fuel, insurance, registration, maintenance, depreciation) based on the business-use percentage. More complex but can yield a higher deduction if your vehicle is expensive to run.

Keep a logbook of business trips with dates, distances, and purposes. The IRD may request this if you are audited.

Pet Care Supplies

  • Dog poo bags and dispensers
  • Treats used during sits (for the pets you care for, not your own pets)
  • Pet first aid supplies
  • Leashes, harnesses, or muzzles you provide for client dogs
  • Cleaning supplies used in client homes or your own home for boarding

Professional Development

  • Pet first aid courses
  • Dog training certifications
  • Animal behaviour workshops
  • Industry conferences or seminars
  • Books and online courses related to pet care

Insurance

  • Pet sitter liability insurance
  • Professional indemnity insurance
  • Contents insurance for business equipment (if you board pets at home)

Marketing and Advertising

  • ZiggySitters platform fees or subscription
  • Business cards and flyers
  • Social media advertising
  • Website hosting and domain costs

Home Office (If You Work From Home)

If you run your pet sitting business from home, you can claim a portion of your household expenses based on the floor area used for business. This includes:

  • A percentage of rent or mortgage interest (not principal repayments)
  • A percentage of power, internet, and phone bills
  • A percentage of rates and insurance

The IRD accepts a reasonable estimate based on the area used. If you have a dedicated home office, calculate the floor area as a percentage of the total house area.

Equipment and Technology

  • A new phone or laptop used primarily for business
  • Pet sitting app subscriptions
  • Accounting software (Xero, MYOB, etc.)
  • A dedicated work phone line (if you have one)

Bank Fees and Accounting Costs

  • Bank fees on your business account
  • Accountant or tax agent fees
  • Tax return preparation costs

What You Cannot Claim

Common mistakes pet sitters make when claiming deductions:

  • Your own pets\' expenses: Food, vet bills, and supplies for your personal pets are not deductible. Only expenses for pets you are paid to care for qualify.
  • Clothing: Regular clothing is not deductible, even if you only wear it while pet sitting. Only branded uniforms or protective clothing (like overalls) qualify.
  • Fines: Parking fines, speeding tickets, or dog registration penalties are not deductible.
  • Personal travel: The trip to the beach with your own dog is personal, not business.
  • Meals: Lunch at a cafe between sits is personal. Only meals away from home for overnight travel (e.g., attending a conference in another city) may qualify.

GST Registration: When Is It Required?

You must register for GST if your annual turnover from pet sitting exceeds $60,000 per year. This is approximately $1,150 per week. If you are approaching this threshold, you can choose to register voluntarily.

Pros of Voluntary GST Registration

  • You can claim GST back on business purchases
  • It can make your business look more established and professional
  • If you are buying significant equipment or supplies, the GST claims can offset the compliance cost

Cons of Voluntary GST Registration

  • More paperwork: you need to file GST returns (monthly, bi-monthly, or six-monthly)
  • You must charge GST on your services, which increases your prices by 15%
  • For small earners, the compliance cost may exceed the GST you can claim back

Most part-time pet sitters will not hit the $60,000 threshold. For full-time sitters running a busy operation, GST registration becomes inevitable and is simply part of running a legitimate business.

Record Keeping: What the IRD Expects

Good records are your best defence if the IRD ever questions your return. You need to keep:

  • Income records: Invoices, payment receipts, bank statements showing deposits, platform payment summaries
  • Expense records: Receipts for every deduction you claim, dated and annotated with the business purpose
  • Vehicle logbook: Dates, destinations, kilometres, and business purpose for every work-related trip
  • Asset register: If you buy items over $1,000 (like a laptop), record the purchase date, cost, and estimated useful life for depreciation

How Long to Keep Records

The IRD requires you to keep records for seven years. This includes both paper and electronic records. Store digital copies of receipts (photos are fine) in a secure cloud service. Physical receipts fade, so digitise them promptly.

Recommended Tools

  • Xero: NZ-made cloud accounting, widely used by sole traders. From $33/month.
  • Wave: Free accounting software. Good for beginners but lacks some NZ-specific features.
  • MYOB: Another popular option with good GST support. From $27/month.
  • Spreadsheet: Perfectly acceptable for simple situations. The IRD does not require accounting software.

Filing Your Tax Return

The NZ tax year runs from 1 April to 31 March. Here is the timeline:

Key Dates

  • 31 March: End of tax year. Make sure your records are up to date.
  • 7 July: Deadline for filing your IR3 if you do not have a tax agent (or if your agent files without an extension).
  • 7 February the following year: Extended deadline if you use a tax agent with an extension of time.
  • 7 April: Terminal tax due (if you owe tax). This date can vary with extensions.

Filing Options

  • Online through myIR: The IRD online portal. Free, straightforward if your affairs are simple.
  • Tax agent: An accountant or tax agent can file on your behalf. Costs $200-600 for a typical sole trader return, but they often save you more than they cost by maximising deductions and ensuring compliance.
  • Accounting software: Xero and MYOB can generate reports that make completing the IR3 much easier.

What Goes on the IR3

As a sole trader pet sitter, your IR3 will include:

  1. Total pet sitting income for the year (1 April to 31 March)
  2. Total business expenses claimed as deductions
  3. Net profit (income minus expenses) — this is your taxable business income
  4. Any other income (wages from a day job, interest, etc.)
  5. Your total taxable income and the tax owed on it

Pet Sitting and ACC Levies

As a self-employed person, you are also liable for ACC earner levies. These are calculated on your taxable income and appear on your tax assessment. The ACC earner levy rate for the 2025-26 year is $1.60 per $100 of liable earnings. This covers you for personal injury that prevents you from working.

If pet sitting is your only income, the levy is modest. If you also have a day job, your employer already pays the earners levy on those wages, and you will only pay on your pet sitting income above that threshold.

Common Mistakes Pet Sitters Make

  1. Not declaring income at all. The IRD can match bank deposits and platform payments. Undeclared income carries penalties of 20% to 150% of the tax owed, plus use-of-money interest.
  2. Mixing personal and business expenses. Open a separate bank account for your pet sitting income and expenses. It makes record keeping dramatically easier and looks better if audited.
  3. Not keeping receipts. The IRD can deny deductions without evidence. Take a photo of every receipt immediately.
  4. Claiming personal pet costs. This is the most common audit trigger for pet-related businesses. Only claim expenses for client pets.
  5. Missing the filing deadline. Late filing incurs penalties. If you cannot file on time, contact the IRD or use a tax agent who can get an extension.
  6. Not registering for GST when required. If you hit $60,000 turnover and have not registered, the IRD can backdate your GST liability.

Working With a Tax Agent

Hiring an accountant or tax agent is not required, but it is often worth the investment, especially in your first year. A good tax agent will:

  • Identify deductions you might miss
  • Ensure your return is compliant and filed on time
  • Handle IRD correspondence on your behalf
  • Get an extension of time for filing (to February instead of July)
  • Advise on GST registration and provisional tax

Typical cost for a sole trader tax return with a tax agent: $200-$600. Deductible as a business expense in the following year.

  • All pet sitting income is taxable in New Zealand, regardless of amount
  • Register as self-employed with the IRD if you earn regularly from pet sitting
