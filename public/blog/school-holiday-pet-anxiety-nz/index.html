School Holiday Pet Anxiety NZ: Helping Pets Cope When Kids Are Home

Published April 18, 2026 | 11 min read

School holidays are exciting for kids — but for many pets, they mean sudden, overwhelming change. When children are home 24/7, pets lose their routine, face constant noise and excitement, and can become anxious, stressed, or even aggressive. This guide helps New Zealand pet parents manage holiday-induced pet anxiety.

Why School Holidays Stress Out Pets

Pets thrive on routine. Dogs and cats quickly learn when meals happen, when walks occur, and when the house is quiet. School holidays disrupt every part of this predictable rhythm:

  • Constant noise levels — Screaming, laughing, and play throughout the day
  • Unpredictable activity — Sudden trips outside, bike rides, visitors
  • Interrupted sleep patterns — Later bedtimes and later mornings upset feeding times
  • Territorial awareness — Dogs particularly feel the need to guard against heightened activity
  • Attention competition — Your pet loses one-on-one time with you

For anxious pets — especially rescue animals, senior pets, or those with historical trauma — this disruption can trigger serious behavioural changes.

Signs Your Pet Is Stressed During Holidays

Watch for these common anxiety signals:

  • Excessive barking or whining — Especially at nothing
  • Hiding — Seeking refuge under furniture or in quiet rooms
  • Clinginess — Following you everywhere, unable to settle
  • Changes in appetite — Eating less or scarfing food anxiously
  • Destruction — Chewing furniture, shoes, or household items
  • Accidents indoors — House-trained dogs having accidents
  • Growling or snapping — Especially around children
  • Excessive grooming — Cats over-grooming to the point of bald patches

If your pet shows severe signs of anxiety, speak with your vet about calming supplements, pheromone products, or behavioural training options.

Preparing Your Pet Before Holidays Start

The best defence is a good offence. Prepare your pet before the chaos begins:

Keep Walks and Routines Consistent

Maintain your pet exercise routine even when life gets busy. If walks happen at 7am and 5pm normally, keep that schedule. Enlist help from a dog walker if needed — consistency matters more than ever during holidays.

Pro tip: Consider booking a dog walker through the holiday period. It ensures your dog gets professional exercise and a calm, predictable walk regardless of family activity.

Create a Quiet Sanctuary

Every pet needs a safe space to escape the holiday chaos. This should be:

  • Quiet and private — Away from play areas
  • Comfortable — With their bed, favourite toys, and a water bowl
  • Accessible — Your pet can come and go freely
  • Consistent — Use the same space every holiday

Introduce this space before holidays begin. Make it positive with treats and calm praise. Cats should have vertical spaces — tall cat trees or shelving — where they can observe from above.

Increase Mental Stimulation

Tired pets are calm pets. Increase mental enrichment before and during holidays:

  • Puzzle feeders — Slow-feeding toys that require problem-solving
  • Snuffle mats — Perfect for dogs that eat too fast
  • Lick mats — Frozen treats keep cats and dogs occupied
  • Training sessions — New tricks tire out dogs mentally
  • Find-it games — Hide treats around the house for searching

Managing Holiday Chaos

Set Boundaries With Children

Teach kids to respect pet space and signals:

  • No chasing — Never run at or chase the family pet
  • No touching while eating — Give pets peace during meals
  • Recognise warning signs — Teach children when a dog wants to be left alone
  • Quiet voices — Whisper around the pet sanctuary
  • Scheduled pet time — Set specific times for one-on-one pet cuddles

Important: Young children may not recognise warning signs. Never leave toddlers and pets unsupervised. A growling or freezing dog is giving a clear warning — respect it and separate the dog from children.

Introduce White Noise or Calming Music

Background sound masks sudden noise spikes. Calming music specifically composed for dogs (available on streaming platforms) can lower anxiety. White noise machines or a low television volume in the background creates consistency despite household activity.

Use Pheromone Products

Synthetic pheromone diffusers and sprays mimic calming scents that reassure pets. Available from New Zealand pet stores and veterinarians, these products plug into wall sockets and release calming signals continuously.

Dogs respond to Adaptil (dog appeasing pheromone). Cats respond to Feliway (feline facial pheromone). Start using these a week before holidays begin for maximum effect.

Maintain Feeding and Toilet Schedules

Stick to normal meal times as closely as possible. Anxiety often shows first in disrupted eating or digestion. If your schedule must change gradually, shift by 30 minutes per day rather than all at once.

Ensure outdoor access is easy — with kids home, garden door traffic increases and anxious pets may hold bladder anxiety until it is too late.

When You Need Professional Help

If your pet struggles significantly during every school holiday, consider these options:

Consult Your Veterinarian

Persistent anxiety may benefit from:

  • Calming supplements — Zylkene, VitalAGE, or other vet-recommended options
  • Pheromone therapy — Long-term diffuser use
  • Medication — For severe cases, anti-anxiety medication may be appropriate

Engage a Professional Trainer

A qualified animal behaviourist can help identify triggers and develop a desensitisation plan. Look for certified professionals in New Zealand who use positive reinforcement methods.

Consider a Holiday Pet Sitter

If your pet genuinely cannot cope with holiday chaos, a pet sitter living in your home provides one-on-one care in a calm environment. This removes your pet from the stress while maintaining their routine as much as possible.

Post-Holiday Transition

After holidays end, give your pet time to readjust:

  • Return to routine gradually — Begin normal schedules on day one
  • One-on-one time — Reconnect with calm cuddles and attention
  • Patience — Expect some regression in behaviour for a few days
  • Extra walks — Help burn off accumulated holiday zoomies

If signs of anxiety persist weeks after holidays end, a vet visit may be appropriate to rule out underlying health issues.

Summary: Managing School Holiday Anxiety

  • Start preparations at least one week before holidays
  • Create a quiet, accessible sanctuary
  • Maintain walks, feeding, and sleep schedules
  • Teach children how to respect pet boundaries
  • Use calming pheromones before and during holidays
  • Increase mental stimulation with puzzle toys
  • Consider a pet sitter if anxiety is severe
  • Book a vet or trainer if problems persist
