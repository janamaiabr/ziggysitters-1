Common Pet Sitting Mistakes & How to Avoid Them (NZ Guide 2026)

2026-07-07 · 13 min read · ZiggySitters Team

Most pet sitting arrangements go smoothly. But when things go wrong, they tend to go wrong in predictable ways — and almost always because of preventable mistakes on both sides. After years of connecting NZ pet owners with sitters, we\'ve seen the same patterns repeat. This guide walks through the most common pet sitting mistakes, the real consequences they cause, and the specific steps you can take to make sure your next arrangement is bulletproof.

Mistake 1: Skipping the Meet and Greet

The single most common mistake pet owners make is booking a sitter based entirely on a profile and a few messages. No matter how impressive someone\'s reviews are, you cannot assess how they interact with your specific pet through text alone. Dogs that are friendly with most people may react differently to a stranger entering their home. Cats that are "chill" at the vet might vanish under the couch the moment a new person walks in.

A proper meet and greet takes 30 to 45 minutes and should happen in your home. Watch how the sitter approaches your pet. Do they let the animal come to them, or do they force interaction? Do they seem comfortable and confident, or uncertain and nervous? Your pet\'s body language will tell you more in five minutes of in-person observation than hours of messaging ever will. If your dog growls, your cat hides and won\'t emerge, or any animal shows clear distress, this sitter may not be the right fit — and that\'s valuable information to have before you\'re halfway through a two-week trip.

The meet and greet is also your opportunity to show the sitter exactly how your home works. Where are the water shutoff valves? How does the tricky back door latch? Which neighbours can they call in an emergency? This walkthrough prevents dozens of small problems that compound into real stress when the sitter is alone in your home at 10pm trying to figure out where the fuse box is.

Mistake 2: Vague or Incomplete Instructions

Most pet owners write instructions that make perfect sense to themselves but leave critical gaps for someone unfamiliar with the household. "Feed the cat twice a day" seems clear until the sitter is standing in front of three different bags of cat food, two of which are the previous vet-recommended diet that your cat no longer eats. "Give medication morning and night" is specific about timing but doesn\'t address what happens when the cat refuses the pill pocket — and you always hide it in a piece of roast chicken that you forgot to mention.

Good instructions include precise quantities (not "a handful" — specify "1/2 cup of the orange bag, morning and evening"), exact medication protocols including what to do if a dose is missed or refused, and the location of every essential item. Photograph your feeding station, medicine cabinet, and emergency supplies. Label food containers with the pet\'s name and feeding schedule. If your routine involves steps that seem obvious to you, write them down anyway — what feels like second nature after years of caring for your pet is anything but obvious to someone doing it for the first time.

Include the unexpected scenarios too. What should the sitter do if the dog has diarrhoea? (Withhold food for how long? Which medication from the medicine cabinet?) Where is the nearest after-hours vet, and under what circumstances should they take your pet there versus calling you first? These details feel like over-planning until the sitter is dealing with a sick animal at midnight and you\'re on a flight over the Pacific.

Mistake 3: Not Having a Veterinary Plan

This mistake has two dimensions: not providing vet contact details, and not authorising treatment. Many owners assume their regular vet will handle everything, but regular vets don\'t operate 24/7. Your sitter needs both your regular vet\'s details and the nearest after-hours emergency clinic. In rural New Zealand, the nearest emergency vet might be an hour\'s drive away — information the sitter needs before there\'s a crisis, not during one.

More importantly, you need to explicitly authorise the sitter to seek veterinary care and set a financial limit. Write a letter authorising the sitter to approve treatment up to a specific dollar amount — most NZ vets recommend at least $500 to $1,000 as an emergency threshold. Provide your credit card details or arrange payment directly with the clinic. A sitter who cannot authorise treatment in an emergency may delay care while trying to reach you, and in medical emergencies, delays can be fatal.

Leave your pet\'s medical history with the sitter, including vaccination records, existing conditions, current medications, and any known allergies or adverse reactions. If your pet has a chronic condition like epilepsy or diabetes, include a one-page summary of what a typical episode looks like and the exact steps to take. This is not the time for brevity — write it like you\'re instructing someone who has never met your pet, because you are.

Mistake 4: Ignoring Red Flags in a Sitter\'s Profile

The temptation to book the first available sitter is strong, especially when you\'re organising a last-minute trip. But certain red flags should never be ignored, regardless of how urgently you need care. A profile with no reviews is not necessarily a dealbreaker — everyone starts somewhere — but it does mean you need extra diligence during the meet and greet and reference check. A sitter who avoids meeting in person, can\'t provide any references, or resists providing identification is not worth the risk.

Watch for inconsistencies between what a profile claims and what you observe. Someone who lists "10 years of experience" but doesn\'t know basic pet care concepts may be inflating their history. Sitters who are vague about their own pets, who don\'t ask you questions about your animal\'s needs, or who seem more interested in the free accommodation than the pet care responsibilities are unlikely to provide the attention your pet deserves.

In New Zealand, look for sitters who have completed police vetting through the Ministry of Justice. While not mandatory, it demonstrates a level of transparency and trustworthiness. Ask specifically about their experience with your type of pet — someone who has only ever cared for dogs may not recognise the subtle signs of illness in cats, and vice versa. Species-specific knowledge matters more than most owners realise until something goes wrong.

Mistake 5: No Written Agreement

Verbal agreements work beautifully until they don\'t. Without a written agreement, you have no recourse if the sitter arrives late, cancels at the last minute, or provides a different level of care than discussed. More importantly, a written agreement forces both parties to think through details that are easy to gloss over in conversation: exact dates and times, specific services included, what happens if the sitter needs to leave early, and how emergencies will be handled.

A good pet sitting agreement covers the basics — dates, services, and payment — but also addresses the edge cases that cause real problems. Who pays for vet visits and under what authority? What happens if the sitter gets sick or has a personal emergency? Are guests allowed in the home? What about the sitter using your car, food, or other resources? What is the policy on posting photos of your pet or home on social media?

ZiggySitters provides a template agreement that covers NZ-specific considerations, including ACC coverage for injuries that occur in your home, privacy act obligations, and consumer guarantees act provisions. Using a template doesn\'t make the arrangement feel adversarial — it makes it feel professional. And professional arrangements are far less likely to end in misunderstandings or disputes.

Mistake 6: Not Preparing the Home (or the Pet)

Your home needs to be sitter-ready in ways that go beyond "clean enough for guests." Secure anything your pet could get into that a sitter might not anticipate — rubbish bins, cleaning products, human food on counters, and small items that could be swallowed. Dogs that are normally well-behaved sometimes act out when their owner leaves, getting into things they\'ve never touched before. The sitter doesn\'t know your pet\'s patterns well enough to anticipate these surprises.

Pet-proof your home more thoroughly than you would for your own use. Move medications to a high cabinet or locked drawer. Ensure all rubbish bins have secure lids. Put away shoes, socks, and other items that anxious dogs may chew. If your cat has a habit of opening certain cupboards, use child locks. The goal is to eliminate the possibility of preventable accidents, not to trust that "the pet will be fine."

Preparing the pet matters just as much. If your dog is crate-trained, make sure the crate is set up and the sitter knows the routine. Leave unwashed bedding so your scent provides comfort. Consider using a pheromone diffuser (Adaptil for dogs, Feliway for cats) in the main living areas — these can significantly reduce anxiety during the first few days of an owner\'s absence. For highly anxious pets, a trial run with the sitter for a few hours before your trip can make the full arrangement dramatically smoother.

Mistake 7: Poor Communication Expectations

One of the fastest ways to damage a pet sitting arrangement is mismatched communication expectations. Some owners want hourly updates with photos. Others are happy with a single daily text. Neither is wrong, but the sitter needs to know what you expect before you leave. Discuss your preferences openly during the meet and greet and agree on a realistic schedule that works for both of you.

Be reasonable about what you ask for. Expecting a sitter to send a detailed play-by-play of every interaction is unrealistic and creates unnecessary pressure. A daily update with a photo and brief summary is standard and reasonable. For pets with health conditions, agree on how and when to communicate any concerns. Establish what constitutes an emergency that warrants an immediate phone call versus a routine question that can wait for a text response.

From the sitter\'s side, communication is equally important. If something seems off — the pet isn\'t eating, there\'s unusual behaviour, or something in the home isn\'t working — tell the owner promptly. The worst thing a sitter can do is hide a problem, hoping it resolves on its own. Most owners would rather hear about a small concern immediately than discover a bigger problem when they return. ZiggySitters encourages sitters to send at least one update per day and to flag any concerns within hours, not days.

Mistake 8: Underestimating Separation Anxiety

You know your pet gets a bit mopey when you leave. What you might not realise is how that "bit mopey" can escalate over days. Many pets experience their worst separation anxiety not on day one, but on days two through five, as the reality of your absence sinks in. Dogs may refuse food, develop diarrhoea, bark or howl for extended periods, or become destructive. Cats may stop eating, hide constantly, or start spraying urine in unfamiliar places.

Prepare the sitter for this possibility. Explain how your pet typically reacts to your absence, how long the adjustment period usually lasts, and what strategies have worked in the past. If your dog has a favourite toy that provides comfort, make sure the sitter knows to offer it. If your cat usually starts eating again after 24 to 36 hours, tell the sitter so they don\'t panic on day two. Having context for what\'s normal versus what\'s concerning helps the sitter respond appropriately rather than overreacting or, worse, underreacting.

For pets with known severe separation anxiety, discuss management strategies with your vet before the trip. In some cases, short-term anxiety medication may be appropriate. Provide the sitter with clear instructions on when and how to administer it. Do not leave medication instructions to chance — write them down, demonstrate the technique, and confirm the sitter is comfortable following through.

Mistake 9: Leaving Too Much to the Sitter\'s Judgement

Good sitters are capable and responsible, but they are not mind readers. Leaving vague instructions like "use your best judgement" for food portions, exercise levels, or behavioural management puts the sitter in an impossible position. Your "moderate walk" might be their "long hike." Your "small amount" of treats might be their entire treat jar. When instructions are vague, the sitter has to guess — and if they guess wrong, the pet suffers.

Be specific about dietary restrictions, including absolute prohibitions. Many NZ dogs have sensitivities to common foods like chicken, beef, or grains, and a sitter who doesn\'t know this might give a treat that triggers days of gastrointestinal distress. Be explicit about exercise limits too — a young, energetic Labrador needs very different activity levels than a senior dog with hip dysplasia, and the sitter cannot tell the difference from a profile description alone.

Behavioural instructions are especially important. If your dog pulls on the lead, tell the sitter what correction method you use. If your cat is not allowed in certain rooms, specify which rooms and how to enforce the boundary. If your dog reacts to other dogs on walks, describe the management technique that works — crossing the street, using a distraction, or taking a different route. These details prevent the sitter from inadvertently reinforcing bad habits or creating dangerous situations.

Mistake 10: No Backup Plan

Even the best arrangements can fall apart. The sitter gets sick, has a family emergency, or simply doesn\'t show up. Your flight gets cancelled, extending your trip by three days. Your pet develops a sudden health issue that requires more specialised care than the sitter can provide. Without a backup plan, these scenarios become crises instead of inconveniences.

Always have a backup sitter identified and briefed before you leave. Share the backup sitter\'s contact details with your primary sitter and vice versa. Give both sitters a key or access code to your home. Provide your vet with authorisation for both sitters. This preparation takes 30 minutes and can save hours of frantic searching when something goes wrong at the worst possible time.

For longer trips, identify a local friend, neighbour, or family member who can serve as emergency backup. Share their contact details with the sitter and provide the emergency contact with the sitter\'s details. In New Zealand, many vet clinics also offer emergency boarding for short periods if a pet sitting arrangement collapses unexpectedly.

The Complete Pet Sitting Checklist

Use this checklist before every pet sitting arrangement to avoid the mistakes covered in this guide:

  • Meet and greet completed — in your home, with your pet, at least a few days before departure
  • Written instructions prepared — feeding, medication, exercise, behaviour, and emergency protocols
  • Vet details and authorisation letter — regular vet, after-hours emergency clinic, and written treatment authorisation with a dollar limit
  • Home prepared and pet-proofed — secure bins, locked cupboards, hazards removed, unwashed bedding left for scent
  • Written agreement signed — dates, services, payment, cancellation terms, emergency procedures, and house rules
  • Communication expectations agreed — update frequency, preferred method, what constitutes an emergency
  • Backup sitter identified — contact details shared with primary sitter, key or access provided
  • Emergency contacts listed — your mobile, a local friend or neighbour, your vet, the after-hours emergency clinic
  • Pet supplies stocked — enough food, medication, litter, and essentials for the entire period plus 3-5 extra days
  • Pheromone diffusers plugged in — Adaptil or Feliway in main living areas, started 24 hours before departure

From the Sitter\'s Side: Mistakes Sitters Make

Pet sitters have their own set of common mistakes that undermine good intentions. Taking on too many assignments at once is the most frequent — a sitter who is running between three houses cannot give adequate attention to any of them. Deviating from the owner\'s instructions because "I know better" is another classic error. Even if you have years of experience, every pet is different, and the owner\'s specific routines exist for reasons you may not understand.

Failing to communicate problems early is arguably the most damaging sitter mistake. A dog that hasn\'t eaten for 24 hours is worth mentioning. A cat that hasn\'t used the litter tray in a day is worth mentioning. A broken appliance, a slipped lead, or a minor scrape — mention them all. Owners would rather hear about small issues immediately than discover large ones on return. Professional sitters communicate proactively because they understand that trust is built on transparency.

Finally, sitters who don\'t ask enough questions during the meet and greet are setting themselves up for problems. If the owner\'s instructions don\'t cover something, ask. If you\'re not sure about a medication dose, confirm it. If the dog\'s body language seems off during the meet and greet, say so. Asking questions doesn\'t make you look inexperienced — it makes you look thorough. The best sitters are the ones who leave the meet and greet with zero ambiguity about the pet\'s care.

Every mistake in this guide is preventable. The common thread is preparation: meeting the sitter, writing detailed instructions, planning for emergencies, and maintaining clear communication. Pet sitting works best when both parties treat it as the professional arrangement it is — not as a casual favour between friends. When owners and sitters each do their part, the result is a pet that stays happy, healthy, and comfortable in their own home while their human is away. That is the standard every arrangement should meet.
