Pet Sitter Red Flags: 15 Warning Signs Every NZ Pet Owner Should Know

Published 22 June 2026 • 8 min read • ZiggySitters NZ

You would not hand your house keys to a stranger you met once. Yet thousands of New Zealand pet owners do exactly that every year — trusting someone with their fur baby, their home, and their peace of mind without proper vetting. This guide reveals the 15 red flags that should make you think twice before hiring a pet sitter, and what to look for instead.

Why Red Flags Matter More in Pet Sitting

Unlike childcare or aged care, pet sitting in New Zealand operates with minimal regulation. Anyone can list themselves online, and the consequences of a bad hire are severe: missed medication, escaped pets, damaged property, or worse. A 2025 survey by the NZ Companion Animal Council found that 1 in 8 pet owners who used an unvetted sitter experienced a serious incident, from pets going missing to emergency vet visits.

The good news? Almost every bad sitter reveals themselves before you hand over the keys — if you know what to look for.

Red Flag #1: No Verifiable Reviews or References

A sitter with zero reviews is not necessarily a bad sitter — everyone starts somewhere. But a sitter who refuses to provide references or brushes off your request for past-client contacts is hiding something. Legitimate sitters are proud of their track record.

Warning: Be especially cautious of profiles that only showcase staged photos or generic testimonials without names or dates. Authentic reviews mention specific details — pet names, neighbourhoods, situations handled.

Red Flag #2: Vague or Evasive Communication

How a sitter communicates before the booking tells you everything about how they will communicate during it. Watch for:

  • Slow, one-word replies that never fully answer your questions
  • Deflection when you ask about emergency procedures or vet contacts
  • Overly vague answers about their experience with your type of pet
  • Reluctance to do a meet-and-greet before the booking

Clear, prompt communication is not optional — it is the baseline.

Red Flag #3: No Insurance or Bonding

Professional pet sitters in New Zealand should carry liability insurance. If something goes wrong — your dog bites someone, your cat knocks over a valuable vase, a key gets lost — insurance protects both parties. A sitter who shrugs off the topic of insurance is either inexperienced or cutting corners.

Tip: Ask for the name of their insurer and policy number. A genuine professional will provide this without hesitation. If they say “I have not needed it yet,” that is exactly when they need it.

Red Flag #4: Refusal to Do a Meet-and-Greet

The meet-and-greet is non-negotiable. It is where your pet reacts to the sitter, where you see how they handle your animal, and where you establish ground rules. A sitter who wants to skip this step is either too busy to care properly or hiding something about their demeanour around animals.

Red Flag #5: Overbooking or Taking Too Many Jobs at Once

Some sitters take on three, four, or even five bookings simultaneously. That means your pet is sharing attention with other animals, and the sitter is running between houses. Ask directly: “How many other bookings will you have during my dates?” If they dodge the question, assume the worst.

Red Flag #6: No Emergency Plan

Every responsible sitter should be able to answer: “What would you do if my pet had a medical emergency at 2 AM?” If they stammer or say something generic like “I would figure it out,” they have not thought it through. You want to hear specifics:

  • The name and address of your regular vet
  • The after-hours emergency vet closest to your home
  • Your preferred contact order (you, your backup person, the vet)
  • A clear understanding of your financial authorisation for emergency care

Red Flag #7: Unwillingness to Follow Your Instructions

If a sitter pushes back on feeding schedules, medication times, or leash rules during the initial conversation, they will absolutely ignore your instructions once you are gone. Your pet, your rules. A good sitter may offer suggestions, but they ultimately respect your decisions.

Red Flag #8: No Written Agreement or Contract

A handshake is not enough. A proper pet sitting agreement outlines services, dates, rates, emergency contacts, and liability. It protects both of you. If a sitter insists on keeping everything verbal, you have no recourse if something goes wrong.

Red Flag #9: Reluctance to Provide Photo Updates

In 2026, every sitter has a smartphone. Regular photo updates are standard practice, not an imposition. If a sitter resists sending photos or updates, ask yourself: what is happening that they do not want you to see?

Green flag: A great sitter sends unsolicited updates — photos, short videos, and brief notes about how your pet is doing. This is the standard you should expect.

Red Flag #10: Suspiciously Low Rates

New Zealand pet sitting rates vary by region, but in 2026, you should expect roughly $25–$50 per visit and $80–$150 per night for house sitting. If someone is charging half that, ask why. It often means inexperience, corner-cutting, or that they are treating it as pocket money rather than a profession.

Red Flag #11: No Background Check or Identity Verification

You are giving a stranger access to your home and your beloved pet. If the platform or sitter cannot verify their identity, that is a dealbreaker. Look for:

  • Verified ID on the platform
  • Police vetting (especially for overnight stays)
  • Social media presence that matches their profile

Red Flag #12: Negative Attitude Toward Your Pet

Watch how a sitter interacts with your pet during the meet-and-greet. Do they crouch down and let the pet come to them, or do they grab and force interaction? Are they patient or irritated by normal pet behaviour? A sitter who is annoyed by barking, shedding, or a cat that hides is the wrong sitter for your home.

Red Flag #13: Cancelling or Rescheduling Frequently Before the Booking

If a sitter reschedules the meet-and-greet twice, there is a real chance they will cancel on you the day before your holiday. Life happens, but a pattern of flakiness before you have even started is a predictor of unreliability.

Red Flag #14: No First Aid or Pet Care Training

You do not need a sitter with a veterinary degree, but basic pet first aid knowledge is reasonable. A sitter who says “I grew up with dogs so I know everything” is not the same as someone who has completed a pet first aid course. New Zealand organisations like St John and the SPCA offer short courses — ask if your sitter has done one.

Red Flag #15: They Do Not Ask You Questions

A good sitter interviews you as much as you interview them. They want to know your pet is health status, behaviour quirks, feeding schedule, and emergency contacts. A sitter who asks no questions is either overconfident or does not care enough to prepare properly.

The Green Flags: What a Great Sitter Looks Like

Now that you know what to avoid, here is what to look for:

  • Responsive, detailed communication — they reply promptly and thoroughly
  • Proactive updates — photos and notes without you asking
  • Professional documentation — contract, insurance, and clear terms
  • Genuine warmth with your pet — patient, calm, observant
  • Questions for you — they want to get it right
  • Verified identity and reviews — transparent track record
  • Emergency preparedness — clear vet contacts and action plan

What to Do if You Spot Red Flags

If something feels off, trust that feeling. You do not owe anyone a booking.

  1. Cancel early. The sooner you walk away, the more time you have to find a better sitter.
  2. Document everything. Save screenshots of conversations, especially if the sitter made commitments they later backed away from.
  3. Report concerning behaviour to the platform you found them on.
  4. Re-start your search with a clearer list of non-negotiables.
Quick Checklist — Red Flags at a Glance:
  • No reviews or references
  • Evasive or vague communication
  • No insurance or bonding
  • Refuses meet-and-greet
  • Overbooking multiple jobs
  • No emergency plan
  • Will not follow your instructions
  • No written agreement
  • Resists sending photo updates
  • Suspiciously low rates
  • No identity verification
  • Negative attitude toward your pet
  • Frequent cancellations
  • No first aid training
  • Does not ask you questions
