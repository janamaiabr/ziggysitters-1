Cat Flu & Common Cat Illnesses NZ Guide 2026: What Every Cat Owner Needs to Know

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  May 26, 2026  •  13 min read

Cats are masters of hiding illness. By the time you notice something is wrong, your cat may have been unwell for days. Cat flu, urinary tract disease, dental problems, and skin conditions are among the most common reasons New Zealand cats end up at the vet — and many of these are preventable or manageable when caught early. This guide covers the illnesses most likely to affect your NZ cat, what symptoms to watch for, and when to get to the vet immediately.

Cat Flu (Feline Upper Respiratory Infection)

Cat flu is the most common infectious disease in New Zealand cats. It is not a single virus — it is caused by a combination of pathogens, primarily feline herpesvirus (FHV-1) and feline calicivirus (FCV). These two viruses account for roughly 80–90% of cat flu cases, with bacteria like Chlamydophila felis and Bordetella making up the rest.

Symptoms to Watch For

  • Sneezing — frequent, sometimes violent bouts
  • Nasal discharge — clear at first, turning thick and yellow-green
  • Watery, red eyes — often with discharge that crusts over
  • Ulcers on the tongue, gums, or lips — more common with calicivirus
  • Fever — above 39.5°C is significant in cats
  • Lethargy and loss of appetite — cats stop eating when they can not smell food
  • Drooling — caused by mouth ulcers making swallowing painful
Emergency: If your cat has not eaten for 24 hours, is having difficulty breathing, or has severe eye ulcers, get to a vet immediately. Cat flu can progress to pneumonia, and cats that stop eating can develop hepatic lipidosis — a dangerous liver condition — within 48 hours.

How Cat Flu Spreads

Cat flu is highly contagious. It spreads through:

  • Direct contact with an infected cat (including mutual grooming)
  • Airborne droplets from sneezing (can travel 1–2 metres)
  • Shared bowls, litter trays, bedding, and toys
  • Human hands and clothing — you can carry the virus home to your indoor cat

The herpesvirus can remain dormant in a cat for life, reactivating during times of stress. Cats that have had cat flu once can shed the virus intermittently without showing symptoms — making them carriers that infect other cats.

Treatment

Most cases of cat flu are viral, which means antibiotics will not cure the infection itself. However, vets often prescribe antibiotics to prevent or treat secondary bacterial infections. Treatment focuses on supportive care:

  • Keep your cat eating. Warm wet food to body temperature to make it smell stronger. Offer strong-smelling foods like sardines or tuna in spring water.
  • Wipe discharge. Gently clean eyes and nose with a warm, damp cloth. Use saline for eyes.
  • Humidify the air. Run a humidifier or bring your cat into the bathroom while you shower. Steam helps clear congested airways.
  • Encourage drinking. Use a pet water fountain — cats prefer flowing water. Add water to wet food.
  • Isolate infected cats. Keep sick cats away from healthy ones in a separate room with their own bowls and litter tray.
  • Vet care for severe cases. Intravenous fluids, antiviral medication (famciclovir for herpesvirus), and eye ointments may be needed.

Prevention

Vaccination is the single most effective way to prevent cat flu. The core F3 vaccine protects against feline herpesvirus, feline calicivirus, and feline parvovirus. The F4 and F5 vaccines add protection against Chlamydophila and feline leukaemia virus.

In New Zealand, kittens should receive their first vaccination at 6–8 weeks, with booster shots every 3–4 weeks until 16 weeks of age. Adult cats need annual boosters. Even indoor cats should be vaccinated — you can carry the virus home on your hands and clothes.

Pro Tip: If you are getting a kitten from a breeder or rescue, ask for vaccination records. Many NZ rescue cats have already had cat flu and may be carriers. This does not mean you should avoid adopting them — it means you should keep them vaccinated and away from unvaccinated cats.

Feline Lower Urinary Tract Disease (FLUTD)

FLUTD is one of the most common reasons cats visit the vet in New Zealand, and one of the most painful. It encompasses a range of conditions affecting the bladder and urethra, including urinary tract infections, bladder stones, urethral plugs, and feline idiopathic cystitis (FIC).

Symptoms

  • Straining in the litter tray with little or no urine produced
  • Frequent trips to the litter tray
  • Blood in the urine (looks pink or red)
  • Urinating outside the litter tray (on beds, carpet, laundry)
  • Excessive licking of the genital area
  • Crying or howling when urinating
Critical: If your male cat is straining and producing no urine, this is a medical emergency. A blocked urethra can kill a cat within 24–48 hours. Do not wait — go to an emergency vet immediately.

Risk Factors

Several factors increase the risk of FLUTD in NZ cats:

  • Diet: Dry-only diets concentrate urine and increase crystal formation
  • Obesity: Overweight cats are significantly more prone to urinary issues
  • Stress: FIC is strongly linked to environmental stress — changes in routine, new pets, building work, or even a new neighbourhood cat outside the window
  • Indoor lifestyle: Indoor cats have less stimulation and more concentrated urine
  • Male cats: Males have a narrower urethra and are more likely to become blocked

Prevention

  • Feed wet food or a mix of wet and high-quality dry food
  • Provide multiple water sources — fountains, bowls in different rooms
  • Keep the litter tray spotless (one tray per cat plus one extra)
  • Reduce stress with consistent routines, vertical space, and hiding spots
  • Encourage exercise to prevent obesity
  • Consider a urinary health diet if your vet recommends one

Dental Disease

By age three, over 70% of cats show signs of dental disease. In New Zealand, where many cats live indoors or semi-indoors with limited access to natural chewing materials, dental problems are incredibly common and often go unnoticed until they become severe.

Types of Dental Disease in Cats

  • Periodontal disease: Plaque hardens into tartar, causing gum inflammation (gingivitis), which progresses to periodontitis — destroying the tissue and bone that hold teeth in place
  • Feline resorptive lesions (FORL): Erosion of the tooth structure itself, extremely painful. Affects up to 60% of cats over 5 years old. The cause is not fully understood
  • Stomatitis: Severe inflammation of the mouth lining, often linked to an overactive immune response to plaque bacteria. Very painful and difficult to treat

Signs Your Cat Has Dental Problems

  • Bad breath (not just “cat food breath” — genuinely foul)
  • Dropping food or eating on one side of the mouth
  • Chewing slowly or preferring soft food
  • Pawing at the mouth
  • Excessive drooling, sometimes with blood
  • Red, inflamed gums (check by gently lifting the lip)
  • Weight loss or reduced appetite

Treatment and Prevention

Professional dental cleanings under general anaesthesia are the gold standard for existing dental disease. Your vet will scale and polish the teeth, extract any that are beyond saving, and assess the overall health of the mouth. In NZ, a dental cleaning with extractions typically costs $400–$900 depending on severity.

At home, prevention focuses on:

  • Daily tooth brushing with cat-specific toothpaste (never human toothpaste — fluoride is toxic to cats)
  • Dental diet kibble that mechanically cleans teeth as the cat chews
  • Dental treats and water additives approved by the Veterinary Oral Health Council
  • Annual vet check-ups that include a dental examination
Pro Tip: Start brushing your kitten's teeth early. Adult cats that have never had their teeth brushed will fight you on it. Even brushing 2–3 times per week makes a significant difference in long-term dental health.

Skin Allergies and Dermatitis

New Zealand cats face a unique set of skin challenges. Fleas thrive in our mild climate, and environmental allergens like grass pollen affect cats just as much as humans. The three main types of skin allergies in cats are:

Flea Allergy Dermatitis (FAD)

The most common skin condition in NZ cats. Some cats are allergic to flea saliva — a single bite can trigger intense itching that lasts for days. You do not need to see fleas for this to be the problem. Treatment: strict flea control with a vet-recommended product (not supermarket spot-ons, which are often ineffective).

Food Allergies

Less common than flea allergies but increasingly recognised. Typical allergens include chicken, beef, fish, and dairy. Symptoms include chronic itching, head and neck scratching, and gastrointestinal issues. Diagnosis requires an 8–12 week elimination diet supervised by a vet.

Environmental Allergies (Atopic Dermatitis)

Pollen, dust mites, and mould can cause year-round or seasonal itching. Common in NZ spring and summer. Often affects the face, ears, and belly. Diagnosed by ruling out fleas and food allergies first. Managed with immunosuppressive medication, omega-3 supplements, and environmental modification.

Kidney Disease (Chronic Renal Failure)

Kidney disease is the leading cause of death in older cats. It is progressive, incurable, but manageable — especially when caught early through routine blood tests. Cats lose kidney function gradually, and because kidneys have a large reserve capacity, symptoms do not appear until roughly 65–75% of function is already gone.

Early Signs

  • Increased thirst and urination (drinking more, larger clumps in the litter tray)
  • Weight loss despite a normal or increased appetite
  • Mild vomiting, especially in the morning
  • Dull coat

Late Signs

  • Severe weight loss and muscle wasting
  • Mouth ulcers (toxin build-up)
  • Lethargy and weakness
  • Dehydration despite increased drinking

Management

While kidney disease can not be cured, early diagnosis and management can add years to your cat's life:

  • Prescription kidney diet (low phosphorus, moderate protein)
  • Subcutaneous fluids at home (many NZ cat owners learn this skill from their vet)
  • Regular blood work to monitor kidney values (creatinine, SDMA, and phosphate)
  • Blood pressure medication if needed (kidney disease often causes hypertension)
  • Appetite stimulants and anti-nausea medication for symptom control
Pro Tip: Ask your vet to include SDMA testing in your senior cat's annual blood panel. SDMA detects kidney disease earlier than creatinine — often when only 25–40% of function is lost, giving you much more time to manage the condition.

Feline Obesity

New Zealand has a significant cat obesity problem. Studies estimate 25–35% of NZ cats are overweight or obese. Indoor cats, neutered cats, and cats over 5 years old are at highest risk. Obesity dramatically increases the risk of diabetes, FLUTD, arthritis, and hepatic lipidosis.

Weight Loss Plan

Cats must lose weight gradually. Rapid weight loss in cats causes hepatic lipidosis, which can be fatal. Aim for 1–2% of body weight per week. Work with your vet to calculate the right calorie intake, transition to a weight management diet, and increase activity through interactive play, puzzle feeders, and vertical climbing spaces.

When to See a Vet Immediately

Some symptoms can not wait until Monday. Go to an emergency vet if your cat shows any of these signs:

  • Difficulty breathing or open-mouth breathing (not after play)
  • Straining to urinate with no output (especially male cats)
  • Sudden collapse or inability to stand
  • Seizures or disorientation
  • Prolonged vomiting or diarrhoea (more than 24 hours)
  • Not eating for more than 24 hours
  • Severe pain (hiding, hissing when touched, panting)
  • Sudden blindness or eye injury
  • Known or suspected poisoning (lilies, rat bait, antifreeze, human medications)
Lily Warning: All parts of the lily plant are extremely toxic to cats. Even licking pollen off their fur or drinking water from a lily vase can cause acute kidney failure. If you have cats, do not bring lilies into your home. This includes all Lilium and Hemerocallis species.

How a Pet Sitter Can Help Monitor Your Cat's Health

One of the biggest risks for cats when their owners travel is not a specific illness — it is the absence of early detection. Cats hide pain exceptionally well, and a pet sitter who visits daily can spot subtle changes that escalate into emergencies if ignored:

  • Changes in eating and drinking habits
  • Litter tray output changes (more or less urine, diarrhoea)
  • Hiding more than usual (a classic sign of illness in cats)
  • Sneezing, discharge, or eye changes that could indicate cat flu
  • Limping, stiffness, or reluctance to jump
