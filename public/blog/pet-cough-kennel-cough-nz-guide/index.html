Kennel Cough & Pet Respiratory Health in New Zealand

Published 23 July 2026 — ZiggySitters NZ

That honking, hacking cough your dog suddenly developed after a stay at boarding kennels or a visit to the dog park? It is probably kennel cough — the common cold of the canine world. And if your cat starts sneezing, squinting, and refusing food, it may be cat flu. Both are highly contagious, both are common in New Zealand, and both need the right response. This guide covers symptoms, treatment, prevention, and how to protect your pets — including what pet sitters and owners need to know.

What Is Kennel Cough?

Kennel cough (infectious tracheobronchitis) is a highly contagious respiratory infection that affects dogs. It is not a single disease — it is a syndrome caused by multiple viruses and bacteria working alone or together. The main culprits in New Zealand are:

  • Bordetella bronchiseptica — the most common bacterial cause
  • Canine parainfluenza virus — the most common viral cause
  • Canine adenovirus type 2
  • Canine respiratory coronavirus
  • Mycoplasma species

Most cases involve a combination of two or more of these pathogens. The infection inflames the trachea (windpipe) and bronchi, causing the characteristic dry, hacking cough.

Symptoms of Kennel Cough

Symptoms typically appear 2–14 days after exposure. The classic signs are:

  • Dry, hacking cough — often described as a “goose honk” or sounding like something is stuck in the throat
  • Gagging or retching — especially after coughing, sometimes producing white foam
  • Coughing triggered by: pulling on the lead, excitement, exercise, or pressure on the trachea
  • Clear nasal discharge — mild in uncomplicated cases
  • Normal appetite and energy — in mild cases, the dog is otherwise bright and well
When to see a vet urgently: If your dog has a cough AND any of these symptoms, skip home care and call your vet immediately:
  • Fever (rectal temperature above 39.5°C)
  • Thick yellow or green nasal discharge
  • Lethargy or loss of appetite lasting more than 24 hours
  • Difficulty breathing or rapid breathing at rest
  • Puppies under 6 months, senior dogs, or immunocompromised dogs
These signs may indicate pneumonia or a more serious secondary infection.

How Dogs Catch Kennel Cough

Kennel cough spreads through airborne droplets (coughing, sneezing), direct contact (nose-to-nose), and contaminated surfaces (shared water bowls, toys, bedding). It is incredibly contagious — a single infected dog in a group setting can pass it to most susceptible dogs within days.

High-risk environments in New Zealand include:

  • Boarding kennels and doggy daycares
  • Dog parks — especially popular ones like those in our NZ dog parks guide
  • Puppy socialisation classes
  • Grooming salons
  • Vet clinic waiting rooms (ironically)
  • Dog events and shows
  • Shared pathways and beaches with heavy dog traffic

Treatment for Kennel Cough

Mild Cases (Uncomplicated Kennel Cough)

Most healthy adult dogs recover from kennel cough on their own in 1–3 weeks, similar to a human cold. Treatment is supportive:

  • Rest: Limit exercise and excitement for 1–2 weeks. Walk on a harness (not a collar) to avoid pressing on the inflamed trachea.
  • Honey: Half a teaspoon of honey (Manuka is ideal, but any works) can soothe the throat. Give 2–3 times daily. Do not give honey to puppies under 12 months (risk of botulism, though this is rare in NZ).
  • Humidity: Use a vaporiser or take your dog into the bathroom while you shower. Warm, moist air soothes the airway.
  • Keep hydrated: Ensure fresh water is always available. Warm (not hot) broth can encourage drinking.
  • Avoid irritants: No smoke, dust, or strong fragrances in the home.

Veterinary Treatment

Your vet may prescribe:

  • Cough suppressants: For severe, non-productive coughing that prevents sleep
  • Antibiotics: If a bacterial infection (Bordetella) is confirmed or suspected, or if there are signs of secondary infection. Doxycycline is the most common choice in NZ.
  • Anti-inflammatories: To reduce airway inflammation
  • Nebulisation: In severe cases, your vet may use a nebuliser to deliver medication directly to the airway
ZiggySitters Tip: Never give human cough medicine to your dog without veterinary advice. Many contain ingredients that are toxic to dogs, including xylitol, acetaminophen, and pseudoephedrine.

Kennel Cough Vaccination in NZ

Vaccination is the most effective prevention. In New Zealand, kennel cough vaccines are available in two forms:

Vaccine TypeHow GivenProtection DurationNotes
IntranasalDrops in the nose12 monthsFastest protection (48–72 hours). Most boarding kennels require this form.
InjectableInjection under the skin12 monthsPart of the standard C5 or C5+ vaccination. Takes 2 weeks for full immunity.

NZ Vaccination Schedule

  • Puppies: First C5 vaccination at 6–8 weeks, second at 10–12 weeks, third at 14–16 weeks. Kennel cough (intranasal) can be given from 3 weeks of age.
  • Adult dogs: Annual C5 booster including kennel cough component. Intranasal kennel cough top-up may be needed separately if only the injectable form was given.
  • Before boarding: Most kennels require intranasal kennel cough vaccination at least 5–7 days before boarding. Check your kennel requirements.
Important: Vaccination does not guarantee complete immunity. It significantly reduces the severity of symptoms and the likelihood of infection, but a vaccinated dog can still contract a mild case. Think of it like the flu vaccine for humans — it makes the illness much less dangerous, not impossible.

Cat Flu (Feline Upper Respiratory Infection)

Cats have their own version of respiratory illness, commonly called “cat flu” in New Zealand. It is caused primarily by:

  • Feline herpesvirus (FHV-1) — causes up to 80% of cat flu cases
  • Feline calicivirus (FCV) — causes oral ulcers and limping syndrome
  • Bordetella bronchiseptica — yes, the same bacteria that causes kennel cough in dogs can affect cats too
  • Chlamydophila felis — causes conjunctivitis and mild respiratory signs

Symptoms of Cat Flu

  • Sneezing (repeated, frequent)
  • Clear or thick nasal discharge
  • Watery or thick eye discharge, squinting, red eyes
  • Ulcers on the tongue, gums, or nose (especially with calicivirus)
  • Fever and lethargy
  • Loss of appetite (cats that cannot smell their food often refuse to eat)
  • Drooling (due to mouth ulcers)
Cat flu can be life-threatening. Unlike kennel cough, which is usually mild in healthy adult dogs, cat flu can be serious — especially in kittens, senior cats, and unvaccinated cats. Cats can stop eating entirely, leading to hepatic lipidosis (fatty liver disease), which is itself life-threatening. If your cat shows cat flu symptoms, call your vet within 24 hours.

Cat Flu Vaccination

The standard F3 vaccine (feline enteritis + two cat flu viruses: herpesvirus and calicivirus) is part of routine cat vaccinations in NZ. Some vets recommend F4 or F5 vaccines that also cover Chlamydophila and feline leukaemia virus (FeLV).

  • Kittens: First vaccination at 6–8 weeks, then boosters every 3–4 weeks until 16 weeks of age
  • Adult cats: Annual booster (some vets extend to every 2–3 years for indoor-only cats with low risk)
  • Cat flu carriers: Cats that have had FHV-1 become lifelong carriers. The virus reactivates during stress, causing mild symptoms and viral shedding. This is why some cats seem to get colds repeatedly.

Kennel Cough and Cat Flu: What Pet Sitters Need to Know

If you are a pet sitter or you are hiring one, respiratory illness creates specific responsibilities. Here is what both sides should know:

For Pet Owners

  • Disclose illness before booking: If your pet has a cough, sneezing, or eye discharge, tell your pet sitter before the booking starts. This allows them to take precautions or, if they have other pets, to decline the booking safely.
  • Keep sick pets home: Do not send a coughing dog to daycare or a boarding kennel. Do not ask a sitter with their own pets to care for a sick animal in their home.
  • In-home sitting is safest: If your pet is recovering from kennel cough, in-home pet sitting is the best option. Your pet stays in their own environment, avoids exposing other animals, and gets one-on-one care.
  • Sanitise before and after: Wash bedding, clean food and water bowls with hot soapy water, and disinfect surfaces your pet has touched.

For Pet Sitters

  • Ask about vaccination status: It is reasonable to ask whether visiting pets are up to date on vaccinations, especially kennel cough and cat flu.
  • Wash your hands between pets: If you care for multiple animals in a day, wash your hands and change your outer layer between visits.
  • Do not share items: Do not use the same toys, bowls, or bedding between different pets.
  • Know the signs: If a pet in your care develops a cough, sneezing, or eye discharge, contact the owner and recommend a vet visit. Isolate the pet from other animals immediately.
  • After a sick pet: Disinfect all surfaces, wash all bedding, and wait at least 48 hours before caring for another pet in the same space.

Preventing Respiratory Illness: A Checklist

  1. Vaccinate on schedule. Keep C5 (dogs) and F3/F4 (cats) vaccinations current. For dogs going to boarding kennels, get the intranasal kennel cough vaccine at least 5–7 days before boarding.
  2. Avoid high-risk environments during outbreaks. If you hear that kennel cough is going around your local dog park, skip it for a week or two.
  3. Use harnesses instead of collars. Collars press on the trachea and can trigger coughing in dogs with any airway irritation.
  4. Keep shared items clean. Wash food bowls, water bowls, and toys regularly. At the dog park, avoid communal water bowls.
  5. Reduce stress. Stress weakens the immune system and reactivates latent infections (especially FHV-1 in cats). Maintain routines, avoid sudden changes, and provide quiet retreat spaces.
  6. Ensure good ventilation. Stuffy, poorly ventilated spaces increase transmission. If you run a boarding or daycare facility, invest in good air circulation.
  7. Isolate sick pets. Keep coughing dogs and sneezing cats away from other animals for at least 7–14 days after symptoms resolve.
  8. Boost immunity with good nutrition. Quality food, appropriate supplements (talk to your vet), and adequate exercise keep the immune system strong. See our pet nutrition guide for details.

Can Humans Catch Kennel Cough?

In short: it is extremely rare. Bordetella bronchiseptica can theoretically infect immunocompromised humans, but this is very uncommon. The viruses that cause kennel cough (canine parainfluenza, canine adenovirus) do not infect humans. If you have a weakened immune system and your dog has kennel cough, mention it to your doctor as a precaution, but do not panic. Practice normal hygiene (hand washing, not sharing face-to-face contact) and you will be fine.

Can Dogs and Cats Share Respiratory Infections?

Bordetella bronchiseptica can cross between dogs and cats, though it is relatively uncommon. If you have both a coughing dog and a sneezing cat in the same household, Bordetella is a possible shared cause. Most other respiratory pathogens are species-specific — canine parainfluenza does not affect cats, and feline herpesvirus does not affect dogs. However, keeping sick pets separated is always wise, regardless of species.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does kennel cough last?

Mild cases resolve in 1–3 weeks. The acute cough typically lasts 5–10 days, but a mild residual cough can linger for several weeks. If the cough is not improving after 2 weeks, or if it gets worse at any point, see your vet.

Can my dog go to daycare or kennels with kennel cough?

No. Keep your dog home until at least 7 days after the cough has completely resolved. Most boarding kennels and daycares in NZ require a vet clearance certificate before readmitting a dog that has had kennel cough.

Is kennel cough seasonal?

In New Zealand, kennel cough peaks in late autumn and winter (May–August) when dogs spend more time indoors in close quarters. However, it can occur year-round, especially in areas with high dog density like Auckland and Wellington.

My vaccinated dog got kennel cough anyway. Why?

Vaccination reduces severity and duration but does not provide 100% protection. There are multiple strains of kennel cough, and the vaccine covers the most common ones. A vaccinated dog that catches kennel cough typically has a much milder case than an unvaccinated dog — less coughing, faster recovery, and lower risk of complications.

My cat keeps getting colds. What can I do?

Recurring colds in cats usually indicate a latent feline herpesvirus (FHV-1) infection. The virus reactivates during stress, illness, or weather changes. Management includes: stress reduction, L-lysine supplementation (talk to your vet about dosage), prompt vet visits at the first sign of symptoms, and keeping vaccinations current. Some cats benefit from antiviral medication during flare-ups.

Should I cancel my pet sitter if my pet is sick?

Yes — call your sitter immediately. A responsible sitter will appreciate knowing. If your pet has a contagious respiratory illness, the safest option is in-home care by someone who does not have other pets, or a vet clinic boarding arrangement for severe cases.
