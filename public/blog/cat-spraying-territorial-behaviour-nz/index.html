Cat Spraying & Territorial Behaviour: Solutions for New Zealand Cat Owners

Walking into a room and catching that unmistakable smell of cat urine on your curtains, walls, or furniture is frustrating, confusing, and honestly pretty gross. But before you get angry with your cat, it helps to understand what is actually happening. Spraying is not a litter box problem. It is a communication behaviour, and your cat is trying to tell you something. Understanding the message is the first step to stopping it.

Spraying vs. Inappropriate Urination: Know the Difference

These are two different problems with different solutions, so getting this right matters.

What Spraying Looks Like

  • Your cat stands upright, usually with their tail quivering or raised high
  • Small amounts of urine are deposited on vertical surfaces: walls, doors, curtains, furniture corners
  • The cat may knead their paws or make a treading motion with their back feet
  • The urine has a strong, pungent odour because it contains extra pheromones from the anal glands
  • Cats often return to the same spot to re-spray

What Inappropriate Urination Looks Like

  • Your cat squats to urinate on horizontal surfaces: beds, carpets, laundry baskets, bath mats
  • Larger volumes of urine, similar to what you would find in a litter box
  • The cat may seem to strain or visit the litter box frequently without producing much
  • Often linked to urinary tract issues, litter box aversion, or stress

If your cat is squatting on horizontal surfaces, start with a vet check for urinary tract problems before treating it as a behavioural issue. Spraying on vertical surfaces is almost always behavioural or territorial.

Why Cats Spray: The Underlying Causes

1. Territorial Marking

Cats are territorial by nature. In the wild, they mark their territory to tell other cats "I live here" and to create a familiar scent environment that makes them feel secure. Indoor cats still have this instinct, and it can be triggered by perceived intruders.

In New Zealand, the most common territorial trigger is neighbourhood cats. If a strange cat walks past your window, sits on your fence, or sprays near your door, your indoor cat may respond by spraying inside to reinforce their claim on the territory. This is incredibly common in NZ suburbs where sections are close together and cats roam freely.

2. Stress and Anxiety

Any change to your cat\'s environment can trigger spraying as a self-soothing mechanism. Common stressors include:

  • Moving house (one of the top triggers for spraying)
  • New pets or people in the household
  • Changes to routine (different feeding times, new work schedule)
  • Building work or renovations in or near the home
  • Absence of a family member (including you travelling)
  • Conflict with another cat in the household

3. Mating Behaviour

Unneutered cats, both male and female, spray as part of mating behaviour. Entire males are the most prolific sprayers. If your cat is not desexed, this is very likely the cause. The good news is that desexing resolves spraying in approximately 90% of males and 95% of females when done before the behaviour becomes habitual.

4. Inadequate Resources in Multi-Cat Homes

In households with more than one cat, spraying often reflects competition over resources. If there are not enough litter boxes, feeding stations, scratching posts, or safe resting spots, cats may spray to claim territory around the limited resources.

Step-by-Step Solutions for Cat Spraying

Step 1: Rule Out Medical Issues

Book a vet appointment first, especially if the spraying started suddenly or is accompanied by other changes like increased thirst, frequent litter box visits, or blood in urine. Urinary tract infections, bladder stones, and kidney disease can all cause urination problems that look like spraying to an untrained eye. Your vet can also check for diabetes and hyperthyroidism, both common in older NZ cats.

Step 2: Neuter or Spay

If your cat is not desexed, this is the single most effective intervention. Talk to your vet about timing. In New Zealand, the SPCA and many vets advocate for desexing at around 4-5 months, before sexual maturity. If your adult cat is already spraying, desexing is still effective, but the longer the behaviour has been established, the longer it may take to resolve.

Step 3: Clean Sprayed Areas Properly

This step is critical and most people get it wrong. Standard household cleaners do not remove the pheromones that tell your cat "this is my spot, I should mark it again."

  • Use an enzymatic cleaner specifically designed for cat urine: Products containing enzymes break down the uric acid crystals that cause the persistent odour. Regular bleach and ammonia-based cleaners actually make the smell worse to a cat\'s nose because urine contains ammonia.
  • Saturate the area: Spray enough enzymatic cleaner to reach the same depth the urine penetrated. On carpets, this means soaking through to the underlay.
  • Let it work: Enzymatic cleaners need time, usually 10-15 minutes of contact. Do not wipe them off immediately.
  • Avoid steam cleaning: Heat sets the proteins in urine, making the odour permanent. Never steam clean a urine spot before treating it enzymatically.
  • Rinse with water after treatment: Once the enzymes have done their job, rinse the area with clean water and blot dry.

Step 4: Block Access to Trigger Areas

If your cat sprays on specific spots (curtains, a particular wall, near doors), physically block access while you work on the underlying cause.

  • Close doors to rooms where spraying occurs
  • Use double-sided sticky tape or aluminium foil on favourite spraying surfaces (cats dislike the texture)
  • Move furniture away from windows where outdoor cats are visible
  • Apply scent deterrents: citrus sprays, eucalyptus oil, or commercial cat repellent on cleaned areas

Step 5: Reduce Territorial Triggers

If neighbourhood cats are the trigger, make your property less appealing to them and less stressful for your cat.

  • Block visual access: Close blinds or curtains in rooms where your cat can see outdoor cats. Even a temporary window film can break the visual trigger cycle.
  • Use motion-activated deterrents: Devices like the ScareCrow sprinkler deter outdoor cats from entering your property.
  • Clean outdoor spraying: If you find cat urine marks around your doors, fences, or garden, clean those with enzymatic cleaner too. Your indoor cat can smell outdoor marks through walls and doors.

Step 6: Increase Resources in Multi-Cat Homes

The magic number for litter boxes in multi-cat households is one per cat plus one extra. So two cats need three boxes, three cats need four. This is non-negotiable for preventing spraying.

Also ensure each cat has their own:

  • Feeding station (in a separate area from other cats)
  • Water bowl
  • Scratching post or pad
  • Elevated resting spot (cats feel safer when they can observe from above)
  • Safe retreat space where other cats cannot follow

Step 7: Use Pheromone Therapy

Feliway is a synthetic feline facial pheromone that mimics the "happy markers" cats leave when they rub their cheeks on furniture and people. Plugging a Feliway diffuser into the room where your cat sprays can help them feel the territory is already marked, reducing the urge to spray. It is available from most NZ vets and pet stores and is well-supported by clinical evidence.

Step 8: Increase Play and Enrichment

Stressed, bored cats spray more. Daily interactive play sessions (10-15 minutes, twice a day) with a wand toy or laser pointer can significantly reduce anxiety. Puzzle feeders, catios, and window perches also provide environmental enrichment that lowers stress and territorial drive.

Special Situations

When You Travel: Pet Sitting and Spraying

Many NZ cat owners discover their cat sprays for the first time when they go on holiday. The absence of their primary person, combined with a stranger in the home (or the cat being moved to a boarding cattery), creates a perfect storm of stress and territorial insecurity.

  • Providing familiar human presence so your cat does not feel abandoned
  • Keeping the home environment stable (no new smells, sounds, or surroundings)
  • Cleaning any spraying quickly with enzymatic products to prevent re-marking

Introducing a New Cat

Introducing a new cat to a household with an existing cat is one of the most common triggers for spraying in both cats. The key is a slow, structured introduction over 1-2 weeks:

  1. Keep the cats in separate rooms with separate resources
  2. Swap bedding between rooms so they learn each other\'s scent
  3. Feed them on opposite sides of a closed door
  4. Introduce visual contact through a baby gate or cracked door
  5. Supervise face-to-face meetings in short sessions
  6. Have Feliway plugged in from day one

After Moving House

Moving is hugely stressful for cats because every scent that told them "this is home" is suddenly gone. To reduce post-move spraying:

  • Confine your cat to one room for the first few days with their familiar belongings
  • Plug in Feliway before you bring the cat inside
  • Do not wash their bedding for the first week (the familiar scent is comforting)
  • Gradually give them access to more rooms as they settle
  • Keep them indoors for at least 2-3 weeks after moving to prevent them trying to return to the old house

When Spraying Does Not Improve

If you have followed all the steps above for 4-6 weeks with no improvement, or if the spraying is severe and causing significant household stress, consult a veterinary behaviourist. In New Zealand, you can ask your vet for a referral to a specialist, or contact the NZ Companion Animal Council for recommendations.

In some cases, anti-anxiety medication prescribed by a vet can be a useful bridge while behaviour modification takes effect. This is not a sign of failure. Some cats, like some people, need medical support to manage anxiety.

Key Takeaways

  • Spraying (vertical surfaces, small amounts) is different from inappropriate urination (horizontal surfaces, larger volumes). Identify which one you are dealing with.
  • Always start with a vet check to rule out medical causes, especially urinary tract issues.
  • Desexing resolves spraying in the vast majority of entire cats.
  • Enzymatic cleaning is essential. Regular cleaners do not remove the pheromones that cause re-marking.
  • Neighbourhood cats are the top territorial trigger for NZ indoor cats. Block visual access and clean outdoor marks.
  • Multi-cat households need one litter box per cat plus one extra, plus separate resources for each cat.
  • Feliway and daily play sessions are simple, evidence-based interventions that make a real difference.
  • If you are travelling, an in-home pet sitter provides routine and presence that can prevent stress-related spraying.

Cat spraying is a problem with solutions. With patience, the right approach, and consistency, most cats stop spraying entirely or reduce it to a manageable level.
