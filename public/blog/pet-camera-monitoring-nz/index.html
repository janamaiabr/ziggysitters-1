Pet Cameras & Remote Monitoring in NZ: The Complete Guide for Pet Owners

Published 9 July 2026 • 9 min read • ZiggySitters NZ

You are at work, your dog is at home, and the question gnaws at you: are they okay? Pet cameras have gone from luxury gadgets to genuinely useful tools for New Zealand pet owners who want eyes on their animals from anywhere. But which camera is worth buying in NZ, and can a gadget really replace a person walking through your door? This guide covers the best pet cameras available in New Zealand, how to set them up properly, and why the smartest pet owners use cameras alongside professional pet sitting — not instead of it.

Why Pet Cameras Matter for NZ Pet Owners

New Zealanders work some of the longest hours in the OECD. If you commute from West Auckland into the city, your dog could be alone for 10 hours. A pet camera lets you check in, talk to them, and even toss a treat — all from your desk.

But cameras are not just for working owners. They are essential when:

  • You have a pet sitter and want to confirm visits happened on time
  • Your pet is recovering from surgery and needs monitoring
  • You have a senior pet with health conditions that could flare up
  • Your pet suffers from separation anxiety and you need to track behaviour patterns
  • You are travelling within NZ and want to check on the house sitter
ZiggySitters Tip: A pet camera is most powerful when paired with a real person. Use the camera to verify your sitter is following instructions, not to skip hiring one. A camera cannot give your cat medication or comfort a frightened dog during a Wellington southerly.

Setting Up Your Pet Camera: A Practical Guide

1. Placement Matters More Than You Think

Where you put the camera determines what you actually see:

  • Dogs: Mount it where your dog spends most alone time — usually the living room or kitchen. Angle it to cover their bed and the door they pace near.
  • Cats: Cats roam. Place one camera near their favourite window perch and another near food/litter. A single camera in one room will miss 70% of what your cat does.
  • Outdoor areas: Use a weatherproof camera (like the Tapo C320WS) to monitor the yard, especially if your dog has outdoor access through a dog door.

2. Wi-Fi Is Your Biggest Bottleneck

New Zealand internet varies wildly. A 1080p camera stream needs at least 2 Mbps upload. If you are on rural broadband or an older Fibre plan, your camera may lag, drop frames, or fail to send bark alerts on time.

  • Fibre users: You are fine. Most NZ fibre plans have 100+ Mbps upload.
  • VDSL users: Usually fine, but test during peak hours (7–9 PM).
  • Rural wireless / Starlink: Position your camera close to the router or use a Wi-Fi extender. Expect some latency on treat-toss features.

3. Privacy Settings You Should Change Immediately

Critical: Pet cameras are internet-connected devices. Default settings are often designed for convenience, not security.
  • Change the default password immediately — not just the Wi-Fi password, the camera admin password
  • Enable two-factor authentication on the camera app
  • Turn off public sharing features
  • Review what data the camera sends to the cloud — some cameras upload continuously, others only on motion
  • If your camera supports local storage (microSD), use it as primary and cloud as backup

What Pet Cameras Can and Cannot Do

What Cameras Do Well

  • Confirm routine: See that your pet is eating, drinking, and sleeping normally
  • Two-way audio: Calm an anxious pet with your voice (works best with dogs; cats mostly ignore you)
  • Treat tossing: Reward good behaviour or lure a hiding pet into view
  • Bark/meow alerts: Get notified when your pet is vocal, which can signal distress
  • Verify sitter visits: See your pet sitter arrive, feed, and interact with your pet

What Cameras Cannot Do

  • Administer medication: A camera will not give your cat its pill or apply your dog eye drops
  • Comfort during storms: Your voice through a tinny speaker will not replace a person sitting next to a terrified dog during a thunderstorm
  • Respond to emergencies: If your pet collapses, a camera can show you the problem but cannot drive to your house
  • Provide exercise: Treat tossing is enrichment, not exercise
  • Handle escape artists: If your dog bolts out the gate, a camera just records it happening
The Winning Combo: Use a pet camera to verify your sitter and check in between visits. Your pet gets human interaction, exercise, and care from the sitter — and you get the reassurance of seeing it happen. This is the setup every NZ pet owner should aim for.

Using Pet Cameras With a Professional Pet Sitter

If you hire a sitter through ZiggySitters, here is how to make cameras work for everyone:

For Pet Owners

  • Tell your sitter about the camera. This is not just polite — in New Zealand, the Privacy Act 2020 means you should inform anyone being recorded in your home. It also builds trust: a good sitter has nothing to hide.
  • Do not micromanage. Watching every visit live and texting corrections mid-visit undermines the sitter and creates stress for your pet. Check highlights, not every minute.
  • Share camera access thoughtfully. Some apps let you share a view-only link. Give your sitter access so they can show you they did the job — transparency builds trust.
  • Use alerts, not constant monitoring. Set up bark alerts and activity notifications. If nothing triggers, your pet is probably fine.

For Pet Sitters

  • Expect cameras. They are standard in NZ homes now. Do not be offended — it is about the pet, not you.
  • Wave at the camera when you arrive. It takes two seconds and shows the owner you are professional and transparent.
  • Send a photo or short video through the ZiggySitters app anyway. Camera footage is low-quality compared to a proper photo, and the personal touch matters.

NZ-Specific Considerations

Power Outages

New Zealand is not famous for stable power. Rural areas in particular can have outages lasting hours. If your camera is plug-in only, it dies with the power. Solutions:

  • Choose cameras with battery backup or use a small UPS (uninterruptible power supply) — available at Noel Leeming for $80–$150
  • Set your camera to notify you when it goes offline, not just when it detects motion
  • Make sure your Wi-Fi router is also on the UPS, or the camera backup is useless

Internet Data Caps

If you are on a capped plan (common with Skinny Broadband, Orcon, and some Spark plans), a 1080p camera streaming 24/7 uses roughly 50–80 GB per month. That can blow through a 200 GB cap fast. Options:

  • Set cameras to record only on motion or pet detection, not continuously
  • Use local microSD storage as primary, cloud as backup
  • Reduce streaming quality to 720p when you are just checking in casually

Legal: Recording in Your Own Home

Under NZ law, you can record inside your own home. However:

  • You must not record audio of visitors without their knowledge (Crimes Act 1961, s216)
  • Cameras must not capture areas where visitors have a reasonable expectation of privacy (bathrooms, bedrooms where a sitter sleeps)
  • Inform your pet sitter in writing that cameras are present — include it in your welcome sheet
Legal Note: Pointing a camera at the room where your overnight sitter sleeps is a privacy violation. Cameras should cover common areas (living room, kitchen, hallway) and outdoor entrances only. When in doubt, ask your sitter what they are comfortable with.

Pet Camera Red Flags: When the Camera Tells You Something Is Wrong

Your camera is not just for reassurance. It can alert you to real problems:

  • Dog pacing for 3+ hours: Possible separation anxiety — time to consider a midday dog walk or pet sitter visit
  • Cat not eating for 24+ hours: Cats stop eating quickly when stressed or ill — call your vet
  • Excessive barking alerts: If your dog barks every 15 minutes, they are distressed, not just vocal
  • Pet sitting visit not happening: If the camera shows no one came during a scheduled visit, contact your sitter immediately and then the ZiggySitters support team
  • Water bowl untouched all day: Dehydration risk, especially in Auckland summers

The Bottom Line: Camera + Sitter = Peace of Mind

A pet camera tells you what is happening. A pet sitter does something about it. The best setup for your pet combines both:

  • Camera gives you real-time visibility between sitter visits
  • Sitter provides exercise, companionship, medication, and emergency response
  • Together, they give you complete confidence that your pet is cared for
