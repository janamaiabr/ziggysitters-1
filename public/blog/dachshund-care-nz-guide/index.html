Dachshund Care Guide NZ: Everything You Need to Know About Your Sausage Dog

By ZiggySitters • March 22, 2026 • 12 min read

The quick version: Dachshunds are bold, affectionate, and surprisingly stubborn little dogs with specific care needs — especially around their backs. This guide covers everything New Zealand owners need to know: exercise, diet, spinal health, grooming, and how to choose a sitter who truly understands the breed.

Walk through any neighbourhood in Auckland, Wellington, or Christchurch and you are almost guaranteed to spot one: that low-slung silhouette, nose to the ground, trotting purposefully as though on a mission only they fully understand. Dachshunds — affectionately known as sausage dogs, wieners, or dachsies — are one of New Zealand's most popular small breeds, and for good reason.

They are charming, loyal, entertaining, and possess a confidence wildly out of proportion to their size. They are also a breed with some very specific needs that first-time owners often underestimate. Get dachshund care right and you will have a devoted companion for 12 to 16 years. Get it wrong and you can end up with a dog in pain, a frustrated household, or an eye-watering vet bill.

Let's make sure you get it right.

Understanding the Dachshund: A Brief History

The dachshund was bred in Germany over 300 years ago specifically to hunt badgers underground. Their name literally means “badger dog” in German. Those short legs and elongated bodies were engineered for crawling into burrows; that deep chest housed impressive lung capacity for sustained effort; and that loud, booming bark was designed to alert hunters above ground.

All of this matters for modern care because your suburban sausage dog still carries that hunting heritage in every cell. They dig. They chase. They follow scent trails with single-minded obsession. They are surprisingly tough and athletic. And they have a stubborn, independent streak that served them well when working alone underground, but can make training a test of patience.

There are three coat varieties — smooth, long-haired, and wire-haired — and two sizes: standard (typically 7–14 kg) and miniature (under 5 kg). In New Zealand, both sizes are popular, with miniatures particularly favoured for apartment and townhouse living.

The Most Important Thing: Back Health and IVDD

⚠ Priority one for every dachshund owner: Intervertebral Disc Disease (IVDD) affects approximately 25% of dachshunds at some point in their lives. Understanding it is non-negotiable.

IVDD occurs when one or more of the discs between the vertebrae of the spine harden and eventually rupture, pressing on the spinal cord. Because dachshunds have unusually long spines relative to their leg length, they are far more susceptible than most breeds.

Symptoms range from reluctance to move or crying out in pain, to partial or full paralysis of the hindquarters. In severe cases, surgery costing $5,000–$10,000+ NZD may be required. Many cases that are caught early can be managed with strict rest and anti-inflammatory medication, but prevention is always better.

How to Protect Your Dachshund's Back

  • Install ramps or steps to furniture and the car. Jumping down from the couch or car seat is one of the most common triggers of IVDD episodes. Dachshund-specific ramps are widely available from NZ pet stores.
  • Keep them lean. Every extra gram of body weight puts additional strain on the spine. A dachshund at healthy weight has a visible waist when viewed from above and ribs you can feel without pressing hard.
  • No high-impact jumping. Avoid fetch games that involve leaping, and do not encourage jumping up at people.
  • Support the whole body when picking up. Always place one hand under the chest and one under the hindquarters. Never pick up a dachshund by the front legs or let them dangle.
  • Watch for early warning signs: reluctance to go up stairs, sensitivity to touch along the back, a hunched posture, a wobbly gait, or crying when moving. If you notice any of these, see a vet promptly.
  • Consider pet insurance. IVDD treatment is expensive. Many NZ owners find that insurance pays for itself with just one episode.

Exercise: Getting the Balance Right

Despite their small size, dachshunds need regular, meaningful exercise — but it needs to be the right kind. Their build does not suit high-impact activities like agility jumping, sustained uphill running, or long-distance jogging on hard surfaces.

Feeding Your Dachshund in NZ

Weight management is not optional for dachshunds — it is spinal health care. Follow these guidelines to keep your dog in ideal condition.

Choosing the Right Food

A high-quality dry kibble formulated for small breeds works well for most dachshunds. Look for real meat as the first ingredient, minimal fillers, and appropriate fat levels for your dog's age and activity level. Several good options are widely available at NZ pet stores including Animates and PetShed, as well as online at pet supply retailers.

Raw feeding (BARF — biologically appropriate raw food) is popular with many NZ dachshund owners and can work well when properly balanced, but requires research and care to ensure nutritional completeness. If you go this route, consult your vet or a canine nutritionist.

Portion Control

Dachshunds are food-motivated almost beyond reason. They will convince you they are starving at every meal, beg relentlessly, and attempt to steal from other pets. Do not be deceived. Follow the feeding guide on your chosen food as a starting point, then adjust based on your dog's body condition. Most adult standard dachshunds need around 180–250g of dry food per day split across two meals. Miniatures need considerably less.

Tip: Use a slow feeder bowl to prevent gulping, which can contribute to bloating and digestive discomfort. Puzzle feeders also double as mental stimulation — two problems solved at once.

Foods to Avoid

Standard canine no-list applies: no grapes or raisins, no onions or garlic, no macadamia nuts, no chocolate, no xylitol (found in some peanut butters and sugar-free products). New Zealand homes often have feijoas, tamarillo, and kiwifruit — keep these out of reach as they can cause digestive upset in some dogs.

Grooming by Coat Type

Grooming needs vary significantly by coat variety.

Smooth-Coated Dachshunds

The lowest maintenance of the three types. A weekly wipe-down with a damp cloth or grooming mitt keeps the coat gleaming. Baths every 4–6 weeks or as needed. Check and clean ears regularly as their floppy ears trap moisture and can be prone to infection.

Long-Haired Dachshunds

Need brushing 3–4 times per week to prevent matting, particularly around the ears, chest, and legs. Professional grooming every 8–12 weeks helps maintain coat health and shape. New Zealand's humid summers mean extra attention to keeping the coat clean and free of grass seeds.

Wire-Haired Dachshunds

Require hand-stripping (pulling out the dead outer coat) twice yearly to maintain proper texture. A professional groomer experienced with wire-haired breeds is essential. Between strippings, regular brushing prevents tangling of the softer undercoat.

All Varieties

Clip nails every 3–4 weeks. Long nails change the way the foot sits and can affect gait and spinal alignment — particularly important for a breed already prone to back problems. Brush teeth regularly and introduce dental chews; dachshunds can be prone to periodontal disease.

Training and Mental Stimulation

Dachshunds are intelligent dogs that trained poorly tend to become wilful, destructive, or incessant barkers. The key is understanding that they were bred to work independently and make their own decisions underground. They are not naturally deferential the way a Labrador or Border Collie might be.

Positive reinforcement works extremely well. Food reward training clicks immediately with most dachshunds given their food motivation. Keep sessions short (5–10 minutes), end on a success, and be patient with recall — once a scent trail captures their attention, it genuinely overrides everything else.

Crate training from puppyhood sets a strong foundation. Dachshunds that have their own comfortable den tend to be less anxious and destructive when left alone. Use the crate positively, never as punishment.

Mental stimulation is as important as physical exercise. Puzzle feeders, sniff games, Kong toys, and training sessions all satisfy the dachshund's busy brain. A bored dachshund will redecorate your garden whether you asked for that or not.

Common Health Considerations in NZ

Beyond IVDD, dachshunds in New Zealand are predisposed to a few conditions worth knowing about:

  • Dental disease: Small breeds are universally prone to periodontal problems. Daily teeth brushing is ideal; dental chews and water additives help if brushing is resisted.
  • Obesity: See above. Worth repeating: a fat dachshund is a dachshund at serious risk of spinal injury.
  • Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA): An inherited eye condition leading to eventual blindness. Reputable NZ breeders screen for this.
  • Ear infections: Those long floppy ears create a warm, moist environment that bacteria and yeast love. Weekly ear checks and gentle cleaning with an appropriate ear solution prevents most infections.
  • Cushing's disease: More common in middle-aged to older dachshunds than in many other breeds. Watch for increased thirst, appetite, pot-bellied appearance, and thinning coat in dogs over 6.

Dachshunds and NZ Living: City, Suburb, and Beyond

One of the great things about dachshunds is how adaptable they are to different living situations, provided their core needs are met.

Apartment and townhouse living suits dachshunds reasonably well. They are small, moderately active, and happy to snuggle indoors. The main challenges are their tendency to bark (which neighbours will notice) and the need to manage stairs — use a carrier or ramp for puppies and dogs with any back history.

Suburban homes with yards are ideal. A secure fence is essential — dachshunds are escape artists who will dig under gaps, squeeze through surprising spaces, and then disappear down the street following a scent trail. Check your fence regularly.

Rural properties work well if managed carefully. Open paddocks create rabbit-chasing temptation, and rough terrain increases injury risk. Many country dachshunds develop a healthy outdoor life, but supervision is important.

Finding the Right Pet Sitter for Your Dachshund

When you need to travel or cannot be home, finding a sitter who genuinely understands dachshund needs makes a significant difference to your dog's wellbeing — and to your peace of mind.

What to Look For

  • Knowledge of IVDD risks: Your sitter should understand that dachshunds cannot jump freely from furniture or the car, and should actively prevent this.
  • Correct handling: Picking up a dachshund correctly (supporting the full length of the body) protects the spine. Any sitter unfamiliar with this needs to be briefed and willing to follow your instructions.
  • Appropriate exercise: Short walks, not high-energy rough-housing with larger dogs.
  • Feeding discipline: Dachshunds will work every angle to get extra food. Your sitter needs to follow your feeding instructions strictly and not be won over by those enormous eyes.
  • Understanding of the breed's stubbornness: A sitter who gets frustrated when the dachshund ignores them outdoors, or who tries to force compliance, will have a difficult time. An experienced sitter knows to use positive redirection and patience.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much exercise does a dachshund need in NZ?

Dachshunds do best with 30 to 45 minutes of moderate exercise daily, ideally split across two shorter walks. Avoid high-impact activities that stress their long spine. Sniff walks on soft surfaces are ideal.

Are dachshunds good for NZ apartments?

Yes, dachshunds can thrive in apartments as long as they get daily walks and mental stimulation. Their small size suits apartment life well, though they can be vocal — check body corporate rules and work on barking management from puppyhood.

What is IVDD and how do I prevent it in my dachshund?

IVDD (Intervertebral Disc Disease) is a spinal condition that affects around one in four dachshunds. Prevention focuses on: keeping your dog at a healthy weight, installing ramps to furniture and the car, avoiding high jumps, and supporting the full body when picking them up. Prompt vet attention at the first sign of back pain is essential.

How do I find a dachshund-experienced pet sitter in NZ?

Look for a sitter who understands dachshund back health and has experience with the breed's independent temperament. ZiggySitters connects you with vetted, breed-aware sitters across New Zealand — from Auckland to Christchurch and everywhere in between.

Do dachshunds get along with other pets?

Most dachshunds get along well with other dogs when properly socialised, though their strong prey drive means smaller animals like rabbits, guinea pigs, and birds should be kept safely separate. Cats they have grown up with are usually tolerated well; new cats may trigger chasing instincts.
