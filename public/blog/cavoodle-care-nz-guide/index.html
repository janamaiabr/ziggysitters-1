Cavoodle Care Guide NZ 2026

Published by ZiggySitters • March 2026 • 10 min read

The Cavoodle has quietly become New Zealand’s most popular dog breed. With their teddy-bear looks, gentle temperament, and low-shedding coats, it is easy to see why Kiwi families have fallen head over heels for them. But behind all that cuteness is a dog with specific care needs that every owner — and every pet sitter — should understand.

What Is a Cavoodle?

A Cavoodle (also known as a Cavapoo in some countries) is a cross between a Cavalier King Charles Spaniel and either a Miniature or Toy Poodle. First bred in Australia in the 1990s, the Cavoodle was designed to combine the Cavalier’s famously gentle and affectionate nature with the Poodle’s low-shedding, hypoallergenic coat and high intelligence.

In New Zealand, Cavoodles are found in homes ranging from inner-city Auckland apartments to sprawling Waikato lifestyle blocks. Their adaptability is one of their greatest strengths — but it also means that owners need to tailor care to the dog in front of them rather than relying on breed generalisations alone.

Cavoodle Quick Facts
  • Weight: 5–12 kg depending on Poodle parent size
  • Lifespan: 12–15 years
  • Coat: Wavy to curly, low-shedding
  • Energy level: Moderate — playful but not hyperactive
  • Good with kids: Yes, excellent family dog
  • Good with other pets: Generally yes, early socialisation helps
  • Apartment-friendly: Yes, with adequate exercise

Temperament and Personality

Cavoodles are people dogs through and through. They thrive on human companionship and are at their happiest when they are part of the action — whether that is sitting on the couch during a movie, following you around the kitchen, or playing in the garden with children.

This social nature is a joy, but it comes with a caveat: Cavoodles are prone to separation anxiety. A dog that has been bred to bond deeply with humans will naturally struggle when left alone for long periods. This is one of the most common challenges Cavoodle owners in New Zealand face, and it is a key reason why so many choose in-home pet sitting over boarding kennels.

Sitter Tip: Always let your Cavoodle’s sitter know about their routine, favourite toys, and any comfort objects. A familiar blanket or soft toy can make a significant difference when their owner is away.

Exercise Requirements

Cavoodles have moderate exercise needs that suit most New Zealand lifestyles. As a general guideline:

  • Adult Cavoodles (1–7 years): 30–45 minutes of exercise per day, split across two walks
  • Puppies (under 12 months): Use the five-minute rule — five minutes of structured exercise per month of age, twice a day. Puppies’ joints are still developing, and overexercising can cause long-term damage.
  • Senior Cavoodles (8+ years): Shorter, gentler walks of 20–30 minutes. Watch for signs of fatigue or joint stiffness.

New Zealand offers some wonderful walking options for Cavoodles. Most are social dogs who enjoy meeting other dogs and people on the trail, making them ideal companions for the many dog-friendly parks and coastal walkways found across the country. Off-leash time in a secure area is valuable for burning energy and mental stimulation.

Summer Warning: Cavoodles with curly or thick coats can overheat quickly. In Auckland, Northland, and the Waikato during summer, exercise them early in the morning or in the evening. Always bring water on walks.

Grooming: The Cavoodle’s Biggest Commitment

The Cavoodle’s coat is arguably their most beautiful feature — and their most demanding. Unlike a Labrador who needs minimal grooming, a Cavoodle requires consistent maintenance to keep their coat healthy and mat-free.

Daily Brushing

Cavoodles need brushing every one to two days, even if they are heading to the groomer regularly. The areas most prone to matting are behind the ears, under the armpits, and around the collar. Use a slicker brush or a comb designed for wavy or curly coats. Starting this routine when your Cavoodle is a puppy makes the experience much less stressful for everyone.

Professional Grooming

Most Cavoodle owners in New Zealand book professional grooming appointments every 6–8 weeks. This includes a bath, blowdry, trim, nail clip, and ear clean. The cost typically ranges from $80–$130 NZD depending on coat condition and the groomer’s location. Regular grooming not only keeps the coat manageable but also allows the groomer to spot early signs of skin conditions, ear infections, or lumps.

Ear Care

Both the Cavalier and the Poodle are prone to ear problems, and Cavoodles inherit this tendency. Their floppy ears create a warm, moist environment where bacteria and yeast can thrive. Check ears weekly for redness, odour, or excessive wax. Ask your vet to show you the correct way to clean your dog’s ears at home — incorrect cleaning can do more harm than good.

Nutrition and Feeding

Cavoodles do well on high-quality commercial dog food tailored to small or medium breeds. Whether you choose dry kibble, wet food, raw, or a combination depends on your dog’s health, your budget, and your vet’s guidance.

Life Stage Feeding Frequency Key Nutritional Focus
Puppy (up to 12 months) 3–4 meals per day High protein, calcium for bone development
Adult (1–7 years) 2 meals per day Balanced macronutrients, weight management
Senior (8+ years) 2 meals per day Joint support (glucosamine), lower calories

Cavoodles can be prone to weight gain, particularly as they age or if they are not getting enough exercise. Measure portions rather than free-feeding, and resist the urge to share table scraps. Obesity in small dogs accelerates joint problems and puts strain on the heart — a particular concern given the Cavalier heritage.

Foods to Avoid: Grapes and raisins (toxic to dogs), onions and garlic, macadamia nuts, xylitol (found in some peanut butters and sugar-free products), chocolate, and avocado. New Zealand homes often have these in the kitchen — keep them out of reach.

Health Considerations for Cavoodles in NZ

Hybrid breeds like the Cavoodle often benefit from what breeders call “hybrid vigour” — a tendency for mixed-breed dogs to be healthier than their purebred parents. That said, Cavoodles can still inherit health conditions from both the Cavalier and Poodle lines. Being aware of these helps you catch problems early.

Heart Conditions

Cavalier King Charles Spaniels have one of the highest rates of Mitral Valve Disease (MVD) of any breed, and this can be passed on to Cavoodles. Reputable Cavoodle breeders in New Zealand should be able to provide cardiac health certificates for both parent dogs. Annual vet checks that include a heart auscultation are important, especially as your dog ages.

Syringomyelia

Another condition inherited from the Cavalier side, Syringomyelia (SM) is a neurological condition where fluid-filled cavities develop in the spinal cord. Symptoms include scratching at the neck or shoulders (often in the air), sensitivity around the head and neck, and in severe cases, difficulty walking. If you notice these signs, consult your vet promptly.

Eye Conditions

Both parent breeds can carry genes for eye conditions including Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA). Ask your breeder about eye testing on parent dogs, and schedule annual eye checks with your vet.

Patellar Luxation

Small-breed dogs, including Cavoodles, are susceptible to patellar luxation — where the kneecap slips out of position. Signs include occasional skipping or holding up a back leg. Mild cases may need no treatment; severe cases can require surgery.

New Zealand Vet Tip: Register with a vet as soon as you bring your Cavoodle home, not just when they are sick. Building a relationship with your vet makes it easier to catch issues early and get prompt advice when concerns arise.

Training Your Cavoodle

Cavoodles are highly intelligent and eager to please, which makes them a genuine joy to train. They respond extremely well to positive reinforcement — treats, praise, and play. Harsh corrections or punishment-based methods are not only unnecessary but can damage the trust-based relationship that makes this breed so endearing.

Key areas to focus on early:

  • Basic obedience: Sit, stay, down, come, and leave it. These commands are safety essentials, not just tricks.
  • Loose-leash walking: Cavoodles can be pullers despite their small size. Start leash training from day one.
  • Socialisation: Expose your puppy to different people, dogs, environments, and sounds during the critical window of 3–16 weeks. Puppy preschool classes are widely available across New Zealand and offer a great structured start.
  • Alone time training: Given their separation anxiety tendencies, gradually teaching your Cavoodle to be comfortable alone is one of the most important investments you can make in their long-term wellbeing.

Managing Separation Anxiety

Separation anxiety is one of the most common challenges Cavoodle owners in New Zealand contact us about. Signs include barking or howling when left alone, destructive behaviour, toileting inside despite being house-trained, and pacing or refusing to eat.

Strategies that help:

  • Start with very short absences (even just leaving the room) and build up gradually
  • Create a safe, comfortable space — a crate with familiar-smelling bedding can become a positive sanctuary
  • Avoid dramatic departures and arrivals, which can inadvertently increase arousal
  • Provide enrichment before you leave: a stuffed Kong, a sniff walk, or a puzzle feeder
  • Consider a dog walker or pet sitter for days when you will be away for more than four to five hours
  • In severe cases, consult a veterinary behaviourist — medication combined with behaviour modification can make a significant difference

Cavoodles and Pet Sitting: What to Look For

Because Cavoodles bond so deeply with their humans, choosing the right pet care arrangement when you travel is particularly important. Here is what to consider:

In-Home Pet Sitting vs. Kennels

Most Cavoodles do significantly better with in-home care — either a sitter who comes to your home or one who takes a small number of dogs into their own home — compared to a commercial boarding kennel. The noise, unfamiliar dogs, and lack of one-on-one attention in kennels can be genuinely distressing for a breed that thrives on close human contact.

Questions to Ask a Prospective Sitter

  • Have you cared for Cavoodles or similar breeds before?
  • How many other dogs will be in the home at the same time?
  • What is your routine for a typical day?
  • How do you handle a dog showing signs of anxiety?
  • Are you comfortable with the grooming needs (daily brushing)?
  • Can we arrange a meet-and-greet before the booking?

Setting Your Sitter Up for Success

Before you leave, provide your sitter with a written care sheet that includes feeding amounts and schedule, walk routine, any medications, vet contact details, your emergency contact number, and your Cavoodle’s quirks and comfort objects. The more information a sitter has, the more confidently they can replicate your dog’s normal routine — and the more settled your Cavoodle will be.

Is a Cavoodle Right for You?

The Cavoodle is a wonderful breed for the right household, but they are not the low-maintenance option some people assume them to be. Their grooming needs are significant, their separation anxiety can be challenging, and they need a family that genuinely wants a dog at the centre of their lives rather than one that is happy to be left in the backyard.

If you can offer daily grooming, moderate exercise, consistent training, plenty of companionship, and thoughtful care arrangements when you travel, a Cavoodle will reward you with years of unconditional affection. They really are that good.
