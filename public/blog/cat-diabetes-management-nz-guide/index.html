Cat Diabetes Management NZ Guide: Symptoms, Treatment & Daily Care

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  June 3, 2026  •  12 min read

Feline diabetes is one of the most common endocrine disorders in New Zealand cats, affecting an estimated 1 in 200. The good news? With early detection, proper treatment, and consistent daily management, most diabetic cats live full, comfortable lives. This guide covers everything Kiwi cat owners need to know about recognising symptoms, working with your vet, and managing day-to-day care — including how pet sitters can safely care for your diabetic cat when you are away.

What Is Feline Diabetes?

Feline diabetes mellitus is a condition where your cat’s body cannot properly regulate blood glucose levels. In healthy cats, the pancreas produces insulin, a hormone that allows cells to absorb glucose from the blood for energy. In diabetic cats, either the pancreas does not produce enough insulin (Type 1) or the body becomes resistant to the insulin that is produced (Type 2).

The vast majority of diabetic cats — around 80-90% — have a form similar to Type 2 diabetes in humans, where insulin resistance is the primary problem. This is important because it means that with the right diet and treatment, many cats can actually achieve diabetic remission, where they no longer need insulin injections.

Key Fact: Unlike humans, cats can go into diabetic remission. With early, aggressive treatment combining insulin therapy and a low-carbohydrate diet, up to 80% of newly diagnosed cats may achieve remission within the first few months.

Recognising the Symptoms

Early detection makes an enormous difference in outcomes. The classic symptoms of feline diabetes form a memorable cluster often called the “three Ps”:

The Three Ps

  • Polyuria — Excessive urination. You may notice your cat visiting the litter tray far more often, or producing larger clumps of urine than usual.
  • Polydipsia — Excessive thirst. Your cat may drink from taps, toilets, puddles, or water bowls far more frequently. You might find yourself refilling the water bowl constantly.
  • Polyphagia — Excessive hunger. Despite eating more, your cat may be losing weight because the body cannot use the glucose from food.

Other Warning Signs

  • Weight loss despite a healthy or increased appetite
  • Lethargy or decreased activity — your cat may sleep more and play less
  • Poor coat condition — fur becomes dull, greasy, or matted
  • Weakness in the hind legs — a distinctive “plantigrade” stance where the cat walks on its hocks instead of its toes
  • Recurrent infections, particularly urinary tract infections
  • Sweet or fruity-smelling breath (a sign of ketoacidosis, a medical emergency)
Emergency: If your cat shows sweet or fruity breath, vomiting, lethargy, or rapid breathing, seek emergency veterinary care immediately. These are signs of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), which is life-threatening.

Risk Factors for NZ Cats

Several factors increase the likelihood of a cat developing diabetes:

  • Obesity — The single biggest risk factor. Overweight cats are up to four times more likely to develop diabetes. New Zealand’s indoor-only cats, particularly in apartment-dense areas like Auckland CBD and Wellington, are especially prone to weight gain.
  • Age — Most cats are diagnosed over 8 years old, with peak incidence around 10-13 years.
  • Male cats — Males are diagnosed roughly twice as often as females.
  • Breed predisposition — Burmese cats, common in New Zealand, have a significantly higher risk of diabetes compared to other breeds.
  • Physical inactivity — Indoor cats with limited stimulation are at higher risk.
  • Chronic pancreatitis — Inflammation of the pancreas can damage insulin-producing cells.
  • Corticosteroid medication — Long-term use of steroids (prescribed for conditions like inflammatory bowel disease or skin allergies) increases diabetes risk.

Diagnosis: What to Expect at the Vet

If you suspect diabetes, your vet will run several tests to confirm the diagnosis and rule out other conditions:

Blood Tests

  • Blood glucose — A single high reading is not enough for diagnosis, since stress can temporarily spike glucose levels in cats (known as stress hyperglycaemia). Your vet may run multiple tests.
  • Fructosamine — This test measures average blood glucose over the past 2-3 weeks, providing a more reliable indicator than a single glucose reading. It is particularly useful for cats who get stressed at the vet.
  • Complete blood count (CBC) and biochemistry panel — To assess overall health and check for concurrent conditions like kidney disease, which is common in older cats.

Urine Tests

  • Urinalysis — Checks for glucose and ketones in the urine, confirming the diagnosis and assessing severity.
  • Urine culture — Diabetic cats are prone to urinary tract infections, so your vet may culture the urine.
NZ Vet Costs (Approximate): Initial diagnosis including consultation, blood tests, fructosamine, and urinalysis typically ranges from $350-$600 at standard NZ veterinary clinics. Follow-up glucose curves and monitoring visits range from $150-$400 per visit.

Treatment Options

Insulin Therapy

Insulin injections are the cornerstone of treatment for most diabetic cats. The most commonly prescribed insulin in New Zealand is Caninsulin (a porcine insulin), though some vets may use Lantus (glargine) or ProZinc.

Injections are given subcutaneously (under the skin), typically twice daily, 12 hours apart. Most owners find the process far less daunting than expected — the needles are extremely fine (similar to insulin needles used by humans), and most cats tolerate the injections well once a routine is established.

How to Give Your Cat an Insulin Injection:
  1. Roll the insulin pen or vial gently between your palms — never shake it.
  2. Draw the correct dose into the syringe or dial it on the pen.
  3. Pinch a tent of loose skin along your cat’s back or side.
  4. Insert the needle at the base of the tent, aiming slightly downward.
  5. Depress the plunger smoothly and steadily.
  6. Withdraw the needle and give your cat a treat immediately to create a positive association.

Dietary Management

Diet is arguably as important as insulin for diabetic cats. The goal is a high-protein, low-carbohydrate diet that minimises post-meal glucose spikes.

Macronutrient Ideal Range for Diabetic Cats Notes
Carbohydrates Under 10% of calories (ideally 5-8%) Most dry foods are 30-50% carbs — far too high
Protein 40-50% of calories Supports lean muscle and satiety
Fat 20-30% of calories Higher if the cat needs to gain weight
NZ Diet Tip: Many New Zealand vet clinics stock prescription diabetic cat foods. The most commonly recommended include Hill’s m/d, Royal Canin Diabetic, and Purina DM. However, many wet foods available at pet stores and supermarkets can also work well — look for brands with under 10% carbohydrates. Your vet can recommend specific products available locally.

Blood Glucose Monitoring

Monitoring your cat’s blood glucose is essential for adjusting insulin doses safely. There are two main approaches:

  • Vet-performed glucose curves — Your cat stays at the clinic for the day while blood glucose is measured every 1-2 hours. This gives a complete picture but is expensive and stressful for the cat.
  • Home monitoring — Using a pet-specific glucometer (or a human one calibrated for cats), you can check blood glucose at home. This is less stressful for the cat and gives more accurate readings since there is no stress-induced spike.

An increasingly popular option in New Zealand is the continuous glucose monitor (CGM) — a small sensor worn on the cat’s skin that records glucose levels throughout the day. The Freestyle Libre is commonly used off-label for cats, and several NZ vet clinics now offer CGM placement and monitoring services.

Managing Your Diabetic Cat Day-to-Day

Establishing a Routine

Consistency is the foundation of successful diabetes management. Your cat thrives on routine:

  • Feed at the same times daily — ideally 30 minutes before insulin injections so food is being absorbed when insulin peaks.
  • Give insulin at 12-hour intervals — for example, 7:00 AM and 7:00 PM.
  • Monitor water intake — track how much water your cat drinks daily.
  • Weigh your cat weekly — sudden weight loss or gain signals a need for vet review.
  • Keep a log — record food intake, water consumption, insulin dose, and any unusual behaviour.

Hypoglycaemia: The Danger of Low Blood Sugar

While high blood sugar is the primary problem in diabetes, treatment with insulin introduces the risk of hypoglycaemia (dangerously low blood glucose). This is a medical emergency.

Signs of Hypoglycaemia: Weakness, wobbling, trembling, disorientation, seizures, collapse, or coma. If you see these signs, rub honey or glucose syrup on your cat’s gums and seek emergency vet care immediately. Always keep a glucose source (honey, glucose gel) at home.

What to Do If You Miss a Dose

If you realise you have missed an insulin dose:

  • If it has only been 1-2 hours since the scheduled time, give the dose as normal.
  • If more than 2 hours have passed, skip that dose and give the next dose at its regular time.
  • Never give a double dose to make up for a missed one.
  • Contact your vet if you are unsure or if missed doses happen frequently.

Pet Sitting a Diabetic Cat: What Sitters Need to Know

If you are travelling or working long hours, a knowledgeable pet sitter can manage your diabetic cat safely. Here is what both owners and sitters should arrange:

For Cat Owners

  • Provide a detailed care sheet — feeding times, insulin type and dose, injection technique, and emergency contacts.
  • Do a training session — have the sitter practise giving insulin while you observe before you leave.
  • Leave emergency supplies — honey or glucose gel for hypoglycaemia, your vet’s phone number, and the after-hours emergency vet address.
  • Pre-draw insulin syringes — if the sitter is uncomfortable drawing insulin, pre-fill syringes and store them in the fridge.
  • Consider a continuous glucose monitor — CGMs let sitters check glucose without needing to draw blood, which is far less stressful for everyone.

For Pet Sitters

  • Never skip or double an insulin dose — if in doubt, contact the owner or vet.
  • Always feed before injecting — injecting insulin into a cat that has not eaten risks hypoglycaemia.
  • Monitor food and water intake — report any significant changes immediately.
  • Know the signs of hypoglycaemia — weakness, wobbling, disorientation, seizures.
  • Keep a log — record feeding times, insulin given, water consumed, and any observations.

Diet Tips Specific to NZ Cats

  • Switch from dry to wet food — This is the single most impactful dietary change. Dry kibble is typically 30-50% carbohydrates, while wet food can be as low as 3-8%. Even “diabetic” dry foods are higher in carbs than standard wet foods.
  • Read the labels on NZ supermarket foods — Many popular brands like Feline Natural, Ziwi Peak, and K9 Natural (wet ranges) are naturally low in carbohydrates and suitable for diabetic cats.
  • Avoid semi-moist pouches — These often contain high levels of sugar and propylene glycol, which can interfere with glucose regulation.
  • Consider raw diets with vet guidance — Some NZ owners successfully manage diabetes with balanced raw diets, but this requires veterinary supervision to ensure nutritional adequacy.
  • Treats matter — Freeze-dried meat treats (like Feline Natural freeze-dried) are low-carb and appropriate for diabetic cats.

Diabetic Remission: The Goal

Diabetic remission means your cat no longer needs insulin injections to maintain normal blood glucose levels. It is the best possible outcome, and it is achievable for many cats — particularly those diagnosed early and treated promptly.

How to Maximise the Chances of Remission

  • Start insulin therapy immediately — Do not try diet alone first. The longer blood glucose stays high, the more damage occurs to pancreatic cells.
  • Switch to a low-carb diet immediately — This is non-negotiable for remission.
  • Monitor blood glucose closely — As glucose levels drop, insulin needs decrease. If you do not reduce the dose, you risk hypoglycaemia.
  • Achieve and maintain a healthy weight — Weight loss in overweight cats significantly improves insulin sensitivity.
  • Be patient — Remission can take weeks to months. Some cats achieve it within the first month; others take 3-6 months.
Good News: Studies show that 60-80% of newly diagnosed cats who receive early, aggressive treatment with insulin and a low-carb diet can achieve diabetic remission. Once in remission, most cats stay off insulin permanently with proper diet management.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a cat be cured of diabetes?

Cats can achieve remission, meaning they no longer need insulin, but they are not truly “cured.” If a cat in remission goes back to a high-carb diet or gains significant weight, diabetes can return. Ongoing dietary management is essential.

How long do diabetic cats live?

With proper management, diabetic cats can live a normal lifespan. The key factors are early diagnosis, consistent treatment, and regular veterinary monitoring. Cats that achieve remission have an excellent prognosis.

Can I give insulin to a cat that has not eaten?

No. If your cat has not eaten, do not give the full insulin dose. A cat with no food in its system is at high risk of hypoglycaemia. Contact your vet for guidance — they may recommend giving a reduced dose or skipping it entirely.

Is pet insurance worth it for a diabetic cat?

In New Zealand, most pet insurance policies exclude pre-existing conditions, so a cat already diagnosed with diabetes will not be covered for diabetes-related costs. However, if your cat is insured before diagnosis, ongoing diabetes treatment would typically be covered. See our NZ Pet Insurance Guide for a detailed comparison of policies.

What should I do if my cat vomits after eating and insulin?

If vomiting occurs within an hour of eating and receiving insulin, contact your vet. The food may not have been absorbed, but the insulin is already working, which could lead to hypoglycaemia. Your vet may recommend offering a small amount of food and monitoring closely.

Working With Your NZ Vet

Building a strong relationship with your vet is crucial for managing feline diabetes. In New Zealand, you have several options:

  • Primary care vets — Your regular vet can diagnose diabetes, prescribe insulin, and provide ongoing monitoring.
  • Referral centres — For complex cases, your vet may refer you to an internal medicine specialist at places like the Massey University Veterinary Teaching Hospital (Palmerston North) or specialist clinics in Auckland and Wellington.
  • After-hours emergency vets — Every major NZ city has 24-hour emergency clinics. Keep their number on your fridge and in your phone. Know the location of your nearest one before you need it.

Key Takeaways

  • Watch for the three Ps: excessive urination, thirst, and hunger — especially in older, overweight, or Burmese cats.
  • Early diagnosis and aggressive treatment give the best chance of diabetic remission.
  • Diet is critical: switch to a high-protein, low-carb wet food immediately.
  • Insulin injections are straightforward once you establish a routine — most cats tolerate them well.
  • Home blood glucose monitoring (or a CGM) is less stressful and more accurate than vet-only glucose curves.
  • Budget $150-$300/month for ongoing management in New Zealand.
  • A well-briefed pet sitter can safely manage your diabetic cat while you are away.
  • Remission is achievable for 60-80% of newly diagnosed cats with prompt, consistent treatment.
