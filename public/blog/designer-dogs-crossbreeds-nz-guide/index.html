Designer Dogs and Crossbreeds in New Zealand: The Complete Guide for 2026

Published 16 July 2026 — ZiggySitters NZ

Walk through any NZ dog park and you will see them: the cavoodles with their soft curls, the groodles bouncing through the grass, the spoodles wagging their way through life. Designer dogs — deliberate crosses between two purebred breeds — have become the most popular type of companion dog in New Zealand. But behind the adorable names and teddy-bear faces, there are real decisions to make. This guide covers everything you need to know about designer dogs in NZ: which crossbreed suits your lifestyle, what health issues to watch for, how to find an ethical breeder, and what ongoing care these dogs need.

What Is a Designer Dog?

A designer dog is a first-generation (F1) cross between two purebred parents of different breeds. The idea is to combine the best traits of both breeds — for example, the low-shedding coat of a poodle with the affectionate nature of a cavalier king charles spaniel (creating the cavoodle).

The term "designer dog" can sound frivolous, but these crosses serve a genuine purpose. Many oodle crosses were originally developed as allergy-friendly companions, and their popularity in NZ reflects a real demand for dogs that suit apartment living, family life, and our increasingly urban lifestyle.

The "Oodle" Family

By far the most common designer dogs in NZ are the "oodle" crosses — poodles crossed with other breeds. Poodles contribute intelligence, a low-shedding coat, and longevity. The other parent contributes temperament and size. The result is a dog that often inherits the best of both worlds.

The Most Popular Designer Dogs in New Zealand

Cavoodle (Cavalier King Charles Spaniel x Poodle)

The cavoodle is NZ's most popular designer dog, and for good reason. These dogs are small (5–12 kg), affectionate, and adaptable. They thrive in apartments and houses alike, get along with children and other pets, and their moderate exercise needs (30–45 minutes daily) suit busy families.

  • Size: 5–12 kg depending on toy or miniature poodle parent
  • Coat: Wavy to curly, low-shedding but requires regular grooming
  • Temperament: Gentle, eager to please, sociable, good with children
  • Lifespan: 12–15 years
  • Exercise: 30–45 minutes daily

For detailed care advice, see our cavoodle care guide.

Groodle (Golden Retriever x Poodle)

The groodle combines the golden retriever's loving, loyal nature with the poodle's intelligence and low-shedding coat. They are larger dogs (15–40 kg) that need more space and exercise, but they make exceptional family companions.

  • Size: 15–40 kg (miniature, medium, or standard)
  • Coat: Wavy to curly, low to moderate shedding
  • Temperament: Loyal, gentle, intelligent, excellent with children
  • Lifespan: 10–14 years
  • Exercise: 60+ minutes daily

See our full groodle care guide for more details.

Spoodle (Cocker Spaniel x Poodle)

Spoodles are medium-sized, energetic, and incredibly affectionate. They are great for active families who want a dog that is happy hiking on weekends and cuddling on the couch during the week.

  • Size: 6–20 kg depending on parent size
  • Coat: Wavy, low-shedding, requires regular grooming
  • Temperament: Happy, eager, playful, loves water
  • Lifespan: 12–15 years
  • Exercise: 45–60 minutes daily

Our spoodle care guide has comprehensive advice.

Labradoodle (Labrador Retriever x Poodle)

The original designer dog, the labradoodle was first bred in Australia in the 1980s as a guide dog for allergy sufferers. They are larger, energetic, and need committed owners. They are wonderful dogs, but they are not the "easy" option some people expect.

  • Size: 20–30 kg (standard), 15–20 kg (medium)
  • Coat: Varies significantly: flat, wavy, or curly
  • Temperament: Intelligent, friendly, energetic, sometimes stubborn
  • Lifespan: 12–14 years
  • Exercise: 60+ minutes daily

The Reality of Designer Dog Genetics

Here is the part many breeders do not talk about: crossing two breeds does not guarantee the best traits of both. Genetics is not a buffet where you pick and choose. Each puppy in a litter can inherit a different mix of traits from each parent.

Coat Unpredictability

The biggest myth about oodle crosses is that they are all hypoallergenic and non-shedding. In reality, coat type varies within a single litter. Some puppies will inherit the poodle's tight curls (low shedding), some will have the other breed's flat coat (moderate to high shedding), and some will land somewhere in between. If allergies are your reason for choosing an oodle, you need to see the actual coat — or buy from an F1B (backcross to poodle) or multi-generational line where the coat type is more predictable.

Health: Hybrid Vigour vs Inherited Risk

"Hybrid vigour" — the idea that crossbred dogs are inherently healthier than purebreds — is real but overstated. First-generation crosses do benefit from greater genetic diversity, which reduces the risk of recessive genetic disorders. However, they can still inherit conditions common to either parent breed.

Common health concerns in NZ's popular crosses:

  • Cavoodles: Mitral valve disease (from cavalier), luxating patellas, eye conditions
  • Groodles: Hip dysplasia (from both breeds), certain cancers (from golden retriever), ear infections
  • Spoodles: Progressive retinal atrophy, ear infections, hip dysplasia in larger lines
  • Labradoodles: Hip and elbow dysplasia, ear infections, Addison's disease
Essential: Always ask the breeder for DNA health testing results for both parents. A responsible breeder tests for breed-specific conditions and can show you clear results. If they cannot, walk away.

Choosing the Right Crossbreed for Your Lifestyle

Low-Exercise Households

If you want a companion dog for a smaller home with moderate daily walks (20–30 minutes), consider a cavoodle, maltipoo, or shipoo. These breeds are content with gentle exercise and indoor play.

Active Families

Families with children who want a dog for beach runs, bush walks, and weekend adventures should look at groodles, labradoodles, or spoodles. These breeds thrive with 60+ minutes of daily exercise and love swimming, fetching, and hiking. Check our best dog parks in NZ guide for ideas.

Apartment Dwellers

Smaller oodles (cavoodles, maltipoos, toy spoodles) can do well in apartments, but they still need daily walks and mental stimulation. See our apartment living with pets guide for detailed advice.

Senior Owners

Cavoodles and maltipoos are excellent companions for older adults. They are small enough to manage, affectionate without being demanding, and their exercise needs match a gentle daily routine.

Finding a Reputable Designer Dog Breeder in NZ

This is the most important section of this guide. The popularity of designer dogs has created a market for unethical breeding. Here is how to tell the difference:

Green Flags (What to Look For)

  • DNA health testing of both parents, with results available for you to see
  • The breeder asks you questions about your lifestyle and experience
  • Puppies are raised in the home, exposed to household sounds, children, and other animals
  • The breeder provides a written health guarantee (typically 2–3 years for genetic conditions)
  • You can visit the breeder and meet the puppy's mother (and ideally the father)
  • The breeder is registered with Dogs NZ (New Zealand Kennel Club) or follows the Dog Breeders Association code of ethics
  • Puppies are not available to go home before 8 weeks of age
  • The breeder provides ongoing support and is willing to take the dog back if circumstances change

Red Flags (Walk Away)

  • Multiple litters available at the same time (puppy farm indicator)
  • No health testing or vague claims about "vet checked"
  • Puppies available to go home before 8 weeks
  • The breeder will not let you visit their home
  • Price is significantly below or above the market average without explanation
  • No questions asked about your suitability as an owner
  • The breeder insists on meeting in a car park or neutral location
  • They pressure you to buy quickly or pay a deposit before seeing the puppy
ZiggySitters Tip: Average prices for designer dogs in NZ range from $3,000 to $6,000. If someone is offering a cavoodle puppy for $1,500, ask why. Ethical breeding with health testing, quality food, vet care, and socialisation costs money. A cheap puppy from a puppy farm can cost thousands more in vet bills down the track.

At-Home Grooming Essentials

Between professional grooms, you need to brush your oodle 2–3 times per week to prevent matting. Invest in:

  • A slicker brush for daily brushing
  • A metal comb to check for tangles close to the skin
  • Dematter tool for any developing mats
  • Dog-safe shampoo and conditioner
  • Nail clippers or a Dremel

If your oodle's coat becomes severely matted, the only option may be a full clip-off — which is uncomfortable for the dog and more expensive. Regular brushing is non-negotiable. See our beginner grooming guide for more.

Training Designer Dogs

Designer dogs, particularly oodle crosses, are intelligent and eager to please. That combination makes them highly trainable — but also means they get bored easily and can develop unwanted behaviours if under-stimulated.

Puppy Training Priorities

  1. Socialisation (8–16 weeks): Expose your puppy to different people, dogs, environments, sounds, and surfaces. This is the most critical period for shaping a confident, well-adjusted adult dog.
  2. Basic obedience: Sit, stay, come, leave it, loose-lead walking. Start early and be consistent.
  3. Toilet training: Oodle crosses are generally quick learners, but consistency is key. Take them out after meals, naps, and play sessions.
  4. Crate training: A crate becomes your dog's safe space and helps with toilet training, travel, and alone-time. See our crate training guide.
  5. Alone-time training: Oodles are prone to separation anxiety. Practice leaving for short periods from day one.

Common Training Challenges

  • Jumping up: Oodles are excitable. Turn away and ignore jumping; reward four paws on the floor.
  • Barking: Some designer breeds (particularly spoodles and schnoodles) can be vocal. Address boredom and teach a "quiet" cue early. See our dog barking solutions guide.
  • Selective hearing: Poodle crosses are smart enough to decide when to listen. Make training rewarding with high-value treats and keep sessions short (5–10 minutes).
  • Lead pulling: Start loose-lead walking early. A front-clip harness can help during the training phase.

Designer Dogs and Pet Sitting

Designer dogs have specific care needs that not every pet sitter understands. Their coats need regular brushing, they may need medication, and their exercise requirements vary dramatically by breed. When booking a pet sitter:

  • Provide a detailed care sheet covering feeding times, amounts, exercise requirements, grooming needs, and any medications
  • Book a meet-and-greet before your first booking so your dog can get comfortable with the sitter. See our meet-and-greet guide.
  • Be specific about grooming: Does your oodle need daily brushing? A face wash? Eye cleaning? Write it down.
  • Mention behavioural quirks: Some designer breeds are anxious around strangers, bark at the door, or counter-surf. Let the sitter know what to expect.
  • Share your vet's contact details in case of emergency

Frequently Asked Questions About Designer Dogs in NZ

Are designer dogs hypoallergenic?

No dog is truly hypoallergenic. All dogs produce dander and saliva proteins that trigger allergies. However, low-shedding breeds like poodle crosses produce less dander because the hair stays in the coat rather than shedding around the house. F1B and multi-generational oodles are more consistently low-shedding than F1 crosses.

Are designer dogs healthier than purebreds?

First-generation crosses do benefit from hybrid vigour, which reduces the risk of inherited recessive conditions. But they can still inherit health issues from either parent breed. The key is health testing of both parents — not the cross itself. A health-tested purebred from a responsible breeder is likely healthier than an untested designer cross from a puppy farm.

Can I register a designer dog with Dogs NZ?

Crossbreeds cannot be shown in conformation classes at Dogs NZ events, but they can participate in dog sports like agility, obedience, and rally-O. You can register your crossbreed on the Dogs NZ associate register.

Should I get an F1, F1B, or multi-generational oodle?

An F1 (first generation cross) has the most hybrid vigour but the most coat variability. An F1B (backcross to poodle) has a more consistent low-shedding coat but slightly less hybrid vigour. Multi-generational oodles have the most predictable coats but need the same health testing as purebreds. For most NZ families, an F1 from health-tested parents is a great choice.

Are designer dogs good with children?

Most popular designer breeds in NZ (cavoodles, groodles, spoodles) were specifically created as family companions and generally do well with children. However, temperaments vary within litters. Always meet the puppy and parents, and socialise early. See our dog socialisation guide.

How do I find a rescue oodle in NZ?

Oodle-specific rescues exist in NZ, and many crossbreeds end up in SPCA shelters. Set up alerts on PetRescue for your preferred breed, and consider fostering through breed-specific rescues. See our rescue dog adoption guide.
