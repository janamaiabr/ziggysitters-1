Pet-Friendly Camping in New Zealand: Complete Guide for 2026

2026-05-25 · 10 min read · ZiggySitters Team

New Zealand is one of the best countries in the world for camping. From coastal campsites with ocean views to remote DOC sites nestled in native bush, the options are extraordinary. But if you have a dog, the rules are not always straightforward. Many campsites restrict or outright ban pets, DOC land has specific regulations, and holiday parks vary wildly in their pet policies. This guide covers everything you need to know about camping with pets in New Zealand, including where you can go, what to pack, how to keep your pet safe, and what to do when camping is not an option.

Can You Take Pets Camping in New Zealand?

Yes, but with important restrictions. The Department of Conservation (DOC) manages a large network of campsites across New Zealand, and their pet policy is clear: dogs and other pets are generally not permitted on DOC land, including national parks, conservation areas, and most DOC campsites. There are limited exceptions for certified assistance dogs, but for the average pet owner, DOC land is off-limits.

Private campsites, holiday parks, and regional council-operated campgrounds are your main options. Many of these welcome well-behaved dogs, especially outside peak holiday periods. The key is always checking before you book. Do not assume a campsite is pet-friendly just because it has open spaces; policies change, and some parks that allow dogs in low season restrict them over summer.

Understanding DOC Rules for Dogs

The DOC dog policy exists to protect New Zealand's native wildlife. Many native species, including kiwi, weka, dotterel, and penguins, are vulnerable to dog attacks. Even well-trained dogs can cause harm through chase or disturbance. DOC takes this seriously, and fines for having a dog in a restricted area can reach $20,000 or prosecution under the Conservation Act.

Specific rules include:

  • Dogs are prohibited in all national parks unless you have a DOC permit
  • Dogs are prohibited on most DOC-managed walking tracks and campsites
  • Some regional parks allow leashed dogs on designated tracks; check with the regional council
  • Beach access varies by season; some nesting bird areas prohibit dogs during breeding season (typically September to March)
  • Certified assistance dogs are allowed in most areas with appropriate identification

The rule of thumb: if the land is DOC-managed, assume dogs are not allowed until you confirm otherwise. Check the DOC website or call the local DOC office before planning your trip.

Best Pet-Friendly Holiday Parks in New Zealand

Holiday parks are generally your safest bet for camping with pets. Many have dedicated dog-friendly zones, grassy areas for exercise, and understanding staff. Here are some of the best pet-friendly holiday parks across the country:

Auckland and Northland

  • Ruakaka Holiday Park — Dogs allowed on-site with direct beach access. A favourite for Northland beach holidays.
  • Tapu Camping Ground — Coromandel Peninsula. Creek-side sites, dogs welcome by arrangement.

Waikato and Bay of Plenty

  • Raglan Holiday Park — Dogs permitted in powered and unpowered sites. Close to the beach and town.
  • Omokoroa Holiday Park — Bay of Plenty. Pet-friendly with harbour views and walking tracks nearby.

Canterbury and Otago

  • Bealey Avenue Motor Camp — Christchurch. Central location, dogs welcome.
  • Lake Wanaka Holiday Park — Dog-friendly sites available. Stunning lake and mountain setting.

Always call ahead to confirm pet availability, especially during school holidays and long weekends. Many parks limit the number of dog-friendly sites and book out quickly.

What to Pack When Camping with Your Dog

Camping with a dog means packing for two. Here is a comprehensive checklist:

Essentials

  • Food for the entire trip plus two extra days (in case of delays)
  • Collapsible food and water bowls
  • Fresh water supply if the campsite does not have potable water
  • Leash (at least two, in case one breaks) and a long tether line for at-site use
  • Poop bags — pack more than you think you need
  • Dog first aid kit (bandages, antiseptic, tick remover, tweezers)
  • Medications and copies of vaccination records

Comfort Items

  • Dog bed or sleeping mat with familiar scent
  • Blanket for cold nights (NZ summer nights can still drop to 8°C in some regions)
  • Favourite toys to reduce anxiety in a new environment
  • Towel for muddy or wet walks

Safety Gear

  • Reflective vest or LED collar for visibility at night
  • Dog jacket for rain or cold weather
  • Muzzle if your dog is reactive around other animals or people
  • Flea and tick prevention (applied before the trip)

Safety Tips for Camping with Pets

Keep Your Dog Under Control at All Times

New Zealand law requires dogs to be under control at all times. At a campsite, this means your dog should be on a leash or tethered within your site. Even well-trained dogs can be unpredictable in new environments with new smells, other animals, and unfamiliar people walking past. A roaming dog at a campsite is a liability risk and will not make you popular with neighbouring campers.

Watch for Wildlife Hazards

New Zealand has fewer natural predators than most countries, but risks still exist. Wasps and bees are common in summer and can cause serious reactions in dogs. Sandflies and mosquitoes are persistent in coastal and bush areas. In some regions, particularly around farmland, dogs may encounter toxic plants like tutu or ragwort. Keep your dog away from unknown plants and check with locals if you are unsure about regional hazards.

Protect Against Heat

New Zealand UV levels are extreme in summer. Dogs can suffer heatstroke quickly, especially breeds with short noses or thick coats. Provide shade at your campsite, offer water constantly, and avoid walking your dog on hot sand or asphalt during peak sun hours. Signs of heatstroke include excessive panting, drooling, lethargy, and vomiting. If you notice these symptoms, move your dog to shade, wet their coat with cool water, and seek veterinary care immediately.

Manage Barking and Noise

Campsites demand consideration for others. If your dog barks at strangers, other dogs, or wildlife, be proactive. Bring chew toys or long-lasting treats to keep them occupied. Consider a crate if your dog is crate-trained and feels secure in one. If your dog is a persistent barker, camping may not be the best option; some dogs are simply not suited to the close-quarters environment of a campsite.

Regional Council Dog-Friendly Parks and Reserves

While DOC land is mostly off-limits, many regional councils maintain parks and reserves where leashed dogs are welcome. These are often excellent alternatives to DOC sites and can provide the outdoor experience you are looking for without the restrictions. Check your local regional council website for dog access maps. Some councils, like Auckland Council and Wellington City Council, publish detailed online maps showing exactly where dogs can go on and off leash.

Notable regional dog-friendly areas include:

  • Woodhill Forest — Auckland. Off-leash walking and mountain biking with dogs.
  • Hunua Ranges — Auckland. Designated dog-walking tracks available.
  • Bottle Lake Forest — Christchurch. Popular off-leash walking area.
  • Port Hills — Christchurch. Leashed dogs on most tracks with stunning views.
  • Otanewainuku Forest — Bay of Plenty. Limited dog access; check before visiting.

When Camping Is Not the Right Choice

Sometimes the best decision for your pet is to leave them at home. If your trip involves DOC land where dogs are banned, if your dog is anxious in new environments, or if you are visiting during peak summer when campsites are packed and temperatures are high, a pet sitter may provide a better experience for everyone involved.

A professional pet sitter keeps your dog in their own home, maintains their routine, and eliminates the stress of travel, heat, and unfamiliar surroundings. For cats, this is almost always the better option, as very few campsites welcome cats and most cats find travel deeply stressful.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I take my cat camping in New Zealand?

Technically some holiday parks may allow cats, but it is rarely a good idea. Most cats find travel and unfamiliar environments extremely stressful. A cat is far better off staying at home with a pet sitter who can maintain their routine and territory.

Do I need to register my dog to camp at a holiday park?

Most holiday parks require proof of current registration and vaccination. Bring your dog registration papers and vaccination certificate when you check in. Some parks also require dogs to be microchipped.

Are there any dog-friendly DOC campsites?

A very small number of DOC campsites allow dogs, but these are exceptions. Check the DOC website for current information. Most DOC land remains off-limits to protect native wildlife.

What about freedom camping with a dog?

New Zealand freedom camping rules apply regardless of whether you have a pet. You must camp in a designated area or on land where the landowner has given permission. Freedom camping with a dog adds the responsibility of waste disposal and control. Many freedom camping spots are on DOC land where dogs are not permitted, so check carefully before you go.

Planning Your Next Trip

Pet-friendly camping in New Zealand is entirely possible with the right preparation. The keys are: always check before you book, pack for your pet as thoroughly as you pack for yourself, keep your dog under control, and be honest about whether camping is genuinely the best option for your pet. Sometimes the most loving choice is leaving your pet at home with a sitter who will treat them like family. Whether you are heading to the beach, the bush, or a holiday park, plan ahead and your trip will be better for both of you.
