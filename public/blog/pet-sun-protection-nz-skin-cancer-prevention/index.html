Pet Sun Protection NZ: Skin Cancer Prevention Guide

Published April 18, 2026 | 12 min read

New Zealand has one of the highest UV radiation levels in the world. While we enjoy our beautiful summers, our pets face real risks from sun exposure — including painful burns, heatstroke, and even skin cancer. This guide covers everything you need to keep your furry friends safe during the sunny months.

Why New Zealand Pets Need Extra Sun Protection

New Zealand sits close to the Antarctic ozone hole, meaning UV radiation reaches the surface at dangerously high levels. During summer, the UV index in New Zealand regularly hits 11+ — categorised as "extreme" by the World Health Organization.

Unlike humans, pets cannot apply sunscreen or seek shade on their own. They rely entirely on you to protect them. Dogs and cats with light fur, pink skin, or short coats are particularly vulnerable — breeds like Dalmatians, Greyhounds, Whippets, and white cats need extra vigilance.

Common Sun-Related Risks for NZ Pets

  • Sunburn — Painful red skin, peeling, and discomfort
  • Heatstroke — Life-threatening overheating, especially in flat-faced breeds
  • Skin cancer — UV damage can cause cancerous growths, particularly on light-coloured noses, ears, and bellies
  • Pad burns — Hot pavement can burn paw pads in seconds

How to Protect Your Dog From Sun Damage

Provide Constant Access to Shade

Whether your dog spends time in the garden or accompanies you on adventures, shade must always be available. Natural shade from trees is ideal, but a proper outdoor shelter with a cool, shaded area works too. Avoid keeping dogs outdoors during the hottest hours — typically 11am to 4pm in New Zealand summer.

Use Pet-Safe Sunscreen

Regular human sunscreen is toxic to pets. Use only pet-specific sunscreen formulated without zinc oxide or octinoxate, which are harmful if ingested. Apply to exposed areas: nose, ears, belly, and any shaved or thin-fur areas. Reapply every two hours if your pet is outside for extended periods.

Pro tip: Test any new sunscreen on a small patch of skin first. Wait 24 hours to check for allergic reactions before full application.

Invest in Protective Gear

Lightweight UV-protective shirts and cooling vests are increasingly popular in New Zealand. These cover vulnerable areas while keeping your dog cool. For dogs that tolerate them, lightweight sun hats with ear flaps provide additional face and ear protection.

Hydration is Critical

Always have fresh, cool water available. On hot days, consider adding ice cubes to water bowls or using a pet water fountain to encourage drinking. Add an extra water bowl around your property so your dog never has to search for hydration.

How to Protect Your Cat From Sun Damage

Cats often seek sunny spots to nap — a behaviour that puts them at risk without them knowing it. Indoor cats napping in sunbeams are just as vulnerable as outdoor cats.

Indoor Cat Sun Protection

Keep curtains or blinds drawn during peak UV hours, especially for light-coloured cats. Apply cat-safe sunscreen to ear tips and noses if your cat relaxes in sunny spots. Consider a cooling mat for hot days.

Outdoor and Feral Cat Considerations

If you have an outdoor cat or care for community cats, provide shaded resting spots. Ensure multiple water stations. Light-coloured and white cats are at highest risk — consider limiting outdoor time during peak sun hours.

Warning: Never shave your cat or dog to keep them cool. Fur actually protects against both sunburn AND overheating. A shaved pet loses their natural UV protection and can burn more easily.

Recognising Signs of Sun Damage in Pets

Watch for these warning signs that your pet has had too much sun:

  • Red or pink skin — Particularly on ears, nose, belly, and groin
  • Peeling or flaking skin — Indicates sunburn
  • Excessive panting — In dogs, this signals overheating
  • Drooling or lethargy — Signs of heatstroke
  • Lumps or growths — Any new bumps should be checked by a vet

If you notice severe sunburn or any signs of heatstroke (collapse, rapid breathing, vomiting), contact your vet immediately. Heatstroke is a medical emergency.

Test surfaces with your hand or bare foot. If it is too hot for you, it is too hot for your dog. Walk during cooler parts of the day: early morning or evening. Stick to grass or shaded paths when possible. Consider protective booties for dogs that need togo outside during hot periods.

Five-second rule: Place your hand on the pavement. If you cannot hold it there for five seconds comfortably, it is too hot for your dog\'s paws.

Heatstroke Prevention and Response

Heatstroke kills pets quickly. Flat-faced breeds (Bulldogs, Pugs, Persian cats), senior pets, and those with health conditions are at highest risk.

Prevention Tips

  • Never leave pets in parked cars — even with windows cracked, interior temperatures skyrocket
  • Provide constant access to cool water and shade
  • Limit exercise to early morning or evening
  • Use cooling mats or frozen treats
  • Avoid humid environments where panting cannot cool them effectively

Heatstroke Response Steps

  1. Move your pet to a cool, shaded area immediately
  2. Apply cool (not cold) water to their neck, armpits, and groin
  3. Offer small amounts of cool water to drink
  4. Contact your vet or emergency clinic en route
  5. Do not use ice or ice-cold water — rapid cooling can cause shock

Emergency: If your pet shows signs of heatstroke — collapse, excessive panting, blue gums, vomiting — this is life-threatening. Get to a vet immediately. Call ahead so they can prepare.

Creating a Sun-Safe Routine

Build sun safety into your daily pet care routine during New Zealand summer months:

  • Morning check: Ensure shade areas are clear and water bowls are full
  • Midday limit: Keep pets indoors during 11am-4pm when possible
  • Evening walk: Save big walks for early morning or after sunset
  • Weekly check: Examine your pet\'s skin, ears, and nose for any signs of sunburn or changes
