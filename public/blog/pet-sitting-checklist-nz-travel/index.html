Pet Sitting Checklist for NZ Travellers: Everything to Prepare Before You Leave

Published on 30 May 2026 | By ZiggySitters

Leaving your pet behind when you travel is never easy, but a well-prepared pet sitter can make all the difference. Whether you are heading overseas for a fortnight or taking a long weekend across the Cook Strait, having a thorough checklist ensures your furry family member gets the care they need. This guide covers every detail you should organise before handing over the keys to your pet sitter in New Zealand.

Why a Pet Sitting Checklist Matters

Pets thrive on routine. When their owner disappears and a stranger takes over feeding and walking, even the most easy-going animal can feel unsettled. A detailed checklist removes guesswork for the sitter and keeps your pet\'s daily life as consistent as possible. It also gives you peace of mind while you are 36,000 feet above the Pacific, knowing that every contingency has been planned for.

In New Zealand, where many households include at least one pet, pet sitting has become the preferred alternative to boarding kennels and catteries. Dogs and cats are generally happier staying in their own home with a sitter who follows their usual schedule. But that only works if the sitter has the information they need.

Essential Information to Leave for Your Pet Sitter

Veterinary Details

Your sitter needs to know who to call if something goes wrong. Write down the following:

  • Your regular vet clinic name, address, and phone number
  • The nearest after-hours emergency vet clinic (this may be different from your regular vet)
  • Your pet\'s registration number and microchip details
  • Current vaccination status and when the next shots are due
  • Any known allergies or chronic conditions
  • Pet insurance policy number and provider contact details if applicable

In New Zealand, after-hours emergency vet clinics are not available in every town. If you live in a smaller centre like Whanganui or Timaru, make sure your sitter knows where the closest 24-hour clinic is, even if it is an hour\'s drive away. For rural properties, include directions to the clinic as GPS can be unreliable on country roads.

Feeding Schedule and Diet

Be specific about what, when, and how much your pet eats. Vague instructions like "feed twice a day" leave too much room for error. Include:

  • Brand and variety of food (dry, wet, or raw)
  • Exact portion sizes in measurable terms (cups, grams, or cans)
  • Feeding times (e.g. 7:00 AM and 5:30 PM)
  • Location of food storage and bowls
  • Treats: what is allowed and how many per day
  • Items that are strictly off-limits (table scraps, certain bones)

If your pet is on a specialised diet, such as a raw feeding plan or a prescription diet for kidney disease, note this clearly. Changing a pet\'s diet abruptly can cause stomach upsets that might look like something more serious to an anxious sitter.

Medication and Supplements

If your pet takes any medication, leave written instructions that a stranger could follow without assistance. Include:

  • Name of each medication and what it is for
  • Dosage and frequency
  • How to administer (pill pocket, crushed in food, liquid syringe)
  • Where medications are stored
  • What to do if a dose is missed or vomited back up

Common New Zealand pet medications include flea and tick preventatives like Bravecto or NexGard, heartworm prevention, and joint supplements such as Zeal or Antinol. If your pet is on long-term medication, ask your vet if they can supply enough to cover the sitting period plus a few extra days as a buffer.

Daily Routine and Exercise

Walk Schedule for Dogs

Dogs are creatures of habit. If your dog normally gets a 30-minute walk at 6:30 AM and a longer walk at 5:00 PM, write that down exactly. Include details your sitter might not think to ask:

  • Lead or harness type and where it is kept
  • Preferred walking routes and any areas to avoid
  • Off-lead areas and recall reliability
  • How your dog reacts to other dogs, cyclists, and wildlife
  • Weather considerations (does your dog refuse to walk in the rain?)

New Zealand\'s changing weather means a walk that takes 30 minutes in fine conditions might take longer when your dog stops to shake off rain every two minutes. Mention whether your dog needs a coat in cold weather and where to find it.

Indoor Play and Enrichment for Cats

Cats may seem independent, but they notice when their routine changes. If your cat is used to play sessions at certain times, include that in the checklist. Note favourite toys, whether they prefer wand toys or laser pointers, and how long they typically engage before losing interest. If your cat is indoor-only, enrichment is especially important to prevent boredom-related behaviour issues.

Sleeping Arrangements

Where does your pet sleep? This matters more than most people realise. A dog that normally sleeps on the bedroom floor may bark or scratch at a closed door if the sitter shuts them out. A cat that is used to sleeping on the bed may yowl outside the door. Be clear about your pet\'s sleeping preferences, including whether they are allowed on furniture and which rooms they can access at night.

House and Property Details

Security and Access

Your sitter needs practical information about your home to keep your pet safe:

  • Alarm code and how to arm and disarm the system
  • Location of spare keys and any tricky locks
  • Gate latches and fencing: can your dog push through or jump over?
  • Garage door codes if the sitter needs access
  • Neighbour contact for emergencies

Household Hazards

Point out anything in or around your home that could be dangerous for your pet. Common New Zealand hazards include:

  • Karaka tree berries (highly toxic to dogs, common in Wellington and coastal areas)
  • Cycad palms (sago palm berries and leaves are deadly)
  • Rat bait stations and slug pellets in the garden
  • Swimming pools and spa pools (unfenced or with pet-accessible gates)
  • Open fireplaces or wood burners that pets might get too close to

Even if your pet knows to avoid these things normally, a change in routine or the excitement of a new person in the house can lead to careless behaviour. Flag every potential hazard clearly.

Waste and Cleaning

Tell your sitter where to find cleaning supplies, bin bags, and the outside bin collection schedule. For dogs, specify where they toilet and how waste should be disposed of. For cats, note how often the litter tray needs cleaning, where spare litter is stored, and what type of litter you use. Changing litter type can cause cats to avoid the tray entirely, leading to accidents around the house.

Emergency Preparedness

New Zealand is prone to earthquakes, and parts of the country experience flooding and severe weather events. Your sitter needs to know what to do in an emergency:

  • Location of your pet emergency kit (food, water, medication, leads, carrier)
  • Where your pet hides when scared (under the bed, behind the couch, in a wardrobe)
  • Evacuation plan: where to go and how to transport your pet
  • Civil Defence contacts for your area
  • Nearest friend or family member who can help if the sitter cannot reach the vet

If you live in a tsunami zone, such as parts of coastal Christchurch or Wellington, include evacuation routes and timing. If your area is prone to flooding, note the safest room in the house and where to move your pet\'s supplies.

Communication and Updates

Agree on a communication schedule with your sitter before you leave. Most owners appreciate a daily photo or message, but some prefer less frequent updates. Be clear about your expectations. Also discuss:

  • Preferred communication method (text, WhatsApp, email)
  • Time zone differences if you are travelling overseas
  • How to reach you in an emergency (include hotel names and phone numbers)
  • A backup contact person if you are unreachable

If you are travelling within New Zealand, mobile coverage is generally reliable in urban areas but can be patchy in remote spots like the West Coast or Fiordland. Make sure your sitter has multiple ways to reach you.

Legal and Financial Considerations

Discuss financial arrangements before your trip. Your sitter should have a clear understanding of:

  • How they will be paid and when
  • A budget for emergency vet visits and how to access funds
  • Whether they have authority to make medical decisions for your pet
  • What to do if your pet needs treatment beyond a certain cost threshold

A written pet sitting agreement protects both parties. It should outline the scope of care, emergency protocols, liability, and payment terms. ZiggySitters provides a template agreement that covers these essentials for New Zealand pet owners.

The Meet and Greet

Never skip the meet and greet. Invite your sitter over before you leave so your pet can get comfortable with them. Watch how your pet reacts and how the sitter interacts with them. This is also the best time to walk through the checklist together, show where everything is kept, and answer questions. A sitter who has visited once will feel far more confident than one arriving cold on day one.

During the meet and greet, pay attention to how your dog responds to the sitter. Some dogs warm up immediately, while others need a few visits before they are comfortable. Cats may hide at first, which is normal. The important thing is that the sitter knows how your pet typically behaves and can recognise when something is off.

Quick Reference: The Essential Checklist

Print this section and hand it to your sitter, or stick it on the fridge:

  • Vet clinic name, phone, and address
  • After-hours emergency vet contact
  • Microchip number and registration
  • Feeding schedule with exact portions
  • Medication list with dosing instructions
  • Walk and exercise routine
  • Sleeping arrangements
  • House security: alarm code, keys, gate latches
  • Household hazards to watch for
  • Litter tray or toilet routine
  • Emergency kit location
  • Where your pet hides when scared
  • Contact numbers: your mobile, hotel, backup person
  • Neighbour contact for emergencies
  • Vet payment arrangement and budget
  • Bin collection day
  • Wi-Fi password (for the sitter)
