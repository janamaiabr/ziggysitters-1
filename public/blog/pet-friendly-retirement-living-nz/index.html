Pet-Friendly Retirement Living in New Zealand: Keeping Your Companion Animal When You Downsize

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  July 22, 2026  •  14 min read

For many older New Zealanders, a pet is not just a companion — it is family. The bond between seniors and their animals is well-documented: reduced loneliness, lower blood pressure, increased physical activity, and a powerful reason to get up each morning. Yet when the time comes to move into a retirement village or rest home, one of the most painful decisions can be whether to give up a beloved pet. This guide covers everything you need to know about pet-friendly retirement living in NZ, how to navigate pet policies, and how pet sitting services can help you keep your animal companion through any transition.

Why Pets Matter for Senior Wellbeing

The research is unequivocal. Pet ownership among older adults is associated with measurable health benefits that go far beyond simple companionship:

  • Reduced isolation and loneliness: Studies from the University of Auckland and international research consistently show that pet-owning seniors report lower loneliness scores than non-pet owners, even when living alone
  • Cardiovascular benefits: The Australian Bureau of Statistics found that pet owners have lower blood pressure and triglyceride levels than non-owners, with the effect strongest in people over 65
  • Physical activity: Dog owners over 65 walk an average of 28 minutes more per day than non-dog owners — a meaningful contribution to the 30 minutes of daily moderate exercise recommended by the NZ Ministry of Health
  • Mental health: The routine of feeding, grooming, and caring for an animal provides structure and purpose, which is particularly important during major life transitions
  • Social connection: Pets act as social lubricants — walking a dog or chatting at a vet clinic creates natural opportunities for human interaction
The Bottom Line: For many seniors, losing a pet during a housing transition compounds the stress of an already difficult move. Preserving the human-animal bond is not a luxury — it is a health intervention.

Understanding NZ Retirement Village Pet Policies

New Zealand has over 400 retirement villages, and their pet policies vary dramatically. There is no single national standard, though the Retirement Villages Act 2003 and the Retirement Villages Code of Practice 2008 provide the regulatory framework within which villages operate.

Typical Pet Conditions in Retirement Villages

Even in pet-friendly villages, conditions commonly include:

  • Pet bond or deposit: A refundable bond (typically $500–$2,000) to cover potential damage
  • Weight or size limits: Dogs under 10-15kg are the most commonly accepted; some villages have no size restriction but require good behaviour references
  • Number limits: Most villages allow one pet; some permit two with approval
  • Behavioural requirements: Dogs must be well-socialised, not excessive barkers, and under effective control at all times
  • Vaccination and registration: Current NZ dog registration and up-to-date vaccinations are mandatory
  • Supervision rules: Dogs must be on-lead in common areas; cats may need to be indoor-only
  • Emergency care plan: Some villages require a documented plan for pet care if the resident is hospitalised
Pro Tip: Always request the full pet policy in writing before signing a village occupation agreement. Verbal assurances are not binding — the written policy in your occupation right agreement (ORA) is what matters legally.

Finding Pet-Friendly Retirement Villages in NZ

Here are strategies for identifying retirement villages that welcome pets, region by region.

Auckland Region

Auckland has the largest number of retirement villages in the country, and many of the newer developments are explicitly marketing themselves as pet-friendly. Key areas to investigate:

  • North Shore and Rodney — several villages with garden units that allow small dogs and cats
  • Howick and Botany — family-oriented villages with more relaxed pet policies
  • West Auckland — rural-fringe villages with larger sections and outdoor space for dogs

Wellington Region

Wellington villages tend to be more compact (apartment-style), which limits pet options. Look for:

  • Lower Hutt and Upper Hutt — more standalone villas with yards
  • Kapiti Coast — popular retirement area with pet-friendly options and beach walking
  • Porirua — newer developments with pet-friendly policies

Canterbury and Christchurch

  • Post-earthquake rebuild has created modern village developments, many with pet-friendly policies
  • Outer suburbs (Halswell, Rolleston, Rangiora) offer larger sections suitable for dogs
  • Several inner-city villages allow cats and small dogs in apartment units

Bay of Plenty and Hawke's Bay

  • Tauranga and Mount Maunganui — high demand retirement areas with growing pet-friendly options
  • Napier and Hastings — lifestyle-oriented villages that often welcome pets
  • Whakatane and the Eastern Bay — rural villages with spacious sections

Useful Resources

  • Retirement Villages Association (RVA) of NZ — maintains a searchable database of villages, with some pet policy information
  • Seniorline (Healthpoint) — NZ government resource for aged care options
  • Age Concern NZ — advocacy and information for older New Zealanders
  • Local veterinary clinics — often know which villages in their area accept pets

Negotiating Pet Permission

If your preferred village has a conditional or unclear pet policy, negotiation is often possible. Here are effective strategies:

Before You Apply

  • Prepare a "pet resume" — include vaccination records, behaviour references from your vet, temperament assessment if available, and photos showing your pet is well-cared-for
  • Get a letter from your current vet confirming your pet is healthy, well-behaved, and up to date on all preventative care
  • Offer to pay a pet bond above the standard amount
  • Document your pet care routine — feeding schedule, exercise plan, and who will care for the pet if you are unwell

During Negotiation

  • Request a trial period — many villages are willing to accept a pet on a probationary basis
  • Emphasise the wellbeing benefits — refer to the health research on pets and seniors
  • Offer to introduce your pet to the village manager — a calm, friendly animal often changes minds
  • Point to the Human Rights Act 1993 — while not a guarantee, disability-related companion animal needs may receive additional protection under reasonable accommodation provisions
Legal Note: The Retirement Villages Code of Practice requires operators to act in good faith. If a village initially agrees to a pet and later changes the policy, you may have grounds for complaint to the Retirement Commissioner. Always get pet permission in writing within your ORA.

When You Cannot Bring Your Pet: Alternatives

Sometimes a village simply cannot accommodate your pet, or your pet would not cope with the transition. Here are compassionate alternatives:

Rehoming With Support

  • Friends and family first: The best outcome is rehoming with someone you know, allowing you to visit
  • SPCA NZ — has a structured surrender process and works to find good homes; they also offer advice on rehoming
  • Breed-specific rescues — many NZ breed rescues (e.g., Greyhounds as Pets, Cavalier Rescue NZ) specialise in rehoming specific breeds with high success rates
  • PetRescue NZ — lists animals for adoption and can help connect surrendering owners with potential adopters

Fostering Arrangements

If your living situation is temporary or uncertain, fostering may be an option. Several NZ organisations offer foster care:

  • Local SPCA branches often have foster networks that can provide temporary care
  • Some vet clinics offer boarding or fostering arrangements for established clients
  • ZiggySitters can connect you with long-term pet sitters who may be able to provide extended care in their home

How Pet Sitters Support Senior Pet Owners

Even after a successful move to a pet-friendly retirement village, pet care needs change. This is where professional pet sitting services become essential.

Daily Dog Walking

Many seniors can no longer walk their dog as far or as frequently as they once did. A daily or twice-daily dog walking service ensures the dog gets adequate exercise without overtaxing their owner. For villages with on-lead requirements, a professional walker who knows the local dog-friendly areas is invaluable.

Medication Administration

Senior pets often need daily medication — arthritis supplements, heart medication, thyroid tablets, or insulin injections. A pet sitter trained in medication administration can handle this reliably, reducing stress for both the owner and the animal.

Vet Visit Transportation

Transport to and from veterinary appointments is one of the most requested services for senior pet owners. A sitter who can transport your pet to routine check-ups or emergency visits removes a significant logistical barrier.

Hospitalisation Care

If a senior is hospitalised or requires a period of residential care, their pet needs immediate, reliable care. Having a pre-arranged pet sitting plan means there is no panic about who will look after the animal. This is one of the most valuable services for senior pet owners and their families.

Companionship Visits

For cats and small animals that do not need walking, a daily companionship visit provides feeding, litter tray cleaning, play, and social interaction — ensuring the pet remains happy and healthy even when the owner has a busy day or is feeling unwell.

Preparing Your Pet for a Move

Moving is stressful for animals. Here are steps to minimise disruption:

  1. Maintain routine: Keep feeding times, walk times, and sleep arrangements as consistent as possible before, during, and after the move
  2. Use pheromone products: Feliway for cats and Adaptil for dogs can reduce anxiety during the transition
  3. Introduce the new space gradually: If possible, bring your pet for visits before the move so they can explore the new environment
  4. Create a safe zone: Set up one room with familiar bedding, toys, and scent items before the pet arrives
  5. Vet check before moving: Ensure vaccinations, registration, and any health concerns are addressed before the transition
  6. Book a pet sitter for moving day: Having someone dedicated to your pet during the chaos of moving day reduces stress for everyone
  7. Register with a local vet: Find and register with a vet near your new home before you move, so you have immediate support if needed

Talking to Family About Pet Care Planning

One of the most important conversations older pet owners can have with family is about pet care contingency planning. Key topics to discuss:

  • Who takes the pet if you can no longer care for it? Have this conversation early and get a commitment in writing
  • Financial provision: Consider setting aside a pet care fund or including pet provisions in your will
  • Emergency contacts: Ensure your village manager, a neighbour, and a family member all know your pet care plan and your sitter contact details
  • Vet authority: Arrange authorisation for a trusted person to make veterinary decisions on your behalf if you are unavailable
  • Standing pet sitting arrangement: Pre-book a recurring sitting service so care is automatic, not crisis-driven
