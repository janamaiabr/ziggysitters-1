Pet-Friendly Christmas in New Zealand: Safety, Travel & Holiday Pet Care Guide

23 May 2026 | ZiggySitters Team

Christmas in New Zealand means barbecues on the beach, long summer evenings, and family get-togethers. But the festive season also brings unique challenges for pet owners. From Christmas hazards lurking in the lounge to the summer heat that hits its peak in December, keeping your pet safe and happy during the holidays takes planning. Whether you are travelling, hosting, or staying home, this guide covers everything you need for a pet-friendly Kiwi Christmas.

Christmas Hazards: What to Watch Out For

Food Dangers

Christmas dinner is a minefield of foods that are dangerous or toxic to pets. Ham and other cured meats are high in salt and fat, which can cause pancreatitis in dogs. Christmas pudding and mince pies contain raisins and sultanas, which are highly toxic to both dogs and cats and can cause kidney failure even in small amounts. Chocolate, onions, garlic, macadamia nuts, and alcohol are all serious hazards. Keep all festive food well out of reach and remind guests not to share their plate with your pet, no matter how persuasive those puppy eyes are.

Make sure bins are secure too. Dogs are notorious bin divers, and a leftover ham bone or discarded Christmas pudding wrapper can be just as dangerous as the food itself. A dedicated pet-proof bin or keeping bins in a closed cupboard is a smart move during the festive period.

Decorations

Christmas trees, tinsel, baubles, and lights all pose risks. Tinsel is particularly dangerous for cats, who love to play with it but can suffer intestinal blockages if they swallow it. Glass baubles can shatter and cut paws or mouths. Christmas tree lights have electrical cords that chew-happy puppies and kittens might target. Consider placing fragile decorations higher on the tree, skipping tinsel altogether, or using a pet-friendly barrier around the tree base.

Snow globes deserve a special mention. Some imported snow globes contain ethylene glycol, the same toxic ingredient in antifreeze. If one breaks and your pet licks the liquid, it can be fatal. Keep them well out of reach or choose pet-safe alternatives.

Presents and Packaging

Wrapping paper, ribbons, and gift bags are irresistible to curious pets. Ribbons can cause intestinal blockages, and small parts from opened gifts can be choking hazards. Keep wrapped presents in a closed room until Christmas morning, and clean up packaging promptly after opening. Batteries from new toys and gadgets are another hazard, containing corrosive chemicals that can burn your pet's mouth and oesophagus.

Plants

Several popular Christmas plants are toxic to pets. Poinsettia can cause vomiting and irritation. Holly berries are moderately toxic. Mistletoe is highly toxic and can cause serious illness. Lilies, often given as gifts at this time of year, are extremely dangerous for cats. Even small amounts of lily pollen on a cat's fur, licked off during grooming, can cause fatal kidney failure. If you have cats, do not bring lilies into the house at all.

Summer Christmas: Heat and Sun Safety

New Zealand Christmas falls in midsummer, which means heat is a genuine concern. Dogs can suffer heatstroke in temperatures above 25 degrees, especially if exercising or left in warm conditions. Never leave your dog in a car, even with windows down. Provide plenty of shade and fresh water. Avoid walking dogs on hot pavement. If you cannot hold the back of your hand on the pavement for five seconds comfortably, it is too hot for your dog's paws.

Cats are more resilient in the heat but still need access to cool, shaded areas and multiple water sources. Consider adding ice cubes to water bowls on particularly hot days. Light-coloured and thin-coated dogs are susceptible to sunburn, particularly on the nose, ears, and belly. Pet-safe sunscreen is available from most vets and pet stores.

Travelling With Pets Over Christmas

Road Trips

If you are hitting the road for a Christmas holiday, plan your journey with your pet in mind. Never let your dog ride with its head out the window, as debris can cause eye injuries. Use a proper harness, crate, or pet seatbelt. Plan stops every two hours for exercise, water, and toilet breaks. Many New Zealand rest areas have water and shade, but carry your own supply as backup.

Finding Pet-Friendly Accommodation

New Zealand has excellent pet-friendly holiday parks, motels, and bach rentals, but they book out fast over Christmas. Book early and confirm pet policies in writing, as policies can change between booking and arrival. Look for properties with secure fencing, shaded outdoor areas, and proximity to dog-friendly beaches or walking tracks. Bring your pet's familiar bedding, bowls, and toys to help them settle into a new environment.

Beach Safety

Many New Zealand beaches have dog restrictions during summer, particularly between 9am and 7pm from December through February. Check local council rules before heading out. Even at dog-friendly beaches, watch for hazards like jellyfish, which are common in NZ waters during summer and can sting curious noses. Salt water can also cause stomach upsets if your dog drinks too much, so bring fresh water to the beach. After a swim, rinse your dog to remove salt and sand that can irritate the skin.

Leaving Your Pet at Home: Holiday Pet Care Options

In-Home Pet Sitting

In-home pet sitting is the gold standard for holiday pet care. Your pet stays in their own environment with familiar smells, sounds, and routines, which dramatically reduces stress compared to boarding. A live-in sitter also provides security for your home and can manage garden watering, mail collection, and other household tasks. Drop-In Visits

For independent cats and low-maintenance pets, drop-in visits work well. A sitter visits once or twice daily to feed, clean litter trays, administer medication, and provide companionship. This is more affordable than live-in care and suits pets that are comfortable being alone for parts of the day.

Boarding Kennels and Catteries

Boarding facilities vary widely in quality. Book well ahead for Christmas, as the best ones fill up months in advance. Visit before booking to check cleanliness, ventilation, exercise areas, and staff-to-animal ratios. Ensure your pet's vaccinations are current, as all reputable facilities require proof. The downside is that boarding can be stressful, particularly for anxious pets or those unused to being away from home.

Managing Pet Anxiety During the Festive Season

Christmas disrupts your pet's routine. Visitors coming and going, unusual noises, changes to feeding and walking schedules, and the general chaos of the holidays can trigger anxiety. Dogs may bark more, become clingy, or show destructive behaviour. Cats may hide, stop eating, or urinate outside the litter box.

Maintain routine as much as possible. Feed at the same times, walk at the same times, and keep bedtime consistent. Create a safe retreat where your pet can escape the festivities, a quiet room with their bed, water, and familiar toys. If your pet is particularly anxious, discuss options with your vet before the season starts. Pheromone diffusers like Adaptil for dogs and Feliway for cats can help reduce stress.

Christmas Pet Safety Checklist

  • Secure all festive food out of pet reach
  • Use pet-proof bins or keep bins in cupboards
  • Place fragile decorations high on the tree
  • Skip tinsel or use it only out of pet reach
  • Keep lilies out of the house entirely if you have cats
  • Secure electrical cords from Christmas lights
  • Keep wrapping materials cleaned up promptly
  • Provide plenty of shade and fresh water during summer heat
  • Check local beach dog restrictions before visiting
  • Maintain your pet's feeding and exercise routine
  • Create a quiet retreat space for your pet
  • Book your pet sitter early for Christmas and New Year
  • Update your pet's microchip details and ensure tags are current
  • Keep your vet's emergency contact and after-hours number handy
