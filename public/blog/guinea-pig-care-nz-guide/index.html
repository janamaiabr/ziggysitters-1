Guinea Pig Care Guide for NZ Owners and Pet Sitters

Published 29 March 2026 • 9 min read • By ZiggySitters

Guinea pigs are one of New Zealand's most popular small pets, particularly in family households with children. Friendly, vocal, and surprisingly expressive, they make wonderful companions. But guinea pigs are also frequently misunderstood — they are not low-maintenance pets and have specific welfare needs around companionship, space, diet, and temperature that many owners underestimate. Whether you're a new guinea pig owner or a pet sitter taking on your first cavy clients, this guide covers what you actually need to know.

Quick Answer: What Do Guinea Pigs Need?

Guinea pigs need a spacious cage (minimum 1.2m x 0.6m for a pair), constant access to grass hay, daily fresh vegetables rich in vitamin C, a companion of their own species, and an environment between 18 and 24 degrees Celsius. They cannot produce vitamin C themselves and will develop scurvy without it. They are social animals and should never be housed alone long-term.

Why Guinea Pigs Are Not Starter Pets

The idea that guinea pigs are a simple starter pet for children is one of the most persistent misconceptions in pet ownership. In reality, they:

  • Live 5–8 years, requiring a long-term commitment
  • Need daily fresh food preparation — not just a handful of dry pellets
  • Require a companion to avoid loneliness and stress
  • Hide illness very effectively — regular health checks are essential
  • Cannot tolerate cold NZ winters without appropriate shelter
  • Need much more space than most standard cages sold in pet shops provide

This does not mean they are difficult pets — it means they deserve an owner who understands their needs. When those needs are met, guinea pigs are genuinely rewarding, interactive companions with distinct personalities.

Housing: Space Matters More Than You Think

The most common welfare failure for guinea pigs in NZ is inadequate space. The small cages sold in many pet stores are not sufficient for adult guinea pigs.

Minimum Space Requirements

Number of Guinea Pigs Minimum Cage Size Recommended Size
1 (not recommended) 0.7m x 0.4m 0.9m x 0.6m
2 (ideal pairing) 1.2m x 0.6m 1.5m x 0.6m
3–4 1.8m x 0.6m 2.0m x 0.7m

C&C (coroplast and cubes) cages are popular among NZ guinea pig owners because they are affordable, customisable, and can be expanded easily. Avoid tall cages with multiple levels as guinea pigs are ground-dwelling animals and do not use height effectively — they need floor space, not vertical space.

Indoor vs Outdoor Housing in NZ

Guinea pigs can live outdoors in NZ but only in appropriate conditions. They are temperature-sensitive, and NZ weather can be unpredictable.

  • Summer: Outdoor hutches are suitable in most parts of NZ, but direct sun and temperatures above 28°C can cause heatstroke. Shade and ventilation are essential.
  • Winter: In the South Island, Waikato, and Central North Island, outdoor housing in winter is risky. Move guinea pigs inside or into an insulated, heated shed. Temperatures below 15°C stress guinea pigs and increase illness risk.
  • Year-round indoor: Many NZ owners now keep guinea pigs indoors in C&C cages. Indoor pigs tend to be more social and interactive because they have more contact with the family.
Predator Warning: NZ has significant predator pressure from cats, dogs, rats, stoats, and weasels. Outdoor hutches must have secure, predator-proof latches and solid flooring or a wire-bottomed frame on legs. A predator attack through the bars of a hutch can cause fatal stress in guinea pigs even without direct injury.

Feeding Guinea Pigs in New Zealand

Diet is where many guinea pig owners go wrong. Guinea pigs have very specific nutritional requirements, and getting this right is the single biggest factor in their health and longevity.

The Three Pillars of Guinea Pig Diet

1. Hay (80% of diet)

Fresh grass hay — Timothy, Meadow, or Orchard grass — should be available at all times and make up the bulk of the diet. Hay is essential for:

  • Dental health: guinea pig teeth grow continuously and hay wears them down
  • Digestive health: fibre keeps the gut moving properly
  • Mental enrichment: foraging through hay is natural behaviour

Lucerne (alfalfa) hay is too high in calcium for adult guinea pigs and should be reserved for juveniles and pregnant or nursing sows.

2. Fresh Vegetables and Leafy Greens (15–20% of diet)

This is where vitamin C is delivered. Aim for approximately one cup of fresh greens per guinea pig per day.

Food Vitamin C Content Frequency
Red or yellow capsicum Very high Daily — excellent staple
Kale High 3–4x per week
Parsley High 3–4x per week
Silverbeet / spinach Moderate 2x per week max (high oxalates)
Lettuce (not iceberg) Low Daily as bulk filler
Cucumber Low Daily as hydration source

3. Pellets (5% of diet)

A small amount of high-quality guinea pig pellets (1–2 tablespoons per pig per day) supplements the diet but should not be the main food source. Choose plain pellets without seeds, dried fruit, or coloured pieces — these are added for visual appeal but offer poor nutrition and encourage selective feeding.

Foods That Are Toxic to Guinea Pigs:
  • Rhubarb (leaves and stalks both toxic)
  • Onion, garlic, and leeks
  • Potato (especially green parts)
  • Avocado
  • Chocolate and any sugary human food
  • Dairy products
  • Seeds and nuts

The Companionship Requirement

Guinea pigs are herd animals. In the wild they live in groups and communicate constantly through vocalisations, grooming, and body language. A solitary guinea pig is a stressed guinea pig.

The best pairings:

  • Two females (sows): Usually peaceful together, especially if introduced young
  • A neutered male with one or two females: Often the most harmonious group dynamic
  • Two males (boars): Possible if introduced as juveniles and given ample space, but boars can become territorial as adults
Bonding Tip: When introducing a new guinea pig to an existing one, use a neutral space that neither pig considers their territory. Place both pigs in a temporary pen with extra food, water, and hiding spots so neither feels cornered. Quarantine new animals for two weeks to check for illness before introduction.

Daily Health Checks for Pet Sitters

Guinea pigs are prey animals and instinctively hide signs of illness. By the time a guinea pig looks obviously unwell, it is often seriously ill. Daily health checks by pet sitters are therefore important.

Check the following each day:

  • Eyes: Clear and bright. Any discharge, cloudiness, or swelling is a concern.
  • Nose: Dry. Runny or crusty nose can indicate respiratory infection.
  • Coat: Smooth and full. Bald patches, dull coat, or excessive scratching can indicate mites, ringworm, or nutritional deficiency.
  • Teeth: Front incisors visible and even. Misaligned or overgrown teeth prevent eating.
  • Appetite: Guinea pigs should eat enthusiastically. Reduced interest in food or hay is a serious early warning sign.
  • Droppings: Small, firm, and plentiful. Soft, misshapen, or absent droppings indicate digestive problems.
  • Movement: Confident walking. Stumbling, tilting, or reluctance to move can indicate inner ear infection or injury.
  • Behaviour: Alert and vocal. A quiet, hunched guinea pig is almost always unwell.
When to Call the Vet: If a guinea pig refuses food for more than 12 hours, is not producing droppings, has laboured breathing, or is unable to walk, seek veterinary care immediately. Guinea pigs can deteriorate very rapidly and waiting is dangerous.

Handling Guinea Pigs Safely

Guinea pigs can be nervous about being picked up, especially by strangers. Pet sitters should approach slowly and speak quietly before attempting to pick up an unfamiliar guinea pig.

Correct handling technique:

  1. Let the guinea pig sniff your hand first
  2. Scoop from underneath with one hand supporting the hindquarters
  3. Use the other hand to secure the back and prevent the pig from jumping
  4. Hold close to your body — guinea pigs feel more secure when held firmly (but not tightly) against you
  5. Never pick up a guinea pig by the scruff or hold it upside down

Guinea pigs do not appreciate being held for long periods if they are not used to it. Short, regular handling sessions are better than infrequent long ones. If the owner's guinea pig is not used to being picked up, it may be best to interact with them in their enclosure rather than lifting them out.

Common Guinea Pig Health Issues in NZ

  • Scurvy (vitamin C deficiency): Lethargy, rough coat, joint pain, weight loss. Prevented entirely by adequate fresh greens. Treated with vitamin C supplementation under vet guidance.
  • Respiratory infections: Common in cold, damp NZ winters. Signs include laboured breathing, nasal discharge, and lethargy. Requires prompt vet treatment as guinea pigs deteriorate quickly.
  • Dental disease: Overgrown molars (often not visible) can make eating painful. A guinea pig that drops food or loses weight rapidly may have dental problems. Requires a vet examination.
  • Skin mites: Cause intense itching, hair loss, and skin irritation. Highly treatable with vet-prescribed medication. Avoid over-the-counter products not specifically labelled for guinea pigs.
  • Bloat and GI stasis: A hard, distended abdomen or complete cessation of droppings is a medical emergency. Caused by digestive disruption, often from diet changes or stress.

What to Tell Your Guinea Pigs' Pet Sitter

When leaving your guinea pigs with a sitter, provide:

  • Their names and individual personalities (guinea pigs have distinct characters)
  • Exact food list — what they eat, how much, how often
  • Which vegetables they like and which they refuse
  • Cage cleaning schedule and products to use
  • Normal behaviour patterns — what is typical for your pigs
  • Any known health conditions and current medications
  • Vet contact details and nearest emergency vet
  • Whether they are used to being held or prefer cage-based interaction

Common Mistakes Guinea Pig Owners Make

  • Buying a single guinea pig: Loneliness is a genuine welfare issue. Always get at least two.
  • Feeding too many pellets and not enough hay: Pellets are a supplement, not the main meal. Hay is the foundation.
  • Ignoring vitamin C: Guinea pigs cannot survive without it. Fresh capsicum daily is the easiest reliable source.
  • Using a cage that is too small: The standard pet store hutch is typically inadequate. Measure before you buy.
  • Waiting too long to see a vet: Because guinea pigs hide illness, by the time you can clearly see something is wrong, it may already be serious.

Key Takeaways

  • Guinea pigs need a companion — they are not suited to solitary living
  • Space is critical: minimum 1.2m x 0.6m for a pair
  • Hay must be available at all times and make up the bulk of the diet
  • Vitamin C from fresh vegetables (especially capsicum) is non-negotiable
  • NZ winters can be too cold for outdoor guinea pigs without proper insulation
  • Daily health checks catch problems early — guinea pigs hide illness until it is advanced
  • With proper care, guinea pigs live 5–8 years and make interactive, vocal companions

Frequently Asked Questions

Can guinea pigs live alone in NZ?

Guinea pigs are social animals and should ideally live in pairs or small groups. A single guinea pig can become lonely and develop stress-related health issues. In Switzerland it is actually illegal to keep a single guinea pig. Two same-sex guinea pigs or a neutered male with a female is the most practical setup.

How often should guinea pigs be fed?

Guinea pigs should have access to unlimited grass hay at all times. Fresh leafy greens and vegetables are given daily (roughly one cup per pig), and a small amount of pellets (1–2 tablespoons) per pig per day. Fresh water must always be available.

What temperature do guinea pigs need in NZ?

Guinea pigs are comfortable between 18 and 24 degrees Celsius. They are sensitive to cold (below 15 degrees) and heat (above 28 degrees). NZ winters can be too cold for outdoor guinea pigs, especially in the South Island and Central North Island.

Do guinea pigs need vitamin C?

Yes, guinea pigs cannot produce their own vitamin C and must get it from food. Without enough vitamin C they develop scurvy. Fresh leafy greens like capsicum, kale, and parsley are excellent sources. Vitamin C drops in water are less reliable as the vitamin degrades quickly.

How long do guinea pigs live?

With good care, guinea pigs live 4 to 8 years. The average is around 5 to 6 years. Providing the right diet, housing, companionship, and regular health checks significantly improves lifespan and quality of life.
