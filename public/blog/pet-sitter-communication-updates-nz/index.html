Pet Sitter Communication: How to Get Great Updates While You\u2019re Away

Published 12 June 2026 • 9 min read • ZiggySitters NZ

You\u2019ve booked the sitter, packed your bags, and you\u2019re ready to go. But as you sit at 30,000 feet or on a beach in Fiji, a nagging thought creeps in: Is my fur baby okay? Good communication with your pet sitter is the difference between a relaxing holiday and a stress-fuelled one. Here\u2019s how to set it up right.

Why Communication Matters More Than You Think

A 2025 survey of New Zealand pet owners found that 73% of pet parents say their biggest worry when travelling is not knowing how their pet is doing. Not the cost, not the logistics \u2014 the silence. When a sitter sends regular updates, it doesn\u2019t just reassure you. It also creates accountability. A sitter who commits to daily photos and reports is a sitter who\u2019s actively engaged with your pet, not just clocking in and out.

The research is clear: Pet owners who establish a communication plan before leaving report 60% less anxiety about their pet\u2019s wellbeing during trips. The plan doesn\u2019t need to be complex \u2014 it just needs to exist.

Setting Up Your Communication Plan

Before you hand over the keys, sit down (or video call) with your sitter and agree on how you\u2019ll stay in touch. A good communication plan covers four things:

1. Frequency of Updates

Be specific. \u201CKeep me posted\u201D is vague. Instead, try:

  • Standard visits (1\u20132 per day): One photo and a brief text after each visit
  • Overnight/house sitting: Morning check-in, evening update, and anytime something unusual happens
  • Extended stays (3+ days): Daily written summary covering food, activity, mood, and any concerns
Be realistic: If you\u2019re asking for updates every two hours, you\u2019re micromanaging. Trust your sitter. One to two meaningful updates per day is the sweet spot for most situations.

2. Preferred Communication Channel

Choose one primary channel and stick to it. Switching between WhatsApp, text, email, and Messenger creates confusion and missed messages.

Channel Best For Limitation
WhatsApp Photos, quick updates, voice notes Needs both parties on the app
Text/SMS Short updates, urgent matters Photos can be lower quality
Messenger Video calls, photo albums Can get lost in other chats
Email Detailed daily reports, medical records Too slow for urgent matters
ZiggySitters platform Bookings, payments, official records Use alongside a direct channel

3. What to Include in Updates

A good update is more than a cute photo. Ask your sitter to cover:

  • Eating: Did the pet eat their full meal? Partially? Not at all?
  • Toilet habits: Normal? Any diarrhoea, constipation, or accidents?
  • Energy and mood: Playful, relaxed, anxious, hiding?
  • Activity: Walk duration and behaviour on walk (dogs), play sessions (cats)
  • Anything unusual: Limping, scratching, vomiting, excessive drinking
Red flag language to watch for: If your sitter says \u201Cseems a bit off\u201D or \u201Cnot quite themselves,\u201D ask follow-up questions. Vague language can mask real problems. A good sitter will be specific: \u201CBuddy didn\u2019t finish breakfast and has been hiding under the bed since 10am.\u201D

4. Emergency Protocols

This is non-negotiable. Before you leave, provide your sitter with:

  • Your vet\u2019s name, address, and phone number
  • After-hours emergency vet contact
  • Your authorisation for emergency treatment (up to a specified amount)
  • Your mobile number and an alternative contact (partner, family member)
  • Pet insurance details if applicable
  • A signed letter authorising the sitter to make veterinary decisions on your behalf
Critical: Agree on a financial limit for emergency vet visits. If your dog needs emergency surgery at 2am and you\u2019re on a flight with no signal, your sitter needs to know whether they\u2019re authorised to approve a $3,000 procedure or whether they should opt for stabilisation only until they reach you. Write it down. Sign it. Give your vet a copy too.

Photo and Video Updates: What to Expect

Photos are the gold standard of pet sitting updates. Here\u2019s what to expect and what to ask for:

What Good Updates Look Like

  • Morning: A photo of your pet eating breakfast or going for their morning walk
  • Afternoon: A short video of play time or a walk \u2014 this shows energy levels better than a still photo
  • Evening: A photo of your pet settled and relaxed, confirming the day ended well

What Photos Should Show

  • Your pet\u2019s face and body \u2014 not just a tail disappearing around a corner
  • Their environment \u2014 is the house tidy? Is their water bowl full?
  • Natural behaviour \u2014 a sleeping cat or a dog with a wagging tail tells you more than a posed shot

Video Updates

A 10-second video is worth a dozen photos. Video shows:

  • Gait and movement (spot limping early)
  • Energy levels and enthusiasm
  • Whether your pet is engaging with the sitter or avoiding them
  • Body language that photos can\u2019t capture

Common Communication Mistakes

Mistake 1: Not Setting Expectations Upfront

The biggest source of pet sitter drama isn\u2019t competence \u2014 it\u2019s mismatched expectations. If you want three photos a day, say so. If one text at night is enough, say that too. The sitter isn\u2019t a mind reader.

Mistake 2: Micromanaging from Afar

Texting every hour to ask \u201CIs Fluffy okay?\u201D doesn\u2019t help your pet. It stresses you and irritates the sitter. Set the communication plan, then trust the process. If something\u2019s wrong, you\u2019ll hear about it.

Mistake 3: Going Silent

This cuts both ways. If you\u2019re going to be unreachable for a period (long-haul flight, remote area with no signal), tell your sitter in advance. Give them an alternative contact. And if your sitter stops updating you, that\u2019s a red flag worth following up on immediately.

Mistake 4: Only Communicating About Problems

If every message from your sitter is about an issue \u2014 the dog wouldn\u2019t eat, the cat vomited, the fence blew down \u2014 you\u2019ll start dreading their messages. Ask for the good stuff too. \u201CBuddy had a great walk\u201D and \u201CMittens is purring on my lap\u201D are updates you want to receive.

What to Do When Updates Are Concerning

Sometimes an update reveals something worrying. Here\u2019s how to handle it:

Minor Concerns (didn\u2019t finish one meal, mild lethargy)

  • Ask for another update in 2\u20134 hours
  • Check if environmental factors could explain it (hot day, disrupted routine, new food)
  • If it resolves within 12 hours, it\u2019s likely stress-related and not concerning

Moderate Concerns (skipped two meals, vomiting once, limping)

  • Ask the sitter to monitor closely and update in 1\u20132 hours
  • Call your vet for phone advice \u2014 most NZ vet clinics offer free phone consultations
  • Have the sitter check for other symptoms (gum colour, hydration, temperature)

Emergency (collapse, difficulty breathing, seizures, severe bleeding)

  • Instruct sitter to go to the nearest emergency vet IMMEDIATELY
  • Call the vet yourself to provide medical history and authorisation
  • Contact your emergency backup person (friend/family) to meet the sitter at the vet
  • Most NZ after-hours emergency vets: Vetcare Auckland, After Hours Vet Wellington, VetCare Christchurch

Communication for Different Sitter Types

Drop-In Visits (1\u20132 per day)

Your sitter is only there for 30\u201360 minutes. You should expect:

  • One update per visit (photo + brief text)
  • Immediate notification of any problems
  • Confirmation that the house is secure when leaving

Overnight House Sitting

Your sitter is living in your home. You can reasonably expect:

  • Morning and evening updates
  • Photos during walks, play, and meals
  • Daily summary of overall wellbeing
  • Immediate notification of anything unusual

Boarding at Sitter\u2019s Home

Your pet is in someone else\u2019s environment. Extra communication is important:

  • Confirmation of safe arrival
  • Settling-in update within the first few hours
  • Daily photos and reports
  • Immediate notification if your pet shows stress signs

Technology That Helps

Several tools can supplement sitter communication:

  • Pet cameras (Furbo, Petcube): Check in visually any time. Best for drop-in visits where the house is empty between visits. Available from $150\u2013$350 from NZ retailers like Noel Leeming and Harvey Norman.
  • Smart feeders: Confirm your pet is eating by tracking food consumption. Some models let you dispense treats remotely.
  • GPS trackers (Tractive, Fi): Essential for dogs with outdoor access. Shows real-time location and activity levels.
  • Shared photo albums: Create a shared Google Photos or iCloud album so both you and the sitter can add photos in one place.
A word on cameras: Be transparent. Tell your sitter about any cameras in your home and where they are. Hidden cameras erode trust and may violate privacy expectations. In New Zealand, you generally can\u2019t record audio without consent, so stick to video-only pet cameras in common areas.

The Meet-and-Greet: Your Communication Foundation

Good communication starts before you leave. During your meet-and-greet:

  • Walk the sitter through your pet\u2019s daily routine in detail
  • Show them where everything is (food, medication, leads, litter scoop)
  • Discuss your pet\u2019s quirks and anxieties honestly
  • Share your communication preferences and agree on the plan
  • Exchange all relevant contact numbers (yours, emergency contact, vet, neighbour)
  • Do a trial run \u2014 have the sitter visit while you\u2019re home so your pet can warm up to them

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should my pet sitter send updates?

For drop-in visits, expect one update per visit. For overnight house sitting, morning and evening updates are standard. If you want more, specify this when booking. Most good sitters are happy to send additional updates if asked.

What if my sitter isn\u2019t sending updates?

First, send a polite message asking for an update. If you don\u2019t hear back within a few hours, call them. If they\u2019re unreachable for an extended period, have your emergency contact check on your home. This is why having a backup contact and a neighbour who knows the sitter is important.

Should I ask for video updates or just photos?

Videos are more informative \u2014 they show movement, energy, and behaviour that photos can\u2019t capture. But they\u2019re also larger files that take longer to send. A good compromise: one video per day (30 seconds is plenty) plus 2\u20133 photos.

Is it okay to ask my sitter to FaceTime me with my pet?

Some sitters are happy to do a quick video call, especially if your pet is settling in. But respect their time \u2014 don\u2019t request daily FaceTime sessions. A pre-arranged video call during the meet-and-greet or on the first day is reasonable. After that, stick to your agreed communication plan.

What information should I leave for my pet sitter?

At minimum: feeding schedule and portions, medication instructions, vet contact details, emergency contact, your travel itinerary, house instructions (alarm, rubbish day, heating), and your pet\u2019s quirks and preferences. Leave it all in one visible place \u2014 a folder on the kitchen counter works well.
