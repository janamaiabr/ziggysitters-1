Introducing a New Baby to Your Pet: A Safe, Step-by-Step Guide for NZ Families

2 April 2026 · 11 min read · ZiggySitters Team

Bringing a new baby home is one of the biggest transitions in a family's life — and it is a significant change for your pets too. From a dog's perspective, a new baby is a strange small creature that smells unusual, makes unpredictable sounds, and suddenly occupies enormous amounts of their family's attention. How you manage this transition can shape the relationship between your pets and your child for years to come.

The good news: with thoughtful preparation, the vast majority of dogs and cats settle into life with a baby beautifully. Many become devoted protectors and lifelong companions. This guide walks you through the process step by step, from months before the birth right through to the first year of life with a baby and pet under the same roof.

Start Preparing Before the Baby Arrives

The preparation phase begins months before your due date, not the day you come home from the hospital. Pets who have been gradually prepared for the coming changes handle the transition far better than those for whom everything shifts overnight.

Update obedience foundations

Before the baby arrives, your dog should reliably respond to a handful of core cues: sit, down, stay, off (for jumping), leave it, and go to your place. You do not need a perfectly trained dog — but these basics give you the ability to manage situations safely once you are exhausted and holding a newborn. If your dog needs a refresher, New Zealand has excellent reward-based trainers in most cities and towns. Look for trainers certified through the New Zealand Association of Professional Dog Trainers (NZAPDT).

Introduce baby-related items gradually

Months before the birth, begin bringing baby equipment into your home: the cot, pram, bouncy chair, changing table. Allow your dog or cat to investigate these items in a calm, supervised way. The goal is that by the time the baby arrives, none of this furniture is new or exciting — it is simply part of the environment.

Play baby sounds

Baby cries and babbling can be startling to animals who have never encountered them. Play recordings of infant sounds at low volume during pleasant activities — feeding time, play sessions — and gradually increase the volume over several weeks. By the time your baby actually cries at 3am, your dog will have heard something like it before and be less likely to react anxiously.

Introduce baby scents

Baby products have distinctive smells — nappy creams, baby lotion, formula — that are unfamiliar to pets. Start using these products on your skin or on a cloth a few weeks before the birth so the smells are not entirely new when the baby arrives. Many parents also recommend bringing home a used hospital blanket or item of clothing before the baby itself comes home, allowing the pet to investigate the baby's specific scent in a calm, no-pressure setting.

Adjust routine and space boundaries now

If you plan to restrict your dog from the nursery, establish that boundary before the baby comes home — not after. Changing rules in response to the baby's arrival can create negative associations between the baby and the loss of access or privileges. Similarly, if feeding or walking times will change, begin shifting them gradually now so the adjustment is not all tied to the arrival of the new family member.

One month before your due date

Book a visit with your vet for a health check and to discuss the upcoming change. Ensure all parasite prevention is current, vaccinations are up to date, and address any behavioural concerns before you are in survival mode with a newborn.

The Day You Come Home from Hospital

The moment of homecoming is emotionally charged for everyone — including your pets, who have missed you and are highly attuned to your energy. Managing this moment well sets a positive tone.

Ideally, have one parent or a support person greet the pets first before the baby comes through the door. Give your dog or cat their usual greeting — calm and warm — to take the edge off the excitement before you introduce the baby. If your dog is highly excitable, a quick walk or play session before you arrive home helps burn off energy.

When you are ready to introduce the baby, keep the dog on a lead for the initial introduction, not because the dog is dangerous but because it gives you control and keeps the interaction calm and brief. Allow your dog to sniff from a distance first while the baby is held securely. Use a relaxed, normal tone of voice — your pets read your emotional state, and an anxious owner creates an anxious pet.

Reward calm behaviour with praise and treats. Keep the first interaction short. There will be many, many more opportunities over the coming weeks and months — there is no need to rush a complete introduction on day one.

Never leave babies and pets unsupervised

No matter how trusted your pet is, infants and toddlers should never be left alone with any animal. This is not a reflection of your pet's character — it is simply the safest practice. Even the most gentle dog can be startled by sudden movement or sound from a baby.

Managing the First Weeks: Maintaining Your Pet's Wellbeing

The weeks after birth are exhausting. Routines collapse, attention is scarce, and your pet suddenly receives a fraction of their usual interaction. This is the period when pets are most at risk of developing anxiety or attention-seeking behaviours.

Protect your pet's routine as much as possible

Even small consistencies matter enormously to pets. If you can maintain feeding at roughly the same time, and provide a short walk or play session each day, this goes a long way toward preventing stress-related behaviours. If you genuinely cannot manage this during the newborn period, ask a friend, family member, or hire a dog walker or pet sitter to fill in. This is exactly the kind of support that NZ pet sitters provide — keeping a dog's walk routine intact when owners are stretched thin.

Give your pet one-on-one time daily

Even five to ten minutes of undivided attention — a short play session, a grooming moment, a calm cuddle — helps your pet feel they still have a place in the family. Try to give this attention proactively rather than only when the pet demands it, which can inadvertently reinforce attention-seeking behaviour.

Create positive associations with the baby

Every time the baby is present, something good happens for the pet: a treat, praise, a favourite toy. The goal is for your dog or cat to build an association between the baby's presence and good things. Feed your dog when you breastfeed. Give your cat a favourite treat when you change a nappy. Over weeks and months, these associations compound into genuine tolerance and often genuine affection.

Create a pet-safe retreat space

Your pet needs somewhere to go when the house is chaotic — a quiet room, a crate with comfortable bedding, a spot on a windowsill. This is especially important for cats, who need vertical space and escape routes when they feel overwhelmed. Ensure children cannot follow the pet into this space; it must be a genuine sanctuary.

Special Considerations for Cats

Cats are often less thought-about in new baby preparations, but they have their own adjustment needs. Cats are sensitive to routine disruption and environmental change, and the arrival of a baby can trigger stress-related behaviours including litter box avoidance, hiding, or overgrooming.

Old myths about cats and babies — such as the idea that cats will "steal a baby's breath" — have no basis in reality. Cats are simply warm, quiet beings who may be attracted to a sleeping baby's warmth and stillness. The sensible precaution is to use a cot net or keep the nursery door closed when the baby is sleeping unsupervised, not because of any malicious intent but simply to prevent a cat inadvertently sleeping on or near an infant.

For cats who become withdrawn or begin showing physical stress symptoms after a baby arrives — vomiting, changes in appetite, urinating outside the litter box — a veterinary assessment is worthwhile. Sometimes a short course of anti-anxiety medication can help bridge the adjustment period.

Pre-baby checklist for pet owners

  • Refresh dog obedience cues: sit, down, off, go to your place
  • Set up nursery equipment and allow pets to explore it calmly
  • Introduce baby sounds at low volume during pleasant activities
  • Establish any new space boundaries (nursery off-limits, etc.) now
  • Begin using baby products so scents are familiar
  • Book a vet check and ensure vaccinations and parasite prevention are current
  • Arrange backup care (dog walker, pet sitter) for the newborn period
  • Identify a certified trainer in case behavioural support is needed

As the Baby Grows: Toddlers and Pets

The toddler stage — roughly 12 months to 3 years — is statistically the highest-risk period for dog bites. Toddlers move unpredictably, squeal loudly, grab tails and ears, fall on animals, and do not yet understand that their behaviour might be unwelcome. Even the most tolerant dog has a threshold.

Your job during this phase is active supervision and ongoing education. At this age, children can begin to understand simple rules: gentle hands only, do not bother a dog who is eating or sleeping, do not approach a dog who has gone to their bed or crate. These rules need to be repeated often and modelled by adults.

Watch your dog's body language carefully during toddler-dog interactions. Signs that the dog is becoming uncomfortable — moving away, lip licking, yawning, stiffening — should prompt you to intervene and give the dog space. The dog is communicating, and respecting those signals prevents escalation. Never scold a dog for growling at a child; a growl is a warning, and punishing warnings teaches the dog to skip the warning and go straight to biting.

Teaching children to read dogs

From around 18 months, children can begin to understand the concept of asking permission before touching an animal, and to recognise a happy dog (loose, waggy) versus a worried one (still, turned away). Making this learning playful — "is this dog happy or worried?" as a game — plants seeds that protect children for life.

When to Get Professional Help

Most pets adjust to a new baby with time and patience. But some animals benefit from professional support, and recognising when to seek it early prevents small problems from becoming serious ones.

Consult a veterinary behaviourist or accredited trainer if:

  • Your dog has ever growled at, snapped at, or bitten a person
  • Your dog shows persistent, increasing anxiety that does not improve over weeks
  • Your dog resource-guards (growls over food, toys, or sleeping spots) and there is a baby in the home
  • Your cat is showing prolonged physical stress symptoms
  • You feel unsafe or uncertain about any interaction between your pet and baby

Getting support is a sign of good judgement, not failure. New Zealand has excellent veterinary behaviourists and reward-based trainers who specialise in exactly these situations. Early intervention is far more effective than waiting until a behaviour is entrenched.

Planning for Hospital Time: Who Cares for Your Pet?

A practical consideration that is easy to overlook: when you are in hospital for the birth, your pets need care. This is especially important if the birth is unexpected, early, or requires an extended stay. Plan this ahead of time rather than scrambling at the last minute.

Options in New Zealand include arranging for a trusted friend or family member, booking a pet sitter through a platform like ZiggySitters, or boarding your dog at a reputable facility. Whatever you choose, brief your backup carer fully on your pet's routine, medical needs, and any behavioural quirks well in advance. A meet-and-greet before the birth ensures your pet and the carer are already comfortable with each other when the time comes.

The Long View: Raising Children and Pets Together

Families who successfully navigate the early transitions often describe the bond that develops between their children and pets as one of the most rewarding parts of family life. Children who grow up with pets tend to develop empathy, responsibility, and confidence. Dogs and cats who are well-managed during the baby years often become the child's first best friend.

The key is investing in the early preparation, maintaining consistency, supervising interactions until children are old enough to be trusted, and advocating for your pet's wellbeing even when you are tired and stretched thin. Your pet has been part of your family since before the baby arrived. With the right support, they will be part of your child's earliest and most cherished memories too.
