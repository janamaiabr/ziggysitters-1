Pet CPR & Emergency First Response NZ Guide: Save Your Pet\'s Life

By ZiggySitters NZ  •  May 15, 2026  •  14 min read

Every pet owner dreads the moment their animal companion collapses, chokes, or stops breathing. In those critical minutes before you reach a vet, knowing pet CPR and emergency first response can be the difference between life and death. This guide covers step-by-step CPR for dogs and cats, how to respond to choking, bleeding, and shock, and how to prepare an emergency kit specific to New Zealand conditions.
Disclaimer: This guide is for educational purposes and does not replace professional veterinary care. Always call your vet or an emergency animal hospital immediately in a pet emergency. CPR and first aid are stopgap measures to keep your pet alive until professional help is available.

Know Your Pet\'s Normal Vital Signs

Before an emergency ever happens, learn your pet\'s resting vital signs. You need to know what is normal to recognise what is not:

Vital Sign Normal Dog Range Normal Cat Range How to Check
Heart rate (bpm) 60–140 (size-dependent) 120–180 Place hand on left side of chest, behind elbow
Breathing rate 10–30 breaths/min 20–30 breaths/min Watch chest rise and fall for 15 seconds, multiply by 4
Gum colour Pink and moist Pink and moist Lift the upper lip and check
Capillary refill time <2 seconds <2 seconds Press gum firmly, release, count seconds until colour returns
Temperature 37.5–39.2°C 37.8–39.2°C Digital rectal thermometer
Practise Now: Check your pet\'s vital signs when they are healthy and relaxed. Write the numbers down. In an emergency, you will immediately know if something is wrong. Practise finding the heartbeat and femoral pulse (inside the hind thigh) so you can do it quickly under pressure.

Pet CPR: Step by Step

Pet CPR follows the same basic principle as human CPR: chest compressions to circulate blood, rescue breaths to oxygenate it. But the technique differs significantly depending on the size of the animal.

Before You Start CPR

  1. Check for danger. Make sure the environment is safe for you (traffic, electrical hazards, other animals).
  2. Check responsiveness. Call your pet\'s name, tap their side, gently pinch the skin between their toes.
  3. Check for breathing. Watch the chest for 5–10 seconds. Is it rising and falling?
  4. Check for heartbeat. Place your hand on the left side of the chest, just behind the elbow.
  5. If no breathing AND no heartbeat: Begin CPR immediately.
Do NOT practise CPR on a healthy pet. Compressions can cause serious injury. Only perform CPR on an unresponsive, non-breathing animal with no heartbeat.

CPR for Dogs

Position: Lay the dog on their right side on a firm, flat surface. The left side (heart side) faces up.

Hand position depends on size:

  • Large dogs (over 15 kg): Stand or kneel over the dog. Place the heel of one hand on the widest part of the chest, with your other hand on top. Interlock your fingers.
  • Medium dogs (5–15 kg): Kneel beside the dog. Place one hand on the chest. You may cup the chest with your hand on the opposite side for more effective compressions.
  • Small dogs (under 5 kg): Wrap your hands around the chest, with your thumbs on the upper side and fingers supporting the underside. Squeeze to compress.

Compression technique:

  • Push hard and fast: compress the chest to one-third to one-half of its width
  • Rate: 100–120 compressions per minute (roughly to the beat of "Stayin\' Alive")
  • Allow the chest to fully recoil between compressions
  • Do not interrupt compressions for more than 10 seconds

Rescue breaths:

  • After every 30 compressions, give 2 rescue breaths
  • Close the dog\'s mouth, extend the neck, and blow into the nostrils
  • Blow just enough to see the chest rise visibly
  • For large dogs, use more air; for small dogs, use small puffs

CPR for Cats

Position: Lay the cat on their right side on a firm surface.
  • Use one hand to cup the chest, with your thumb on one side and fingers on the other
  • Compress at 100–120 compressions per minute
  • Compress the chest to one-third of its width
  • Give 2 rescue breaths after every 30 compressions
  • For rescue breaths: close the cat\'s mouth, extend the neck slightly, and blow gently into both nostrils
  • Cat lungs are small — use very gentle puffs. Over-inflating can damage the lungs

When to Stop CPR

  • Your pet starts breathing on their own and has a steady heartbeat
  • You reach a veterinary hospital and professionals take over
  • You have performed CPR for 20 minutes with no response (the chance of survival after this is extremely low)
  • You are physically exhausted and cannot continue
Transport During CPR: If possible, continue CPR while someone drives you to the vet. Lay your pet on the seat or floor and do compressions during transit. Every minute counts.

Choking: What to Do

Choking is one of the most frightening emergencies a pet owner can face. Dogs and cats explore the world with their mouths, making them vulnerable to obstruction from toys, bones, food, and household items.

Signs of Choking

  • Pawing at the mouth or face
  • Difficulty breathing or noisy breathing
  • Excessive drooling
  • Gagging or retching without producing anything
  • Blue or pale gums
  • Panicked behaviour, rubbing face on the ground

Choking First Aid for Dogs

  1. Stay calm. Your dog is already panicked. Your composure helps.
  2. Check inside the mouth. Open the mouth and look. If you can see the object and it is reachable, carefully try to remove it with your fingers or pliers. Do not push it further in.
  3. If you cannot remove it: Perform the Heimlich manoeuvre:
    • Large dogs: Stand behind the dog, place your fists just below the rib cage, and thrust upward and forward (similar to human Heimlich)
    • Small dogs: Hold the dog with their back against your chest. Place your fist just below the rib cage and thrust upward. Alternatively, lift the dog by their hind legs and give 5 sharp back blows between the shoulder blades
  4. If the dog is unconscious: Lay them on their side, place your hands on the abdomen just below the rib cage, and thrust upward toward the chest.
  5. Go to the vet immediately even if you dislodge the object. There may be tissue damage or remaining fragments.

Choking First Aid for Cats

  1. Gently open the mouth and look for the object. If visible and accessible, remove it carefully.
  2. If you cannot remove it, hold the cat with their back against your chest, place your fist just below the rib cage, and give 5 sharp upward thrusts.
  3. Alternatively, for small cats: hold them upside down by the hips (supporting the chest) and give 5 back blows between the shoulder blades.
  4. Never perform abdominal thrusts on a conscious cat that is still breathing — you could cause more harm.

Bleeding: How to Control It

Whether from a dog fight, a cut paw, or a traffic accident, bleeding must be controlled quickly.

Steps to Control Bleeding

  1. Apply direct pressure. Use a clean cloth, gauze, or clothing. Press firmly on the wound and do not lift to check. If the cloth soaks through, add another layer on top.
  2. Elevate the wound above the heart if possible (limb wounds).
  3. Apply a pressure bandage. Wrap firmly but not so tight that it cuts off circulation. You should be able to slip two fingers underneath.
  4. Do NOT remove embedded objects. If there is a stick, glass, or other object in the wound, stabilise it and let the vet remove it.
  5. Transport to the vet immediately while maintaining pressure.
Arterial Bleeding: Bright red, spurting blood indicates an artery. Apply maximum pressure and get to a vet immediately. This is life-threatening and cannot be managed at home.

Shock: Recognising and Responding

Shock is a life-threatening condition where the body\'s organs do not receive enough blood flow. It often follows trauma, severe bleeding, or allergic reactions.

Signs of Shock

  • Pale or white gums
  • Rapid heartbeat but weak pulse
  • Rapid, shallow breathing
  • Cold extremities (ears, paws)
  • Lethargy, weakness, or collapse
  • Dilated pupils

Shock First Aid

  1. Control any bleeding (see above)
  2. Lay your pet on their right side
  3. Elevate the hindquarters slightly (use a folded towel or cushion)
  4. Cover with a blanket or jacket to preserve body heat
  5. Keep your pet as still and calm as possible
  6. Transport to the vet immediately — shock is a veterinary emergency

New Zealand-Specific Emergency Considerations

Common NZ Hazards That Cause Pet Emergencies

  • Karaka tree berries: Highly toxic to dogs. Found in North Island and upper South Island. Symptoms: vomiting, weakness, seizures, death. If you suspect ingestion, go to the vet immediately.
  • Blue-green algae (cyanobacteria): Common in NZ lakes and slow-moving rivers during summer. Highly toxic. Keep dogs out of stagnant, greenish water.
  • Stingrays and jellyfish: Common at NZ beaches. Stingray barbs can cause deep puncture wounds. Jellyfish stings cause pain and swelling. Rinse with vinegar (jellyfish) or hot water (stingray).
  • Grass seeds (awns): Common in NZ grasslands. Can burrow into ears, eyes, and paws, causing abscesses. Check your dog after walks in long grass.
  • Earthquake injuries: NZ is seismically active. Pets may be injured by falling objects, or panicked and run onto roads.

Building Your Pet First Aid Kit

Every NZ pet household should have a dedicated pet first aid kit. Here is what to include:

Essentials

  • Gauze rolls and pads (various sizes)
  • Non-stick wound dressings
  • Adhesive tape (not sticking to fur)
  • Blunt-tipped bandage scissors
  • Antiseptic wipes or solution (chlorhexidine)
  • Antibiotic ointment
  • Digital rectal thermometer
  • Tweezers (for tick removal and splinters)
  • Disposable gloves
  • Cotton balls and cotton swabs
  • Emergency blanket (space blanket)
  • Pet first aid book or instruction card

NZ-Specific Additions

  • Tick removal tool (for NZ cattle ticks, which can affect dogs)
  • Vinegar (for jellyfish stings at the beach)
  • Hydrocortisone cream (for insect bites, common in NZ summer)
  • Saline solution (for flushing eyes after beach sand or grass seeds)
  • Tick twister or fine-point tweezers

For the Car

  • Keep a smaller version of the kit in your car
  • Include a spare leash, collar, and muzzle
  • Add a bottle of water and collapsible bowl
  • Keep a printed card with your vet\'s number, emergency vet number, and national poisons centre

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I hurt my pet by doing CPR?

CPR can cause rib fractures, lung bruising, or other injuries. However, these are treatable. If your pet is not breathing and has no heartbeat, they are already in the worst possible situation. The potential benefit (saving their life) far outweighs the risk of injury.

Should I do mouth-to-snout on my cat?

Yes. Close the cat\'s mouth, extend the neck slightly, and blow gently into both nostrils. Cat lungs are small — use very gentle puffs. Over-inflation can damage lung tissue.

What if my pet has a heartbeat but is not breathing?

Do rescue breaths only. Give one breath every 3–5 seconds for dogs (one breath every 3 seconds for cats). Check for a heartbeat every minute. If the heartbeat stops, begin full CPR.

Where can I learn pet CPR in New Zealand?

Several organisations offer pet first aid and CPR courses in NZ, including the New Zealand Red Cross (pet first aid modules), local veterinary clinics, and organisations like St John. Ask your vet about upcoming workshops — many clinics run them annually.

Conclusion

No one wants to use pet CPR. But if the moment comes, knowing what to do can save your animal companion\'s life. Learn the steps, practise checking your pet\'s vital signs when they are healthy, build a first aid kit, and save your emergency vet\'s number in your phone.

The most important thing is to stay calm and act quickly. In an emergency, seconds matter. Preparation — knowing CPR, having a kit, knowing where your nearest emergency vet is — turns panic into action.

Share this guide with your pet sitter, your family, and anyone who cares for your pet. The more people who know pet CPR, the safer our animals are.
