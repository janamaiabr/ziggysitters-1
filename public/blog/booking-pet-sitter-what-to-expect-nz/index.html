Stage 1: Searching and Shortlisting

Before you can book, you need to find the right sitter. Start your search at least two to three weeks before your dates, especially during peak periods like Christmas, school holidays, and long weekends. New Zealand pet sitters get booked out fast during December and January.

What to Look For

  • Location — Choose a sitter close to your home. Drop-in visits are more reliable when travel time is short, and overnight sitters settle in better when they are not commuting across the city.
  • Pet type experience — Filter for sitters who have experience with your type of pet. A dog-savvy sitter may not know how to handle a flighty cockatiel or a territorial cat.
  • Reviews and ratings — Prioritise sitters with verified reviews from pet owners in situations similar to yours. Detailed reviews beat a high star rating with no context.
  • Availability — Check their calendar matches your dates. Some sitters block out dates well in advance, so early searching gives you more options.
  • Services offered — Not every sitter does overnight stays. Some offer drop-in visits only, others specialise in dog walking, and some will house-sit with your pets. Know what you need before you start filtering.

Create a shortlist of three to five sitters. Having backups matters because your first choice may already be booked.

Stage 2: Making the First Enquiry

Once you have identified potential sitters, send an enquiry message. This is your chance to share key details and gauge the sitter is responsiveness. A good enquiry message includes:

  • The dates you need
  • The type and number of pets you have
  • What service you need (overnight stay, drop-in visits, dog walking, house-sit)
  • Any special requirements (medication, separation anxiety, breed-specific needs)
  • Your suburb or area

Be upfront about anything that might affect the sit. If your dog barks at strangers, your cat hides under the bed for two days, or your rabbit needs a specific type of hay, say so now. Surprises on day one help no one.

What to Expect in Response

A professional sitter will typically respond within 24 hours, often much sooner. Their response should acknowledge your specific needs, confirm availability, and ask follow-up questions. If a sitter takes days to reply or sends a generic copy-paste response, that tells you something about their communication style — and communication is one of the most important factors in a successful sit.

Stage 3: The Meet-and-Greet

This is the most important step in the entire process and one you should never skip. A meet-and-greet is an in-person (or video call) introduction between you, your pet, and the sitter before the booking is confirmed.

What Happens at a Meet-and-Greet

  • Your pet meets the sitter — Watch how they interact. Does your dog approach the sitter willingly? Does your cat retreat or come out to investigate? First impressions matter, and animals are excellent judges of character.
  • You discuss the routine — Walk through your pet is daily schedule: feeding times, walk lengths, medication, bedtime preferences, and any quirks. The more detail you share, the better the sitter can maintain normalcy.
  • You show them around your home — Point out where the food is stored, where the litter tray lives, where the leads hang, and any areas that are off-limits to the pet or the sitter.
  • You establish expectations — How often should the sitter send updates? Photos? Video calls? Are there specific times you will be unreachable? Clear expectations prevent misunderstandings later.

If you are booking from overseas or cannot meet in person, a video call is a solid alternative. It still lets your pet see and hear the sitter and gives you a chance to assess their energy and communication.

Red Flags at the Meet-and-Greet

  • The sitter seems disinterested or distracted
  • They do not ask questions about your pet is needs
  • Your pet shows clear distress that the sitter ignores or dismisses
  • They are vague about their availability or experience
  • They try to skip the meet-and-greet entirely

Trust your instincts. If the meet-and-greet does not feel right, move on to your next shortlisted sitter.

Stage 4: Confirming the Booking

After a successful meet-and-greet, it is time to confirm. On ZiggySitters, this means formally booking through the platform. Here is what to expect:

The Booking Details

  • Dates and times — Confirm exact arrival and departure times. If your flight lands at 10pm, make sure the sitter knows they need to stay until late.
  • Service type — Overnight house-sit, twice-daily drop-in visits, or another arrangement. Be specific.
  • Rate agreed — Confirm the total cost and what it includes. Does the rate cover all pets, or is there a per-pet surcharge? Are additional walks extra?
  • Cancellation policy — Understand what happens if you need to cancel or change dates. Good platforms have clear policies that protect both sides.

Paperwork

Even if you trust the sitter completely, a written agreement is wise. A pet sitting agreement should cover:

  • Emergency veterinary care authorisation
  • Vet contact details
  • Your contact details and an emergency backup contact
  • Specific care instructions (feeding, medication, exercise)
  • House rules (which rooms are accessible, alarm codes, plant watering)
  • What happens in a natural disaster (especially relevant in NZ — earthquakes, flooding)

Stage 5: Preparing Your Home and Pet

In the days before the sit, preparation makes everything smoother. Here is a checklist:

For Your Pet

  • Stock up on enough food, treats, and medication for the entire period plus a few extra days
  • Write a clear care sheet with feeding times, portions, and any medication instructions
  • Make sure vaccinations and registration are up to date (NZ law requires microchipping for dogs)
  • Leave something that smells like you — a worn t-shirt or blanket can comfort an anxious pet
  • If your pet has a favourite toy or bed, make sure it is accessible and in good condition

For Your Home

  • Clean and tidy so the sitter feels comfortable living there
  • Stock basic supplies — toilet paper, cleaning products, bin liners
  • Show where the fuse box, water shutoff, and emergency supplies are located
  • Leave clear instructions for appliances (heat pump, oven, alarm)
  • Arrange for bins to be put out and brought in if your sit will span collection day
  • Inform a trusted neighbour that a sitter will be staying

Emergency Contacts

  • Your vet is name, phone number, and address
  • The nearest after-hours emergency vet clinic
  • A friend or family member who can be reached if you are unavailable
  • Your own contact details, including where you will be staying

Stage 6: During the Sit

You have handed over the keys, met the sitter, and now you are away. What should you expect during the actual sit?

Updates and Communication

Most sitters send daily updates by default. This might include photos, short videos, or a text summary of how the day went. If you want more or fewer updates, say so upfront. Some owners prefer twice-daily check-ins; others are happy with a single evening photo. The key is that expectations are set and both sides stick to them.

If Something Goes Wrong

Pets get sick. Weather causes problems. Things break. A good sitter will:

  • Contact you immediately for anything serious
  • Use their judgment for minor decisions (e.g., your dog skipped one meal but seems fine otherwise)
  • Take your pet to the vet if needed, using the emergency veterinary authorisation you provided
  • Keep you informed, not keep problems from you

Trusting the Process

The hardest part for many pet owners is letting go. You chose this sitter for a reason — you read their reviews, met them, and felt confident. Trust them. Micromanaging from a distance creates stress for both you and the sitter. If you set clear instructions and expectations upfront, you should be able to enjoy your time away.

Stage 7: Coming Home

The sit is over and you are back. A few things make the transition smoother:

  • Arrive at the agreed time — Do not surprise the sitter by coming home half a day early or several hours late without notice.
  • Do a walk-through together — The sitter can point out anything that happened, share observations about your pet is behaviour, and hand back keys or alarm codes.
  • Ask questions — How did your pet eat? Were they anxious when you left? Did anything unusual happen? This information helps you and your next sitter.
  • Pay promptly — If payment has not already been processed through the platform, settle up immediately. Good sitters deserve timely payment.
  • Leave a review — Honest, detailed reviews help future pet owners and reward good sitters. ZiggySitters only allows reviews from verified bookings, so your feedback carries real weight.

Stage 8: Building a Long-Term Relationship

The best pet sitting arrangement is an ongoing one. Once you find a sitter you trust, the benefits compound:

  • Your pet gets familiar with the sitter, reducing anxiety with each visit
  • The sitter learns your pet is quirks and preferences, providing better care over time
  • You can skip the meet-and-greet for repeat bookings (though a quick check-in is always nice)
  • Last-minute bookings become easier when the sitter already knows your home and routine

Many NZ pet owners develop long-term relationships with their sitters — birthday cards for the dog, Christmas treats, and genuine friendship. It is one of the quiet benefits of using a platform that connects you with local people who genuinely love animals.

Common Questions About the Booking Process

How far in advance should I book?

During quiet periods, one to two weeks is usually enough. For Christmas, summer holidays, and long weekends, book three to four weeks ahead. Easter, Matariki, and Labour Weekend are also busy periods in NZ.

What if my pet does not like the sitter?

This is exactly what the meet-and-greet is for. If your pet clearly does not warm to the sitter, try another shortlisted candidate. Pets are honest, and their reaction is valuable data.

Can I book a sitter for just one day?

Yes. Many sitters offer single drop-in visits, which typically last 30 to 60 minutes. These are ideal for cats, small animals, and dogs who just need a walk and a feed during the workday.

What if I need to cancel?

Check the cancellation policy before you book. Most platforms, including ZiggySitters, have tiered policies — full refund if cancelled well in advance, partial refund for short-notice cancellations, and no refund for same-day cancellations. This protects both you and the sitter, who may have turned down other work to hold your dates.

Is it safe to give a stranger my house key?

Using a reputable platform adds a layer of trust. ZiggySitters verifies sitter identity, checks references, and provides insurance coverage. Combined with a meet-and-greet, reviews, and a written agreement, the risk is minimal. Many NZ pet owners also swap keys through a secure lockbox rather than handing them over in person.
