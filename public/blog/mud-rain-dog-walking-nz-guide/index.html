Mud & Rain Dog Walking Guide NZ: Keep Walking Through Winter

Published 1 June 2026 · ZiggySitters NZ

New Zealand winters bring rain — a lot of it. From Auckland drizzle to Dunedin downpours, the wet season can turn your daily dog walk into a muddy adventure. But your dog still needs exercise, rain or shine. The good news? With the right preparation, wet-weather walks can be safe, comfortable, and even enjoyable for both of you.

This guide covers everything you need to know about walking your dog in New Zealand rain and mud, from essential gear to paw protection and post-walk cleanup.

Why You Should Still Walk Your Dog in the Rain

Dogs need daily exercise regardless of the weather. Skipping walks because of rain can lead to:

  • Weight gain — reduced activity adds kilos quickly, especially in winter
  • Destructive behaviour — bored, under-exercised dogs chew furniture, dig holes, and bark excessively
  • Anxiety and restlessness — pent-up energy becomes nervous energy
  • Depression — yes, dogs can get the winter blues too

A 30-minute walk in light rain is perfectly fine for most dogs. The key is being prepared and knowing when to stay home.

Essential Wet Weather Dog Walking Gear

Rain Coats and Body Suits

A good dog rain coat keeps your dog dry and reduces the amount of mud and water they bring home. Look for:

  • Waterproof outer shell with a soft inner lining
  • Adjustable straps around the chest and belly
  • Reflective strips for visibility in low light
  • A belly flap that extends underneath — this is where most mud splashes
Tip: Pet stores across New Zealand stock rain coats sized for local breeds. Measure your dog from the base of the neck to the base of the tail before buying.

Dog Boots

Not every dog needs boots, but they are worth considering if you walk on rough terrain, hot footpaths in summer (yes, the same gear applies), or muddy tracks in winter. Boot options available in NZ include:

  • Stretchy silicone boots — lightweight, good for short walks on wet pavement
  • Neoprene boots — warmer and more durable, ideal for cold muddy trails
  • Rugged sole boots — best for bush walks and rough terrain

Introduce boots gradually. Let your dog wear them indoors for 5-minute sessions before heading outside. Most dogs adjust within a few days.

High-Vis and Lighting

New Zealand winter means shorter daylight hours. If you walk before or after work, you are likely in the dark. Essential visibility gear includes:

  • LED collar lights or clip-on blinkers
  • Reflective harnesses and leads
  • A head torch for yourself so you can see the path ahead
  • Reflective vest for the dog walker (you)

Paw Care in Wet Weather

Dog paws take a beating in rain and mud. Here is how to protect them:

Before the Walk

  • Apply paw balm or paw wax to create a moisture barrier
  • Check for existing cracks or cuts — salt and grit can get into open wounds
  • Trim the fur between paw pads to reduce mud accumulation

After the Walk

  • Rinse paws with warm water immediately — keep a jug by the door
  • Dry thoroughly between each toe
  • Apply paw balm again if the pads look dry or cracked
  • Check for ticks after bush walks — they are more active in humid, wet weather
Warning: Standing water and muddy puddles can contain leptospirosis bacteria, which is present in New Zealand. Avoid letting your dog drink from puddles or stagnant water on walks.

Choosing the Right Walking Route in the Rain

Not all walks are created equal when it rains. Consider these factors:

Surface Matters

  • Sealed paths and footpaths — easiest to walk on, least mud
  • Gravel tracks — drain reasonably well but can be slippery
  • Grass parks — turn to mud quickly, especially popular dog parks
  • Bush tracks — can have tree root hazards and deep mud in low spots

New Zealand Cities With Covered Options

Several NZ cities have walking options that provide some shelter from the rain:

  • Auckland: Cornwall Park has sealed paths; Wynyard Quarter waterfront has covered areas nearby
  • Wellington: The waterfront promenade is exposed but well-drained; botanic gardens have some tree cover
  • Christchurch: Hagley Park paths drain well; Riccarton Bush offers tree shelter
  • Dunedin: The Town Belt tracks can be very muddy — stick to formed paths in the Botanic Garden instead

When to Skip the Walk

Sometimes staying home is the right call. Skip the outdoor walk when:

  • There is a thunderstorm or electrical storm forecast
  • Wind gusts exceed 60 km/h — falling branches are a real danger
  • Your dog is a brachycephalic breed (French Bulldog, Pug, Bulldog) and temperatures are very low
  • Flooding warnings are in place for your area
  • Your dog is recovering from illness or surgery

On those days, try indoor exercise instead: treat puzzles, indoor fetch in a hallway, training sessions, or a snuffle mat.

Post-Walk Cleanup: Keeping the Mud Outside

The biggest challenge of wet-weather dog walking is what happens when you get home. Here is a system that works:

The Mud Station

Set up a dedicated area at your door with:

  • Old towels for the initial wipe-down
  • A portable dog shower or jug of warm water for paw rinsing
  • A mud mat — rubber-backed mats that trap dirt and water
  • Dog-safe wet wipes for quick clean-ups
  • A designated hook for rain coats and leads to dry

The Towel Technique

Work from top to bottom: ears, back, sides, belly, legs, then paws. Use one towel for the body and a separate one for paws (which are the muddiest part). If your dog has a long coat, consider a quick blow-dry on a low, warm setting to prevent that wet-dog smell from settling in.

Protecting Your Floors

  • Keep mud mats at every exterior door
  • Use washable runner rugs in hallways
  • Restrict your dog to hard-floor areas until fully dry
  • Keep a dedicated set of dog towels — do not use your good ones

Indoor Exercise Alternatives for Really Wet Days

When the weather is truly dreadful, you can still give your dog a good workout indoors:

Mental Stimulation

  • Snuffle mats — hide treats in the fabric strips and let your dog forage
  • Kong toys — frozen peanut butter or wet food keeps dogs busy for 30+ minutes
  • Hide and seek — have someone hold your dog, hide in another room, then call them
  • Training sessions — teach a new trick. Mental work tires dogs out as much as physical exercise

Physical Exercise

  • Indoor fetch — use a soft toy in a hallway or garage
  • Stair runs — stand at the top, throw a toy down, let your dog retrieve it (supervise closely)
  • Tug-of-war — great for building impulse control with a "drop it" command
  • Indoor agility — set up a simple course with broom handles, boxes, and cushions
Tip: A 15-minute training session can tire your dog out as much as a 30-minute walk. Mixing mental and physical indoor exercise is the most effective approach on very wet days.

Special Considerations for NZ Conditions

Leptospirosis Risk

Leptospirosis is present in New Zealand, particularly in rural areas and near standing water. The bacteria thrive in warm, wet conditions. Talk to your vet about vaccination if your dog frequently swims or drinks from natural water sources.

Slippery Surfaces

New Zealand has many volcanic and clay-based soils that become extremely slippery when wet. Auckland and Waitakere ranges tracks, Wellington hill paths, and Banks Peninsula trails are notorious for becoming treacherous in rain. Stick to sealed or gravelled paths on wet days.

Stock and Farm Animals

If you walk in rural or semi-rural areas (common on the outskirts of most NZ towns), wet weather drives stock closer to fences. Keep your dog on a lead near paddocks — a wet, muddy dog can spook cattle and sheep, and farmers have the right to control dogs worrying stock.

River and Stream Safety

Rain swells NZ rivers and streams quickly. A shallow stream you crossed yesterday may be waist-deep and fast-flowing today. Never let your dog swim in swollen waterways. Even strong swimmers can be swept away in flood conditions.

When to Use a Pet Sitter

If you are working long hours through winter and struggling to fit in proper walks between the rain, a pet sitter can help. A professional pet sitter will:

  • Walk your dog during the day when rain is lighter or in dry windows
  • Dry and clean your dog properly after every walk
  • Provide indoor play and mental stimulation if outdoor walks are not possible
  • Monitor your dog for any signs of cold-weather illness

Winter is one of the busiest times for pet sitters in New Zealand, so book early — especially in Auckland and Wellington where rain is most frequent.

Quick Checklist: Wet Weather Dog Walk

  • Check the weather forecast and radar before heading out
  • Rain coat on your dog (and yourself)
  • Reflective gear and lights for low-visibility conditions
  • Paw balm applied before the walk
  • Lead (not extendable — more control in wet conditions)
  • Poop bags (they work fine in the rain)
  • Towel and warm water waiting at the door for your return
  • Mud mat in position
