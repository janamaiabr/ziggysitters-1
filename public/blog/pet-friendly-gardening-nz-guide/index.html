Pet-Friendly Gardening in New Zealand: Creating a Safe Outdoor Space for Your Pets

2026-05-04 · 7 min read · ZiggySitters Team

New Zealand gardens are something special — lush, green, and a big part of why we love living here. But if you share your home with a dog or cat, that beautiful backyard can hide surprising dangers. From common ornamental plants that are toxic to pets, to garden chemicals that can cause serious harm, creating a truly pet-safe garden takes more than just good intentions.

The good news? With a bit of planning and the right plant choices, you can build an outdoor space that is both stunning and safe for your furry family members. Here is how to do it, with New Zealand-specific guidance you will not find in generic overseas guides.

Pet-Safe Native Plants for New Zealand Gardens

New Zealand native plants are an excellent foundation for a pet-friendly garden. Many are non-toxic to dogs and cats, and they are perfectly adapted to our climate zones — from the subtropical north of Northland to the cooler, windier conditions of Southland. Choosing natives also supports local biodiversity, which the Department of Conservation (DOC) actively encourages in home gardens.

Here are some reliable pet-safe native options:

  • Pittosporum species — Hardy hedging and screen plants. Pittosporum tenuifolium (kohuhu) is widely available and safe around pets. Great for creating wind shelter in exposed coastal or Canterbury gardens.
  • Hebe species — Low-growing shrubs with attractive flower spikes. Hebe salicifolia and Hebe stricta are non-toxic and provide good ground-level cover without posing a risk to curious dogs.
  • Carex species — Native sedges like Carex testacea (orange sedge) and Carex flagellifera are safe, hardy, and add texture. They thrive in damp areas and are a good choice for Auckland and Waikato clay soils.
  • Coprosma species — Coprosma robusta (karamu) and Coprosma repens (taupata) are tough, pet-safe shrubs. Taupata is particularly good in coastal gardens from the Bay of Plenty to Wellington.
  • Pohuehue (Muehlenbeckia complexa) — A scrambling ground cover that is safe for pets and great for covering banks or fences. It also provides habitat for native lizards, which DOC identifies as a garden biodiversity bonus.
  • Rengarenga lily (Arthropodium cirratum) — Despite the name, this is not a true lily and is non-toxic to cats and dogs. It thrives in shaded spots under taller trees.

Toxic Plants Commonly Found in New Zealand Gardens

Many popular garden plants in New Zealand are dangerous to pets, and some are genuinely lethal. The SPCA NZ regularly sees cases of poisoning from common ornamental species that most homeowners do not realise are harmful.

Highly Toxic — Remove or Fence Off

  • Lily species (Lilium and Hemerocallis) — The single most dangerous plant for cats in New Zealand. All parts — petals, leaves, stems, pollen, even the water in a vase — can cause acute kidney failure. If you have cats, lilies should not exist anywhere on your property. This includes Christmas lilies, which are common in NZ gardens during summer.
  • Oleander (Nerium oleander) — Grows well in warmer NZ regions like Northland and Auckland. Every part is highly toxic to both dogs and cats. Even a small amount can cause heart arrhythmias and death.
  • Rhododendron and Azalea — Widely planted in cooler NZ climates (Otago, Southland, Central Plateau). All parts contain grayanotoxins that cause vomiting, drooling, and potentially fatal heart issues.
  • Foxglove (Digitalis purpurea) — Naturalised in many parts of New Zealand and sometimes deliberately planted. Contains cardiac glycosides; ingestion can be fatal to pets.
  • Arum lily (Zantedeschia aethiopica) — Common in damp NZ gardens and along stream banks. Causes intense oral irritation, swelling, and difficulty swallowing in dogs and cats.
  • Cycads (Cycas and Zamia species) — Popular in subtropical North Island gardens. Seeds and leaves are hepatotoxic and can cause liver failure in dogs.

Moderately Toxic — Use with Caution

  • Daffodil and jonquil bulbs — Common throughout NZ in spring. Bulbs are the most toxic part; can cause severe gastrointestinal upset and cardiac issues.
  • Wisteria — Seeds and pods are toxic if chewed. Common on pergolas in older NZ gardens.
  • Brunfelsia (yesterday-today-tomorrow plant) — Found in warmer NZ gardens. Causes neurological symptoms in dogs, including tremors and seizures.

If you are unsure about a plant in your garden, the SPCA NZ website maintains a toxicity reference list, and your local vet can advise on regional risks.

Hidden Garden Hazards for Pets

Plants are not the only danger. Several common garden products and features pose risks that many Kiwi pet owners overlook.

Chemical Hazards

  • Slug and snail pellets — Metaldehyde-based slug bait is one of the most common causes of pet poisoning in New Zealand. The SPCA and veterinary clinics see cases every spring. Use iron phosphate-based alternatives (like organic slug pellets), which are far safer. Even better, use beer traps or copper tape around vulnerable plants.
  • Cocoa mulch — Available at some NZ garden centres. Contains theobromine, the same compound that makes chocolate toxic to dogs. Dogs are attracted to the smell. Use bark or wood chip mulch instead.
  • Lawn care products — Many weed-and-feed products contain chemicals harmful to pets. Look for pet-safe options at your local garden centre, and keep pets off treated areas for at least 48 hours. Organic options like corn gluten meal for weed prevention are effective and completely safe.
  • Compost — Mouldy compost can contain tremorgenic mycotoxins that cause seizures in dogs. Keep compost bins securely covered or fenced off, and never add dairy, meat, or cooked food that accelerates mould growth.

Physical Hazards

  • Water features — Even shallow ponds can be a drowning risk for small pets and puppies. Install mesh covers or ensure easy exit points (ramps or low edges). Stagnant water also harbour bacteria and mosquito larvae.
  • Garden tools — Rakes, shears, and secateurs left on the ground cause injuries. Store tools in a shed or hang them up after use.
  • Sharp mulches — Some stone and shell mulches can cut paw pads. Use rounded river stones or soft wood chip instead of sharp gravel.

Designing for Dogs: Dig Zones and Boundaries

Dogs need outdoor enrichment as much as they need safety. A well-designed dog-friendly garden gives them space to be dogs without destroying your flower beds.

Create a Dedicated Dig Zone

Set aside a corner of the garden — perhaps behind the shed or along a side fence — as an approved digging area. Loosen the soil, mix in some sand for easier digging, and bury a few toys or treats to encourage use. When your dog digs elsewhere, redirect them to this zone. It satisfies their natural instinct and saves your garden beds.

Boundary Design

New Zealand properties often have a mix of fencing types. For dog safety:

  • Check that gaps under gates and fences are too narrow for your dog to squeeze through. Coastal properties with uneven terrain often have gaps that small dogs can escape through.
  • Raised garden beds with low timber edging define dog-free zones clearly and are easy to install on typical NZ clay or volcanic soils.
  • Consider chicken wire or mesh barriers buried 15-20cm underground along fence lines if your dog is a digger. This prevents escape without damaging the fence.

Designing for Cats: Safe Boundaries and Plant Choices

Cats are harder to garden for because they climb, chew, and explore differently than dogs. The lily issue alone makes cat-safe garden design a matter of life and death.

  • Eliminate all lily species — This cannot be overstated. If a neighbour has lilies that overhang your fence, speak with them or install a barrier on your side.
  • Plant cat-friendly herbs — Catnip (Nepeta cataria) and catmint (Nepeta faassenii) are safe and provide enrichment. Valerian and wheatgrass are also good options that give cats safe greens to chew.
  • Avoid loose netting — Cats can become entangled in bird netting over fruit trees or garden beds. Use rigid mesh instead.
  • Provide outdoor shade and shelter — Cats need cool retreats in summer. Dense shrubs like coprosma or a covered cat shelter give them safe resting spots away from the sun.

Seasonal Considerations for Pet-Safe Gardens

Autumn and Winter Prep

As temperatures drop across New Zealand from April onwards, several garden tasks need pet-awareness:

  • Leaf accumulation — Wet autumn leaves can harbour mould and bacteria. Rake regularly from areas where pets walk, especially in damp South Island and West Coast gardens.
  • Frost protection products — Some frost sprays contain chemicals unsafe for pets. Use physical barriers like frost cloth instead.
  • Rat and mouse control — As rodents move indoors in autumn, many Kiwi households set bait stations. These are extremely dangerous to pets. Use enclosed, tamper-proof bait stations, or better yet, use snap traps in pet-inaccessible locations.
  • Winter mulch — Avoid cocoa mulch in winter garden prep. Stick with pea straw, which is widely available in NZ and completely pet-safe.

Spring and Summer

  • Christmas lily season (December-February) — If you have cats, be vigilant. Well-meaning friends may bring lily bouquets into your home. Decline them firmly.
  • Hot pavement — In regions like Hawke's Bay and Central Otago, summer ground temperatures can burn paw pads. Provide grass pathways and shaded areas.
  • Grass seeds (awns) — Barley grass and other seed heads in late summer can burrow into paws, ears, and nostrils. Keep lawns mowed and check your dog after outdoor time, especially in rural or lifestyle block areas.

What Your Pet Sitter Needs to Know About Your Garden

If you are going away and have arranged for a pet sitter, taking a few minutes to brief them on your garden can prevent emergencies. Here is what to share:

  • Toxic plant locations — If you have any toxic plants you have not yet removed, mark their location and tell your sitter to keep pets away from those areas.
  • Dig zone location — Show the sitter where the approved digging area is so they can redirect your dog if needed.
  • Chemical storage — Point out where garden chemicals are stored and confirm they are securely closed. Ideally, lock the garden shed.
  • Water features — Explain any safety features (mesh covers, ramps) or restrictions around ponds and pools.
  • Compost and green waste areas — Let the sitter know which bins or areas are off-limits to pets.
  • Emergency contacts — Provide your regular vet's number and the nearest after-hours emergency clinic. The SPCA NZ also has a poison advice line.

A good pet sitter will appreciate this information. It shows you take your pet's safety seriously and helps them provide the best care while you are away.

Building Your Pet-Safe NZ Garden: A Quick Checklist

  • Remove or fence off all lily species if you have cats
  • Replace metaldehyde slug pellets with iron phosphate or organic alternatives
  • Choose pet-safe native plants: pittosporum, hebe, carex, coprosma, rengarenga lily
  • Eliminate oleander, foxglove, and rhododendron from pet-accessible areas
  • Create a designated dig zone for dogs
  • Switch cocoa mulch to bark, wood chip, or pea straw
  • Secure compost bins and garden chemical storage
  • Add safe exit points to water features
  • Brief your pet sitter on garden hazards and safe zones
  • Use pet-safe lawn care products and observe post-application waiting periods

Creating a pet-friendly garden in New Zealand is not about giving up on beauty — it is about making smart choices. Our native plants offer stunning, pet-safe options that thrive in local conditions. Removing a few toxic species and swapping harmful chemicals for safer alternatives takes an afternoon but could save your pet's life. Start with the biggest risks (lilies and slug bait), then work through the rest at your own pace. Your pets will thank you — probably by rolling in something they should not, but safely.

Key Takeaways

  • New Zealand native plants like pittosporum, hebe, carex, coprosma, and rengarenga lily are beautiful, hardy, and safe for pets
  • Lilies are lethal to cats — even pollen or vase water can cause kidney failure; remove all lily species from your property
  • Metaldehyde slug pellets and cocoa mulch are among the most common causes of garden pet poisoning in NZ — switch to safer alternatives
  • Create dedicated dig zones for dogs and clear boundaries to protect garden beds while giving pets enrichment
  • Always brief your pet sitter on garden hazards, toxic plant locations, chemical storage, and emergency vet contacts before you travel
