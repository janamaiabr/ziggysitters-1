Pet First Aid for Tramping and Hiking in New Zealand: The Essential Guide

25 July 2026 — ZiggySitters

New Zealand is a tramping paradise — from the misty valleys of the Milford Track to the volcanic slopes of Tongariro. If you hike with your dog, you already know the joy of watching them bound through native bush or cool off in an alpine stream. But the backcountry does not care about your plans. Cuts, ticks, heat stress, and worse can happen miles from the nearest road. Knowing pet first aid for the trail is not optional — it is the difference between a story you laugh about later and an emergency vet dash that ends badly.

This guide covers the injuries and hazards most likely to affect dogs on NZ trails, how to treat them in the field, and what to pack so you are never caught unprepared.

Build Your Trail Dog First-Aid Kit

Before you think about techniques, get the gear. A dedicated dog first-aid kit lives in your pack on every tramp. It does not need to be large — a zip pouch the size of a sunglasses case covers the essentials.

Essentials

  • Gauze pads (non-stick, 5 cm x 5 cm) — for wound coverage and pressure
  • Vet wrap (2 rolls) — self-adhesive bandage that sticks to itself, not fur
  • Antiseptic wipes — individually sealed, for wound cleaning
  • Saline flush (30 ml squeeze bottle) — the safest way to irrigate debris from eyes and wounds
  • Tweezers — fine-tipped, for tick and splinter removal
  • Tick hook — the plastic hook-and-twist kind; faster and safer than tweezers for embedded ticks
  • Blunt-nosed scissors — for cutting bandages and tape
  • Adhesive tape (micropore) — for securing dressings
  • Styptic powder — stops bleeding from torn nails fast
  • Digital thermometer — dog normal range is 38.0-39.2 degrees Celsius; above 40 degrees is an emergency
  • Emergency space blanket — for shock and hypothermia
  • Muzzle or soft restraint — injured dogs bite, even gentle ones
  • Vet phone number card — include your regular vet and the nearest after-hours clinic for your tramping region

Nice-to-Have Additions

  • Hydrogel burn dressing — for camp stove or hot surface burns
  • Tick twister card — flat, credit-card-sized, fits in a wallet
  • LED headlamp — for night emergencies (you likely already carry one)
  • Disposable gloves — hygiene for wound management
  • Water purification tablets — if you need to use stream water for wound irrigation

Check your kit every six months. Replace antiseptic wipes and saline before they expire. Vet wrap loses elasticity over time — swap it yearly.

The Five Most Common Trail Injuries (And How to Treat Them)

1. Paw Pad Cuts and Abrasions

Paw pads take a beating on NZ trails. Sharp scree, cutty grass, shell fragments on coastal tracks, and volcanic rock on the Central Plateau can slice or abrade pads quickly. You will notice your dog limping, licking a paw obsessively, or leaving blood prints on the track.

Field treatment:

  1. Stop and assess. If the cut is superficial (no deep flap of pad), clean it with saline or clean water.
  2. Apply antiseptic wipe around the wound.
  3. Cover with a non-stick gauze pad and secure with vet wrap. Wrap firmly but not tight — you should be able to slide a finger under the bandage.
  4. If the pad is deeply cut or a flap is hanging, do not attempt to trim or stitch it. Cover gently, minimise walking, and carry your dog out if possible. Deep pad injuries need a vet.

Prevention: Dog hiking boots (yes, they exist) protect pads on rough terrain. Condition your dog's pads with shorter walks on gravel before big tramps. Avoid running your dog on hot tarmac or sharp pumice.

2. Ticks and Tick-Borne Disease

New Zealand ticks are not the Lyme disease carriers you find in North America, but they can transmit Theileria (tick fever) to dogs, which causes anaemia, lethargy, and jaundice. Ticks are most active from spring through autumn, particularly in long grass and scrubby areas — exactly where your dog loves to sniff.

Field treatment:

  1. Part the fur and locate the tick. Use a tick hook or fine-tipped tweezers.
  2. Slide the hook between the tick and the skin, then twist gently and lift. The tick should come away with its mouthparts intact.
  3. If the head remains embedded, do not dig for it. Clean the area and monitor — the body will usually expel it naturally.
  4. Save the tick in a sealed bag. If your dog becomes lethargic or pale-gummed in the following weeks, your vet can identify the species.

Prevention: Use a vet-recommended tick prevention product (oral or spot-on) before tramping season. Check your dog thoroughly after every hike — ticks favour the ears, neck, armpits, and between toes.

3. Heat Stress and Overheating

New Zealand summers can be deceptively hot, especially on exposed ridgelines and riverbeds where there is no shade. Dogs cannot sweat — they cool themselves by panting, which is far less efficient than human sweating. Brachycephalic breeds (French bulldogs, pugs, bulldogs) are at particular risk.

Signs of heat stress: Excessive panting, thick saliva, bright red gums, stumbling, vomiting, collapse.

Field treatment:

  1. Stop immediately. Find shade or create it with a pack and jacket.
  2. Offer small amounts of cool water — do not let them gulp.
  3. Wet your dog with stream water or pour water over their belly, armpits, and groin. These areas dissipate heat fastest.
  4. If you have an emergency blanket, place it shiny-side-out over a shaded spot to reflect heat away.
  5. Monitor temperature if you have a thermometer. Below 40 degrees with improving demeanour means you can continue slowly. Above 40.5 degrees or worsening signs — evacuate to a vet immediately.

Prevention: Start early in the morning. Carry more water than you think you need. Plan routes with stream crossings. Take shade breaks every 30 minutes in hot conditions. Leave brachycephalic breeds at home on days over 25 degrees.

4. Wasp and Bee Stings

German wasps and common wasps are abundant in NZ beech forests, particularly from January through April. Dogs sniffing in the undergrowth are prime targets for stings on the face, muzzle, and inside the mouth. Most stings cause local swelling and pain, but some dogs are allergic and can go into anaphylactic shock.

Field treatment:

  1. If you see a stinger, scrape it off with a credit card or fingernail — do not squeeze it with tweezers, which injects more venom.
  2. Apply a cold compress (a wet bandana or stream-soaked cloth) to reduce swelling.
  3. Watch for anaphylaxis: facial swelling that closes the airway, difficulty breathing, collapse, vomiting. If any of these occur, get to a vet immediately. This is a life-threatening emergency.
  4. For mild swelling only, monitor your dog for 30 minutes. If swelling worsens or they become distressed, cut the tramp short.

If your dog has had a severe reaction before, ask your vet about carrying an EpiPen-style auto-injector on tramps.

5. Sprains, Strains, and Lameness

Uneven ground, root networks, and slippery river crossings mean your dog can twist a leg or strain a muscle just like you can. You will see a sudden limp, favouring one leg, or reluctance to bear weight.

Field treatment:

  1. Stop and rest. Most mild sprains improve with 10-15 minutes of rest.
  2. Check the affected leg for cuts, swelling, or foreign bodies (grass seeds between toes are common).
  3. If the limp resolves after rest, continue slowly and monitor. Take shorter rest breaks more frequently.
  4. If the dog cannot bear weight at all, or is clearly in pain (whimpering, guarding the leg), do not continue. Carry out if small enough, or send someone for help. A torn cruciate ligament will only get worse with continued walking.

NZ-Specific Trail Hazards

No Snakes — But Plenty of Other Risks

New Zealand has no native land snakes, which is one less thing to worry about. But the backcountry has its own set of hazards:

  • Stock on DOC tracks: Cattle and sheep on unfenced sections of tracks. Dogs can be kicked or trampled. Keep your dog on a lead near livestock.
  • 1080 bait drops: DOC periodically drops 1080 bait for possum control. Dogs are extremely sensitive to 1080 — even a small amount is lethal. Check the DOC website for upcoming drops in your tramping area and avoid those zones. If your dog eats bait or a carcass near a drop zone, go to a vet immediately. There is no effective first aid for 1080 poisoning — it requires immediate veterinary treatment.
  • Wasp nests: As above, abundant in beech forest. Watch for ground nests on track edges.
  • Toxic plants: Karaka berries (in autumn), tutu (in summer), and ngaio are all toxic to dogs. Keep your dog from foraging.
  • Rivers and flash floods: NZ rivers rise fast after rain. Do not attempt crossings with your dog in flood. If your dog is swept away, do not risk your own life attempting a rescue.

DOC Dog Rules

Not all DOC tracks allow dogs. Many national parks, conservation areas, and kiwi zones prohibit dogs entirely, even on a lead. Check the DOC website before you go. In permitted areas:

  • Dogs must be on a lead at all times on public conservation land
  • You must carry a lead, even if your dog is off-lead on a designated beach or dog exercise area
  • Remove and carry out all dog waste
  • Dogs must not disturb wildlife, especially ground-nesting birds

When to Turn Back

Sometimes the bravest thing is turning around. Head for home or the trailhead if:

  • Your dog cannot bear weight on a leg
  • Temperature exceeds 40.5 degrees and is not dropping with cooling
  • Facial swelling is closing the airway after a sting
  • A deep cut will not stop bleeding after 10 minutes of pressure
  • Your dog collapses, has a seizure, or is non-responsive
  • Weather is deteriorating and you are more than an hour from shelter
  • You suspect 1080 exposure

There is no shame in a shortened tramp. The mountain will be there next weekend.

Before You Go: Pre-Trip Checklist

  • Verify your dog is permitted on the track (check DOC website)
  • Check for 1080 drops in the area
  • Pack your trail first-aid kit
  • Ensure tick prevention is current
  • Know the location and hours of the nearest vet to your trailhead
  • Tell someone your route and expected return time
  • Carry enough water for both of you (1 litre per hour of walking for your dog in warm conditions)
  • Condition your dog progressively — do not go from couch to 6-hour tramp

Final Thoughts

Tramping with your dog is one of the best things about living in New Zealand. The trails are spectacular, the access is generous, and the adventure is real. But adventure means risk, and risk means preparation. A small first-aid kit and a bit of knowledge weigh almost nothing and can save your dog's life. Pack it. Learn it. Hope you never need it — and be ready if you do.
